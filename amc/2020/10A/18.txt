<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/e/faedb9d30dc3763d92cbcacf2cc17e2bf64f3e8b.png" class="latex" alt="$(a,b,c,d)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="71" height="18" /> be an ordered quadruple of not necessarily distinct integers, each one of them in the set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/d/5cd00e351e2ec1689429fd086a4f1e7d78078d32.png" class="latex" alt="${0,1,2,3}.$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="64" height="16" /> For how many such quadruples is it true that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/5/7e5a374db4f793a955f3490cda70d8239bc93968.png" class="latex" alt="$a\cdot d-b\cdot c$" width="83" height="12" /> is odd? (For example, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/a/d2aceb2e11c79a1014a715aac58241fbeb85a572.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,3,1,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="73" height="18" /> is one such quadruple, because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/9/8e9b642cbb414575ecc1ffc95e193c30357d6cc5.png" class="latex" alt="$0\cdot 1-3\cdot 1 = -3$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="131" height="13" /> is odd.)
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/5/d751f3cc5b69feb21b57c8f190bd119648cb80d5.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 48 \qquad \textbf{(B) } 64 \qquad \textbf{(C) } 96 \qquad \textbf{(D) } 128 \qquad \textbf{(E) } 192$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="416" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions">Solutions</span></h2>
<h3><span id="Solution_1_(Parity)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1_.28Parity.29">Solution 1 (Parity)</span></h3>
<p>In order for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/5/7e5a374db4f793a955f3490cda70d8239bc93968.png" class="latex" alt="$a\cdot d-b\cdot c$" width="83" height="12" /> to be odd, consider parity. We must have (even)-(odd) or (odd)-(even). There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/5/265bc1cf76167551c0a22d54a46050f19d6e7d61.png" class="latex" alt="$2(2 + 4) = 12$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="105" height="18" /> ways to pick numbers to obtain an even product. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/6/8b69aa4309847d47e4aed15a0b00891106fb1807.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot 2 = 4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="64" height="12" /> ways to obtain an odd product. Therefore, the total amount of ways to make <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/5/7e5a374db4f793a955f3490cda70d8239bc93968.png" class="latex" alt="$a\cdot d-b\cdot c$" width="83" height="12" /> odd is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/6/2163dca4907d2e17cfa6148b938aa52fa4637936.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot (12 \cdot 4) = \boxed{\bold{(C)}\ 96}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="165" height="29" />.
</p><p>-Midnight
</p>
<h3><span id="Solution_2_(Basically_Solution_1_but_more_in-depth)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2_.28Basically_Solution_1_but_more_in-depth.29">Solution 2 (Basically Solution 1 but more in-depth)</span></h3>
<p>Consider parity. We need exactly one term to be odd, one term to be even. Because of symmetry, we can set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/f/5df74cf2db193657a668ecbe12f59c0630c0fab2.png" class="latex" alt="$ad$" width="19" height="12" /> to be odd and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/2/fe207dd510fa3e2012d9747d93de6f9cc71bf3f6.png" class="latex" alt="$bc$" width="16" height="12" /> to be even, then multiply by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/7/e4797950b5e812b65381cbc5fc33ad2db5f57aff.png" class="latex" alt="$2.$" width="12" height="12" /> If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/f/5df74cf2db193657a668ecbe12f59c0630c0fab2.png" class="latex" alt="$ad$" width="19" height="12" /> is odd, both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> must be odd, therefore there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/a/6ba992ff879ca3baa77e206d794cf1ce76802bc1.png" class="latex" alt="$2\cdot2=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="64" height="12" /> possibilities for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/a/cdad1cca352a1b578cac3f299c75b127ac4705be.png" class="latex" alt="$ad.$" width="22" height="12" /> Consider <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/9/a89d4116debdce283fd9be357d46a4eb83540aa9.png" class="latex" alt="$bc.$" width="19" height="12" /> Let us say that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> is even. Then there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/f/a9f2aab8c71f7caee70a3e1cd0db136459243229.png" class="latex" alt="$2\cdot4=8$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="64" height="13" /> possibilities for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/9/a89d4116debdce283fd9be357d46a4eb83540aa9.png" class="latex" alt="$bc.$" width="19" height="12" /> However, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> can be odd, in which case we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/a/6ba992ff879ca3baa77e206d794cf1ce76802bc1.png" class="latex" alt="$2\cdot2=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="64" height="12" /> more possibilities for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/9/a89d4116debdce283fd9be357d46a4eb83540aa9.png" class="latex" alt="$bc.$" width="19" height="12" /> Thus there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> ways for us to choose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/2/fe207dd510fa3e2012d9747d93de6f9cc71bf3f6.png" class="latex" alt="$bc$" width="16" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> ways for us to choose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/a/cdad1cca352a1b578cac3f299c75b127ac4705be.png" class="latex" alt="$ad.$" width="22" height="12" /> Therefore, also considering symmetry, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/9/1091f215c9dddf33dfce69fb8e181aab9faae53b.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot 4 \cdot 12= \boxed{ \text{ (C) } 96}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="154" height="30" /> total values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/8/8382cb09349cc278d79f0313b4edb14f153cd90b.png" class="latex" alt="$ad-bc.$" width="61" height="12" />
</p><p>~lpieleanu (reformatting and minor edits)

<h3><span id="Solution_3_(Complementary_Counting)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3_.28Complementary_Counting.29">Solution 3 (Complementary Counting)</span></h3>
<p>There are 4 ways to choose any number independently and 2 ways to choose any odd number independently.
To get an even products, we count: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/5/075b029d48dc299ee65ae20bd441da1889082fae.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{P(any number)} \cdot \text{P(any number)}-\text{P(odd)}\cdot\text{P(odd)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="370" height="17" />, which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/f/ebf9cc5825328924c19cd2152d8ae34647366ba6.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \cdot 4 - 2 \cdot 2=12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="126" height="12" />.
The number of ways to get an odd product can be counted like so: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/8/fa800d1ee5d53d3d7c424373f5c188f01ce75ca6.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{P(odd)}\cdot\text{P(odd)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="114" height="17" />, which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/d/00d8ec46cc4acc7d15403c12ded759eed182f962.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot 2$" width="30" height="12" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />.
So, for one product to be odd the other to be even: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/c/61cac223c0891e5c005d8a853b8420336617cd64.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot 4 \cdot 12=\boxed{(C)96}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="145" height="29" />(order matters). 
~ Anonymous and Arctic_Bunny
</p>
<h3><span id="Solution_4_(Solution_3_but_more_in-depth)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4_.28Solution_3_but_more_in-depth.29">Solution 4 (Solution 3 but more in-depth)</span></h3>
<p>We use complementary counting: If the difference is even, then we can subtract those cases. There are a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/6/8363ed69eec2c796d736ca92fd66766d63548981.png" class="latex" alt="$4^4=256$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="67" height="15" /> cases.
</p><p>For an even difference, we have (even)-(even) or (odd-odd). 
</p><p>From Solution 3:
</p><p>"There are 4 ways to choose any number independently and 2 ways to choose any odd number independently.
even products:(number)*(number)-(odd)*(odd): <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/f/ebf9cc5825328924c19cd2152d8ae34647366ba6.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \cdot 4 - 2 \cdot 2=12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="126" height="12" />.
odd products: (odd)*(odd): <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/4/10466e3bd19a3228fc80c21b754af62ac7e74ebd.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot 2 =4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="64" height="12" />."
</p><p>With this, we easily calculate <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/7/b072c18b424a9c2f4bb8774bfa5c8d53faa9bc88.png" class="latex" alt="$256-12^2-4^2=\textbf{(C)96}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="185" height="19" />.
</p><p>~kevinmathz
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5-Casework">Solution 5-Casework</span></h3>
<p>As in solution 1, we must have (even)-(odd) or (odd)-(even). We see that there are two cases, if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/f/5df74cf2db193657a668ecbe12f59c0630c0fab2.png" class="latex" alt="$ad$" width="19" height="12" /> is even and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/2/fe207dd510fa3e2012d9747d93de6f9cc71bf3f6.png" class="latex" alt="$bc$" width="16" height="12" /> is odd and if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/f/5df74cf2db193657a668ecbe12f59c0630c0fab2.png" class="latex" alt="$ad$" width="19" height="12" /> is odd and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/2/fe207dd510fa3e2012d9747d93de6f9cc71bf3f6.png" class="latex" alt="$bc$" width="16" height="12" /> is even. Because of symmetry, we can multiply by two for when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/f/5df74cf2db193657a668ecbe12f59c0630c0fab2.png" class="latex" alt="$ad$" width="19" height="12" /> is odd and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/2/fe207dd510fa3e2012d9747d93de6f9cc71bf3f6.png" class="latex" alt="$bc$" width="16" height="12" /> is even. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/a/a3a59bb1293ee3f6dec19de4019a7178874219ae.png" class="latex" alt="$e$" width="8" height="8" /> denote an even number and let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/a/3eabe6c97f03e413d1aa3c292e9478f286e64df1.png" class="latex" alt="$o$" width="9" height="8" /> denote an odd number. 
</p><p>If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/f/5df74cf2db193657a668ecbe12f59c0630c0fab2.png" class="latex" alt="$ad$" width="19" height="12" /> is even and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/2/fe207dd510fa3e2012d9747d93de6f9cc71bf3f6.png" class="latex" alt="$bc$" width="16" height="12" /> is odd, there are three cases:
</p><p><br />

<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/8/bb89ef9a02927ae1a383a0c4f80e12a3c46790fd.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(e,o,o,o)\]" width="70" height="18" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/f/94fd5329e8a4177cd3bed61773a9d7ab892fbe85.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(o,o,o,e)\]" width="70" height="18" />
</p><p><br />
For each of these cases, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/0/420813c47b9ba0edd71bba84716bd485967ac81a.png" class="latex" alt="$2^4$" width="15" height="15" /> ways to choose from the set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/6/b16da376bf1f6d1397ce26c6ff660a91ec51ecf4.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,1,2,3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="73" height="18" /> as there are 2 even's and 2 odd's; because there are three cases, we multiply this by 3. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Additional_Note_1">Additional Note 1</span></h3>
<p>When calculating the number of even products and odd products, since the only way to get an odd product is to multiply two odd integers together, and there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> odd integers, it can quickly be deduced that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/6/8b69aa4309847d47e4aed15a0b00891106fb1807.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot 2 = 4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="64" height="12" /> possibilities for an odd product. Since the product must be either odd or even, and there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/0/6/c0637a8a49dd1bc93c7ea16d0b1d22c26f9714e5.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \cdot 4 = 16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="73" height="13" /> ways to choose factors for the product, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/5/4f5259e7ccf508eb0d8b90f04b203a4a75f21692.png" class="latex" alt="$16 - 4 = 12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="91" height="13" /> possibilities for an even product. ~<a href="/wiki/index.php/User:Emerald_block" title="User:Emerald block">emerald_block</a>
</p>
