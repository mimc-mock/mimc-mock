<p>As shown in the figure below, a regular dodecahedron (the polyhedron consisting of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> congruent regular pentagonal faces) floats in space with two horizontal faces. Note that there is a ring of five slanted faces adjacent to the top face, and a ring of five slanted faces adjacent to the bottom face. How many ways are there to move from the top face to the bottom face via a sequence of adjacent faces so that each face is visited at most once and moves are not permitted from the bottom ring to the top ring?

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Since we start at the top face and end at the bottom face without moving from the lower ring to the upper ring or revisiting a face, our journey must consist of the top face, a series of faces in the upper ring, a series of faces in the lower ring, and the bottom face, in that order.
</p><p>We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> choices for which face we visit first on the top ring. From there, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> choices for how far around the top ring we go before moving down: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/1/1614f2a4d8d2f0e92b1b76f39417aacfbaba9e2c.png" class="latex" alt="$1,2,3,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="47" height="16" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> faces around clockwise, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/1/1614f2a4d8d2f0e92b1b76f39417aacfbaba9e2c.png" class="latex" alt="$1,2,3,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="47" height="16" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> faces around counterclockwise, or immediately going down to the lower ring without visiting any other faces in the upper ring.
</p><p>We then have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> choices for which lower ring face to visit first (since every upper-ring face is adjacent to exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> lower-ring faces) and then once again <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> choices for how to travel around the lower ring. We then proceed to the bottom face, completing the trip.
</p><p>Multiplying together all the numbers of choices we have, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/2/38216ecde96b4b36e9603d333832c1726484c918.png" class="latex" alt="$5 \cdot 9 \cdot 2 \cdot 9 = \boxed{\textbf{(E) } 810}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="172" height="28" />.
</p>
