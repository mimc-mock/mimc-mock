<p>A positive integer divisor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/6/e56d51636d8b4eee856c5409e18de61ac06b40ff.png" class="latex" alt="$12!$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="21" height="13" /> is chosen at random. The probability that the divisor chosen is a perfect square can be expressed as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/f/27f583697295fc6f723862c13228ee55107c33ca.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{m}{n}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="18" height="33" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> are relatively prime positive integers. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/d/02dbab3601fa6cce490d54263017048f69e1a35d.png" class="latex" alt="$m+n$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="48" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/5/635f84a378488ea1de6ddcbf5081ba779eaad50d.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 3\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 5\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 12\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 18\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 23$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="376" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>The prime factorization of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/6/e56d51636d8b4eee856c5409e18de61ac06b40ff.png" class="latex" alt="$12!$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="21" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/8/6c842a03ba520fb9e01f87ebd52ad313b1da3b6c.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{10} \cdot 3^5 \cdot 5^2 \cdot 7 \cdot 11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="135" height="15" />. 
This yields a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/a/b1af856dd06a4208e14bd8f30416e7e0d7b49601.png" class="latex" alt="$11 \cdot 6 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 2$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="106" height="13" /> divisors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/4/6d42d092b1949f37b87c923023bfe1e624539cad.png" class="latex" alt="$12!.$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />
In order to produce a perfect square divisor, there must be an even exponent for each number in the prime factorization. Note that the divisor can't have any factors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/8/c6878713578626763c38433b3f4c8c2205ad0c15.png" class="latex" alt="$11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> in the prime factorization because there is only one of each in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/4/6d42d092b1949f37b87c923023bfe1e624539cad.png" class="latex" alt="$12!.$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> Thus, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/4/5c412028020034e2bbf482dd09645d6d2ff3b824.png" class="latex" alt="$6 \cdot 3 \cdot 2$" width="53" height="12" /> perfect squares. (For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, you can have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />s, etc.)
The probability that the divisor chosen is a perfect square is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/4/db4113777e6a63ed02b4da07eadda444458a7ab4.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{6\cdot 3\cdot 2}{11\cdot 6\cdot 3\cdot 2\cdot 2}=\frac{1}{22} \implies \frac{m}{n}=\frac{1}{22} \implies m\ +\ n = 1\ +\ 22 = \boxed{\textbf{(E) } 23 }\]" width="530" height="38" />
</p><p>~mshell214, edited by Rzhpamath
</p>
