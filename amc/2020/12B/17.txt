<p>How many polynomials of the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/b/e2b6d9466174a05e56d1e80dca0956b09d73c54e.png" class="latex" alt="$x^5 + ax^4 + bx^3 + cx^2 + dx + 2020$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="263" height="16" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> are real numbers, have the property that whenever <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> is a root, so is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/9/ad9296c5631d66871cd69a87f44003f8af5714f9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{-1+i\sqrt{3}}{2} \cdot r$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="101" height="41" />? (Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/9/75952e52a3dc061d24146c0df9062a54a5ce5a5e.png" class="latex" alt="$i=\sqrt{-1}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="69" height="18" />)
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174e8d98e88e1546423c59de54c9af327b5d8eae.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 0 \qquad \textbf{(B) }1 \qquad \textbf{(C) } 2 \qquad \textbf{(D) } 3 \qquad \textbf{(E) } 4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="353" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/a/88ac2b64f8f8c2921162c7a081b50fbc0cc7df4b.png" class="latex" alt="$P(x) = x^5+ax^4+bx^3+cx^2+dx+2020$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="326" height="19" />. We first notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/8/fc8dfad0238efb2114df16ce854a7db0002173eb.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{-1+i\sqrt{3}}{2} = e^{2\pi i / 3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="145" height="41" />.  That is because of Euler's Formula&#160;: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/5/a1566689b11e8fa8d1c3935d78219df0013951ae.png" class="latex" alt="$e^{ix} = \cos(x) + i \cdot \sin(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="181" height="19" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/d/76d4203d19162753092662883ea87292eba67496.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{-1+i\sqrt{3}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="78" height="41" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/6/d36424546e91debd89214496789e6a9b08659d2d.png" class="latex" alt="$-\frac{1}{2} + i \cdot \frac {\sqrt{3}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="95" height="41" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/e/faeac4c20500ae262b259964340a924ad1005c64.png" class="latex" alt="$\cos(120^\circ) + i \cdot \sin(120^\circ) = e^{ 2\pi i / 3}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="251" height="20" />.
</p><p>In order <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> to be a root of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/2/bc25b8077cd902b49fe996e163333c8be207e9e6.png" class="latex" alt="$re^{2\pi i / 3}$" width="49" height="16" /> must also be a root of P, meaning that 3 of the roots of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/4/62445951346653d4cad39abac99cf9d6799cb7dc.png" class="latex" alt="$re^{i\frac{2\pi}{3}}$" width="37" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/6/b46e964b1ce4236d2f25c7360280653b921b910d.png" class="latex" alt="$re^{i\frac{4\pi}{3}}$" width="37" height="18" />. However, since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> is degree 5, there must be two additional roots. Let one of these roots be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/e/9ee4b825a2e36ae093ed7be5e4851ef453b34914.png" class="latex" alt="$w$" width="13" height="8" />, if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/e/9ee4b825a2e36ae093ed7be5e4851ef453b34914.png" class="latex" alt="$w$" width="13" height="8" /> is a root, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/1/7415072be4071a014ec5745b0a28f76d9fec6ae6.png" class="latex" alt="$we^{2\pi i / 3}$" width="54" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/5/585911b7f5b96674ac14fac1e82ff9847183eee7.png" class="latex" alt="$we^{4\pi i / 3}$" width="54" height="16" /> must also be roots. However, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> is a fifth degree polynomial, and can therefore only have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> roots. This implies that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/e/9ee4b825a2e36ae093ed7be5e4851ef453b34914.png" class="latex" alt="$w$" width="13" height="8" /> is either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/2/bc25b8077cd902b49fe996e163333c8be207e9e6.png" class="latex" alt="$re^{2\pi i / 3}$" width="49" height="16" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/6/0d6617b892b45b26a4d4e08cf17affdcd0b9a360.png" class="latex" alt="$re^{4\pi i / 3}$" width="49" height="16" />. Thus we know that the polynomial <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> can be written in the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/1/e912f16d9107f08408a8a5201e4d905c25e53fa8.png" class="latex" alt="$(x-r)^m(x-re^{2\pi i / 3})^n(x-re^{4\pi i / 3})^p$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="279" height="20" />. Moreover, by Vieta's, we know that there is only one possible value for the magnitude of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f50e5f1c13786a798392cd2f47067f568693dea2.png" class="latex" alt="$||r||^5 = 2020$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="96" height="19" />, meaning that the amount of possible polynomials <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> is equivalent to the possible sets <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/9/7790c624ee285273f63771ee45b335198488b0a3.png" class="latex" alt="$(m,n,p)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="64" height="18" />. In order for the coefficients of the polynomial to all be real, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/b/93b0cd0e4226853aa7497c62ecc2ea0181109137.png" class="latex" alt="$n = p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="11" /> due to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/2/bc25b8077cd902b49fe996e163333c8be207e9e6.png" class="latex" alt="$re^{2\pi i / 3}$" width="49" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/6/94678d10489a279329b43038df71fff622b2bdfb.png" class="latex" alt="$re^{4 \pi i / 3}$" width="49" height="16" /> being conjugates and since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/8/1f89fa7b503091f5e09a9ead815eeffa0cf6764c.png" class="latex" alt="$m+n+p = 5$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="113" height="16" />, (as the polynomial is 5th degree) we have two possible solutions for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/f/7ef02dac342fced80407fbebb00514b2c931ef59.png" class="latex" alt="$(m, n, p)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="64" height="18" /> which are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/f/12f6451c56971edb3106b28f513ebf12afc9f1d8.png" class="latex" alt="$(1,2,2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="56" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/0/530aabd61c34add287fbad3d9dee71157f32a4c3.png" class="latex" alt="$(3,1,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="56" height="18" /> yielding two possible polynomials. The answer is thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/2/11205d1d11ae4f2570176638fc529a7196bdb91b.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) } 2}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="55" height="28" />.
</p><p>~Murtagh
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/d/40dc25f36b35ff8fb05c70baf234a160bed1a5cc.png" class="latex" alt="$x_1=r$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="50" height="10" />, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/8/3e824b5abf5dd5222cface3eb7049df65a4b9c92.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x_2=\frac{-1+i\sqrt{3}}{2} r,\]" width="133" height="41" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/2/972da1e947069838125434cf46fad9bc9f308357.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x_3=\left( \frac{-1+i\sqrt{3}}{2} \right) ^2 r =\left( \frac{-1-i\sqrt{3}}{2} \right) r,\]" width="316" height="56" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/7/0/6707162df38f1749402d879e24a3e5f50986d47b.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x_4=\left( \frac{-1+i\sqrt{3}}{2} \right) ^3 r=r,\]" width="204" height="56" /> which means <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/0/420543d9a4f93ed4180b45b3149b663a8aa7674d.png" class="latex" alt="$x_4$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="17" height="10" /> is the same as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/a/ccada11db7b2b90693e2fac4f887a57fce6f96bf.png" class="latex" alt="$x_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="16" height="10" />.
</p><p>Now we have 3 different roots of the polynomial, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/a/ccada11db7b2b90693e2fac4f887a57fce6f96bf.png" class="latex" alt="$x_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="16" height="10" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/7/6a7d010bbff66a0c41e43310a51efbaa6bf63396.png" class="latex" alt="$x_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="16" height="10" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/6/ee60922c5fc5ee1941df04f838cdae71fbc29d6f.png" class="latex" alt="$x_3$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="16" height="10" />. Next, we will prove that all 5 roots of the polynomial must be chosen from those 3 roots. Let us assume that there is one root <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/6/8c632192ec22a1c351e769f41208ca64512bfa9c.png" class="latex" alt="$x_4=p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="51" height="11" /> which is different from the three roots we already know, then there must be two other roots, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/a/2da51f0b7cc47ef79972403ab546041f8cd8cc0e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x_5=\left( \frac{-1+i\sqrt{3}}{2} \right) ^2 p =\left( \frac{-1-i\sqrt{3}}{2} \right) p,\]" width="317" height="56" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/f/76fd41839e255742ebeab282a90c82366342d2d8.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x_6=\left( \frac{-1+i\sqrt{3}}{2} \right) ^3 p=p,\]" width="206" height="56" /> different from all known roots. We get 6 different roots for the polynomial, which contradicts the limit of 5 roots. Therefore the assumption of a different root is wrong, thus the roots must be chosen from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/a/ccada11db7b2b90693e2fac4f887a57fce6f96bf.png" class="latex" alt="$x_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="16" height="10" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/7/6a7d010bbff66a0c41e43310a51efbaa6bf63396.png" class="latex" alt="$x_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="16" height="10" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/6/ee60922c5fc5ee1941df04f838cdae71fbc29d6f.png" class="latex" alt="$x_3$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="16" height="10" />.
</p><p>The polynomial then can be written like <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/0/8e0b47b45d6c039bb0066f4ecdd3409ee221acc5.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)=(x-x_1)^m (x-x_2)^n (x-x_3)^q$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="281" height="18" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/1/0615acc3725de21025457e7d6f7694dab8e2f758.png" class="latex" alt="$q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="8" height="11" /> are non-negative integers and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/4/724dfd7bdd918c4f76fd58ab3c69a543578243c2.png" class="latex" alt="$m+n+q=5$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="113" height="16" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> are real numbers, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> must be equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/1/0615acc3725de21025457e7d6f7694dab8e2f758.png" class="latex" alt="$q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="8" height="11" />. Therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/a/aaabd3ab712701f6298ebfa5c1dd49276e681770.png" class="latex" alt="$(m,n,q)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="64" height="18" /> can only be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/f/12f6451c56971edb3106b28f513ebf12afc9f1d8.png" class="latex" alt="$(1,2,2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="56" height="18" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/0/530aabd61c34add287fbad3d9dee71157f32a4c3.png" class="latex" alt="$(3,1,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="56" height="18" />, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/e/d0e45c03dbb4b28e6843e7f243db9beb457efa03.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\mathbf{(C)} 2}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="50" height="29" />.
</p><p>~Yelong_Li
</p>
