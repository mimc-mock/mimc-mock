<p>How many positive integers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> are there such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> is a multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, and the least common multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/3/2933f407c21ceb3f3ec8370f43cd5b939250ffca.png" class="latex" alt="$5!$" width="12" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> equals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> times the greatest common divisor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/2/c824ea490ff073932a1c17d8ce0b9a4215f90d95.png" class="latex" alt="$10!$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="21" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/8/a28578f91569670ce8f5d1c27b4ea637a09a75a9.png" class="latex" alt="$n?$" width="19" height="12" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/8/b18cb34f194027141cc51dbec9763bad5ebdbf95.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 12 \qquad \textbf{(B) } 24 \qquad \textbf{(C) } 36 \qquad \textbf{(D) } 48 \qquad \textbf{(E) } 72$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="398" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We set up the following equation as the problem states:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/1/e4182bee46f971119822c1847de1c6dd5e26a37f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\text{lcm}{(5!, n)} = 5\text{gcd}{(10!, n)}.\]" width="191" height="18" />
</p><p>Breaking each number into its prime factorization, we see that the equation becomes
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/6/356f8d9a66c1cdd0ad995e7193a7bed51528f404.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\text{lcm}{(2^3\cdot 3 \cdot 5, n)} = 5\text{gcd}{(2^8\cdot 3^4 \cdot 5^2 \cdot 7, n)}.\]" width="312" height="19" />
</p><p>We can now determine the prime factorization of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />. We know that its prime factors belong to the set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/8/fb80775466cdd77221b3caa59054784159cdae44.png" class="latex" alt="$\{2, 3, 5, 7\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="77" height="18" />, as no factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/2/c824ea490ff073932a1c17d8ce0b9a4215f90d95.png" class="latex" alt="$10!$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="21" height="13" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/8/c6878713578626763c38433b3f4c8c2205ad0c15.png" class="latex" alt="$11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> in its prime factorization, nor anything greater. Next, we must find exactly how many different possibilities exist for each.
</p><p>There can be anywhere between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />'s and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />'s. However, since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> is a multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, and we multiply the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/5/90532c5e1e39f45c92592c869e856d05ae869465.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{gcd}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="25" height="15" /> by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, there can only be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />'s in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />'s prime factorization. Finally, there can either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" />'s.
</p><p>Thus, we can multiply the total possibilities of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />'s factorization to determine the number of integers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> which satisfy the equation, giving us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/e/b6e240d02c4a0312d73dbf806cee804ac2000595.png" class="latex" alt="$6 \times 4 \times 1 \times 2 = \boxed{\textbf{(D) } 48}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="193" height="28" />. ~ciceronii
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Like the Solution 1, we starts from the equation:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/1/e4182bee46f971119822c1847de1c6dd5e26a37f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\text{lcm}{(5!, n)} = 5\text{gcd}{(10!, n)}.\]" width="191" height="18" />
Assume <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/e/98e5448715aa5a3999dbb65f064dcb7f2b452900.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{lcm}{(5!, n)}=k\cdot5!$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="133" height="18" />, with some integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />. It follows that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/f/f8fd7e2826c68c32653fc3a0573d79395a0c310d.png" class="latex" alt="$k\cdot 4!=\text{gcd}{(10!, n)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="141" height="18" />. It means that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> has a divisor <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/a/2da866442e69c040daf44260b4728d37e98de313.png" class="latex" alt="$4!$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="12" height="13" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> is a multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> has a divisor <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/3/2933f407c21ceb3f3ec8370f43cd5b939250ffca.png" class="latex" alt="$5!$" width="12" height="13" />. Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/d/3edbb38985062ad29eefb20344d352b8a85f1291.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{lcm}{(5!, n)}=n=k\cdot5!$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="168" height="18" />. The equation can be changed as
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/1/d31c9697395029464e6406de5cf7d6476252298e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[k\cdot5!=5\text{gcd}{(10!, k\cdot5!)}\]" width="175" height="18" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/3/fc3a8c770bc89280e8eb9162077d861b996025b1.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[k=5\text{gcd}{(6\cdot7\cdot8\cdot9\cdot10, k)}\]" width="205" height="18" />
We can see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> is also a multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, with a form of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/f/73f5e0239d740ddf8df179f77b1d6d81f27c8669.png" class="latex" alt="$5\cdot m$" width="37" height="12" />. Substituting it in the above equation, we have
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/9/f89ecf44bcf7f87a44bc62df275846bd1931d16f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[m=5\text{gcd}{(6\cdot7\cdot8\cdot9\cdot2, m)}\]" width="208" height="18" />
Similarly, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> is a multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, with a form of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/8/268cb55a7fc1750bae44c2712d8ff4283bb36aef.png" class="latex" alt="$5\cdot s$" width="30" height="12" />. We have 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/a/7da21de3c788627db510b0d6e41ecd7f19478dc2.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[s=\text{gcd}{(6\cdot7\cdot8\cdot9\cdot2, 5\cdot s)}=\text{gcd}{(2^5\cdot3^3\cdot7, s)}\]" width="355" height="19" />
The equation holds, if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" /> is a divisor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/f/10f8db5eb57499c0e8a8fd00ec9449c716aa13e6.png" class="latex" alt="$2^5\cdot3^3\cdot7$" width="68" height="15" />, which has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/8/c4830c71e4efea7683c89d892a9401c67a6e29d9.png" class="latex" alt="$(5+1)(3+1)(1+1)=\boxed{(\textbf{D})48}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="246" height="29" /> divisors.
</p><p>by Linty Huang
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>As in the previous solutions, we start with
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/6/6068059d0e947abfe440e5b9b1ce3449076c1c0a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\text{lcm}(5!,n) = 5\text{gcd}(10!,n)\]" width="186" height="18" />
</p><p>From this we have that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/f/4ef58d0c9a5a08ab3783f96d180336d97a61b0a5.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{lcm}(5!,n) \,|\, 5\text{gcd}(10!,n)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="173" height="18" /> , and in particular, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/1/65127add6157aaff46df40d6b2e1fe61ba262ca4.png" class="latex" alt="$n \,|\, 5\text{gcd}(10!,n)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="111" height="18" />. However, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/a/c8abfee781bcdd36648565b3765defcc30a8cbb6.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{gcd}(10!,n)\, |\, n$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="103" height="18" />, so we must have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/f/aef2d8e01007e0c4b0c5ea0b76ac0b05a7058eb4.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{gcd}(10!,n) = n$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="116" height="18" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/9/8e918f9bd38398cb3b0c8f0cf78f7daa64f6f6b3.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{gcd}(10!,n) = n/5$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="134" height="18" />. If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/f/aef2d8e01007e0c4b0c5ea0b76ac0b05a7058eb4.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{gcd}(10!,n) = n$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="116" height="18" />, then we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/b/b1bea33ad5ef50915737f06fab4c86110b69c5c1.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{lcm}(5!,n) = 5n$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="117" height="18" />; because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/3/1f37c03e74c30f731e259959437671f820b1a60e.png" class="latex" alt="$5\, |\, 5!$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="32" height="18" />, this implies that 5 does not divide <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, so we must have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/9/8e918f9bd38398cb3b0c8f0cf78f7daa64f6f6b3.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{gcd}(10!,n) = n/5$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="134" height="18" />.
</p><p>Now we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/d/98d2f2b1aeb2172271cd88ccc8aa92add5f8ad72.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{lcm}(5!,n) = n$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="108" height="18" />, implying that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/2/e52109b0a0028baddead9c43763a7d84b1ebbba5.png" class="latex" alt="$5!\, |\, n$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="36" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/c/08c78d2a655de854782c6c1376ba40cf1f4aaa8c.png" class="latex" alt="$n/5\, |\, 10!$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="61" height="18" />. Writing out prime factorizations, this gives us
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/e/b0eb55065ccc5991ef28ad44bf5c18ee16b4e115.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[2^3 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \,|\, n\]" width="82" height="19" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/7/ca76c1325659c16b4301a055f60ee9fdfa8ad551.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[n \,|\, 2^8 \cdot 3^4 \cdot 5^2 \cdot 7\]" width="119" height="19" />
</p><p>So <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> can have 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, or 8 factors of 2; 1, 2, 3, or 4 factors of two; and 0 or 1 factors of 7. Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/6/106d87c7c9bdc9f1f7da940643f198fdbbf0ac8d.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{gcd}(2^8 \cdot 3^4 \cdot 5^2 \cdot 7,n) = n/5$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="209" height="19" /> implies that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> has 2 factors of 5. Thus, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/4/5d4f00e1e9a16789f1496479014383e1832a17c8.png" class="latex" alt="$6 \cdot 4 \cdot 2 = 48$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="95" height="13" /> possible choices for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, and our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/d/2ed4dfd7393ac0f517b99c3471d1f5f714ef3275.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) 48}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="66" height="28" />.
</p><p>-gumbymoo
