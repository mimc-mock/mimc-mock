<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/2/3227a8be2a2cfe499867a794fe7efaa5ca9713da.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(15cm,0); import olympiad; draw((0,0)--(0,2)--(6,4)--(4,0)--cycle); label(&quot;A&quot;, (0,2), NW); label(&quot;B&quot;, (0,0), SW); label(&quot;C&quot;, (4,0), SE); label(&quot;D&quot;, (6,4), NE); label(&quot;E&quot;, (1.714,1.143), N); label(&quot;F&quot;, (1,1.5), N); draw((0,2)--(4,0), dashed); draw((0,0)--(6,4), dashed); draw((0,0)--(1,1.5), dashed); label(&quot;20&quot;, (0,2)--(4,0), SW); label(&quot;30&quot;, (4,0)--(6,4), SE); label(&quot;$x$&quot;, (1,1.5)--(1.714,1.143), NE); draw(rightanglemark((0,2),(0,0),(4,0))); draw(rightanglemark((0,2),(4,0),(6,4))); draw(rightanglemark((0,0),(1,1.5),(0,2))); [/asy]" width="712" height="485" />
</p><p>It's crucial to draw a good diagram for this one. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/7/f07beb633addcca15137a3dd14227b2ac740dd42.png" class="latex" alt="$AC=20$" width="69" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/8/1086112d22fa4b4792174411521fe2384c1aab62.png" class="latex" alt="$CD=30$" width="71" height="12" />, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/6/2062b33b0819d4fb2252fd58af6907898fedef19.png" class="latex" alt="$[ACD]=300$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="104" height="18" />. Now we need to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/3/d33cc80fa8f093e155c5be46d2e5d9da3d7e1ef5.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABC]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="50" height="18" /> to get the area of the whole quadrilateral. Drop an altitude from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/7/a179ff2638e4799cadd820db205c2beff6299ce9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC$" width="27" height="13" /> and call the point of intersection <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/9/aa99aeb9dbb4d9897238fa0ae9cd6d96b9d2f318.png" class="latex" alt="$FE=x$" width="63" height="12" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/8/5c884057c1218625814fc38808eb0f395aac6928.png" class="latex" alt="$AE=5$" width="61" height="13" />, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/f/73fc0113f85cc1288f645f7a324e7bbc3ac49247.png" class="latex" alt="$AF=5-x$" width="93" height="13" />. 
</p><p>By dropping this altitude, we can also see two similar triangles, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/f/bcf0bd62e0281658289200bb35662d328c9bdc80.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BFE \sim \triangle DCE$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="146" height="18" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/7/a771251838750a4cb9926c5e2b03324b2b2edae7.png" class="latex" alt="$EC$" width="28" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/d/17d78ae2f05f140c2c69a76ab90d1bf5d1116205.png" class="latex" alt="$20-5=15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="91" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/c/73ca1ce9d9910447786a5f731ec3e0bde4efeb9a.png" class="latex" alt="$DC=30$" width="71" height="12" />, we get that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/3/393056ad2d7abf67e300da9b1fc13f322d7704cb.png" class="latex" alt="$BF=2x$" width="72" height="12" />. 
</p><p>Now, if we redraw another diagram just of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" />, we get that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/e/2ee112c2efcd21cb7ac959feb18a9bb791a6909a.png" class="latex" alt="$(2x)^2=(5-x)(15+x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="184" height="19" /> because of the altitude geometric mean theorem which states that in any right triangle, the altitude squared is equal to the product of the two lengths that it divides the base into. 
</p><p>Expanding, simplifying, and dividing by the GCF, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/b/c1b96af84ac9b38f3cdd25c03f12b8b9b77d8df6.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2+2x-15=0$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="132" height="16" />. This factors to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/e/0be3e22fe49ae0fdf5fbaca022fb019e34da4e0b.png" class="latex" alt="$(x+5)(x-3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="110" height="18" />, which has roots of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/5/7d5c1caf99f00b560bf4d3facd29caad925ee9c6.png" class="latex" alt="$x=-5, 3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="77" height="17" />. Since lengths cannot be negative, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/2/b022dc8ef6d33931fc1ae3d27df3ea0a81b52936.png" class="latex" alt="$x=3$" width="43" height="12" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/2/b022dc8ef6d33931fc1ae3d27df3ea0a81b52936.png" class="latex" alt="$x=3$" width="43" height="12" />, that means the altitude <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/b/9db8d1cf0d94564fdef81c8362dce0d09f1ed3f5.png" class="latex" alt="$BF=2\cdot3=6$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="120" height="14" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/2/1129c62df2342b4a57cefaebbb7423a1ef78f1c5.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABC]=60$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="94" height="18" />. Thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/5/04500260dd4542efd8fa1d12463b8e954d4728a3.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABCD]=[ACD]+[ABC]=300+60=\boxed {\textbf{(D) }360}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="412" height="28" />
