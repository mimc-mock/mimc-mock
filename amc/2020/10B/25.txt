<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/c/fac95dd82546821132c3e9adc3ad5e83eaf301ec.png" class="latex" alt="$D(n)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="39" height="18" /> denote the number of ways of writing the positive integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> as a product<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/5/7f52b53b0b17822b9bde3837f27c952c28b38559.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[n = f_1\cdot f_2\cdots f_k,\]" width="129" height="16" />where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/8/518773c4c14231c816d4656a6180e1391cbf308d.png" class="latex" alt="$k\ge1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="42" height="15" />, the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/c/61c594c97b61f72af93af4e61894b3f16a3bbb30.png" class="latex" alt="$f_i$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> are integers strictly greater than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, and the order in which the factors are listed matters (that is, two representations that differ only in the order of the factors are counted as distinct). For example, the number <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> can be written as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/3/47352a9c4ca17d3a1d5f61063ef3abf1510e96b0.png" class="latex" alt="$2\cdot 3$" width="30" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/0/60070619a4e65e13f60e6f83ae342a27431b413d.png" class="latex" alt="$3\cdot2$" width="30" height="12" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/6/746bc603fcaa7e0736904119976047d736eb3f9c.png" class="latex" alt="$D(6) = 3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="71" height="18" />. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/2/772a9d5e294233db4b4761f6b4c5c5f2cdebd701.png" class="latex" alt="$D(96)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="46" height="18" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/c/ecc8968706e266c806315966c475339ac03ad0a4.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 112 \qquad\textbf{(B) } 128 \qquad\textbf{(C) } 144 \qquad\textbf{(D) } 172 \qquad\textbf{(E) } 184$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="444" height="17" />

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/d/16d2d34a748fb267c1ed411ef7c9fc96f9cbcfe7.png" class="latex" alt="$96 = 2^5 \cdot 3$" width="80" height="15" />. Since there are at most six not necessarily distinct factors <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/3/7138ea9d8e0085e69265c1bb756794e065a6d41b.png" class="latex" alt="$&gt;1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="27" height="12" /> multiplying to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/7/367f6b15d47102fd8fdfc1bafed72c1daeadb35a.png" class="latex" alt="$96$" width="17" height="12" />, we have six cases: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/9/1b9602abf97976ad97247a4a8c2c8dc114bf7c16.png" class="latex" alt="$k=1, 2, ..., 6.$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="104" height="16" /> Now we look at each of the six cases.
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/d/5ed8470bc717a49aafe4556c7e2c2e11ddc7b687.png" class="latex" alt="$k=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="13" />: We see that there is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> way, merely <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/7/367f6b15d47102fd8fdfc1bafed72c1daeadb35a.png" class="latex" alt="$96$" width="17" height="12" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/6/4f681bd84648c174d45eed0037702cae77ed0686.png" class="latex" alt="$k=2$" width="43" height="12" />: This way, we have the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> in one slot and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> in another, and symmetry. The four other <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />'s leave us with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> ways and symmetry doubles us so we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/5/3359f43f0fd0ed3b672d389374aecd0360921a47.png" class="latex" alt="$k=3$" width="43" height="12" />: We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/3/fc3c3e2bb519a4952641c49d35d882191a0be720.png" class="latex" alt="$3, 2, 2$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="43" height="16" /> as our baseline. We need to multiply by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> places, and see that we can split the remaining three powers of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> in a manner that is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/8/6a8d09ced36134fc3003985682f264a1f83b95ad.png" class="latex" alt="$3-0-0$" width="71" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/9/839d5aca698c81cbe68861c93aca45ef55b70152.png" class="latex" alt="$2-1-0$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="71" height="13" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/8/b38605ec27913502d813ef38bec2e14d4b6c724c.png" class="latex" alt="$1-1-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="70" height="12" />. A <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/8/6a8d09ced36134fc3003985682f264a1f83b95ad.png" class="latex" alt="$3-0-0$" width="71" height="12" /> split has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/6/0b61cf951de68b8874c7e26679bfc90d16e09009.png" class="latex" alt="$6 + 3 = 9$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="73" height="14" /> ways of happening (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/7/5e70042adfa02f6e53409299f762979b7b460eb4.png" class="latex" alt="$24-2-2$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="80" height="12" /> and symmetry; <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/5/cf5647faf981338230bd0cea9ec7921abe783be5.png" class="latex" alt="$2-3-16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="80" height="13" /> and symmetry), a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/9/839d5aca698c81cbe68861c93aca45ef55b70152.png" class="latex" alt="$2-1-0$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="71" height="13" /> split has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/2/ba26076dd15c09c98adae221689262d39a8da893.png" class="latex" alt="$6 \cdot 3 = 18$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="73" height="13" /> ways of happening (due to all being distinct) and a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/8/b38605ec27913502d813ef38bec2e14d4b6c724c.png" class="latex" alt="$1-1-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="70" height="12" /> split has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> ways of happening (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/8/2e8c7b813c52bbf6a86a821cf9c56d6f1fbe29cb.png" class="latex" alt="$6-4-4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="71" height="13" /> and symmetry) so in this case we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/e/fce288036dca514a20865c7d81b0e9c7c157fd02.png" class="latex" alt="$9+18+3=30$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="122" height="14" /> ways.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/a/eeae143c5c9dbd376ffa1ffa8bdb93a58e8c9889.png" class="latex" alt="$k=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="13" />: We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/4/1d4afb130939f5c85bdf4e89ffcb036a5ae3c407.png" class="latex" alt="$3, 2, 2, 2$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="60" height="16" /> as our baseline, and for the two other <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />'s, we have a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/8/8487da4349e0cadd8f265106e78032ccb5044e01.png" class="latex" alt="$2-0-0-0$" width="102" height="12" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/0/d302a1c3608a5f2834ccdafde9ff795a8a71ca18.png" class="latex" alt="$1-1-0-0$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="102" height="13" /> split. The former grants us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/7/59784f6860d51b8009bb0f53f4479968a006ce14.png" class="latex" alt="$4+12=16$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="91" height="14" /> ways (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/2/7422025e262bacfcec3534a47f017412594eb5fe.png" class="latex" alt="$12-2-2-2$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="111" height="12" /> and symmetry and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/5/04550e63bfc8521caf9dfdcd391dd22d54d2e9b8.png" class="latex" alt="$3-8-2-2$" width="102" height="12" /> and symmetry) and the latter grants us also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/9/2b92c94bd3c6e74534ca32388e0171ed1d4beb71.png" class="latex" alt="$12+12=24$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="101" height="13" /> ways (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/f/aef8f196f45f69dacd1c1f4dd4ed4a6327c31810.png" class="latex" alt="$6-4-2-2$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="102" height="13" /> and symmetry and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/6/ae6bce5a33aba77b3f4caf42eedd651c6d9503e5.png" class="latex" alt="$3-4-4-2$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="102" height="13" /> and symmetry) for a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/0/9/c094260a3bca833eb01ccb3f8f34a139fd4872d0.png" class="latex" alt="$16+24=40$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="100" height="14" /> ways.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/5/ce5bb215d21ddfbe2ec17a99cb7118ca80e4a2ae.png" class="latex" alt="$k=5$" width="43" height="12" />: We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/f/3cf94ca7d7cfc048cd3bd3c600dd204ce6ab8dc2.png" class="latex" alt="$3, 2, 2, 2, 2$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="77" height="16" /> as our baseline and one place to put the last two: on another two or on the three. Thus, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/3/7435bc38e5a35f3e697def36afbecd60efced7f2.png" class="latex" alt="$5+20=25$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="91" height="14" /> ways.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/9/a295acb9662ee1bae4bb3aa92a1f42b2cfd83f10.png" class="latex" alt="$k=6$" width="43" height="12" />: We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/e/49edb2afb0a056108a030aba3471c2be06ebb95a.png" class="latex" alt="$3, 2, 2, 2, 2, 2$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="94" height="16" /> and symmetry and no more twos to multiply, so by symmetry, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> ways.
</p><p><br />
Thus, adding, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/9/d8909662e8fa699d4553c2dd00edad94bfb4d606.png" class="latex" alt="$1+10+30+40+25+6=\boxed{\textbf{(A) } 112}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="301" height="28" />.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>As before, note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/0/8307bac7d67b3e9412b652b5968f6b1835d4f539.png" class="latex" alt="$96=2^5\cdot3$" width="80" height="15" />, and we need to consider 6 different cases, one for each possible value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />, the number of factors in our factorization. However, instead of looking at each individually, find a general form for the number of possible factorizations with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> factors. First, the factorization needs to contain one factor that is itself a multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> to choose from. That leaves <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/c/7dc6fab4c2fdb4e9046030fbfdcf6ea721b05602.png" class="latex" alt="$k-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="13" /> slots left to fill, each of which must contain at least one factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />. Once we have filled in a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> to each of the remaining slots, we're left with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/7/0c72a9f3ec76ba9cb599bd4f50c0080ee2c8bc9f.png" class="latex" alt="$5-(k-1)=6-k$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="152" height="18" /> twos. 
</p><p>Consider the remaining <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/d/aed82abe8e5a0919c17218a7e58050510c78cfd8.png" class="latex" alt="$6-k$" width="41" height="12" /> factors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> left to assign to the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> factors. Using stars and bars, the number of ways to do this is:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/1/9b1062d9aa2e7f0490c7a3abcb5e9ffdc497cfd3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[{{(6-k)+k-1}\choose{6-k}}={5\choose{6-k}}\]" width="233" height="53" /> This makes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/6/4b661d8111b1fb1eae6d2e26f802a1f8d249f14c.png" class="latex" alt="$k{5\choose{6-k}}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="75" height="53" /> possibilities for each k.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
