<p>The decimal representation of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/e/6ce6559c22b38f698ff7b67ddc88f2d4d89c4084.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\dfrac{1}{20^{20}}\]" width="32" height="37" /> consists of a string of zeros after the decimal point, followed by a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> and then several more digits. How many zeros are in that initial string of zeros after the decimal point?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/a/61ac1a4b5062d6c547943a876e3c8c2161ae2278.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)} \text{ 23} \qquad \textbf{(B)} \text{ 24} \qquad \textbf{(C)} \text{ 25} \qquad \textbf{(D)} \text{ 26} \qquad \textbf{(E)} \text{ 27}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="387" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We have
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/8/ad8d5ac8a5d2aa9c9319a5d7a0f4bb219e791abe.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\dfrac{1}{20^{20}} = \dfrac{1}{(10\cdot2)^{20}}=\dfrac{1}{10^{20}\cdot2^{20}}\]" width="226" height="41" />
</p><p>Now we do some estimation. Notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/7/b27adc0a8e8a598d8bc4e701cc94411d2b28954f.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{20} = 1024^2$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="89" height="15" />, which means that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/f/f6f4fb515f1d3a096a15a3bdce0db5aab3c5503f.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{20}$" width="21" height="15" /> is a little more than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/4/1e46242b6ae49af1fbf9566e27e2565faf1969e8.png" class="latex" alt="$1000^2=1,000,000$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="147" height="18" />. Multiplying it with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/5/655d6f5e5e2e41425fcdb6f35573a19062a10b58.png" class="latex" alt="$10^{20}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="30" height="15" />, we get that the denominator is about <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/6/40609675b302564c0bb98f78bb425f4acc8eb2a5.png" class="latex" alt="$1\underbrace{00\dots0}_{26 \text{ zeros}}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="67" height="34" />. Notice that when we divide <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> by an <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> digit number as long as n is not a power of 10, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/d/4bdca93962a2beaffc172ca394e04fe05bd74ffd.png" class="latex" alt="$n-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="41" height="12" /> zeros before the first nonzero digit. This means that when we divide <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> by the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/5/1051e7d41ec0b8bd688a872be17538413f4ee97b.png" class="latex" alt="$27$" width="18" height="12" /> digit integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/6/40609675b302564c0bb98f78bb425f4acc8eb2a5.png" class="latex" alt="$1\underbrace{00\dots0}_{26 \text{ zeros}}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="67" height="34" />, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/5/eb56cf14b3753a3b637d6f3c1488e61d7b39a611.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) } \text{26}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="64" height="28" /> zeros in the initial string after the decimal point. -PCChess
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>First rewrite <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/2/8a28e2d85f1a09e91bc2cf2367b6def0652e0f34.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{20^{20}}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="34" height="37" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/5/b75a5af95e034fe65d2f78cda260e44847b1f892.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5^{20}}{10^{40}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="34" height="39" />. Then, we know that when we write this in decimal form, there will be 40 digits after the decimal point. Therefore, we just have to find the number of digits in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/f/79f3b5039d3b8e5fbe569dd2e7df0b176dc5f2d8.png" class="latex" alt="${5^{20}}$" width="21" height="15" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/b/a1b1fca3a19735162ac72cdc1c11e36c6f86c947.png" class="latex" alt="$\log{5^{20}} = 20\log{5}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="130" height="18" /> and memming <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/d/6dd439da34f8e148ca1cc7c510e18caee6ec9f8f.png" class="latex" alt="$\log{5}\approx0.69$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="91" height="16" /> (alternatively use the fact that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/5/2c512a6ac67c90f3c61f10bc86868bf3343d90f0.png" class="latex" alt="$\log{5} = 1 - \log{2}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="126" height="16" />), <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/8/528a66c6a5a21c4867d7ace01fb4b4780a91e16a.png" class="latex" alt="$\lfloor{20\log{5}}\rfloor+1=\lfloor{20\cdot0.69}\rfloor+1=13+1=14$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="355" height="19" /> digits. 
</p><p>Our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/5/eb56cf14b3753a3b637d6f3c1488e61d7b39a611.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) } \text{26}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="64" height="28" />.
</p>
