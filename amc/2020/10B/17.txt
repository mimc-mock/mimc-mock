<p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> people standing equally spaced around a circle. Each person knows exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> of the other <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> people: the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> people standing next to her or him, as well as the person directly across the circle. How many ways are there for the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> people to split up into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> pairs so that the members of each pair know each other?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/7/db742e2f33cccc20107ed85146f0a07fa7f7a84c.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 11 \qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 12 \qquad\textbf{(C)}\  13 \qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 14 \qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 15$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="394" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Consider the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> people to be standing in a circle, where two people opposite each other form a diameter of the circle.
</p><p>Let us use casework on the number of pairs that form a diameter of the circle.
</p><p>Case 1: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> diameters
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> ways: either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> pairs with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> pairs with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, and so on or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> pairs with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> pairs with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />, etc.
</p><p>Case 2: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> diameter
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> possible diameters to draw (everyone else pairs with the person next to them).
</p><p>Note that there cannot be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> diameters since there would be one person on either side that will not have a pair adjacent to them. The only scenario forced is when the two people on either side would be paired up across a diameter. Thus, a contradiction will arise.
</p><p>Case 3: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> diameters
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> possible sets of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> diameters to draw.
Notice we are technically choosing the number of ways to choose a pair of two diameters that are neighbors to each other. This means we can choose the first diameter in the pair, and have only two diameters to choose from for the second in the pair. This means we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/5/fb5062e174d144d0b4287a80565fe4b0f8252b5c.png" class="latex" alt="$5*2=10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="77" height="13" /> possibilities for choosing 5 neighboring diameters. However, notice that there are duplicates, so we divide the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> possibilities by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />.
</p><p>Note that there cannot be a case with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> diameters because then there would have to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> diameters for the two remaining people as they have to be connected with a diameter. A contradiction arises.
</p><p>Case 4: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> diameters
</p><p>There is only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> way to do this.
</p><p>Thus, in total there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/c/79c26830da0cd35701d33e5b9fb9325c9dc2aebc.png" class="latex" alt="$2+5+5+1=\boxed{\textbf{(C) }13}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="192" height="28" /> possible ways.
- Minor edits by Pearl2008
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>If each person knows exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> people, that means we form "<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />-person groups". That is, all the people in the group knows every other person. We can rotate until the circle is filled. We want pairs of people, so the first pair has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/8/218cac1c888eb03e613d452eb1c40f3466c75d77.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{4}{2}=6$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="70" height="53" />. The <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />nd pair is just <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/0/2501b1cbeaee8ed54fa34fbc258ff82846f7fa54.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{2}{2} =1$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="69" height="53" />. We need to multiply these together since these are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> group. The <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />rd pair would be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/8/218cac1c888eb03e613d452eb1c40f3466c75d77.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{4}{2}=6$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="70" height="53" />. The <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />th pair is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/7/1177f030aaddb4dccbb3407584102b5ee332701e.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{2}{2}=1$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="69" height="53" />. We multiply these <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> together and get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />. The final group would be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/7/1177f030aaddb4dccbb3407584102b5ee332701e.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{2}{2}=1$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="69" height="53" />. So we add these up and we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/b/99b542f20ba2c8af553ac233df0e7834b4a36894.png" class="latex" alt="$6 + 6 + 1 = \boxed{\textbf{(C) }13}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="162" height="30" /> possible ways.
</p><p>~Arcticturn
</p>
