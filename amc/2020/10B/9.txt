<p>How many ordered pairs of integers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/f/adfd9ae8a3fac031d3b8b470a52a709a23d4d6d2.png" class="latex" alt="$(x, y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="40" height="18" /> satisfy the equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/5/1757ccd73d86f39d350e40eb82b34f965e4e625c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x^{2020}+y^2=2y?\]" width="127" height="18" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/7/5/675431c8b161a3055346318672aaa69e02cd45b6.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 1 \qquad\textbf{(B) } 2 \qquad\textbf{(C) } 3 \qquad\textbf{(D) } 4 \qquad\textbf{(E) } \text{infinitely many}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="453" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions">Solutions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>Rearranging the terms and and completing the square for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> yields the result <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/e/84eca65e4b799419bff2b31f20b1811f46ed0b02.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{2020}+(y-1)^2=1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="154" height="19" />. Then, notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> can only be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/e/bae5aba07d37ff6ff813107e76260fb31ad5794e.png" class="latex" alt="$-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="22" height="12" /> because any value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/9/619c47a9dc9904156cd7a559fa1a4a65c9c7630e.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{2020}$" width="36" height="15" /> that is greater than 1 will cause the term <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/a/1fa614c545860bb5af6477dd5acfa0a01b19d21a.png" class="latex" alt="$(y-1)^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="61" height="19" /> to be less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />, which is impossible as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> must be real. Therefore, plugging in the above values for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> gives the ordered pairs <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/6/f9603ca3089464e548fc6f1366bc474e7efef8d9.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/7/ae7a465ffe5255961382f5777569608be052b783.png" class="latex" alt="$(1,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/e/65e0bc049577502741df50e34e88ae0654f438b6.png" class="latex" alt="$(-1,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="53" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/0/4e0ef68f274ec95f4a8f94eebff49accdcf689ec.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> gives  a  total  of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/0/e40af792bb8c6fd11211a2eee10e525fc7841b4f.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) }4}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="56" height="28" /> ordered pairs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>Bringing all of the terms to the LHS, we see a quadratic equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/a/46acdac8fee6b0221ad2c9c94536a78ecba36477.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[y^2 - 2y + x^{2020} = 0\]" width="150" height="18" /> in terms of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />. Applying the quadratic formula, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/a/1bac2da4afa9f5bcf7d3e53ca51be9ac5f97a803.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[y = \frac{2\pm\sqrt{4-4\cdot1\cdot x^{2020}}}{2}=\frac{2\pm\sqrt{4(1-x^{2020})}}{2}.\]" width="370" height="41" /> In order for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> to be real, which it must be given the stipulation that we are seeking integral answers, we know that the discriminant, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/a/60af4a5635db19c2d341be35dbc6cfa5a9e55191.png" class="latex" alt="$4(1-x^{2020})$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="90" height="19" /> must be nonnegative. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/f/c/6fc6a51aa7d216b7937490109b8e1c6ff3290d48.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[4(1-x^{2020}) \geq 0 \implies x^{2020} \leq 1.\]" width="249" height="19" /> Here, we see that we must split the inequality into a compound, resulting in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/5/ef51a02265f91b0702e0172d1cc22842fca6e467.png" class="latex" alt="$-1 \leq x \leq 1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="90" height="14" />. 
</p><p>The only integers that satisfy this are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57e5540fc7695cdd932f6c5633b2bdeb341d8828.png" class="latex" alt="$x \in \{-1,0,1\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="107" height="18" />. Plugging these values back into the quadratic equation, we see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/d/0dd7323f668f71824e677f781964284651fc1936.png" class="latex" alt="$x = \{-1,1\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="92" height="18" /> both produce a discriminant of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />, meaning that there is only 1 solution for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />. If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/6/116726256155e47187efeac538a18e4c941b3877.png" class="latex" alt="$x = \{0\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="60" height="18" />, then the discriminant is nonzero, therefore resulting in two solutions for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />. 
</p><p>Thus, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/3/fd30b06721be4648866606f66c48b02b6be1ee7a.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 2 = \boxed{\textbf{(D) }4}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="165" height="28" />.
</p><p>~Tiblis
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3:_Solve_for_x_first">Solution 3: Solve for x first</span></h3>
<p>Set it up as a quadratic in terms of y:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/3/57302fcd989d1abb3190666693fe5a475f8fdd1c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[y^2-2y+x^{2020}=0\]" width="150" height="18" />
Then the discriminant is
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/a/2fa32c16b6896590c31f648e13db1aafd9439433.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\Delta = 4-4x^{2020}\]" width="115" height="15" />
This will clearly only yield real solutions when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/c/fcc7ce64198c59d6bb69c779c8183caf4e59e9c2.png" class="latex" alt="$|x^{2020}| \leq 1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="80" height="19" />, because the discriminant must be positive.
Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/a/1/ea1699524f26559d1e40b94029ec512f51012123.png" class="latex" alt="$x=-1,0,1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="91" height="16" />. Checking each one:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/e/bae5aba07d37ff6ff813107e76260fb31ad5794e.png" class="latex" alt="$-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="22" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> are the same when raised to the 2020th power:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/a/0eab1c8bfe244a00f60be70bfd800c5b1508a574.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[y^2-2y+1=(y-1)^2=0\]" width="208" height="19" />
This has only has solutions <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/e/4ae8240d2252f975313c481e836edd9410c59078.png" class="latex" alt="$(\pm 1,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="53" height="18" /> are solutions.
Next, if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/e/34ea5b6f6de584d56d19a89cc4923a9d9a5cfa41.png" class="latex" alt="$x=0$" width="43" height="12" />:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/0/f8013ac173c27329a53fc847933f70ef425181de.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[y^2-2y=0 \Rightarrow y(y-2)=0\]" width="216" height="19" />
Which has 2 solutions, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/0/4e0ef68f274ec95f4a8f94eebff49accdcf689ec.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/6/f9603ca3089464e548fc6f1366bc474e7efef8d9.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />.
</p><p>These are the only 4 solutions, so our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/1/6610f2b3c9359c5864a02ef6fe9b4a3bd66a826d.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) } 4}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="56" height="28" />.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4:_Solve_for_y_first">Solution 4: Solve for y first</span></h3>

</p><p>When <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/3/6c3b4834614e5e7e189cb7d621cfb4fb0bd36dd7.png" class="latex" alt="$y=1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="15" />, the right side become <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/f/26f6e8d5232098bc24de6202792a701296650309.png" class="latex" alt="$x=1,-1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="74" height="15" />.
