<p>Ms. Carr asks her students to read any <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> books on a reading list. Harold randomly selects <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> books from this list, and Betty does the same. What is the probability that there are exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> books that they both select?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/5/955a063f60a42be87a026dcf858f91f82360d09a.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{1}{8} \qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{5}{36} \qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{14}{45} \qquad\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{25}{63} \qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="395" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We don't care about which books Harold selects. We just care that Betty picks <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> books from Harold's list and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> that aren't on Harold's list.
</p><p>The total amount of combinations of books that Betty can select is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/a/23aefc1c38edfa11fe6e62e133ca9faba21b5016.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{10}{5}=252$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="97" height="53" />. 
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/5/ee559317aed18491b8093c9dffcc7931e41f1991.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{5}{2}=10$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="79" height="53" /> ways for Betty to choose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> of the books that are on Harold's list.
</p><p>From the remaining <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> books that aren't on Harold's list, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/5/ce5894fb25890d6b71779b81a580a39fe1b1cdcd.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{5}{3}=10$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="79" height="53" /> ways to choose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> of them.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/a/aca06d083fb4c6ad78b04970d136bdc93ce70414.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{10\cdot10}{252}=\boxed{\textbf{(D) }\frac{25}{63}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="147" height="48" /> ~quacker88
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We can analyze this as two containers with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> balls each, with the two people grabbing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> balls each. First, we need to find the probability of two of the balls being the same among five: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/a/afa83f8b0e6cf5f2aefc1691fd3224f68e7d31cd.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{4}{9} \cdot \frac{3}{8} \cdot \frac{5}{7} \cdot \frac{2}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="116" height="38" />. After that we must multiply this probability by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/c/96c9544caef563e6ec7efdf92aa0071947f7006f.png" class="latex" alt="${5 \choose 2}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" />, for choosing the 2 balls that are the same chosen among 
5 balls. The answer will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/6/b667eeb8b4ac1d7026ee22f297d12fe79d298661.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5}{126}*10 = \frac{25}{63}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="111" height="38" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/5/965406f5c7d3f1652372469ec723fc66707a1bc6.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) }\frac{25}{63}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="69" height="48" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Firstly, we know that the denominator will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/0/1f017e5b1e219c0273e8daefadb891b1bdb507c9.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{10}{5} \cdot \dbinom{10}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="101" height="53" />. To calculate the numerator, or successful events, we first find the number of ways both Betty and Harold can choose the same 2 books. Then we find the number of ways for Betty to choose 3 different other books and the number of ways for Harold to choose 3 different other books. That is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/d/2dd8f3a0bba3bbaa17b1dfafcffecd8ff99d68b0.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{\binom{10}{2} \cdot \binom{8}{3} \cdot \binom{5}{3}}{\tbinom{10}{5} \cdot \tbinom{10}{5}} = \dfrac{45 \cdot 56 \cdot 10}{252 \cdot 252}$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="213" height="49" />. From here, do not multiply, instead cancel common factors, then simplify. In fact, to make this expression even more manageable, leave the combinations in simplest factored form (for example, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/a/4eae9bb878983f64a572ac3d1719899d4e1783e2.png" class="latex" alt="$\tbinom{10}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="27" height="22" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/c/0cc2118a389934488de0525b58fe1e5fce8f1a73.png" class="latex" alt="$5 \cdot 3 \cdot 3$" width="53" height="12" />). After doing so, we get, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/e/beef2299fef05f28e6426f56a0749f770825181f.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) } \dfrac{25}{63}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="69" height="48" />.
</p><p>~BakedPotato66
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>Say that there is a set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/c/95c44eb1417e423eaa3f21f3f7e524aae647576b.png" class="latex" alt="${a,b,c,...,j}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="83" height="18" /> which represent the 10 books. We know that intersection of the subsets that Bob and Alice choose has 2 elements. Thus there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/d/4ad8d8a25cdb4f594d1831d5858d0d2676aaa77e.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{10}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="48" height="55" /> ways for that to happen. Out of the 8 remaining in the set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" />, Bob and Alice both need to choose 3 different elements = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/3/923a14074f8746ab9c651ccc845f6a40b8d6069b.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{8}{3} * \dbinom{5}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="93" height="55" />. Thus the number of ways this works is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/7/ed7b726eb33ccb6c3ec379851004900fa4438272.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{10}{2} * \dbinom{8}{3} * \dbinom{5}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="157" height="55" />. The denominator for the fraction is just <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/8/d9828952e2bb8bc552a6e04334a34522b5360308.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{10}{5} * \dbinom{10}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="112" height="55" />. Simplifying, you get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/a/dda7c9bd1c7fa394b94757a15a036128991e039c.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\frac{25}{63}}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="35" height="49" />. 
</p><p>~YBSuburbanTea
</p>
