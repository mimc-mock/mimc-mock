<p>Many television screens are rectangles that are measured by the length of their diagonals. The ratio of the horizontal length to the height in a standard television screen is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/f/def7726c2babb527f7ab2ae4c436cc012ebbd351.png" class="latex" alt="$4:3$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="32" height="13" />. The horizontal length of a "<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/5/1051e7d41ec0b8bd688a872be17538413f4ee97b.png" class="latex" alt="$27$" width="18" height="12" />-inch" television screen is closest, in inches, to which of the following?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/b/77bcb0fa3d35aa757337b6a71419731ddb78f974.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 20 \qquad\textbf{(B) } 20.5 \qquad\textbf{(C) } 21 \qquad\textbf{(D) } 21.5 \qquad\textbf{(E) } 22$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="426" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>If you divide the television screen into two right triangles, the legs are in the ratio of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/f/8afd5df585afa29bc9aa6f75b7b7a308e79d4e92.png" class="latex" alt="$4&#160;: 3$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="32" height="13" />, and we can let one leg be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/4/ed4977828049d99f20ccd3b4acb6bbd43c2eb70f.png" class="latex" alt="$4x$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="19" height="12" /> and the other be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/e/70e634348f8df9eaeac4ced35db64d08a906532e.png" class="latex" alt="$3x$" width="19" height="12" />. Then we can use the Pythagorean Theorem.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/3/ae3feb4f3a5e87fd9f2395f803259581a80deddc.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*}(4x)^2+(3x)^2&amp;=27^2\\ 16x^2+9x^2&amp;=729\\ 25x^2&amp;=729\\ x^2&amp;=\frac{729}{25}\\ x&amp;=\frac{27}{5}\\ x&amp;=5.4\end{align*}" width="156" height="183" />
</p><p>The horizontal length is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/3/c73a391dbb1d030d449c87475301a466068866be.png" class="latex" alt="$5.4\times4=21.6$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="110" height="13" />, which is closest to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/e/92ed595f64a705081a2c8ff76daf6fe36b9e6956.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) \ } 21.5}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="86" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>One can realize that the diagonal, vertical, and horizontal lengths all make up a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/7/827255dbd48aaf645900571ce04461a84082908a.png" class="latex" alt="$3,4,5$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="43" height="16" /> triangle. Therefore, the horizontal length, being the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> in the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/f/8afd5df585afa29bc9aa6f75b7b7a308e79d4e92.png" class="latex" alt="$4&#160;: 3$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="32" height="13" /> ratio, is simply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/d/4fd03d5b164ae8b40db27a6f8ddb20d9d43f95fd.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{4}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> times the hypotenuse. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/2/46264210dfb17560c5efaee5adc7a8de0e0c28a9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{4}{5}\cdot27=21.6 \approx \boxed{\textbf{(D) } 21.5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="205" height="37" />.
</p>
