<p>A square and an equilateral triangle have the same perimeter. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> be the area of the circle circumscribed about the square and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> the area of the circle circumscribed around the triangle. Find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/6/7a62b8258c58af76a0fee3a933fad0423586d577.png" class="latex" alt="$A/B$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="36" height="18" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/d/81d2cf60efa4fda29edcec70f78fd329c13ed4b8.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A) \ } \frac{9}{16}\qquad \mathrm{(B) \ } \frac{3}{4}\qquad \mathrm{(C) \ } \frac{27}{32}\qquad \mathrm{(D) \ } \frac{3\sqrt{6}}{8}\qquad \mathrm{(E) \ } 1$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="400" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Suppose that the common perimeter is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />.
Then, the side lengths of the square and triangle, respectively, are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/3/8231abf2fe99d28c9cacac8738f4431222ad07c3.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{P}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="16" height="38" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/1/c913be109f1b9135f8940ed3e533080dc0ffb12d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{P}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="16" height="37" />
The circle circumscribed about the square has a diameter equal to the diagonal of the square, which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/d/77d756cd19e7f47f94df8d3886effe55d1d77d0f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{P\sqrt{2}}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="41" height="41" />
Therefore, the radius is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/0/390bc566d433c477e125e17bc285fa1da0f30a4f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{P\sqrt{2}}{8}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="41" height="41" /> and the area of the circle is
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/e/10ed5d8e8b56c228fac6e22e700476942fac7a35.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi \cdot \left(\frac{P\sqrt{2}}{8}\right)^2 = \pi \cdot \frac{2P^2}{64}=\frac{P^2 \pi}{32}=A$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="284" height="56" />
</p><p>Now consider the circle circumscribed around the equilateral triangle. Due to symmetry, the circle must share a center with the equilateral triangle. The radius of the circle is simply the distance from the center of the triangle to a vertex.
This distance is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/4/7547747b133453d3c209cff6cfbf129aa67a6947.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> of an altitude. By <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/c/47c78b85058ee5e17f88e2cde302e48aad826b35.png" class="latex" alt="$30-60-90$" width="98" height="12" /> right triangle properties, the altitude is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/b/aaba61e9951e3be28085497e14becffe14e48624.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \cdot s$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="49" height="41" /> where s is the side.
So, the radius is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/8/6388f91bea4f37cde9445bd70e144211fc6566e6.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{3} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \cdot \frac{P}{3} = \frac{P\sqrt{3}}{9}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="151" height="41" />
The area of the circle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/6/cd66b16c933ae5fc6f1998e8b12ea4a38fc4ad33.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi \cdot \left(\frac{P\sqrt{3}}{9}\right)^2=\pi \cdot \frac{3P^2}{81}=\frac{P^2\pi}{27}=B$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="284" height="56" />
So, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/3/053dc2ad0a228aec44d3b27a378cc289204f2480.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{A}{B}=\frac{\frac{P^2 \pi}{32}}{\frac{P^2 \pi}{27}}=\frac{P^2 \pi}{32} \cdot \frac{27}{P^2\pi}=\boxed{\frac{27}{32} \implies \mathrm{(C) \ } \frac{27}{32}}$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="349" height="51" />
</p>
