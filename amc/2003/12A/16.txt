<p>A point P is chosen at random in the interior of equilateral triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" />. What is the probability that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/8/a781c1980ff6e4c2f2ae64ead9e4dd3e87dbb1bb.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABP$" width="58" height="13" /> has a greater area than each of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/2/1c200a6633194effb7442839a148548c6e687173.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ACP$" width="58" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/f/1bfc7b6d29dae3f7d49733e6d8f1eb691af4e6ab.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BCP$" width="59" height="13" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/2/9d2ad931cf0512c3a0377268b82c1898a67e483a.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{1}{6}\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{1}{4}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{1}{3}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{1}{2}\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="367" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/1/7317615fe926227cc67fff473db1007fdca5896a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] draw((0,10)--(8.660254037844385792,-5)--(-8.660254037844385792,-5)--cycle); dot((0,0)); label(&quot;$P$&quot;,(0,0),N); [/asy]" width="252" height="218" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>After we pick point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />, we realize that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> is symmetric for this purpose, and so the probability that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/4/1e489c2e6fe536f8d01b4b29f213364699766b9c.png" class="latex" alt="$ACP$" width="42" height="13" /> is the greatest area, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/b/a7b510ddc7cf8a87b5cc0dd15adf8480cd2e6945.png" class="latex" alt="$ABP$" width="42" height="13" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/3/0c33da4d16bf5f54ce503e3c1277199b0ff3b3d3.png" class="latex" alt="$BCP$" width="43" height="12" />, are all the same. Since they add to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, the probability that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/4/1e489c2e6fe536f8d01b4b29f213364699766b9c.png" class="latex" alt="$ACP$" width="42" height="13" /> has the greatest area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/0/8a0504275f7d45aad4e22faf864768ca13facf3e.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\mathrm{(C)}\ \dfrac{1}{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="57" height="48" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>We will use geometric probability. Let us take point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />, and draw the perpendiculars to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/5/e657259c0995d2c36d46744bb4aa81471e6575ec.png" class="latex" alt="$CA$" width="28" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" />, and call the feet of these perpendiculars <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> respectively. The area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/2/1c200a6633194effb7442839a148548c6e687173.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ACP$" width="58" height="13" /> is simply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/d/e1ddf6753cb70c99e7789ca69a16083154ccc413.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2} * AC * PF$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="103" height="37" />. Similarly we can find the area of triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/3/0c33da4d16bf5f54ce503e3c1277199b0ff3b3d3.png" class="latex" alt="$BCP$" width="43" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/b/a7b510ddc7cf8a87b5cc0dd15adf8480cd2e6945.png" class="latex" alt="$ABP$" width="42" height="13" />. If we add these up and realize that it equals the area of the entire triangle, we see that no matter where we choose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/2/e426394422670dd803f44fa57a6dba381b67b1a1.png" class="latex" alt="$P, PD + PE + PF$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="151" height="16" /> = the height of the triangle. Setting the area of triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/b/a7b510ddc7cf8a87b5cc0dd15adf8480cd2e6945.png" class="latex" alt="$ABP$" width="42" height="13" /> greater than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/4/1e489c2e6fe536f8d01b4b29f213364699766b9c.png" class="latex" alt="$ACP$" width="42" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/3/0c33da4d16bf5f54ce503e3c1277199b0ff3b3d3.png" class="latex" alt="$BCP$" width="43" height="12" />, we want <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/4/0d4c608dda15ba21b9c90aed681902587fcb50cf.png" class="latex" alt="$PF$" width="28" height="12" /> to be the largest of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/4/0d4c608dda15ba21b9c90aed681902587fcb50cf.png" class="latex" alt="$PF$" width="28" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/d/b9d6394023b7f71962bce796b61343dcc276679c.png" class="latex" alt="$PD$" width="29" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/d/0edebe902653b343eee6f0a6a93a09f80884504e.png" class="latex" alt="$PE$" width="28" height="12" />. We then realize that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/c/60c3c3cb422c8c5aa1efa0767dfcef854fc79510.png" class="latex" alt="$PF = PD = PE$" width="135" height="12" /> when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> is the incenter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" />. Let us call the incenter of the triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />. If we want <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/4/0d4c608dda15ba21b9c90aed681902587fcb50cf.png" class="latex" alt="$PF$" width="28" height="12" /> to be the largest of the three, by testing points we realize that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> must be in the interior of quadrilateral <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/a/5bae0a6699db470c5d460d83f828bbf301859691.png" class="latex" alt="$QDCE$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="58" height="16" />. So our probability (using geometric probability) is the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/a/5bae0a6699db470c5d460d83f828bbf301859691.png" class="latex" alt="$QDCE$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="58" height="16" /> divided by the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" />. We will now show that the three quadrilaterals, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/a/5bae0a6699db470c5d460d83f828bbf301859691.png" class="latex" alt="$QDCE$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="58" height="16" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/4/e747f8a51183fadc9ee108f84e2cbce28f0571cf.png" class="latex" alt="$QEAF$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="56" height="16" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/a/cea1d3ecd82f03c5f7eb86e09b7c4df5e26c1c60.png" class="latex" alt="$QFBD$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="58" height="16" /> are congruent. As the definition of point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> yields, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/3/ef347af55f519ed794c20feab5f64b7c88fa1946.png" class="latex" alt="$QF$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="28" height="16" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/e/dfe3f0862fc765c6a4384b9fd9e0182b086f06a7.png" class="latex" alt="$QD$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="29" height="16" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/9/089ed8a955d97f704030f6a1e9350a3f3a466437.png" class="latex" alt="$QE$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="28" height="16" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> is equilateral, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> is also the circumcenter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/c/40cb2af1bde1d6edc2a3f1cccde7e565c8618df8.png" class="latex" alt="$QA = QB = QC$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="134" height="16" />. By the Pythagorean Theorem, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/f/e2fb8a677b127ecdaf1a802828a3fac845b13a18.png" class="latex" alt="$BD = DC = CE = EA = AF = FB$" width="294" height="13" />. Also, angles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/e/8aec7d5b67ae03c993f2601b22f11ac8d3bb8432.png" class="latex" alt="$BDQ, BFQ, CEQ, CDQ, AFQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="249" height="16" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/5/90582839fc3b9e03c2646c65450a6d5788205a34.png" class="latex" alt="$AEQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="41" height="16" /> are all equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/e/22ec0d2e4202839322d26a23dcb13f79821df5d7.png" class="latex" alt="$90^\circ$" width="24" height="13" />. Angles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/1/35122c53edb0889d53720c8bb0fe47111565873e.png" class="latex" alt="$DBF, FAE, ECD$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="144" height="16" /> are all equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" /> degrees, so it is now clear that quadrilaterals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/0/9f0dd82b431583b4bb99068f0e2539cf9fd65d38.png" class="latex" alt="$QDCE, QEAF, QFBD$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="188" height="16" /> are all congruent. Summing up these areas gives us the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/a/5bae0a6699db470c5d460d83f828bbf301859691.png" class="latex" alt="$QDCE$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="58" height="16" /> contributes to a third of that area so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/6/626c91e1d58db28da0bb6b7b237827d447598e9e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{[QDCE]}{[ABC]}=\boxed{\mathrm{(C)}\ \dfrac{1}{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="154" height="48" />.
</p>
