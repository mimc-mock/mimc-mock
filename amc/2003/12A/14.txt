<p>Points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/a/2/2a230e9e7115f0bd96711a39650ff2e6e272a985.png" class="latex" alt="$K, L, M,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="67" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" /> lie in the plane of the square <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/2/a42a626828b86fa849503f4f5dc2519147308dfd.png" class="latex" alt="$AKB$" width="44" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/d/54df32ebbe6c4f6c4493d8531ec0ef1937ed7dbd.png" class="latex" alt="$BLC$" width="41" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3f7db9e5618aa83bb58ddccac7693cf92a75d0.png" class="latex" alt="$CMD$" width="49" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/4/4d47f702cf57821a24badb53da336d10a027aca6.png" class="latex" alt="$DNA$" width="45" height="13" /> are equilateral triangles. If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> has an area of 16, find the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/b/c8baa210c35a8d98d01f9f4d4c956c5a7bcd15ae.png" class="latex" alt="$KLMN$" width="65" height="12" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/0/14057a8f4f08bad17471fdaed56a758f1e2fcea7.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(2cm); defaultpen(fontsize(8)+linewidth(0.8)); pair A=(-0.5,0.5), B=(0.5,0.5), C=(0.5,-0.5), D=(-0.5,-0.5); pair K=(0,1.366), L=(1.366,0), M=(0,-1.366), N=(-1.366,0); draw(A--N--K--A--B--K--L--B--C--L--M--C--D--M--N--D--A); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,SE); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,SW); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,NW); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,NE); label(&quot;$K$&quot;,K,NNW); label(&quot;$L$&quot;,L,E); label(&quot;$M$&quot;,M,S); label(&quot;$N$&quot;,N,W); [/asy]" width="295" height="288" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/3/5d3e2552e9bcb332d6f073db2a58513e3a67f65f.png" class="latex" alt="$\textrm{(A)}\ 32\qquad\textrm{(B)}\ 16+16\sqrt{3}\qquad\textrm{(C)}\ 48\qquad\textrm{(D)}\ 32+16\sqrt{3}\qquad\textrm{(E)}\ 64$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="505" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>Since the area of square <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/d/63d6c106db68e00b44df7f3172065e1079a703af.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{ABCD}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="48" height="12" /> is 16, the side length must be 4. Thus, the side length of triangle AKB is 4, and the height of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/e/0eeabcf5e6c486a928bd172cdb51948a67571656.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{AKB}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="39" height="13" />, and thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/a/29a58b2432577d411e5873a31c9849ec02287b8d.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{DMC}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="42" height="13" />, is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/5/e455d80f4657b1112ba302a6e2c42e9c81f780ee.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />. 
</p><p>The diagonal of the square <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/c/c6c7af1c8c923b1b7379a2760a001b59e2f196a1.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{KNML}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="53" height="13" /> will then be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/f/91fe377b3152f390001b2ab87f24a32ff559073b.png" class="latex" alt="$4+4\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="65" height="18" />. From here there are 2 ways to proceed:
</p><p>First: Since the diagonal is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/f/91fe377b3152f390001b2ab87f24a32ff559073b.png" class="latex" alt="$4+4\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="65" height="18" />, the side length is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/1/2e1fcdc1c674ec6183d95537a823acac81d5ede7.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{4+4\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="67" height="45" />, and the area is thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/4/9e4b1ef1f47fe74e864801c06ae48863cfc73331.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{16+48+32\sqrt{3}}{2}=\boxed{\textbf{(D) } 32+16\sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="283" height="43" />.
</p><p>Second: Since a square is a rhombus, the area of the square is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/2/1a2db988959c34f6006b7b0e0baddf48f8601fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{d_1d_2}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="36" height="37" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/6/44612d9d6ec37da7f4edd0f205d65cdac5954dc0.png" class="latex" alt="$d_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/7/117778db99ba8932dc35128d085c2619f4f690a1.png" class="latex" alt="$d_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="15" /> are the diagonals of the rhombus. Since the diagonal is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/f/91fe377b3152f390001b2ab87f24a32ff559073b.png" class="latex" alt="$4+4\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="65" height="18" />, the area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/7/a47c8a12b6a3dbcaf1a47ab24a864748b8a0ee85.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{(4+4\sqrt{3})^2}{2}=\boxed{\textbf{(D) } 32+16\sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="246" height="43" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>Because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> has area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/5/9a5b4928c8fe50ce3c2428da3bee3505e891b788.png" class="latex" alt="$16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />, its side length is simply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/f/ccfdc8810c791453a7a0d927240385d31e08689e.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{16}\implies 4$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="92" height="18" />.
</p><p>Angle chasing, we find that the angle of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/8/e689cb93d82dd98c66e9e16601cd596892dbbdf0.png" class="latex" alt="$KBL=360-(90+2(60))=360-(210)=150$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="379" height="18" />.
</p><p>We also know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/9/5e9025ebfdbc4b21e7d19f6e45f1cd88efeb811e.png" class="latex" alt="$KB=4, BL=4$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="133" height="16" />.
</p><p>Using Law of Cosines, we find that side <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/8/6a830bb972f32308de1615d2b45e2dd91548c4de.png" class="latex" alt="$(KL)^2=16+16-2(16)cos{150}\implies (KL)^2=32+\frac{32\sqrt{3}}{2}\implies (KL)^2=32+16\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="668" height="41" />.
</p><p>However, the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/b/c8baa210c35a8d98d01f9f4d4c956c5a7bcd15ae.png" class="latex" alt="$KLMN$" width="65" height="12" /> is simply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/2/0c25c637a914e945fe05566b55c2afa8d2356f75.png" class="latex" alt="$(KL)^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="49" height="19" />, hence the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/4/0b42cc159405319d9c4090a5368c38fd9a333a21.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) } 32+16\sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="132" height="35" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h3>
<p>First we show that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/b/c8baa210c35a8d98d01f9f4d4c956c5a7bcd15ae.png" class="latex" alt="$KLMN$" width="65" height="12" /> is a square. How? Show that it is a rhombus that has a right angle.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/6/a860cbacb852418c3441c879c75315c8b93b89fa.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle{NAK}=\angle{KBL}=\angle{LCM}=\angle{DNM}=150^\circ$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="374" height="15" />. All the sides of the equilateral triangles are equal, so the triangles are congruent. Notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/e/0ee72a561a6cddf7e6327cdc05e4b472608fd49f.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle{KNL}=45^\circ$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="110" height="15" />, etc, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/1/6d1c7efceba1bd86103e3749728f9d313947926f.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle{KNM}=90^circ$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="140" height="14" />. So we have a square.
</p><p>Instead of going to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/b/15b902cd5392b3e4bbda5878d8ae7f99eb8291eb.png" class="latex" alt="$KN$" width="33" height="12" /> using Law of Cosines, we can inscribe the square in another bigger one and then subtract the four right triangles in the corners, so after all of this, we find that the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/4/0b42cc159405319d9c4090a5368c38fd9a333a21.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) } 32+16\sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="132" height="35" />
</p><p>~hastapasta (edited)
</p>
