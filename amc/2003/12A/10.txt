<p>Al, Bert, and Carl are the winners of a school drawing for a pile of Halloween candy, which they are to divide in a ratio of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/9/9b9bcc40e8f0b531e8eed13bbe4c52b67ceb0255.png" class="latex" alt="$3:2:1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="56" height="13" />, respectively. Due to some confusion they come at different times to claim their prizes, and each assumes he is the first to arrive. If each takes what he believes to be the correct share of candy, what fraction of the candy goes unclaimed?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/4/e4419da9fe984800426f028aa1a6487d9f1f2114.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A) \ } \frac{1}{18}\qquad \mathrm{(B) \ } \frac{1}{6}\qquad \mathrm{(C) \ } \frac{2}{9}\qquad \mathrm{(D) \ } \frac{5}{18}\qquad \mathrm{(E) \ } \frac{5}{12}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="388" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Because the ratios are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/9/9b9bcc40e8f0b531e8eed13bbe4c52b67ceb0255.png" class="latex" alt="$3:2:1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="56" height="13" />, Al, Bert, and Carl believe that they need to take <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/3/1f3cc0ad966b3e637cd199607cbf0d1befc0e491.png" class="latex" alt="$1/2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/7/217aedbdc339bacc8ba075a2ec16902b098194e3.png" class="latex" alt="$1/3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/4/f34b3cf20be89133434c1a52cb3bbc3342a16664.png" class="latex" alt="$1/6$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" /> of the pile when they each arrive, respectively. After each person comes, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/3/1f3cc0ad966b3e637cd199607cbf0d1befc0e491.png" class="latex" alt="$1/2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/b/38bd36e353df831303c0de895a9ec614cf3e7202.png" class="latex" alt="$2/3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/b/3db6c519daa05cfffb9baaeb8d6128b09a13a731.png" class="latex" alt="$5/6$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" /> of the pile's size (just before each came) remains. The pile starts at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, and at the end <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/8/6283c29a68238d0a43668c9e6f4a407c696adc2d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}\cdot \frac{2}{3}\cdot \frac{5}{6}\cdot 1 = \frac{5}{18}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="132" height="38" /> of the original pile goes unclaimed. (Note that because of the properties of multiplication, it does not matter what order the three come in.) Hence the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/c/b1c7ae3bd7cc4fe05e4cca1edb00e4e7d009c2b0.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\mathrm{(D)}\ \dfrac{5}{18}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="67" height="48" />.
</p>
