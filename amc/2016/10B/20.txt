<p>A dilation of the plane—that is, a size transformation with a positive scale factor—sends the circle of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> centered at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/2/7525b2564d035900ed440ae7f4cf3949b7c713b9.png" class="latex" alt="$A(2,2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="52" height="18" /> to the circle of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> centered at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/8/6683774f3c478d70a5c9c7075d8514a79af37ef0.png" class="latex" alt="$A’(5,6)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="57" height="18" />. What distance does the origin <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/3/7e3b2bed68368fd0beec505935098ace79ee8022.png" class="latex" alt="$O(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="53" height="18" />, move under this transformation?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/f/d1f0bb24ed0c54c85c16c812ea6222801a3404fd.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 0\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 3\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \sqrt{13}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 4\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 5$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="373" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1:_Algebraic">Solution 1: Algebraic</span></h2>
<p>The center of dilation must lie on the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/8/85871169e6e2a7dafa5856bee6e00a88c977ed21.png" class="latex" alt="$A A&#39;$" width="30" height="14" />, which can be expressed as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/5/5755b4b24d3cf52877cc4cd606a94c0e405ea825.png" class="latex" alt="$y = \dfrac{4x}{3} - \dfrac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="91" height="37" />. Note that the center of dilation must have an <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-coordinate less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />; if the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-coordinate were otherwise, then the circle under the transformation would not have an increased <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-coordinate in the coordinate plane. Also, the ratio of dilation must be equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/7/c/67ce01cd97c81718e787d0abf8093ec9dfc01b05.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{3}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />, which is the ratio of the radii of the circles. Thus, we are looking for a point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/3/e53b1fe25be1c679117fb44a6a886fe1247d189a.png" class="latex" alt="$(x,y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="40" height="18" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/a/4aa47c85e04e0f37a277e256d9c18d67e8be45b9.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{3}{2} \left( 2 - x \right) = 5 - x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="137" height="37" /> (for the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-coordinates), and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/e/a9eb91f47a9e30d1ec1d2657a35d85826cf55f2c.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{3}{2} \left( 2 - y \right) = 6 - y$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="135" height="37" />. We do not have to include absolute value symbols because we know that the center of dilation has a lower <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-coordinate, and hence a lower <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />-coordinate, from our reasoning above. Solving the two equations, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/d/b7d924f912a4072521e9bb4c86d868591e01d0f5.png" class="latex" alt="$x = -4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="57" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/a/17a8e3f179bbac178d0abe73f1d6733c5d19ca01.png" class="latex" alt="$y = - 6$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="56" height="16" />. This means that any point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/8/8e8da0aef2d19017da7471378386d61620f288f5.png" class="latex" alt="$(a,b)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> on the plane will dilate to the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/c/07cf8dc974996ad40b3d520ea878f3d294efb13a.png" class="latex" alt="$\left( \dfrac{3}{2} (a + 4) - 4, \dfrac{3}{2} (b + 6) - 6 \right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="227" height="43" />, which means that the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/6/f9603ca3089464e548fc6f1366bc474e7efef8d9.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> dilates to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/1/3110d779584d5873464cbc4283c47f958acabc92.png" class="latex" alt="$\left( 6 - 4, 9 - 6 \right) = (2,3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="166" height="18" />. Thus, the origin moves <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/b/75b28f6cd3a032756d7c81dc8bb9486e35a9c41a.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{2^2 + 3^2} = \boxed{\sqrt{13}}$" style="vertical-align: -7px" width="139" height="30" /> units.
</p>

Using analytic geometry, we find that the center of dilation is at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/6/ab6fe8e820b5d8aeb5d978dac31b946f7b0eee8b.png" class="latex" alt="$(-4,-6)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" /> and the coefficient/factor is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/7/15788e198a53ced87268aea78fcde68a84de92c0.png" class="latex" alt="$1.5$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="22" height="13" />. Then, we see that the origin is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/b/47bc55e66a315043dda2ba6114af517f9e402177.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{13}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="42" height="18" /> from the center, and will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/d/2fdd23e111f74fb85046b653f5968b223167d6aa.png" class="latex" alt="$1.5 \times 2\sqrt{13} = 3\sqrt{13}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="155" height="18" /> from it afterwards.
</p><p>Thus, it will move <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/6/56616cab572182d57ef1ac4d6af732bd2d22fad9.png" class="latex" alt="$3\sqrt{13} - 2\sqrt{13} = \boxed{\sqrt{13}}$" style="vertical-align: -7px" width="176" height="30" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3:_Logic_and_Geometry">Solution 3: Logic and Geometry</span></h2>
<p>Using the ratios of radii of the circles, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/d/cfdbd19877c13d1755e01b54ba640f512c883a34.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{3}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />, we find that the scale factor is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/7/15788e198a53ced87268aea78fcde68a84de92c0.png" class="latex" alt="$1.5$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="22" height="13" />. If the origin had not moved, this indicates that the center of the
circle would be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/0/9a078b263b69698546e4c4300872e978a126f665.png" class="latex" alt="$(3,3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />, simply because of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/5/2155d546f9497994e2edd68746ba386ad652a11f.png" class="latex" alt="$(2 \cdot 1.5,  2 \cdot 1.5)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="111" height="18" />. Since the center has moved from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/0/9a078b263b69698546e4c4300872e978a126f665.png" class="latex" alt="$(3,3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/9/799d02a02fb177ab095513b6c692834f9fe62b8e.png" class="latex" alt="$(5,6)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />, we apply the distance formula and get: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/8/53875f789d6cb6809749b1ff3ad5a65b2a356e73.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{(6-3)^2 + (5-3)^2} = \boxed{\sqrt{13}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="233" height="33" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4:_Simple_and_Practical">Solution 4: Simple and Practical</span></h2>
<p>Start with the size transformation. Transforming the circle from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/7/a0736cc24657e4fbe87410091de1c4ebd44e26db.png" class="latex" alt="$r=2$" width="41" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/f/31faa8c684ba5811ac60ea1bd0c855d8ef4d8b16.png" class="latex" alt="$r=3$" width="41" height="12" /> would mean the origin point now transforms into the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/1/a11af309f4f98a632b52d2972cd9de92053c603c.png" class="latex" alt="$(-1,-1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" />. Now apply the position shift: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> to the right and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> up. This gets you the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/6/876b66789c31005ef23e41081d8f7c10137faaa5.png" class="latex" alt="$(2,3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />. Now simply apply the Pythagorean theorem with the points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/6/f9603ca3089464e548fc6f1366bc474e7efef8d9.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/6/876b66789c31005ef23e41081d8f7c10137faaa5.png" class="latex" alt="$(2,3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> to find the requested distance.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5:_Using_the_Axes">Solution 5: Using the Axes</span></h2>
<p>Before dilation, notice that the two axes are tangent to the circle with center <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/b/b2b596a120dcf60e33c7bfe19003bb1675bf37a7.png" class="latex" alt="$(2,2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />. Using this, we can draw new axes tangent to the radius 3 circle with center <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/9/799d02a02fb177ab095513b6c692834f9fe62b8e.png" class="latex" alt="$(5,6)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />, resulting in a "new origin" that is 3 units left and 3 units down from the center <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/9/799d02a02fb177ab095513b6c692834f9fe62b8e.png" class="latex" alt="$(5,6)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/6/876b66789c31005ef23e41081d8f7c10137faaa5.png" class="latex" alt="$(2,3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />. Using the distance formula, the distance from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/6/f9603ca3089464e548fc6f1366bc474e7efef8d9.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/6/876b66789c31005ef23e41081d8f7c10137faaa5.png" class="latex" alt="$(2,3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/c/72cd0e8787b5137ee6878bbd9b499452a4807f3b.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\sqrt{13}}$" style="vertical-align: -7px" width="45" height="30" />.
~Mightyeagle
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_6:_Answers">Solution 6: Answers</span></h2>

Knowing AMC it's probably not that simple and thus ends in a non-integer number (this is usually not the usual case but since this is the only non-integer number it has an excuse). Thus we guess the answer is C and get it correct. -RealityWrites
