<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> be a unit square. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/6/c16e423c8f499e0bef8c20078b04126aeb9417bc.png" class="latex" alt="$Q_1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="20" height="16" /> be the midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/0/ce0bfc603f9a044e308b1a1fdadca917e81ad2dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CD}$" width="30" height="15" />. For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/e/91e0d4ba9a75a31facffe534268765b4250f3241.png" class="latex" alt="$i=1,2,\dots,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="93" height="16" /> let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/6/e86919caff4a26af2c8867c56bcd9134bfd241ac.png" class="latex" alt="$P_i$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="16" height="15" /> be the intersection of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/4/f14387afbb1ea59c4ba7a0492d2e1b6a36a6f392.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AQ_i}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="34" height="19" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/7/4373e7b4be983c0315e82e1fda1df66ed34dd7fb.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BD}$" width="30" height="15" />, and let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/6/6968e749d0bc189a936d49665880a66faecc27d9.png" class="latex" alt="$Q_{i+1}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="16" /> be the foot of the perpendicular from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/6/e86919caff4a26af2c8867c56bcd9134bfd241ac.png" class="latex" alt="$P_i$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="16" height="15" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/0/ce0bfc603f9a044e308b1a1fdadca917e81ad2dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CD}$" width="30" height="15" />. What is 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/6/f26db6c3abab6986b4e2eba63be28fe7d962a56e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \text{Area of } \triangle DQ_i P_i \,&#160;?\]" width="167" height="48" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/f/b6f3c42866016ee7f98ba304038c342666db9fdf.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{1}{6} \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ \frac{1}{4} \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ \frac{1}{3} \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ \frac{1}{2} \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 1$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="364" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions">Solutions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>(By Qwertazertl)
</p><p>We are tasked with finding the sum of the areas of every <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/2/462cf5689b140a1042ee87fab478b53b86b40b87.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DQ_i^{}P_i^{}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/8/34857b3ba74ce5cd8607f3ebd23e9015908ada71.png" class="latex" alt="$i$" width="5" height="12" /> is a positive integer. We can start by finding the area of the first triangle, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/b/e0b5c66b9ce0517e7516ef5adef77a7c86b6ed5b.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DQ_1^{}P_1^{}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="70" height="18" />. This is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> &#8901; <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/c/dbcb53fcb742599770582a9be06a96353454ac46.png" class="latex" alt="$DQ_1^{}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="35" height="17" /> &#8901; <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/2/c92501f711e5d11885eb878b344226eedc4694d7.png" class="latex" alt="$P_1^{}Q_2^{}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="39" height="17" />. Notice that since triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/b/e0b5c66b9ce0517e7516ef5adef77a7c86b6ed5b.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DQ_1^{}P_1^{}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="70" height="18" /> is similar to triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/a/d3a68189257d29c3cc1cd01005ec14ea6ddd0f16.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABP_1^{}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="61" height="18" /> in a 1&#160;: 2 ratio, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/2/c92501f711e5d11885eb878b344226eedc4694d7.png" class="latex" alt="$P_1^{}Q_2^{}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="39" height="17" /> must equal <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/1/4e1965f8d70832ded66434c75a7bd52b75090af0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> (since we are dealing with a unit square whose side lengths are 1). <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/c/dbcb53fcb742599770582a9be06a96353454ac46.png" class="latex" alt="$DQ_1^{}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="35" height="17" /> is of course equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> as it is the mid-point of CD. Thus, the area of the first triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> &#8901; <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> &#8901; <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/1/4e1965f8d70832ded66434c75a7bd52b75090af0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. 
</p><p>The second triangle has a base <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/c/f5c82405ebc3111dc595467924aaec470ff321bb.png" class="latex" alt="$DQ_2^{}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="36" height="17" /> equal to that of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/2/c92501f711e5d11885eb878b344226eedc4694d7.png" class="latex" alt="$P_1^{}Q_2^{}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="39" height="17" /> (see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/f/fef1e9689b9daa07b50acf1750dd1689c1a6f436.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DQ_2^{}P_1^{}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="70" height="18" /> ~ <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/1/4f1f3c143a5b768484f9a287efb337faa4bdf70e.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DCB$" width="60" height="13" />) and using the same similar triangle logic as with the first triangle, we find the area to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> &#8901; <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/1/4e1965f8d70832ded66434c75a7bd52b75090af0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> &#8901; <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c339afa03e060d959e4942be7522c0c992f4c41c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="11" height="37" />. If we continue and test the next few triangles, we will find that the sum of all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/2/462cf5689b140a1042ee87fab478b53b86b40b87.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DQ_i^{}P_i^{}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" /> is equal to 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/d/c4d5a3219b0806df360fb2330c2c376dec622d1c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{2} \sum\limits_{n=2}^\infty \frac{1}{n(n+1)}\]" width="112" height="48" /> 
or 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/d/5dd22ec274e95a04fe219ff642688b4c57da18c3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{2} \sum\limits_{n=2}^\infty \left(\frac{1}{n} - \frac{1}{n+1}\right)\]" width="148" height="48" />
</p><p>This is known as a telescoping series because we can see that every term after the first <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/d/83dacecc3f852b6d1fc31accf43e249930c0b27f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{n}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="13" height="37" /> is going to cancel out. Thus, the summation is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> and after multiplying by the half out in front, we find that the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/2/cb2e56c9c9ee169b0326bdc1e6c19551db5ce71f.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B) }\frac{1}{4}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="59" height="48" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>(By mastermind.hk16)
</p><p>Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26e60bfcfeb67bc215480d22e821639250bce823.png" class="latex" alt="$AD \|\ P_iQ_{i+1}\  \forall i \in \mathbb{N}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="154" height="19" />. So <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/e/29ef9c11c4ffc52c91a5822d5b815ea48f1c98bc.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ADQ_i \sim \triangle P_{i}Q_{i+1}Q_{i} \ \forall i \in \mathbb{N}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="236" height="17" />
</p><p>Hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/3/37335636286f935516918dd2e6309b295d1121f4.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{Q_iQ_{i+1}}{DQ_{i}}=\frac{P_{i}Q_{i+1}}{AD} \ \ \Longrightarrow DQ_i \cdot P_iQ_{i+1}=Q_iQ_{i+1}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="374" height="41" />
</p><p>We compute <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/a/4da02d3a6c82a4428e3b6c9bc7bd66b20da84087.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{\infty}DQ_i \cdot P_iQ_{i+1}= \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{\infty}Q_iQ_{i+1}=\frac{1}{2} \cdot DQ_1 =\frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="396" height="48" /> 
because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/6/896a1b0c0142fa1c9305dc5781bc42a295c184ca.png" class="latex" alt="$Q_i \rightarrow D$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="63" height="16" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/d/84d41f414febc2ee81715533c2b63548debdcc83.png" class="latex" alt="$i \rightarrow \infty$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="52" height="13" />.
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h3>
<p>(By user0003)
</p><p>We plot the figure on a coordinate plane with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/c/66c8bd013ea7bc3e200bdbb0adbdb7706c592787.png" class="latex" alt="$D=(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="78" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> in the positive y-direction from the origin. If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/4/d1427fbfd3fd25cd63218d95b9424f4d9a6e1eb7.png" class="latex" alt="$Q_i=(k, 0)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="87" height="20" /> for some <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/3/3e382be12e5c9272e25f196b60dab1752cd07077.png" class="latex" alt="$k \neq 0$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="43" height="17" />, then the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/2/522da70a0c79b8993026a37684484d6d97764905.png" class="latex" alt="$AQ_i$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="32" height="16" /> can be represented as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/f/b3f0e6f2318897e86abfde54cb98ceffe2e909b3.png" class="latex" alt="$y=-\frac{x}{k}+1$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="95" height="34" />. The intersection of this and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/f/89fff82bb65d0215e49c8c91cb7c553da52205e2.png" class="latex" alt="$BD$" width="29" height="12" />, which is the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/1/741a74d86df899c10724dc2f80638f5e7f599194.png" class="latex" alt="$y=x$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="11" />, is
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/4/574f10f9ee939d2c97a9449f29b5c4b41b66209d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[P_i = \left(\frac{k}{k+1}, \frac{k}{k+1}\right)\]" width="162" height="43" />.
</p><p>As <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/6/6968e749d0bc189a936d49665880a66faecc27d9.png" class="latex" alt="$Q_{i+1}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="16" /> is the projection of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/6/e86919caff4a26af2c8867c56bcd9134bfd241ac.png" class="latex" alt="$P_i$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="16" height="15" /> onto the x-axis, it lies at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/4/584ca94c084e4b3d4506ec47522a6af8da0eb5a1.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{k}{k+1}, 0\right)$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="90" height="45" />. We have thus established that moving from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/e/a1ef6fb9bb3f4c7cde39e9fe8ef9cd72dddbc484.png" class="latex" alt="$Q_i$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="19" height="16" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/6/6968e749d0bc189a936d49665880a66faecc27d9.png" class="latex" alt="$Q_{i+1}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="16" /> is equivalent to the transformation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/3/8d378504339aa296bad9f74877c32db3a1477437.png" class="latex" alt="$x \rightarrow \frac{x}{x+1}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="86" height="36" /> on the x-coordinate. The closed form of of the x-coordinate of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/e/a1ef6fb9bb3f4c7cde39e9fe8ef9cd72dddbc484.png" class="latex" alt="$Q_i$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="19" height="16" /> can be deduced to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/c/f4c3475f17083ec6049eb476d2b46f5303d2d1b6.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{1+i}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="43" height="40" />, which can be determined empirically and proven via induction on the initial case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/6/486437ddb0794ca8f64f697e49f21e3adec435e2.png" class="latex" alt="$Q_1 = \left(\frac{1}{2}, 0\right)$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="104" height="45" />. Now
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/7/1378bee4e3643388c9c48677e0e0f35e974ccb4c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[[\Delta DQ_iP_i] = \frac{1}{2}(DQ_i)(Q_{i+1}P_i) = \frac{1}{2}(DQ_i)(DQ_{i+1})\]" width="382" height="37" />,
</p><p>suggesting that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/e/dbece6f9dd0a6620669b1b615f51d897de734691.png" class="latex" alt="$[\Delta DQ_iP_i]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="80" height="20" /> is equivalent to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/0/830cad531c1439945b2d6086cbfa82fb9ca3de0e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2(i+1)(i+2)}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="118" height="43" />. The sum of this from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/e/50ed4b89a6aeb4814026a2be01bde8bd7d264b71.png" class="latex" alt="$i=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="38" height="13" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/7/b671f1bb7e4ee86584347d5d22f1dc8abdb5bef2.png" class="latex" alt="$\infty$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="9" /> is a classic telescoping sequence as in Solution 1 and is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/2/cb2e56c9c9ee169b0326bdc1e6c19551db5ce71f.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B) }\frac{1}{4}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="59" height="48" />.
</p>
