<p>The sum of an infinite geometric series is a positive number <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" />, and the second term in the series is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. What is the smallest possible value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/7/4d753026173a63cfed0ea918be191cc3b451677e.png" class="latex" alt="$S?$" width="20" height="12" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/5/005618c9563fac3bc6e4c33239cb85fe48ba31aa.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2} \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 2 \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ \sqrt{5} \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 3 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 4$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="414" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>The second term in a geometric series is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/7/5475f6be0844f58393f1e044af5dc31231d3d7ae.png" class="latex" alt="$a_2 = a \cdot r$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="72" height="10" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> is the common ratio for the series and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is the first term of the series. So we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/9/b59f924fc808e63cf6e5037657dc76e5066943bb.png" class="latex" alt="$a\cdot r = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="63" height="12" /> and we wish to find the minimum value of the infinite sum of  the series. We know that: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/0/6a0c8c298993be8e51e64b83f984ae682026f1e9.png" class="latex" alt="$S_\infty = \frac{a}{1-r}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="92" height="33" /> and substituting in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/7/2f741fcb869b68b9c0e0f421e04d5364f73d7b09.png" class="latex" alt="$a=\frac{1}{r}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="45" height="37" />, we get that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/b/c5b6ecd93ce826c1bbcc517501dd05162a33bbb1.png" class="latex" alt="$S_\infty = \frac{\frac{1}{r}}{1-r} = \frac{1}{r(1-r)} = \frac{1}{r}+\frac{1}{1-r}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="286" height="45" />. From here, you can either use calculus or AM-GM.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/6/ba6291f9fd38c86c247467e8d750c1075905f6ff.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{Calculus}$" width="71" height="11" />
</p><p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/b/fab76cfab21419e0c199b22f38af6e1a35e38782.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x) = \frac{1}{x-x^2} = (x-x^2)^{-1}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="219" height="37" />, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/5/6053e0033ba6cb609c3f6777199d14b10f0d4584.png" class="latex" alt="$f&#39;(x) = -(x-x^2)^{-2}\cdot (1-2x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="237" height="19" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/f/fbff8c2d6247813a08aab2e4c24e40178d8e1bcd.png" class="latex" alt="$f(0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="32" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/5/bb5b19bf7575fdc3655bb5767980f09dbb747a8d.png" class="latex" alt="$f(1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="32" height="18" /> are undefined <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/5/be5b006dac6cd1e842b26fbf95f3be25e7cafe86.png" class="latex" alt="$x \neq 0,1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="60" height="17" />. This means that we only need to find where the derivative equals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />, meaning <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/8/5a819ca11b70c027a0d720e505471f07964c2499.png" class="latex" alt="$1-2x = 0 \Rightarrow x =\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="158" height="37" />. So <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/5/875b1512877521e24e5424a39aba90a573f7dc7c.png" class="latex" alt="$r = \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="44" height="37" />, meaning that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/b/c2ba494b640edd91c410b2a0047b1df9443fe9d1.png" class="latex" alt="$S_\infty = \frac{1}{\frac{1}{2} - (\frac{1}{2})^2} = \frac{1}{\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{4}} = \frac{1}{\frac{1}{4}} = 4$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="262" height="43" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/f/b/6fb9dbd5eccbbfa446c1cffeef067e8364d3cf76.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{AM-GM}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="71" height="12" />
</p><p>For 2 positive real numbers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/3/b73e0f7af64965747075769afc5036e4b023fdeb.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a+b}{2} \geq \sqrt{ab}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="100" height="37" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/f/25fc98ce7c1ebbf6af32826fdb5c18c4fd1d95f9.png" class="latex" alt="$a = \frac{1}{r}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="45" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/9/b0970097680ac7cecd570f0d4aad4b481124b966.png" class="latex" alt="$b = \frac{1}{1-r}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="74" height="37" />. Then: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/0/e505bb719811b32ad9df44594ad94f70b009d6e3.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\frac{1}{r}+\frac{1}{1-r}}{2} \geq \sqrt{\frac{1}{r}\cdot\frac{1}{1-r}}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{r}+\frac{1}{1-r}}$" style="vertical-align: -14px" width="298" height="44" />. This implies that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/8/878d74775741df14a1a9d3fe504c6ebaa2f188a8.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{S_\infty}{2} \geq \sqrt{S_\infty}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="96" height="37" />. or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/6/6d61af639bd61c8e04999bc9fc3c72a81a9eb2f2.png" class="latex" alt="$S_\infty^2 \geq 4 \cdot S_\infty$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="95" height="20" />. Rearranging&#160;: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/3/913dd9521fb3dd755244ca0e8be633973de5743f.png" class="latex" alt="$(S_\infty-2)^2 \geq 4 \Rightarrow S_\infty - 2 \geq 2 \Rightarrow S_\infty \geq 4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="316" height="19" />. Thus, the smallest value is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/6/9066ae05efa8f9da24f844ca3b5545c98cb91929.png" class="latex" alt="$S_\infty = 4$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="58" height="15" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The sum of the geometric sequence is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/5/c25254df1b852b1d0ec16a32a5c9495efe447621.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a}{1 - r}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="42" height="33" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is the first term and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> is the common ratio. We know the second term, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/7/037eeb5479371f307fe955cfba587416b2e7e3e1.png" class="latex" alt="$ar,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="21" height="11" /> is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/d/6cdd1b53e5faf7a118222e4b7571d1e9afa0cf56.png" class="latex" alt="$1.$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="12" height="12" /> Thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/8/b48cbdb4e2bc29a425f2801bf9610e6ad5dd9d05.png" class="latex" alt="$ar = 1 \Rightarrow a = \frac{1}{r}.$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="134" height="38" /> This means,
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/6/336ae70bf9007f04ca14e24d9764b1f6506ac12a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[S = \frac{a}{1 - r} = \frac{1/r}{1 - r} = \frac{1}{r(1 - r)}.\]" width="243" height="43" />
In order to minimize <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/2/2f2e8e8dd78b26ddc9ac278cc179101d30644cae.png" class="latex" alt="$S,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="15" height="16" /> we maximize the denominator. By AM-GM, 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/7/de7fd203d1c06585da65692337e6b196a22191d3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{(r) + (1 - r)}{2} \ge \sqrt{r(1-r)} \Rightarrow \frac{1}{4} \ge r(1-r).\]" width="338" height="38" />
Equality occurs at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/9/ad98bd6ee6e6bfbb44838d4e9262aef39ebd8eda.png" class="latex" alt="$r = 1-r \Rightarrow r = \frac{1}{2}.$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="154" height="38" /> This gives the minimum value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/4/6340f11969d6702c357a6a74c30059e5ea8718f9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\frac{1}{1/2}}{1 - \frac{1}{2}} = \boxed{(E)~4}.$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="132" height="52" />
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
