<p>A <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/d/8adb649f063149ebc97d3ee58c4fd38d4c440846.png" class="latex" alt="$45^\circ$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="24" height="13" /> arc of circle A is equal in length to a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/9/919bf5d461970fc8d905d63704ef763c08594184.png" class="latex" alt="$30^\circ$" width="24" height="13" /> arc of circle B. What is the ratio of circle A's area and circle B's area?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/1/1e1dad83cffbca91040308a4d6714abd8e2517e2.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 4/9 \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 2/3 \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 5/6 \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 3/2 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 9/4$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="442" height="20" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions">Solutions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/c/adc143c3f887fa7e92fd80d91bb22af51b4abd77.png" class="latex" alt="$r_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="13" height="10" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/4/0b4f3f4b698ce35667432041e168f7653533fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$r_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="14" height="10" /> be the radii of circles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, respectively. 
</p><p>It is well known that in a circle with radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />, a subtended arc opposite an angle of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/e/52e8ed7a3ba22130ad3984eb2cd413406475a689.png" class="latex" alt="$\theta$" width="8" height="13" /> degrees has length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/e/a0e1f3f43e22ff989094b278f0c146c32cbf02ab.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\theta}{360} \cdot 2\pi r$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="72" height="38" />. 
</p><p>Using that here, the arc of circle A has length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/e/c5e70216e1727af8319edd9b1a8bcfb5cb60b3f4.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{45}{360}\cdot2\pi{r_1}=\frac{r_1\pi}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="133" height="38" />. The arc of circle B has length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/b/16b869cc07d1516171b0333878412a809357d491.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{30}{360} \cdot 2\pi{r_2}=\frac{r_2\pi}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="133" height="37" />. We know that they are equal, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/b/1fb28df43e3b4c0e9fdfa9b1e6e054256e11daff.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{r_1\pi}{4}=\frac{r_2\pi}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="84" height="33" />, so we multiply through and simplify to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/b/77babbbaa9720dc0f670fd8d430944a6c8e04ef6.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{r_1}{r_2}=\frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="55" height="39" />. As all circles are similar to one another, the ratio of the areas is just the square of the ratios of the radii, so our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/4/9349dd4e9924e9c8b4f5aad75a5573846fac1831.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(A) } 4/9}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="76" height="32" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>The arc of circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/d/94d7945fbe1f7f8053206ab05c1f69ca7a14dbe0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{45}{30}=\frac{3}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="58" height="37" /> that of circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />.
</p><p>The circumference of circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/4/7547747b133453d3c209cff6cfbf129aa67a6947.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> that of circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> is the larger circle).
</p><p>The radius of circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/4/7547747b133453d3c209cff6cfbf129aa67a6947.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> that of circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />.
</p><p>The area of circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/b/36bec6bfb15f55ac7be6521c3677f04b9468e506.png" class="latex" alt="${\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)}^2=\boxed{\textbf{(A) } 4/9}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="147" height="48" /> that of circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h3>
<p>We can use a very cheap way to find the answer based on the choices. Firstly, if a higher angle measure equals a lower angle measure, that means that the ratio of areas must be lower, cutting D and E. Then we realize that the ratio has to be with square numbers, as there are no square roots. Therefore, it is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/5/565ab5e3715db52b9176b04dd9991b71f4e93a86.png" class="latex" alt="$(A)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" />
</p><p>-dragoon
