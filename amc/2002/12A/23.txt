<p>In triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" />, side <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/7/a179ff2638e4799cadd820db205c2beff6299ce9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC$" width="27" height="13" /> and the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Perpendicular_bisector" title="Perpendicular bisector">perpendicular bisector</a> of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" /> meet in point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/f/89fff82bb65d0215e49c8c91cb7c553da52205e2.png" class="latex" alt="$BD$" width="29" height="12" /> bisects <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/a/a3ad57710b6810476e6017ea2c2cc6d41219c218.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle ABC$" width="55" height="13" />. If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/a/23ac8b58cec5fb91be14e67010dcb0c64b683a9f.png" class="latex" alt="$AD=9$" width="62" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/7/39715f638eb74671bd892ea092d7fa6723d5ce1b.png" class="latex" alt="$DC=7$" width="63" height="12" />, what is the area of triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/a/a6adfd3ad27a2dddeb0af7131d73c33c86991e93.png" class="latex" alt="$ABD$" width="43" height="13" />? 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/f/31f81ad3b896f0f4ebe377c89902c2ee676b673e.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{(A)}\ 14 \qquad \text{(B)}\ 21 \qquad \text{(C)}\ 28 \qquad \text{(D)}\ 14\sqrt5 \qquad \text{(E)}\ 28\sqrt5$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="425" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/e/4deb1610580c4eae6c5d06124365b18655edbd9a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(0.25 cm); pair A, B, C, D, M; A = (0,0); B = (88/9, 28*sqrt(5)/9); C = (16,0); D = 9/16*C; M = (B + C)/2; draw(A--B--C--cycle); draw(B--D--M); label(&quot;$A$&quot;, A, SW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;, B, N); label(&quot;$C$&quot;, C, SE); label(&quot;$D$&quot;, D, S); [/asy]" width="235" height="128" />
Looking at the triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/a/d7ada763e99852a6e44a15d46cce3c3709a944d5.png" class="latex" alt="$BCD$" width="44" height="12" />, we see that its perpendicular bisector reaches the vertex, therefore implying it is isosceles. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/e/69e9788b81e2544b1eeb5ac188edabdc4084cf04.png" class="latex" alt="$x = \angle C$" width="61" height="12" />, so that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/2/542ae45787f72a3c44eb9bcd500d0c954f41a9bc.png" class="latex" alt="$B=2x$" width="58" height="12" /> from given and the previous deducted. Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/d/b3d5f618f4ac2339de9e09ef95f6e70b9c55a583.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle ABD=x, \angle ADB=2x$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="199" height="16" /> because any exterior angle of a triangle has a measure that is the sum of the two interior angles that are not adjacent to the exterior angle. That means <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/5/f75d6d58b03031bd7cb52a7ca886a8ddf369d21c.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD$" width="59" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/1/e5170b3a48d96da708932026f0e94194f415b0b3.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ACB$" width="58" height="13" /> are <a href="/wiki/index.php/Similarity_(geometry)" title="Similarity (geometry)">similar</a>, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/0/460efa679771899abaa83ea680d181deb2c0f65c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {16}{AB}=\frac {AB}{9} \Longrightarrow AB=12$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="198" height="38" />.
</p><p>Then by using <a href="/wiki/index.php/Heron%27s_Formula" title="Heron&#39;s Formula">Heron's Formula</a> on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/a/a6adfd3ad27a2dddeb0af7131d73c33c86991e93.png" class="latex" alt="$ABD$" width="43" height="13" /> (with sides <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/e/91e06b58d9169bd64a5903669448bf3ce3d0dfc6.png" class="latex" alt="$12,7,9$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="52" height="16" />), we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/3/b83147bdfd7a18000dfc73b2e8f65244b6a7e326.png" class="latex" alt="$[\triangle ABD]= \sqrt{14(2)(7)(5)} = 14\sqrt5 \Longrightarrow \boxed{\text{D}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="330" height="32" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Let M be the point of the perpendicular bisector on BC. By the perpendicular bisector theorem, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/2/1a2d4cbd2aaa83c894fdae8947e379b4b8f73351.png" class="latex" alt="$BD = DC = 7$" width="117" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/8/cc83f3827b3cdac28752a0359ed12d585947734e.png" class="latex" alt="$BM = MC$" width="92" height="12" />. Also, by the angle bisector theorem, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/3/9630ce9228c2fc280e295dd247f19f52d21ca8b6.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {AB}{BC} = \frac{9}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="68" height="38" />. Thus, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/f/97f2d19304a61829d11a0f527263fc5c9571623c.png" class="latex" alt="$AB = 9x$" width="71" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/3/9a32d24cc792453d0deb2174777809925394a31a.png" class="latex" alt="$BC = 7x$" width="72" height="12" />. In addition, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/f/f/6ff01a67b9bbeba0a196ac826c4ff01f293ebe67.png" class="latex" alt="$BM = 3.5x$" width="91" height="12" />. 
</p><p>Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/f/27f03d41d306cef9a6bf57b94476fc30db96001b.png" class="latex" alt="$\cos\angle CBD = \frac {3.5x}{7} = \frac {x}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="183" height="37" />. Additionally, using the Law of Cosines and the fact that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/1/6d1d0138c3ec5f82316225bb13411399c0073632.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle CBD = \angle ABD$" width="138" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/e/4aeb93343cc3d37609bf3a7b208ca4e649db490c.png" class="latex" alt="$81 = 49 + 81x^2 - 2(9x)(7)\cos\angle CBD$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="294" height="19" />
</p><p>Substituting and simplifying, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/7/457f0ac269f31c5f519550ef22ba271ee0b96e4d.png" class="latex" alt="$x = 4/3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="61" height="18" />
</p><p>Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/7/437e65d3735fb1dadf06a8dc9c3d588e451252d1.png" class="latex" alt="$AB = 12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="70" height="13" />. We now know all sides of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/5/f75d6d58b03031bd7cb52a7ca886a8ddf369d21c.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD$" width="59" height="13" />. Using <a href="/wiki/index.php/Heron%27s_Formula" title="Heron&#39;s Formula">Heron's Formula</a> on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/5/f75d6d58b03031bd7cb52a7ca886a8ddf369d21c.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD$" width="59" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/6/9a6ceb68ef7e57d53ef9720e3b524afdf1eaf621.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{(14)(2)(7)(5)} = 14\sqrt5 \Longrightarrow \boxed{\text{D}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="250" height="32" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Note that because the perpendicular bisector and angle bisector meet at side <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/7/a179ff2638e4799cadd820db205c2beff6299ce9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC$" width="27" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/2/ae24ff5696ae9a0e6d67eaaec0beaa6c4e1ca288.png" class="latex" alt="$CD = BD$" width="84" height="12" /> as triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/5/4057c3e63298c2ed6715003afb6f2d9dc6272037.png" class="latex" alt="$BDC$" width="44" height="12" /> is isosceles, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/a/1ca03d13f9aec9dfe69c8f6af2075bcefee35647.png" class="latex" alt="$BD = 7$" width="63" height="12" />. By the angle bisector theorem, we can express <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/4/354fe2e85d24b20d964aff45a4d4774ba4baaa3e.png" class="latex" alt="$9x$" width="19" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/d/62d8dd5f375b5f3874868d24a8820fe854a68a28.png" class="latex" alt="$7x$" width="19" height="12" /> respectively. We try to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> through Stewart's Theorem. So
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/9/d3943044ff4fc401b7b93dd55b1c780c35cfa78a.png" class="latex" alt="$16(7^2+9\cdot7) = (7x)^2 \cdot 9 + (9x)^2 \cdot 7$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="275" height="19" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/b/3cb44a7ad21e70dc67f5f7d4abd15bba176a325d.png" class="latex" alt="$16(49+63) = (49 \cdot 9 + 81 \cdot 7)x^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="248" height="19" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/e/fcee4d9e3c2dc2465744dc9bae8b7818b4f640ba.png" class="latex" alt="$16(49+63) = 9(49+63) \cdot x^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="226" height="19" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/1/a01d514b2f6cfc74db87037cc081f017f7c6980d.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2 = \frac{16}{9}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="62" height="37" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/d/4ddd238ee6b51f359689037b6342c4402d761e7a.png" class="latex" alt="$x=\frac{4}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="46" height="37" />
</p><p>We plug this to find that the sides of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/5/f75d6d58b03031bd7cb52a7ca886a8ddf369d21c.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD$" width="59" height="13" /> are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/e/91e06b58d9169bd64a5903669448bf3ce3d0dfc6.png" class="latex" alt="$12,7,9$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="52" height="16" />. By Heron's formula, the area is  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/6/9a6ceb68ef7e57d53ef9720e3b524afdf1eaf621.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{(14)(2)(7)(5)} = 14\sqrt5 \Longrightarrow \boxed{\text{D}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="250" height="32" />. ~skyscraper
</p>
