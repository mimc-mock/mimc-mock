<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/b/70b97eb4ad6e4e7a5a97e2547d9ed6e965960d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$C_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/5/b65e6facfe122c182fe14d503aeace2c52a9739d.png" class="latex" alt="$C_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" /> be circles defined by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/1/461c0cc1fd1b3a26f4c26e4b9f6f7dd45b24aa8a.png" class="latex" alt="$(x-10)^2 + y^2 = 36$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="153" height="19" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/0/1c008b8fe7ff3e1121172fe1a504079d48a6f040.png" class="latex" alt="$(x+15)^2 + y^2 = 81$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="153" height="19" />
respectively. What is the length of the shortest line segment <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" /> that is tangent to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/b/70b97eb4ad6e4e7a5a97e2547d9ed6e965960d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$C_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> and to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/5/b65e6facfe122c182fe14d503aeace2c52a9739d.png" class="latex" alt="$C_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" /> at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/f/ecfbfb907f3052074d87a1016b8c06f8c94f4e79.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{(A) }15 \qquad \text{(B) }18 \qquad \text{(C) }20 \qquad \text{(D) }21 \qquad \text{(E) }24$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="376" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>First examine the formula <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/8/4/184ca3435f550edc68277b5412ba0c7fbc0c27db.png" class="latex" alt="$(x-10)^2+y^2=36$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="153" height="19" />, for the circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/b/70b97eb4ad6e4e7a5a97e2547d9ed6e965960d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$C_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" />. Its center, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/c/88c7f79185a0b48d33454e85f44b4bfdf81d2420.png" class="latex" alt="$D_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="20" height="15" />, is located at (10,0) and it has a radius of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/1/261825f61d0fd76099d9a5914fe3b0d823f07fa6.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{36}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" /> = 6. The next circle, using the same pattern, has its center, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/0/4d01cc94a123a0c2b5239d4342be2e6ce7f3fc53.png" class="latex" alt="$D_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="21" height="15" />, at (-15,0) and has a radius of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/33776ce7a17ad91c3d5acbd378e89173bc51c8c7.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{81}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" /> = 9. So we can construct this diagram:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/0/0508baf85f5a81d2a76d70f551d727c0256a26a9.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(0.3cm); defaultpen(0.8); path C1=circle((10,0),6); path C2=circle((-15,0),9);  draw(C1); draw(C2); draw( (-25,0) -- (17,0) ); dot((10,0)); dot((-15,0));  pair[] p1 = intersectionpoints(C1, circle((5,0),5) ); pair[] p2 = intersectionpoints(C2, circle((-7.5,0),7.5) ); dot(p1[1]); dot(p2[0]); draw((10,0)--p1[1]--p2[0]--(-15,0));  label(&quot;$C_1$&quot;,(10,0) + 6*dir(-45), SE ); label(&quot;$C_2$&quot;,(-15,0) + 9*dir(225), SW ); label(&quot;$D_1$&quot;,(10,0), SE ); label(&quot;$D_2$&quot;,(-15,0), SW ); label(&quot;$Q$&quot;, p2[0], NE ); label(&quot;$P$&quot;, p1[1], SW ); label(&quot;$O$&quot;, (0,0), SW ); [/asy]" width="598" height="258" />
Line PQ is tangent to both circles, so it forms a right angle with the radii (6 and 9). This, as well as the two vertical angles near O, prove triangles S<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/c/26c10609613028124765ea882730af0af63aa699.png" class="latex" alt="$_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="6" height="7" />QO and S<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/1/731f86cf32e585e6283f27c5e12a6ee67c44d296.png" class="latex" alt="$_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="5" height="7" />PO similar by AA, with a scale factor of 6:9, or 2:3. Next, we must subdivide the line D<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/c/26c10609613028124765ea882730af0af63aa699.png" class="latex" alt="$_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="6" height="7" />D<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/1/731f86cf32e585e6283f27c5e12a6ee67c44d296.png" class="latex" alt="$_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="5" height="7" /> in a 2:3 ratio to get the length of the segments D<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/c/26c10609613028124765ea882730af0af63aa699.png" class="latex" alt="$_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="6" height="7" />O and D<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/1/731f86cf32e585e6283f27c5e12a6ee67c44d296.png" class="latex" alt="$_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="5" height="7" />O. The total length is 10 - (-15), or 25, so applying the ratio, D<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/c/26c10609613028124765ea882730af0af63aa699.png" class="latex" alt="$_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="6" height="7" />O = <b>15</b> and D<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/1/731f86cf32e585e6283f27c5e12a6ee67c44d296.png" class="latex" alt="$_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="5" height="7" />O = <b>10</b>. These are the hypotenuses of the triangles. We already know the length of D<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/c/26c10609613028124765ea882730af0af63aa699.png" class="latex" alt="$_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="6" height="7" />Q and D<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/1/731f86cf32e585e6283f27c5e12a6ee67c44d296.png" class="latex" alt="$_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="5" height="7" />P, <b>9</b> and <b>6</b> (they're radii). So in order to find PQ, we must find the length of the longer legs of the two triangles and add them. 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/9/029073199e1402b9158765044564ace271caf057.png" class="latex" alt="$15^2 - 9^2 = (15-9)(15+9) = 6 \times 24 = 144$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="341" height="19" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/2/2725d2e870207dcbb349cf09758b59c7713506f7.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{144} = 12$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="84" height="18" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/e/b9e6fb3d17412f80f988195515211badbe8bb394.png" class="latex" alt="$10^2-6^2 = (10-6)(10+6) = 4 \times 16 = 64$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="332" height="19" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/7/177a09b753a2409cd42d7a80ee4561d219610cc0.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{64} = 8$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="66" height="18" />
</p><p>Finally, the length of PQ is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/9/5293a2092ae31755088bd8c6954b2af0f0be08ed.png" class="latex" alt="$12+8=\boxed{20}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="103" height="23" />, or <b>(C)</b>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Using the above diagram, imagine that segment <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/a/5eae25d42f737f7912cc16e8ef9d473b4d23a2d0.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{QS_2}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="33" height="19" /> is shifted to the right to match up with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/b/e8b736caf62a37abf5339e974c28fb79baca5a86.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{PS_1}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="33" height="18" />. Then shift <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/a/beaa0e5f7e88eb989790403cdd02071b35544279.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{QP}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="29" height="19" /> downwards to make a right triangle. We know <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/1/1918725ff8be76e2ce7ce18d5016be10fdb91d0e.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{S_2S_1} = 25$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="79" height="18" /> from the given information and the newly created leg has length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/7/917cde0280e906507c9764155be8ebb535f095d1.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{QS_2} + \overline{PS_1} = 9 + 6 = 15$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="194" height="19" />. Hence by Pythagorean theorem <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/1/171d9f952dccaa300c68f4a82d1ae4a36b2a4f23.png" class="latex" alt="$15^2 + {\overline{QP}}^2 = 25^2$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="132" height="23" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/5/83590a41510687dcde29646d26cbfcf4ea603d93.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{QP} = \boxed{20}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="82" height="23" />, or C.
</p>
