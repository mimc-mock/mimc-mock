<p>According to the standard convention for exponentiation, 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/d/29dee8eac6c7812722f0013ad002044b30d8f220.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[2^{2^{2^{2}}} = 2^{(2^{(2^2)})} = 2^{16} = 65536.\]" width="222" height="21" />
</p><p>If the order in which the exponentiations are performed is changed, how many other values are possible?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/b/e1b43c42263ca15ff716ac204e8358d6d66b86d9.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 0\qquad \textbf{(B) } 1\qquad \textbf{(C) } 2\qquad \textbf{(D) } 3\qquad \textbf{(E) } 4$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="355" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>The best way to solve this problem is by simple brute force. 
</p><p>It is convenient to drop the usual way how exponentiation is denoted, and to write the formula as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/a/95ad6ef62ef46568f4225fb48c5035c466cd6594.png" class="latex" alt="$2\uparrow 2\uparrow 2\uparrow 2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="93" height="17" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/9/d39cad522ccbe5a31209793365754665a4d65556.png" class="latex" alt="$\uparrow$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="9" height="17" /> denotes exponentiation. We are now examining all ways to add parentheses to this expression. There are 5 ways to do so:
</p>
<ol><li><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/5/b051cc37e3e1d846f5ad278cd99b38452212ea85.png" class="latex" alt="$2\uparrow (2\uparrow (2\uparrow 2))$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="120" height="18" /></li>
<li><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/2/e02627e764c70ea23d57e018ebe3c404e9711ae6.png" class="latex" alt="$2\uparrow ((2\uparrow 2)\uparrow 2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="120" height="18" /></li>
<li><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/4/0347537c5d3c7210d985a80c52a7ce8b0c5ad508.png" class="latex" alt="$((2\uparrow 2)\uparrow 2)\uparrow 2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="121" height="18" /></li>
<li><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/3/fd3aa5d6c5c79cf4887a0cb32d96cdcc64ae2527.png" class="latex" alt="$(2\uparrow (2\uparrow 2))\uparrow 2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="121" height="18" /></li>
<li><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/e/79edd4a1120216c42cac680d09944f37a7754ab6.png" class="latex" alt="$(2\uparrow 2)\uparrow (2\uparrow 2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="120" height="18" /></li></ol>
<p>We can note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/8/378c9aecdd9367ed7bd377c63445c4fcd005d3f7.png" class="latex" alt="$2\uparrow (2\uparrow 2) = (2\uparrow 2)\uparrow 2 =16$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="225" height="18" />. Therefore options 1 and 2 are equal, and options 3 and 4 are equal.
Option 1 is the one given in the problem statement. Thus we only need to evaluate options 3 and 5.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/f/2df7432bf23dd99404db9a849a46559491c910a4.png" class="latex" alt="$((2\uparrow 2)\uparrow 2)\uparrow 2 = 16\uparrow 2 = 256$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="243" height="18" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/d/b1daea895707743f04d22f20424cd895e320c7b8.png" class="latex" alt="$(2\uparrow 2)\uparrow (2\uparrow 2) = 4 \uparrow 4 = 256$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="234" height="18" />
</p><p>Thus the only other result is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/d/abd678361b0f40ec2723283c0f2bab3df5cadc68.png" class="latex" alt="$256$" width="26" height="12" />, and our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/9/619254fd3d431079b23def51e7eedb31d1088158.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B) } 1}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="55" height="28" />.
</p>
