<p>The sum of the base-<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> logarithms of the divisors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/b/02bedd46306390e4c9b9293a00c7de6620b71b9b.png" class="latex" alt="$10^n$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/e/0ced7bf3e304ebb9fb9c073a8e5c5fde1740873a.png" class="latex" alt="$792$" width="26" height="12" />. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/8/2387a93acc2a27138978fb166fa35ca20afb51b6.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{(A)}\ 11\qquad \text{(B)}\ 12\qquad \text{(C)}\ 13\qquad \text{(D)}\ 14\qquad \text{(E)}\ 15$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="375" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions">Solutions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>Every factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/b/02bedd46306390e4c9b9293a00c7de6620b71b9b.png" class="latex" alt="$10^n$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> will be of the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/f/00f689771449c723b9c061c322eac816a5dc22a4.png" class="latex" alt="$2^a \times 5^b , a\leq n , b\leq n$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="158" height="19" />. Not all of these base ten logarithms will be rational, but we can add them together in a certain way to make it rational. Recall the logarithmic property <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/4/6e403a4f6aba0b21b006739e89c2e49581ca7bc8.png" class="latex" alt="$\log(a \times b) = \log(a)+\log(b)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="215" height="18" />. For any factor <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/5/095949f58b548c3e3a3af1aeaa455d2686f7077b.png" class="latex" alt="$2^a \times 5^b$" width="54" height="15" />, there will be another factor <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/2/ae26e8d7813cd548f522a9c63f37212f93cab33b.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{n-a} \times 5^{n-b}$" width="91" height="15" />. Note this is not true if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/b/02bedd46306390e4c9b9293a00c7de6620b71b9b.png" class="latex" alt="$10^n$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> is a perfect square. When these are multiplied, they equal <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/e/83e07efca5e2789d730160e0b43fe500b93a7953.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{a+n-a} \times 5^{b+n-b} = 10^n$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="176" height="16" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/c/54c4046907467b32838252b04ed0461ba08d7906.png" class="latex" alt="$\log 10^n=n$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="88" height="16" /> so the number of factors divided by 2 times n equals the sum of all the factors, 792. 
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/3/0e3efd9b14723a92c2ae891fe27780d5f8e2b215.png" class="latex" alt="$n+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="41" height="13" /> choices for the exponent of 5 in each factor, and for each of those choices, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/3/0e3efd9b14723a92c2ae891fe27780d5f8e2b215.png" class="latex" alt="$n+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="41" height="13" /> factors (each corresponding to a different exponent of 2), yielding <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57efb5c0b218135087ac110b8467426b528d7856.png" class="latex" alt="$(n+1)^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="62" height="19" /> total factors. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/c/50c0621e3055d14adb0fa4c05c6ebe14f97db1a3.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{(n+1)^2}{2}*n = 792$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="146" height="39" />. We then plug in answer choices and arrive at the answer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/f/eef74a6d9807de1430cd166c76eec1260199eadc.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed {11}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="30" height="23" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>We are given <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/5/56589b20a27d17fe83c721d0e2bd223f73e0d9e5.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\log_{10}d_1 + \log_{10}d_2 + \ldots + \log_{10}d_k = 792\]" width="311" height="17" /> The property <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/0/370ca7cdf35237898cf202373aa503994dbb1579.png" class="latex" alt="$\log(ab) = \log(a)+\log(b)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="192" height="18" /> now gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/3/80372cedd8b1188582f0569720bf36269e9b64a3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\log_{10}(d_1 d_2\cdot\ldots d_k) = 792\]" width="190" height="18" /> The product of the divisors is (from elementary number theory) <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/9/f59e548337b5c76ccfbfa44e45ebe67a97e782cd.png" class="latex" alt="$a^{d(n)/2}$" width="47" height="16" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/5/b05725a6508fe8df761c6e91d2735b697ebd87d9.png" class="latex" alt="$d(n)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="33" height="18" /> is the number of divisors. Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/6/9c6d141d925d12ffe7fd49968e15f0d0f009de21.png" class="latex" alt="$10^n = 2^n\cdot 5^n$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="99" height="13" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/a/dca5b9f769b87cd1f72b60ad73b12df0825d5d2e.png" class="latex" alt="$d(n) = (n + 1)^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="121" height="19" />. Substituting these values with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/4/6347f811b411663ade9d971010ffbb39f5f8a70d.png" class="latex" alt="$a = 10^n$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="60" height="13" /> in our equation above, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/4/3b4df64be582d8dab6867dfb86378b05220c06c1.png" class="latex" alt="$n(n + 1)^2 = 1584$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="135" height="19" />, from whence we immediately obtain <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/d/f8ddf002fb952ca8e675a6203b24d8d8bdc967e7.png" class="latex" alt="$\framebox[1.2\width]{(A)}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="31" height="28" /> as the correct answer.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h3>
<p>For every divisor <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/b/02bedd46306390e4c9b9293a00c7de6620b71b9b.png" class="latex" alt="$10^n$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/2/032192e19811c0715ae14a9f386dcb23a452ddd5.png" class="latex" alt="$d \le \sqrt{10^n}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="76" height="19" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/6/d06b308fd136eec027f386b833d0509067184c84.png" class="latex" alt="$\log d + \log \frac{10^n}{d} = \log 10^n = n$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="228" height="37" />. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/b/dbb3d839d5ede2cff78681306acb636d94f5381f.png" class="latex" alt="$\left \lfloor \frac{(n+1)^2}{2} \right \rfloor$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="84" height="43" /> divisors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/c/d5c8df5d77246a2c8b416a362b08671a0533c5f3.png" class="latex" alt="$10^n = 2^n \times 5^n$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="109" height="13" /> that are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/7/1b750dae7b5a45431f78f4896114b09055747062.png" class="latex" alt="$\le \sqrt{10^n}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="62" height="19" />. After casework on the parity of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, we find that the answer is given by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/f/bbf54cf19e1aa6172ac1b2cb7ff9f3de2a88d44f.png" class="latex" alt="$n \times \frac{(n+1)^2}{2} = 792 \Longrightarrow n = 11\ \mathrm{(A)}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="276" height="39" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h3>
<p>The sum is
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/3/1c38f57f0aa17c542dcf7ebdff1c7c83adc74e38.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum_{p=0}^{n}\sum_{q=0}^{n} \log(2^p5^q) = \sum_{p=0}^{n}\sum_{q=0}^{n}(p\log(2)+q\log(5))\]" width="358" height="49" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/b/cabf3a8d814d391dfcd62a4bac26b8ef3a1d699a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[= \sum_{p=0}^{n} ((n+1)p\log(2) + \frac{n(n+1)}{2}\log(5))\]" width="315" height="49" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/d/d3d31bcd2c41b361df4a1c5ce95cbe62b881c123.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[= \frac{n(n+1)^2}{2} \log(2) + \frac{n(n+1)^2}{2} \log(5)\]" width="295" height="39" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/9/1495da22c9df4bd8efee515f7eb9c21deac3f879.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[= \frac{n(n+1)^2}{2}\]" width="95" height="39" />
Trying for answer choices we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/3/093e72c1ebd91ade8c33ce3bcc3ccd3e640cad8c.png" class="latex" alt="$n=11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="52" height="12" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5">Solution 5</span></h2>
<p>Let integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> be the number of divisors <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/b/02bedd46306390e4c9b9293a00c7de6620b71b9b.png" class="latex" alt="$10^n$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> has. Then, we set up <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/4/0049275faba66d1146c7cab42dc026f0b71cee89.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{d}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> pairs of divisors such that each pair <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/8/8e8da0aef2d19017da7471378386d61620f288f5.png" class="latex" alt="$(a,b)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> satisfies <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/7/b77845dfa6d849654bd365564208ac2df3d206c9.png" class="latex" alt="$ab = 10^n$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="71" height="14" /> -- ex. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/a/93a2457cee7b130363579b8dbbcf9c9a6781a769.png" class="latex" alt="$(1, 10^n), (2, 5*10^{n-1})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="170" height="21" />, etc. Then the sum of the base-<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> logarithms is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/e/c8ed7ab65743b2de574d1dcb16ea63c557a0e5a4.png" class="latex" alt="$\sum_{j=1}^{\frac{d}{2}} \log_{10} a_j + \log_{10} b_j = \sum_{j=1}^{\frac{d}{2}} \log_{10} a_j b_j = \sum_{j=1}^{\frac{d}{2}} \log_{10} 10^n = \sum_{j=1}^{\frac{d}{2}} n = \frac{nd}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -25px" width="523" height="63" />
We can use the property that only perfect squares have an odd number of factors, as for perfect square <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/1/591f689f950feb4a9cff58ff3166d915f916a405.png" class="latex" alt="$p^2$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="16" height="18" />, we have ordered pair <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/9/ff947434e8d911cc2c48214043c07d950858857d.png" class="latex" alt="$(p,p)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="39" height="18" /> that works. For even <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, then, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/a/cfacc2a6cef04ec148c098d066422567a127cc2a.png" class="latex" alt="$10^{\frac{n}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="32" height="17" /> can be multiplied by itself to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/b/02bedd46306390e4c9b9293a00c7de6620b71b9b.png" class="latex" alt="$10^n$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> is odd. But, in our summation, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/4/0049275faba66d1146c7cab42dc026f0b71cee89.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{d}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> does not exist for even <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> is then odd, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> must be even. And, since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/a/e8a4dc574295060f1f0b23c92fe8bb87a168e911.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{nd}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="26" height="39" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/e/0ced7bf3e304ebb9fb9c073a8e5c5fde1740873a.png" class="latex" alt="$792$" width="26" height="12" />, We want to find a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> for our odd options <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/d/56d18fe37d0a4ef803d3c18da7382c157b40696f.png" class="latex" alt="$nd$" width="20" height="12" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/1/b31820e0ac338552f157d7b484d16eebcca4b903.png" class="latex" alt="$1584$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="38" height="14" />. For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/3/093e72c1ebd91ade8c33ce3bcc3ccd3e640cad8c.png" class="latex" alt="$n=11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="52" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/a/19a0f9e946dfa8ad9aaba3da35798cb0c590039b.png" class="latex" alt="$d=144$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="60" height="13" /> works, and integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> can not be found for other odd <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />. So, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/f/acf9528c9a455960535d1f08f0e8f4ecf67c69ef.png" class="latex" alt="$\framebox[1.2\width]{(A) 11}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="59" height="30" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_thinking">Alternative thinking</span></h2>
<p>After arriving at the equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/a/90a1118afc011d353982ec53a3e182ce4e1baaea.png" class="latex" alt="$n(n+1)^2 = 1584$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="135" height="19" />, notice that all of the answer choices are in the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/c/55c1d3de510a33234a7e5b97ecd3fa49df05ef48.png" class="latex" alt="$10+k$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="50" height="14" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/d/8edc124c7f7a2d3729f66545564dea7041aee003.png" class="latex" alt="$1-5$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="13" />. We notice that the ones digit of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/e/92ef0e959dd40d5c084352e0efb757e605f33225.png" class="latex" alt="$n(n+1)^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="73" height="19" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, and it is dependent on the ones digit of the answer choices. Trying <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/d/8edc124c7f7a2d3729f66545564dea7041aee003.png" class="latex" alt="$1-5$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="13" /> for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, we see that only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> yields a ones digit of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, so our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/8/c6878713578626763c38433b3f4c8c2205ad0c15.png" class="latex" alt="$11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" />.
</p>
