<p>Points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> lie on a <a href="/wiki/index.php/Circle" title="Circle">circle</a> centered at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/2/9023248d6c30dddbacd640f69f9347269d9f8763.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle AOB = 60^\circ$" width="104" height="13" />. A second circle is internally <a href="/wiki/index.php/Tangent" title="Tangent">tangent</a> to the first and tangent to both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/c/37ca7a38f7eed488e5f646482c71b0cf56222e28.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{OA}$" width="28" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/b/3fb5b6369d95e2d66ab4cd9acd69168b7a605c2a.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{OB}$" width="29" height="15" />. What is the ratio of the area of the smaller circle to that of the larger circle?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/1/f7104fe5f4583244636b77cee9c1a68e907d0991.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ \frac{1}{16}\qquad\mathrm{(B)}\ \frac{1}{9}\qquad\mathrm{(C)}\ \frac{1}{8}\qquad\mathrm{(D)}\ \frac{1}{6}\qquad\mathrm{(E)}\ \frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="370" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/2/5a2ab064db0b848b4eb7704a6b729afbca25398f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy]size(200); defaultpen(fontsize(10)); pair O=(0,0), A=(3,0), B=(3/2,3/2*3^.5), C=(3^.5,1), D=(3^.5,0), F=(1.5*3^.5,1.5), G=(2*3^.5,2); picture p = new picture;  draw(p,Circle(O,0.2)); clip(p,O--C--A--cycle); add(p); draw(Circle(O,3)); dot(A); dot(B); dot(C); dot(O); draw(A--O--B); draw(O--C--D); draw(C--F); draw(D-(0.2,0)--D-(0.2,-0.2)--D-(0,-0.2)); draw(Circle(C,1)); label(&quot;\(30^{\circ}\)&quot;,(0.65,0.15),O); label(&quot;\(r\)&quot;,(C+D)/2,E); label(&quot;\(2r\)&quot;,(O+C)/2,NNE); label(&quot;\(O\)&quot;,O,SW); label(&quot;\(r\)&quot;,(C+F)/2,SE); label(&quot;\(R\)&quot;,(O+A)/2-(0,0.3),S); label(&quot;\(P\)&quot;,C,NW); label(&quot;\(A\)&quot;,(3,0), A); label(&quot;\(B\)&quot;,(3/2,3/2*3^.5), B); label(&quot;\(C\)&quot;,(1.5*3^.5,1.5), G); label(&quot;\(Q\)&quot;,D,SE);[/asy]" width="335" height="312" />
Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> be the center of the small circle with radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />, and let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> be the point where the small circle is <a href="/wiki/index.php/Tangent" title="Tangent">tangent</a> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/a/b7a762ac8b4e23ffdd778ad6d5a8b97df8c7ce01.png" class="latex" alt="$OA$" width="28" height="13" />. Also, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> be the point where the small circle is tangent to the big circle with radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" />.
</p><p>Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/3/8d3e207d8997e0622840471fe83b7a66949beab3.png" class="latex" alt="$PQO$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="16" /> is a right triangle. Angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/f/0af8bd418bbfcc17ee85fd2d959d120c3df86222.png" class="latex" alt="$POQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="16" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/1/651ba418a810d971dbc8a326d49a71fe1541fae0.png" class="latex" alt="$30$" width="17" height="12" /> degrees because line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/d/25dcc9ecb8e7c6fb80766c186721353e6c3dc398.png" class="latex" alt="$OP$" width="28" height="12" /> bisects angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/9/cd97737c9a1eb5146725902d4e4c4bda1388129b.png" class="latex" alt="$AOB$" width="42" height="13" /> (this can be proved by dropping a perpendicular line from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> to line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/6/0964c9cf406ef8e5ec8beaa6a92125f2d8f90460.png" class="latex" alt="$OB$" width="28" height="12" />, letting their intersection be point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" />, and proving triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/3/8d3e207d8997e0622840471fe83b7a66949beab3.png" class="latex" alt="$PQO$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/6/e06df19f0a61385b245c77c6234b44fc9971366c.png" class="latex" alt="$PSO$" width="40" height="12" /> congruent), meaning that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/3/8d3e207d8997e0622840471fe83b7a66949beab3.png" class="latex" alt="$PQO$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="16" /> is a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/c/47c78b85058ee5e17f88e2cde302e48aad826b35.png" class="latex" alt="$30-60-90$" width="98" height="12" /> triangle. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/2/8425c926c4a6715b6e223d6124b403a02338685b.png" class="latex" alt="$OP=2PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="89" height="16" />.
</p><p>Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/5/7e5b6270a90f3261639b1778c53e570d4671f12b.png" class="latex" alt="$OP=OC-PC=OC-r=R-r$" width="284" height="12" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/d/9ed6747e8456267ff1c365a7815e7d7c46c175b8.png" class="latex" alt="$R-r=2PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="106" height="16" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/1/641531b0f4524e4a31c24cf7947cb0d55ab8ed18.png" class="latex" alt="$R-r=2r$" width="86" height="12" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/2/f12e19989fab3309536ec7c2c898feae67d29018.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}=\frac{r}{R}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="53" height="37" />.
</p><p>Ratio of areas of circles is ratio of radii squared, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/6/916fe32a0ba7f40bc2ada4ad513e28f97b0d8a26.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{9} \Rightarrow \boxed{B}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="138" height="46" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Like in Solution 1, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> be the center of the small circle with radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />, and let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> be the point where the small circle is <a href="/wiki/index.php/Tangent" title="Tangent">tangent</a> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/a/b7a762ac8b4e23ffdd778ad6d5a8b97df8c7ce01.png" class="latex" alt="$OA$" width="28" height="13" />.
</p><p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" /> be the tangency point of the two circles. As shown in Solution 1, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/0/170ab4cacfd07103a1d82e5812150076d6b19817.png" class="latex" alt="$POQ = 30$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="84" height="16" /> degrees, so angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/5/c5524cee19d26ee72e0087c009036c473fc7289b.png" class="latex" alt="$NOA$" width="44" height="13" /> is also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/1/651ba418a810d971dbc8a326d49a71fe1541fae0.png" class="latex" alt="$30$" width="17" height="12" /> degrees. Let the line tangent to the two circles at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" /> intersect <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/a/b7a762ac8b4e23ffdd778ad6d5a8b97df8c7ce01.png" class="latex" alt="$OA$" width="28" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/6/0964c9cf406ef8e5ec8beaa6a92125f2d8f90460.png" class="latex" alt="$OB$" width="28" height="12" /> at points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />, respectively. Since line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/4/0948661e822f2a953c43a57ac9e40b2734476de4.png" class="latex" alt="$CD$" width="29" height="12" /> is tangent to circles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />, it must be perpendicular to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/b/afb3aa28627d70a5c4421063effb76bd2241a7e3.png" class="latex" alt="$ON$" width="31" height="12" />, meaning that angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/8/5283aee6a1a3b87ce49749cccd899750344cd3b2.png" class="latex" alt="$ONC$" width="45" height="12" /> must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/c/bfcfee7bd8a5e15e5f6b7881c138de43651572a9.png" class="latex" alt="$90$" width="17" height="12" /> degrees. Because angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/5/c5524cee19d26ee72e0087c009036c473fc7289b.png" class="latex" alt="$NOA$" width="44" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/1/651ba418a810d971dbc8a326d49a71fe1541fae0.png" class="latex" alt="$30$" width="17" height="12" /> degrees, angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/e/c4eacc5a2bcc93cccd0fe7ec82056be4f7494633.png" class="latex" alt="$DCO$" width="43" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/d/24d34a272eaf62e103cde57be21f0d0452fcfc2a.png" class="latex" alt="$180-30-90 = 60$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="150" height="13" /> degrees. Angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/5/d6509e22d9e5fa1534c90758fa40092a7c338234.png" class="latex" alt="$DOC$" width="44" height="12" /> is also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" /> degrees, so triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/5/d6509e22d9e5fa1534c90758fa40092a7c338234.png" class="latex" alt="$DOC$" width="44" height="12" /> is equilateral.
</p><p>Note that an equilateral triangle's incenter is also its centroid. This means the center of the inscribed circle is also the centroid. From properties of median lengths, the radius of the large circle is 3 times the radius of the small circle. 
</p><p>Then the ratio of areas will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/1/4e1965f8d70832ded66434c75a7bd52b75090af0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> squared, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/d/66d0f64d1cc50d3016e180bd89ee099d9ba6ccba.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{9}\Rightarrow \boxed{\text{B}}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="65" height="37" />.
</p>
