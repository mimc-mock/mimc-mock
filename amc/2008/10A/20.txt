<p><a href="/wiki/index.php/Trapezoid" title="Trapezoid">Trapezoid</a> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> has bases <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/0/ce0bfc603f9a044e308b1a1fdadca917e81ad2dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CD}$" width="30" height="15" /> and diagonals intersecting at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/8/9f86ef08bd2efc36f00246e468c2c24257a0e40e.png" class="latex" alt="$K.$" width="19" height="12" /> Suppose that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/f/a9fe059ea3506a79afe2df3583a0b5e584fb92d6.png" class="latex" alt="$AB = 9$" width="61" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/0/3c06c6800727ffb2aee19c25d3c3c6c4c54b0f37.png" class="latex" alt="$DC = 12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="71" height="13" />, and the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/4/4a426f22363bc6600863560ea8f3337759b320c0.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AKD$" width="61" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/e/c6e7f55f92a9769af1f0dcf23985cc7a3d87d636.png" class="latex" alt="$24.$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="21" height="12" /> What is the area of trapezoid <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/0/270aae21a079d3c87eb2675293aa1d0f8ea372f1.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ 92\qquad\mathrm{(B)}\ 94\qquad\mathrm{(C)}\ 96\qquad\mathrm{(D)}\ 98 \qquad\mathrm{(E)}\ 100$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="396" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/5/a8512170c7bb9c8c44a4195790890290d8352b64.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pointpen = black; pathpen = black + linewidth(0.62);  /* cse5 */ pen sm = fontsize(10);             /* small font pen */ pair D=(0,0),C=(12,0), K=(7,16/3); /* note that K.x is arbitrary, as generator for A,B */ pair A=7*K/4-3*C/4, B=7*K/4-3*D/4; D(MP(&quot;A&quot;,A,N)--MP(&quot;B&quot;,B,N)--MP(&quot;C&quot;,C)--MP(&quot;D&quot;,D)--A--C);D(B--D);D(A--MP(&quot;K&quot;,K)--D--cycle,linewidth(0.7)); MP(&quot;9&quot;,(A+B)/2,N,sm);MP(&quot;12&quot;,(C+D)/2,sm);MP(&quot;24&quot;,(A+D)/2+(1,0),E); [/asy]" width="668" height="535" /></center>
<p>Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/4/f84a7716f13b8bc8ee2750f47cbd26eca19ea532.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB} \parallel \overline{DC}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="77" height="20" /> it follows that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/d/94d6e8ff630070c2f4c4ca43d55ec1361793cc38.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABK \sim \triangle CDK$" width="148" height="13" />. Thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/5/5d5a81ce3eb1d89cafbda445df4d6d2a331e8478.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{KA}{KC} = \frac{KB}{KD} = \frac{AB}{DC} = \frac{3}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="189" height="38" />. 
</p><p>We now introduce the concept of <a href="/wiki/index.php/Area_ratios" title="Area ratios">area ratios</a>: given two triangles that share the same height, the ratio of the areas is equal to the ratio of their bases. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/1/ab145053829a24d217380ddb430f9d447007898e.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AKB, \triangle AKD$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="131" height="17" /> share a common <a href="/wiki/index.php/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/7/4373e7b4be983c0315e82e1fda1df66ed34dd7fb.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BD}$" width="30" height="15" />, it follows that (we let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/8/4/18412a965332198f2b2d5c0bb1c491330047d92a.png" class="latex" alt="$[\triangle \ldots]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="48" height="18" /> denote the area of the triangle) <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/d/eed58cd07515b1733fcc6a5b95ea36fb385b7c10.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{[\triangle AKB]}{[\triangle AKD]} = \frac{KB}{KD} = \frac{3}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="172" height="43" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/c/01c8718ee2a2aa3bd4eb6e803892547b8d61fa1b.png" class="latex" alt="$[\triangle AKB] = \frac{3}{4}(24) = 18$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="182" height="38" />. Similarly, we find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/4/ed41cd2ecadd00ff045b470f1e65bf28da3d6dfe.png" class="latex" alt="$[\triangle DKC] = \frac{4}{3}(24) = 32$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="184" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/c/e7ce9a8758dfdea64da26bdff894339c1379bfe7.png" class="latex" alt="$[\triangle BKC] = 24$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="114" height="18" />.
</p><p>Therefore, the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/8/e2891d9d69739764a5a6e2042414a91fa836d880.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD = [AKD] + [AKB] + [BKC] + [CKD] = 24 + 18 + 24 + 32 = 98\ \mathrm{(D)}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="611" height="18" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We denote <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/1/da106a1676da5205f5c12c490b671acb3e63a490.png" class="latex" alt="$KA$" width="30" height="13" /> with length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/e/52e136d945e3a5185f6e7693f4f4981f9dd5ff9c.png" class="latex" alt="$KD$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="34" height="14" /> with length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/8/2c8ca7f90260aae1b92a54ded47804dde58405fb.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{4x}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="21" height="37" /> (which follows from similar triangles), and we denote <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/7/947ee86eee22f741a185fafe43728151758a6e07.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle{AKD}=\theta$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="94" height="14" />. Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/a/7aa840de36fed1c4abd935cd3f53106111c9ad85.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{4x^2}{3}\cdot \sin\theta=48\implies 4x^2\cdot \sin\theta=36$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="289" height="41" />. The areas of triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/8/6c8691d2314f3cb16450506e54a5a4277592813d.png" class="latex" alt="$ABK$" width="45" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/4/6c4f63f3ca997a2d8cb1ab074f6e7cea204daba4.png" class="latex" alt="$CDK$" width="46" height="12" /> combined are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/3/fb39cf37a5608be3bb0ed6927ff070c1e91cf2fa.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{x^2\cdot\sin\theta+\frac{16x^2}{9}\cdot\sin\theta}{2}=\frac{25x^2}{18}\cdot\sin\theta=36\cdot\frac{25}{18}=50$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="404" height="45" />. Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/f/f8f6a419ce463967f6cf452d524cbad2c754f5c9.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABCD]=[ABK]+[BCK]+[CDK]+[ADK]=48+50=98\ \mathrm{(D)}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="544" height="20" />, as desired.
-mop
</p>
