<p>Triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/6/f7661519159a6678741c369a2e897dfd24579670.png" class="latex" alt="$AC=3$" width="60" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/3/a5322c7f896502c208bbb4a354efc8d212620667.png" class="latex" alt="$BC=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="62" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/f/7ff29028b2432b4e2cf3d924ac82114813de066d.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=5$" width="61" height="13" />. Point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> is on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/0/ce0bfc603f9a044e308b1a1fdadca917e81ad2dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CD}$" width="30" height="15" /> bisects the right angle. The inscribed circles of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/e/b8eabc87afe4c11a47e4aa27628d5517af4866b9.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ADC$" width="59" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/f/12f372757d55c2d07adc720932c6d6e1396aa389.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BCD$" width="60" height="13" /> have radii <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/5/5f51b54576318cbb9dcd328a0ff444361cff9f0d.png" class="latex" alt="$r_a$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="10" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/0/cb01353fabf2257b6bde123ad5970eb7891a59b4.png" class="latex" alt="$r_b$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="13" height="10" />, respectively. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/7/6577ecdcb3d8100aabb7614ea8332deb39769151.png" class="latex" alt="$r_a/r_b$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/9/b4900a393c93b1c977e5c95efbceb20aae50468c.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ \frac{1}{28}\left(10-\sqrt{2}\right)\qquad\mathrm{(B)}\ \frac{3}{56}\left(10-\sqrt{2}\right)\qquad\mathrm{(C)}\ \frac{1}{14}\left(10-\sqrt{2}\right)\qquad\mathrm{(D)}\ \frac{5}{56}\left(10-\sqrt{2}\right)\\\mathrm{(E)}\ \frac{3}{28}\left(10-\sqrt{2}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="682" height="75" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/0/f003e5fe565c49bade1dc1e2b332a46d1a084ca9.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] import olympiad; size(300); defaultpen(0.8); pair C=(0,0),A=(0,3),B=(4,0),D=(4-2.28571,1.71429); pair O=incenter(A,C,D), P=incenter(B,C,D); picture p = new picture;  draw(p,Circle(C,0.2)); draw(p,Circle(B,0.2)); clip(p,B--C--D--cycle); add(p); draw(A--B--C--D--C--cycle); draw(incircle(A,C,D)); draw(incircle(B,C,D)); dot(O);dot(P); label(&quot;\(A\)&quot;,A,W); label(&quot;\(B\)&quot;,B,E); label(&quot;\(C\)&quot;,C,W); label(&quot;\(D\)&quot;,D,NE); label(&quot;\(O_A\)&quot;,O,W); label(&quot;\(O_B\)&quot;,P,W); label(&quot;\(3\)&quot;,(A+C)/2,W); label(&quot;\(4\)&quot;,(B+C)/2,S); label(&quot;\(\frac{15}{7}\)&quot;,(A+D)/2,NE); label(&quot;\(\frac{20}{7}\)&quot;,(B+D)/2,NE); label(&quot;\(45^{\circ}\)&quot;,(.2,.1),E); label(&quot;\(\sin \theta = \frac{3}{5}\)&quot;,B-(.2,-.1),W); [/asy]" width="502" height="372" /></center>
<p>By the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Angle_Bisector_Theorem" class="mw-redirect" title="Angle Bisector Theorem">Angle Bisector Theorem</a>, 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/9/9c9f464a5fad91f50fc120b232c9b27b70d1c93f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{BD}{4} = \frac{5-BD}{3} \Longrightarrow BD = \frac{20}7\]" width="236" height="38" />
By <a href="/wiki/index.php/Law_of_Sines" title="Law of Sines">Law of Sines</a> on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/f/12f372757d55c2d07adc720932c6d6e1396aa389.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BCD$" width="60" height="13" />, 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/f/9df704c41b92bf28dfdabff885b9a06d3fcb4441.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{BD}{\sin 45^{\circ}} = \frac{CD}{\sin \angle B} \Longrightarrow \frac{20/7}{\sqrt{2}/2} = \frac{CD}{3/5} \Longrightarrow CD=\frac{12\sqrt{2}}{7}\]" width="416" height="47" />
Since the area of a triangle satisfies <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/d/66dc3f0f937032d1109f338a8d694db762e4ea41.png" class="latex" alt="$[\triangle]=rs$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="67" height="18" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/7/467175292b1af782aeaad23dfb360cdd5756eb40.png" class="latex" alt="$r =$" width="27" height="8" /> the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Inradius" title="Inradius">inradius</a> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/3/ae31665bdc726f2295afd483283b44cbf3d43ea7.png" class="latex" alt="$s =$" width="27" height="8" /> the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Semiperimeter" title="Semiperimeter">semiperimeter</a>, we have 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/7/af76ed60fa2fdfb3bfb69329f9c290478535c124.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{r_A}{r_B} = \frac{[ACD] \cdot s_B}{[BCD] \cdot s_A}\]" width="135" height="43" />
Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/7/0f7a55113a84ee4c04eb66f66d08310c32533ca2.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ACD$" width="59" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/f/12f372757d55c2d07adc720932c6d6e1396aa389.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BCD$" width="60" height="13" /> share the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> (to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" />), their areas are the ratio of their bases, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/d/efd8576e6845c12c5b4c017aa1280a1b061fd3a6.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{[ACD]}{[BCD]} = \frac{AD}{BD} = \frac{3}{4}\]" width="151" height="43" />
The semiperimeters are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/8/e581fff4cba3c21d41b4e3888cb1dafeca949abd.png" class="latex" alt="$s_A = \left(3 + \frac{15}{7} + \frac{12\sqrt{2}}{7}\right)\left/\right.2 = \frac{18+6\sqrt{2}}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="320" height="53" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/b/5abaa90f65ea26f7f65fec8fb24e7f8d1c65b523.png" class="latex" alt="$s_B = \frac{24+ 6\sqrt{2}}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="120" height="41" />. Thus,
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/3/b439f853787f7bda3a9170414daae8b3f8056e69.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} \frac{r_A}{r_B} &amp;= \frac{[ACD] \cdot s_B}{[BCD] \cdot s_A} = \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{(24+ 6\sqrt{2})/7}{(18+6\sqrt{2})/7} \\ &amp;= \frac{3(4+\sqrt{2})}{4(3+\sqrt{2})} \cdot \left(\frac{3-\sqrt{2}}{3-\sqrt{2}}\right) = \frac{3}{28}(10-\sqrt{2}) \Rightarrow \mathrm{(E)}\qquad \blacksquare \end{align*}" width="456" height="107" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/b/c4b25343784f830ea4d327aef305fb52a67bbc4a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] import olympiad; import geometry; size(300); defaultpen(0.8);  pair C=(0,0),A=(0,3),B=(4,0),D=(4-2.28571,1.71429); pair O=incenter(A,C,D), P=incenter(B,C,D); line cd = line(C, D);  picture p = new picture; picture q = new picture;  picture r = new picture; picture s = new picture;  draw(p,Circle(C,0.2)); clip(p,P--C--D--cycle);  draw(q, Circle(C, 0.3)); clip(q, O--C--D--cycle);  line l1 = perpendicular(O, cd); draw(r, l1); clip(r, C--D--O--cycle);  line l2 = perpendicular(P, cd); draw(s, l2); clip(s, C--P--D--cycle);   add(p); add(q); add(r); add(s);  draw(A--B--C--D--C--cycle); draw(incircle(A,C,D)); draw(incircle(B,C,D)); draw(C--O); draw(C--P); dot(O); dot(P);  point inter1 = intersectionpoint(l1, cd); point inter2 = intersectionpoint(l2, cd); dot(inter1); dot(inter2);  label(&quot;\(A\)&quot;,A,W); label(&quot;\(B\)&quot;,B,E); label(&quot;\(C\)&quot;,C,W); label(&quot;\(D\)&quot;,D,NE); label(&quot;\(O_a\)&quot;,O,W); label(&quot;\(O_b\)&quot;,P,E); label(&quot;\(3\)&quot;,(A+C)/2,W); label(&quot;\(4\)&quot;,(B+C)/2,S); label(&quot;\(\frac{15}{7}\)&quot;,(A+D)/2,NE); label(&quot;\(\frac{20}{7}\)&quot;,(B+D)/2,NE); label(&quot;\(M\)&quot;, inter1, 2W); label(&quot;\(N\)&quot;, inter2, 2E); [/asy]" width="502" height="372" /></center>
<p>We start by finding the length of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc56862b1fdb71204894da278e42ef75de73a40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AD$" width="28" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/f/89fff82bb65d0215e49c8c91cb7c553da52205e2.png" class="latex" alt="$BD$" width="29" height="12" /> as in solution 1. Using the angle bisector theorem, we see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/4/5a4437a642286d2aee04ca0e10e596290b014967.png" class="latex" alt="$AD = \frac{15}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="74" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/1/9f1460c7c98f3072b255e0ec80249978dba0c94f.png" class="latex" alt="$BD = \frac{20}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="75" height="37" />. Using Stewart's Theorem gives us the equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/6/cc640f4e13d18e2926c51389f8a099811b76732d.png" class="latex" alt="$5d^2 + \frac{1500}{49} = \frac{240}{7} + \frac{180}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="196" height="38" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> is the length of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/4/0948661e822f2a953c43a57ac9e40b2734476de4.png" class="latex" alt="$CD$" width="29" height="12" />. Solving gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/c/0fcddd00d18da3fd12114b461b6a16b2f6c6c071.png" class="latex" alt="$d = \frac{12\sqrt{2}}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="78" height="41" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/9/a293f2739845dd2791f5c2d79bf1b3334da211e3.png" class="latex" alt="$CD = \frac{12\sqrt{2}}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="99" height="41" />.
</p><p>Call the incenters of triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/e/6ce2cd123db24a7b8373999aea7a98927e3b3c81.png" class="latex" alt="$ACD$" width="43" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/a/d7ada763e99852a6e44a15d46cce3c3709a944d5.png" class="latex" alt="$BCD$" width="44" height="12" />  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/5/2f589508ca4f105cd721213b2ea57a2b1bbf6845.png" class="latex" alt="$O_a$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="21" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/1/471c719d8420d688129385e2704aea695cbbb208.png" class="latex" alt="$O_b$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" /> respectively. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/5/2f589508ca4f105cd721213b2ea57a2b1bbf6845.png" class="latex" alt="$O_a$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="21" height="15" /> is an incenter, it lies on the angle bisector of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/b/55b591cd4cbc3cd3d8746972dae656279220ce9a.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle ACD$" width="56" height="13" />. Similarly, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/1/471c719d8420d688129385e2704aea695cbbb208.png" class="latex" alt="$O_b$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" /> lies on the angle bisector of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/3/23386faeefe37629243fa264877b288499db5048.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BCD$" width="57" height="12" />. Call the point on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/4/0948661e822f2a953c43a57ac9e40b2734476de4.png" class="latex" alt="$CD$" width="29" height="12" /> tangent to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/5/2f589508ca4f105cd721213b2ea57a2b1bbf6845.png" class="latex" alt="$O_a$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="21" height="15" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" />, and the point tangent to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/1/471c719d8420d688129385e2704aea695cbbb208.png" class="latex" alt="$O_b$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/0/0d00f71184ceb781b314311d89340f9587a1d202.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle CO_aM$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="71" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/6/066ab8afd20683679682f8036e5fdba87cfa3c15.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle CO_bN$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="67" height="16" /> are right, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/c/1dcadf192a09f18d695596b1d27ddb0452d4a121.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle O_aCM = \angle O_bCN$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="157" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/e/bbe2dcfe5feed16575768660fbde3bfbc4e53f31.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle CO_aM \sim \triangle CO_bN$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="163" height="16" />. Then, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/1/021b3c0131014c43e57679390573b1e1e6c58dcb.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{r_a}{r_b} = \frac{CM}{CN}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="80" height="40" />. 
</p><p>We now use common tangents to find the length of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/3/fa38fd42cdaa5db0dd4e0f7df98ae79a598eb627.png" class="latex" alt="$CM$" width="33" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/3/ba369b33271c6fb52789f86292cc68559a2e0411.png" class="latex" alt="$CN$" width="31" height="12" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/0/cc040bb4c0e70ad388d2f94dd6d4c05d88dcea8e.png" class="latex" alt="$CM = m$" width="73" height="12" />, and the length of the other tangents be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/f/36f73fc1312ee0349b3f3a0f3bd9eb5504339011.png" class="latex" alt="$p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" />. Since common tangents are equal, we can write that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/4/56452c86fb2a90c3e76074f4c54857d52cc689fa.png" class="latex" alt="$m + n = \frac{12\sqrt{2}}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="118" height="41" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/e/08ee1ae2b67d5b43cb831029d3b0e0d6f2cc0a0e.png" class="latex" alt="$n + p = \frac{15}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="87" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/c/65ca408794f1fbb3055730fd9e1e834ea863b170.png" class="latex" alt="$m + p = 3$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="80" height="16" />. Solving gives us that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/0/7d0d34a9ca411d7142923d725988c3954d549b58.png" class="latex" alt="$CM = m = \frac{6\sqrt{2} + 3}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="165" height="41" />. Similarly, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/f/1cfa802cbd9defedb76172642020ea64eac030ce.png" class="latex" alt="$CN = \frac{6\sqrt{2} + 4}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="122" height="41" />. 
</p><p>We see now that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/7/917275e173f97146708ac07c6c09a718347182b0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{r_a}{r_b} = \frac{\frac{6\sqrt{2} + 3}{7}}{\frac{6\sqrt{2} + 4}{7}} = \frac{6\sqrt{2} + 3}{6\sqrt{2} + 4} = \frac{60-6\sqrt{2}}{56} = \frac{3}{28}(10 - \sqrt{2}) \Rightarrow \boxed{E}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="468" height="53" />
</p>
