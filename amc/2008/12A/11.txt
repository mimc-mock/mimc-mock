<p>Three cubes are each formed from the pattern shown. They are then stacked on a table one on top of another so that the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/0/030e19e4e2f1c074127cb50581e8496470c5419f.png" class="latex" alt="$13$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> visible numbers have the greatest possible sum. What is that sum?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/2/a32f7c9839807c1cb204fd5a2f87f33747244a18.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(.8cm);  pen p = linewidth(1); draw(shift(-2,0)*unitsquare,p); label(&quot;1&quot;,(-1.5,0.5)); draw(shift(-1,0)*unitsquare,p); label(&quot;2&quot;,(-0.5,0.5)); draw(unitsquare,p); label(&quot;32&quot;,(0.5,0.5)); draw(shift(1,0)*unitsquare,p); label(&quot;16&quot;,(1.5,0.5)); draw(shift(0,1)*unitsquare,p); label(&quot;4&quot;,(0.5,1.5)); draw(shift(0,-1)*unitsquare,p); label(&quot;8&quot;,(0.5,-0.5)); [/asy]" width="155" height="118" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/2/3c2ace5dac185bbeade095036708f15cc6bc2a2f.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ 154\qquad\mathrm{(B)}\ 159\qquad\mathrm{(C)}\ 164\qquad\mathrm{(D)}\ 167\qquad\mathrm{(E)}\ 189$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="432" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>To maximize the sum of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/0/030e19e4e2f1c074127cb50581e8496470c5419f.png" class="latex" alt="$13$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> faces that are showing, we can minimize the sum of the numbers of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> faces that are not showing. 
</p><p>The bottom <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> cubes each have a pair of opposite faces that are covered up. When the cube is folded, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/4/074a3b804e4a509836b0978681294fd2286aa4be.png" class="latex" alt="$(1,32)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="47" height="18" />; <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/5/1a58f666c83cc1d6af19f092994ccfc341d00a7b.png" class="latex" alt="$(2,16)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="47" height="18" />; and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/f/57f3b3e653b86ce72901cd1bcf7dd2a5caa3dcfe.png" class="latex" alt="$(4,8)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="42" height="20" /> are opposite pairs. Clearly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/4/a14c6975c1087c1b937fa7d857b1394bfa29990e.png" class="latex" alt="$4+8=12$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="82" height="14" /> has the smallest sum. 
</p><p>The top cube has 1 number that is not showing. The smallest number on a face is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. 
</p><p>So, the minimum sum of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> unexposed faces is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/4/c148107cb8aa83a47efddb5689e98af2a188ff53.png" class="latex" alt="$2\cdot12+1=25$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="113" height="14" />.  Since the sum of the numbers on all the cubes is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/9/97991456473f4dc9d0ca30d573e5ffa87aa4b58b.png" class="latex" alt="$3(32+16+8+4+2+1)=189$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="258" height="18" />, the maximum possible sum of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/0/030e19e4e2f1c074127cb50581e8496470c5419f.png" class="latex" alt="$13$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> visible numbers is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/6/456e59fba2e43d7dac4af7660726d87e405ed0d9.png" class="latex" alt="$189-25=164 \Rightarrow C$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="161" height="13" />. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Conversely, maximize the sum. Two cubes have 4 exposed faces. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/9/7193ac8b0e51b6601c96bcff8e6fbaf3a2a52bf2.png" class="latex" alt="$32&gt;16+8+4+2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="154" height="14" />, 32 must be on the side. There are two distinct (asymmetrical) configurations with 32 on the side, but <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/6/ed6c6d85adb1cd6ff6ce2a4108f509c57628b631.png" class="latex" alt="$(32,16,2,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="91" height="18" /> is the greatest at 51. There are 2 such cubes, so 51*2. The top cube has one unexposed face, so use 1 as the unexposed face. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/7/3776eaa3a8d61412bb6f462b8395657c5db830b8.png" class="latex" alt="$2(51)+32+16+8+4+2=164 \implies \boxed{\textbf{(C)} 164}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="384" height="28" /> 
</p><p>~BJHHar
</p>
