<p>A dart board is a regular octagon divided into regions as shown. Suppose that a dart thrown at the board is equally likely to land anywhere on the board. What is the probability that the dart lands within the center square?
</p>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/1/68141b2184d1682ae8f8d03c273456191e2cb1bd.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(10mm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt)+fontsize(10pt)); dotfactor=4;  pair A=(0,1), B=(1,0), C=(1+sqrt(2),0), D=(2+sqrt(2),1), E=(2+sqrt(2),1+sqrt(2)), F=(1+sqrt(2),2+sqrt(2)), G=(1,2+sqrt(2)), H=(0,1+sqrt(2));  draw(A--B--C--D--E--F--G--H--cycle); draw(A--D); draw(B--G); draw(C--F); draw(E--H);  [/asy]" width="165" height="165" />
</center>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/e/77e5b31a4cf66884f473bb5281f7d2e8012e469d.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{\sqrt{2} - 1}{2} \qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{1}{4} \qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{2 - \sqrt{2}}{2} \qquad\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4} \qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 2 - \sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="520" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/4/dc4877d213827aa8426c61a0f2dec96c9f470de2.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(10mm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt)+fontsize(10pt)); dotfactor=1;  pair A=(0,1), B=(1,0), C=(1+sqrt(2),0), D=(2+sqrt(2),1), E=(2+sqrt(2),1+sqrt(2)), F=(1+sqrt(2),2+sqrt(2)), G=(1,2+sqrt(2)), H=(0,1+sqrt(2)); pair I=(1,1), J=(1+sqrt(2),1), K=(1+sqrt(2),1+sqrt(2)), L=(1,1+sqrt(2));  draw(A--B--C--D--E--F--G--H--cycle); draw(A--D); draw(B--G); draw(C--F); draw(E--H);  pair[] ps={A,B,C,D,E,F,G,H,I,J,K,L}; dot(ps); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,W); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,S); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,S); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,E); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,E,E); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,F,N); label(&quot;$G$&quot;,G,N); label(&quot;$H$&quot;,H,W); label(&quot;$I$&quot;,I,NE); label(&quot;$J$&quot;,J,NW); label(&quot;$K$&quot;,K,SW); label(&quot;$L$&quot;,L,SE); label(&quot;$\sqrt{2}$&quot;,midpoint(B--C),S); label(&quot;$1$&quot;,midpoint(A--I),N); [/asy]" width="218" height="205" />
</center>
<p>If the side lengths of the dart board and the side lengths of the center square are all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/5/505ee066b7bae0313a6d0a6f974e588e46cd5263.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{2},$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="28" height="20" /> then the side length of the legs of the triangles are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/f/caf3278e667091d723eeb0373115b790e5a51625.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} \text{area of center square} &amp;: \sqrt{2} \times \sqrt{2} = 2\\ \text{total area} &amp;: (\sqrt{2})^2 + 4(1 \times \sqrt{2}) + 4(1 \times 1 \times \frac{1}{2}) = 2 + 4\sqrt{2} + 2 = 4 + 4\sqrt{2} \end{align*}" width="643" height="66" />
</p><p>Use <a href="/wiki/index.php/Geometric_probability" title="Geometric probability">Geometric probability</a> by putting the area of the desired region over the area of the entire region.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/b/2bbad9c1ddee3983e69e37fd039410e18bd0b83a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{2}{4+4\sqrt{2}} = \frac{1}{2+2\sqrt{2}} \times \frac{2-2\sqrt{2}}{2-2\sqrt{2}} = \frac{2-2\sqrt{2}}{-4} = \boxed{\textbf{(A)} \frac{\sqrt{2}-1}{2}}\]" width="468" height="53" />
</p><p><br />
Explanation:
The area of the octagon consists of the area of the triangles, the rectangles, and the square in the middle. Assume the octagon has side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. The triangles are right isosceles triangles with the hypotenuse 1, so their side length is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/7/cc7c4b51e06b26289d792878cbf9efac28aac903.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" /> and the area of one triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/d/33d00132c3eab8b8542c321492000ea9382297ae.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\cdot\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2}=\frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="135" height="43" />. The area of all 4 triangles is then just <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. The rectangles share one side with the octagon, and another with the triangle. The octagon has side length 1, and the triangle has side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/7/cc7c4b51e06b26289d792878cbf9efac28aac903.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" /> as found earlier. So the area of one rectangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/7/cc7c4b51e06b26289d792878cbf9efac28aac903.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" />. The area of all 4 is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/d/f7d1b0389a638a0cabed4ec69cc26ba9d666c3a5.png" class="latex" alt="$4\cdot\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}=2\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="110" height="43" />. Finally, the area of the square in the middle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/d/e5d4f8730d6f40b317864c41f0a26366fb04b232.png" class="latex" alt="$1\cdot1=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="63" height="12" />. The total area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/9/b295987c707316c33923a2cf6ea36e90e25664de.png" class="latex" alt="$1+1+2\sqrt{2}=2+2\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="187" height="21" />. We want the area of the square over the area of the octagon, which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/1/8f15ddb32a9fd7120ad1d53b59f65ccfba97751f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2+2\sqrt{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="70" height="43" />. Rationalize by multiplying both numerator and denominator by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/e/5de0852e1f9d4c59322ab4a9c958ca544e359495.png" class="latex" alt="$2-2\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="65" height="18" />: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/8/a78ed3c599866750e9b386a9f64d8f3914449ed9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2+2\sqrt{2}}\cdot\frac{2-2\sqrt{2}}{2-2\sqrt{2}}=\frac{2-2\sqrt{2}}{\left(2+2\sqrt{2}\right)\left(2-2\sqrt{2}\right)}$" style="vertical-align: -31px" width="355" height="61" />. By the difference of squares, the denominator reduces to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/d/72d85c6959aaabfff6e0104544ff25c1445f3f1e.png" class="latex" alt="$-4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="23" height="12" /> and the fraction is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/b/2fb6be7a66f7a929700352691038f95d8eaad613.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2-2\sqrt{2}}{-4}=\frac{\sqrt{2}-1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="154" height="45" /> which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/c/3dcc3eec7fe7ca54f6611fc9e9ee403bb28ffabe.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(A) } \frac{\sqrt{2}-1}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="108" height="54" />.
</p><p>~Explanation by JH. L
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Area of a regular octagon is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/c/b7cedae9fcc3da864581ec1fac6853dd62505b74.png" class="latex" alt="$2(1+\sqrt{2})a^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="94" height="21" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is the side. Hence the answer is obvious now.
</p>
