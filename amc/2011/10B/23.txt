<p>What is the hundreds digit of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/c/f0c522b9a1aa80756ef8bbe73b690e91daedd0f5.png" class="latex" alt="$2011^{2011}?$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="71" height="15" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/4/fa481fab5ce0083cb2aaa4289d45ee3eaace7dcc.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 1 \qquad \textbf{(B) } 4 \qquad \textbf{(C) }5 \qquad \textbf{(D) } 6 \qquad \textbf{(E) } 9$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="353" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/9/5f9607cb9281ad4aa64d97a916131d928ce15c92.png" class="latex" alt="$2011 \equiv 11  \pmod{1000},$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="182" height="18" /> we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/5/d1512d0198fbfef915f6ce26a06d260d60079cfe.png" class="latex" alt="$2011^{2011} \equiv 11^{2011}  \pmod{1000}.$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="236" height="19" />
</p><p>To compute this, we use a clever application of the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Binomial_theorem" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial theorem">binomial theorem</a>.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/f/87fc9e624924165545fa4b1d6e58ed0f8abd5252.png" class="latex" alt="$\begin{aligned} 11^{2011} &amp;= (1+10)^{2011} \\ &amp;= 1 + \dbinom{2011}{1} \cdot 10 + \dbinom{2011}{2} \cdot 10^2 + \cdots \end{aligned}$" style="vertical-align: -36px" width="367" height="81" />
</p><p>In all of the other terms, the power of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> is greater than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> and so is equivalent to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> modulo <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/3/5d357c2ec1bbc5f9f3b5562a24549f1f23a1a340.png" class="latex" alt="$1000,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="40" height="16" /> which means we can ignore it. We have:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/4/fa45b93f65037df7a2b936937d2685f1eb75c141.png" class="latex" alt="$\begin{aligned}11^{2011} &amp;\equiv 1 + 2011\cdot 10 + \dfrac{2011 \cdot 2010}{2} \cdot 100 \\ &amp;\equiv 1+20110 + \dfrac{11\cdot 10}{2} \cdot 100\\ &amp;= 1 + 20110 + 5500\\ &amp;\equiv 1 + 110 + 500\\&amp;=611 \pmod{1000} \end{aligned}$" style="vertical-align: -72px" width="323" height="155" />
</p><p>Therefore, the hundreds digit is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/0/ef0f8e6e46b73a9ead0a23a82c2571bb39042961.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) } 6}.$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="59" height="28" />
</p><p>Side note: By <a href="/wiki/index.php/Euler%27s_Totient_Theorem" title="Euler&#39;s Totient Theorem">Euler's Totient Theorem</a>, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/5/f158f380ca4864c02498efaef7d33653a040f1d0.png" class="latex" alt="$a^{\phi (1000)} \equiv 1 \pmod{1000}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="186" height="20" /> for any <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> relatively prime with 1000, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/2/5925c91ec6d2a2ff0c3104d22eeba1ba256a6b29.png" class="latex" alt="$a^{400} \equiv 1 \pmod{1000}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="161" height="19" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/5/e65291c6b0edad219ccb356d80d563c4be0e5fd1.png" class="latex" alt="$11^{2011} \equiv 11^{11} \pmod{1000}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="199" height="19" />. We can then proceed using the clever application of the Binomial Theorem.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We need to compute <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/b/d1b5393c8937f4f8f0000e30418260439dc880a5.png" class="latex" alt="$2011^{2011} \pmod{1000}.$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="166" height="19" /> By the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Chinese_Remainder_Theorem" title="Chinese Remainder Theorem">Chinese Remainder Theorem</a>, it suffices to compute <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/7/c/67c13dcb160fa193b7f944aeeb1aba1999e7a155.png" class="latex" alt="$2011^{2011} \pmod{8}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="134" height="19" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/5/945b3febcce042c83e6120ae7d0d4465e1f6b22c.png" class="latex" alt="$2011^{2011} \pmod{125}.$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="157" height="19" />
</p><p>In modulo <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/a/12a9fb4c5b23667e57f28d844ad99715f18ba3fa.png" class="latex" alt="$8,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/7/1c7938120f4825eab9e1352a38f92899b018236c.png" class="latex" alt="$2011^4 \equiv 1 \pmod{8}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="147" height="19" /> by Euler's Theorem, and also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/6/24641d61ffec403a7c74c8ed60d02c221d153032.png" class="latex" alt="$2011 \equiv 3 \pmod{8},$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="146" height="18" /> so we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/0/0005cb6d3a60d8bfe27bc0ad83d3686a04cb0ff2.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[2011^{2011} = (2011^4)^{502} \cdot 2011^3 \equiv 1^{502} \cdot 3^3 \equiv 3 \pmod{8}.\]" width="425" height="19" />
</p><p>In modulo <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/3/ef35fa6169ebdc4a156ffc357f72cd8c624be699.png" class="latex" alt="$125,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="31" height="16" /> we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/7/14716c2fcaca03901d810359a72bb1a6c8f4bb40.png" class="latex" alt="$2011^{100} \equiv 1 \pmod{125}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="179" height="19" /> by Euler's Theorem, and also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/f/46fcac8b5c6427666c305fb2e5cec0732f55c1f7.png" class="latex" alt="$2011 \equiv 11 \pmod{125}.$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="172" height="18" /> Therefore, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/3/be33deefa21fd3ee1a5e1a10c9f2fb26f7374e60.png" class="latex" alt="$\begin{aligned} 2011^{2011} &amp;= (2011^{100})^{20} \cdot 2011^{11} \\ &amp;\equiv 1^{20} \cdot 11^{11} \\ &amp;= 121^5 \cdot 11 \\ &amp;= (-4)^5 \cdot 11 = -1024 \cdot 11 \\ &amp;\equiv -24 \cdot 11 = -264 \\ &amp;\equiv 111 \pmod{125}. \end{aligned}$" style="vertical-align: -71px" width="272" height="152" />
</p><p>After finding the solution <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/4/0f454425bc50b295e01c49738cd720a16b34ae33.png" class="latex" alt="$2011^{2011} \equiv 611 \pmod{1000},$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="218" height="19" /> we conclude it is the only one by the Chinese Remainder Theorem. Thus, the hundreds digit is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/0/ef0f8e6e46b73a9ead0a23a82c2571bb39042961.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) } 6}.$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="59" height="28" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Notice that the hundreds digit of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/c/e0ccc49cc5b5d0d6da4c125df51ec3938c073116.png" class="latex" alt="$2011^{2011}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="61" height="15" /> won't be affected by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/f/05f1ffc8a6829f25b0b0366087a70dfd4e06fef6.png" class="latex" alt="$2000$" width="35" height="12" />. Essentially we could solve the problem by finding the hundreds digit of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/a/7ea561b5fb9fd7b2d1f263e735fca58745aa1b4f.png" class="latex" alt="$11^{2011}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="15" />. Powers of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/8/c6878713578626763c38433b3f4c8c2205ad0c15.png" class="latex" alt="$11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> are special because they can be represented by the Pascal's Triangle. Drawing the triangle, there is a theorem that states the powers of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/8/c6878713578626763c38433b3f4c8c2205ad0c15.png" class="latex" alt="$11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> can be found by reading rows of the triangle and adding extra numbers up. [add source] For example, the sixth row of the triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/8/9c8240ebcdef17a04e38dc12fc852bab8180d257.png" class="latex" alt="$1, 5, 10, 10, 5,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="100" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. Adding all numbers from right to left, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/d/44d460a03c4238b1ec9cda9b74179f7ef3b330cb.png" class="latex" alt="$161051$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="53" height="13" />, which is also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/9/329d81c2731bb10d94022ea29a0a220a18d47d56.png" class="latex" alt="$11^5$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="24" height="15" />. In other words, each number is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/b/02bedd46306390e4c9b9293a00c7de6620b71b9b.png" class="latex" alt="$10^n$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> steps from the right side of the row. The hundreds digit is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />. We can do the same for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/a/7ea561b5fb9fd7b2d1f263e735fca58745aa1b4f.png" class="latex" alt="$11^{2011}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="15" />, but we only need to find the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> digits from the right. Observing, every <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> number from the right is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/b/72b2cabf550cfbfd5d152976d95b7f4b81ccd8e5.png" class="latex" alt="$1 + 2 + 3... + n$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="119" height="14" />. So to find the third number from the right on the row of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/a/7ea561b5fb9fd7b2d1f263e735fca58745aa1b4f.png" class="latex" alt="$11^{2011}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/0/1c090e87561137083ac8bedce48c9e982251de82.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[f(11^n) = 1 + 2 + 3... + (n-1),\]" width="245" height="18" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/0/9/c09d3bd7173402523b2b6d0b8e21d0630132fffa.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{(2010 \cdot 2011)}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="102" height="38" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/3/cb3d29fe8c9ec47286d6235c50357c880fdc2476.png" class="latex" alt="$2021055$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="62" height="13" />. The last digit is five, but we must remember to add the number on the right of it, which, by observing other rows is obviously <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/e/efe8f67ac18da14c8aa767b8131b0650ac9b9720.png" class="latex" alt="$2011$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="13" />. We must carry the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/e/efe8f67ac18da14c8aa767b8131b0650ac9b9720.png" class="latex" alt="$2011$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="13" />'s tens digit to the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/3/cb3d29fe8c9ec47286d6235c50357c880fdc2476.png" class="latex" alt="$2021055$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="62" height="13" />'s unit digit to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/0/2c024459b191f13b58f34b5e366d4e49e32cad7c.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) } 6}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="56" height="28" />. The one at the very end of the row doesn't affect anything, so we can leave it alone.
