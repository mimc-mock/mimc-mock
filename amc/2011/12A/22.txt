<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> be a unit square region and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/8/83870118206bd0e612b5c9e3def2ef95460e368b.png" class="latex" alt="$n \geq 4$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="44" height="14" /> an integer. A point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> in the interior of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> is called <i>n-ray</i> partitional if there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> rays emanating from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> that divide <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> triangles of equal area. How many points are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />-ray partitional but not <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" />-ray partitional?

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>There must be four rays emanating from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> that intersect the four corners of the square region. Depending on the location of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" />, the number of rays distributed among these four triangular sectors will vary. We start by finding the corner-most point that is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />-ray partitional (let this point be the bottom-left-most point). 
</p><p>We first draw the four rays that intersect the vertices. At this point, the triangular sectors with bases as the sides of the square that the point is closest to both do not have rays dividing their areas. Therefore, their heights are equivalent since their areas are equal. The remaining <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/7/367f6b15d47102fd8fdfc1bafed72c1daeadb35a.png" class="latex" alt="$96$" width="17" height="12" /> rays are divided among the other two triangular sectors, each sector with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/f/5afbad0b221f0ef70186fe70b08e6fc1efdd8dfa.png" class="latex" alt="$48$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> rays, thus dividing these two sectors into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/c/34c8e6bf4d57cd6f2bb0d7d43e00f0f442f53b84.png" class="latex" alt="$49$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> triangles of equal areas. 
</p><p>Let the distance from this corner point to the closest side be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and the side of the square be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" />. From this, we get the equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/2/fb252afee39dc1836454f2800ce17f70545a7b05.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a\times s}{2}=\frac{(s-a)\times s}{2}\times\frac1{49}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="200" height="39" />. Solve for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/b/2bb013d2491056718e9a9ec6b29d83bf651b3322.png" class="latex" alt="$a=\frac s{50}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="54" height="33" />. Therefore, point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/a/4/ea45c01fc95d372765b28c0e95e033ba08de1d82.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac1{50}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" /> of the side length away from the two sides it is closest to. By moving <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/a/e5aaca096c16f979d99586457cc2ca15308c2e24.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac s{50}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="33" /> to the right, we also move one ray from the right sector to the left sector, which determines another <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />-ray partitional point. We can continue moving <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> right and up to derive the set of points that are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />-ray partitional. 
</p><p>In the end, we get a square grid of points each <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/a/e5aaca096c16f979d99586457cc2ca15308c2e24.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac s{50}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="33" /> apart from one another. Since this grid ranges from a distance of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/a/e5aaca096c16f979d99586457cc2ca15308c2e24.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac s{50}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="33" /> from one side to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/4/aa452669f1a2459dc90027ede283a0f0ee69e3f3.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{49s}{50}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="29" height="37" /> from the same side, we have a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/f/10f9c158aa1858fe7648de5814145a8574f6c417.png" class="latex" alt="$49\times49$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="58" height="13" /> grid, a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b1370e2a33f573ee3f17696e80c999656e6456ab.png" class="latex" alt="$2401$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="13" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />-ray partitional points. To find the overlap from the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" />-ray partitional, we must find the distance from the corner-most <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" />-ray partitional point to the sides closest to it. Since the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />-ray partitional points form a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/f/10f9c158aa1858fe7648de5814145a8574f6c417.png" class="latex" alt="$49\times49$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="58" height="13" /> grid, each point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/a/e5aaca096c16f979d99586457cc2ca15308c2e24.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac s{50}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="33" /> apart from each other, we can deduce that the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" />-ray partitional points form a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/b/2cb840f4b032d0b53119d2ae6abb9188f3f6958c.png" class="latex" alt="$29\times29$" width="58" height="12" /> grid, each point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/5/b65f312e82674fb692306ee04fe17478027ad251.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac s{30}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="33" /> apart from each other. To find the overlap points, we must find the common divisors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/1/651ba418a810d971dbc8a326d49a71fe1541fae0.png" class="latex" alt="$30$" width="17" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/6/906734e2f1010e9a171ffee8050cbc0649cea37c.png" class="latex" alt="$50$" width="17" height="12" /> which are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/5/7e5086c60da51d0c544f7ec7a3a94f74092b9a93.png" class="latex" alt="$1, 2, 5,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="47" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. Therefore, the overlapping points will form grids with points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/f/08f495532b847164c91660fbf489b8bfd1d178f1.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac s{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="33" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/5/955e914022d5e56a70d7db59089b52393efbf1a3.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac s{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="33" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/7/c67f18cbb4725c6a7d93dd0c7e66a34a0524839e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac s{10}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="33" /> away from each other respectively. Since the grid with points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/7/c67f18cbb4725c6a7d93dd0c7e66a34a0524839e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac s{10}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="33" /> away from each other includes the other points, we can disregard the other grids. The total overlapping set of points is a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/8/8d8e44ac5dbe890daf86ba3414fbfb7ccd80dfa3.png" class="latex" alt="$9\times9$" width="40" height="12" /> grid, which has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/e/87edbdda753b32453027dc7cab023c0743b6b243.png" class="latex" alt="$81$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> points. Subtract <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/e/87edbdda753b32453027dc7cab023c0743b6b243.png" class="latex" alt="$81$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b1370e2a33f573ee3f17696e80c999656e6456ab.png" class="latex" alt="$2401$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="13" /> to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/c/e6c6c8074877fa514ae584cf3358880cf9aea079.png" class="latex" alt="$2401-81=\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ 2320}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="183" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Position the square region <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> so that the bottom-left corner of the square is at the origin. Then define <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" /> to be the sidelength of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> to be the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/b/22b7dbd9bf7331c898fe12f5ef54442e5b1c1892.png" class="latex" alt="$(rs, qs)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="55" height="18" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/3/7a39392c9e9c5b61879523f968490f8afcb0cff8.png" class="latex" alt="$0&lt;r,q&lt;1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="90" height="16" />. 
</p><p><br />
There must be four rays emanating from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> that intersect the four corners of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" />. The areas of the four triangles formed by these rays are then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/0/bd02d7a780388606ed5f291e33094e513ec4db6e.png" class="latex" alt="$A_1=\frac{qs\times s}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="96" height="34" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/1/4a16c5c863d1686f3bd0ef460028506f1e2930b7.png" class="latex" alt="$A_2=\frac{(s-rs)\times s}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="140" height="38" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/1/b210ed0e080286da690e74fd31f94dceda40dabc.png" class="latex" alt="$A_3=\frac{(s-qs)\times s}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="140" height="38" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/f/cafe12b2f057eebbd15b1a6be4b6124ea4fbcad1.png" class="latex" alt="$A_4=\frac{rs\times s}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="95" height="34" />. 
</p><p><br />
If a point is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />-ray partitional, then there exist positive integers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/c/42c06de735d79f0b28c30bcbbcf96e67bb1ca42c.png" class="latex" alt="$a, b, c, d$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="59" height="16" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/d/e1d3162fc1267ac7834c98b7d3d209ebc529e266.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b+c+d=n$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="136" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/6/e36bb44ae43bbada01b495e4693d371600011444.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{A_1}{a}=\frac{A_2}{b}=\frac{A_3}{c}=\frac{A_4}{d}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="171" height="38" />. Substituting in our formulas for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/c/36c7f3f828cf12ab9cafeffad282468a95219600.png" class="latex" alt="$A_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/a/e3a8ae66ded7f33f9385a37c9cadfb82720013d8.png" class="latex" alt="$A_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/1/791fe65c9d1ed7d8ed33a5025f40c5c7f9e476af.png" class="latex" alt="$A_3$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/6/3b6f936abe426272c6ed7300a218f88be89175a4.png" class="latex" alt="$A_4$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="20" height="15" /> and canceling equal terms, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/f/a7f68301e3127bc678df76d3be2f0881417e6fdb.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{q}{a}=\frac{1-r}{b}=\frac{1-q}{c}=\frac{r}{d}.\]" width="189" height="37" /> 
</p><p><br />
Taking <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/e/6ae2ba89a5b30f4ba48a885644d11518fcbfc519.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{q}{a}=\frac{1-q}{c}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="80" height="37" /> and solving for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/1/0615acc3725de21025457e7d6f7694dab8e2f758.png" class="latex" alt="$q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="8" height="11" />, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/b/d5bc876ab895d0e1627d5fbe16c73724b726cb6e.png" class="latex" alt="$q=\frac{a}{a+c}$" style="vertical-align: -14px" width="75" height="34" />, and taking <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/e/2eed039947a43214e0054fc67b707db44a2fc02a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1-r}{b}=\frac{r}{d}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="79" height="37" /> and solving for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/d/60df8d80946a9b369add0df5d292401fe8c62c32.png" class="latex" alt="$r=\frac{d}{b+d}$" style="vertical-align: -14px" width="74" height="39" />. Finally, from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/8/1985816bc5e2a869c3b52b2042dee9ce84dfcdad.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{q}{a}=\frac{r}{d}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="49" height="33" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/d/89dedd43b75c0d03888b55f2873073f500a78342.png" class="latex" alt="$qd=ar$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="60" height="16" />   <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/e/a6ec0db174f3b705df19623976bf4b88ff502f3a.png" class="latex" alt="$\Leftrightarrow$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="10" />   <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/7/e474483e9d8615aeab62b5ebfbf678f734ef295d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{ad}{a+c}=\frac{ad}{b+d}$" style="vertical-align: -14px" width="109" height="39" />   <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/e/a6ec0db174f3b705df19623976bf4b88ff502f3a.png" class="latex" alt="$\Leftrightarrow$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="10" />   <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/4/6140ba2a06761567d23e084839c876b137e00d6d.png" class="latex" alt="$a+c=b+d$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="103" height="14" />. 
</p><p><br />
So for a point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />-ray partitional, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/6/3f65346b796c5dfc1237133d7936982b425e77e6.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b+c+d=100$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="152" height="14" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/e/83ec1c779450190f058977f0bd2a456624cdb371.png" class="latex" alt="$a+c=b+d=50$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="145" height="14" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> must then be of the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/a/45a0f0499981c6355ddaef5299c374eeabd3f554.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{d}{50}s, \frac{a}{50}s\right)$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="97" height="45" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> is in the interior of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> can be any positive integer from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />  to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/c/34c8e6bf4d57cd6f2bb0d7d43e00f0f442f53b84.png" class="latex" alt="$49$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> (with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> just equaling <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/a/3caeed3cebc7c223abef2f19d1c12a2f3d9651bf.png" class="latex" alt="$50-d$" width="50" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/0/52049eab3602a7c792962fe85efc4acbdbc57310.png" class="latex" alt="$50-a$" width="50" height="12" />, respectively). Thus, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/9/399cac7e7dfc2f0d6d9d6be35531161c4ddfbea2.png" class="latex" alt="$49\times 49=2401$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="118" height="13" /> points that are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />-ray partitional.
</p><p><br />
However, the problem asks for points that are not only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />-ray partitional but also not <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" />-ray partitional. Points that are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" />-ray partitional are of the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/c/71ce6d56c1dad20f0c1c53dd3e3481bd6a4d3573.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{m}{30}s, \frac{n}{30}s\right)$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="93" height="35" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> are also positive integers. We count the number of points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/a/45a0f0499981c6355ddaef5299c374eeabd3f554.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{d}{50}s, \frac{a}{50}s\right)$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="97" height="45" /> that can also be written in this form. For a given <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/2/9d2735c871d4ffe6ea6e241103658061282a3de2.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{d}{50}=\frac{m}{30}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="67" height="37" /> if and only if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/3/ac37a3c10263e06b67ea4c5e524aa2f867170ee0.png" class="latex" alt="$m=\frac{3}{5}d$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="63" height="37" />, and likewise with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />. We can then see that a point is both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />-ray partitional and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" />-ray partitional if and only if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> are both divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> integers between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/c/34c8e6bf4d57cd6f2bb0d7d43e00f0f442f53b84.png" class="latex" alt="$49$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> that are divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, so out of our <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b1370e2a33f573ee3f17696e80c999656e6456ab.png" class="latex" alt="$2401$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="13" /> points that are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />-ray partitional, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/d/85d01ce10e8a388f92b81b46970260984adf18a4.png" class="latex" alt="$9\times 9=81$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="81" height="13" /> are also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" />-ray partitional.
</p><p><br />
Our answer then is just <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/c/e6c6c8074877fa514ae584cf3358880cf9aea079.png" class="latex" alt="$2401-81=\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ 2320}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="183" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>For the sake of simplicity, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> be a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/e/caeb6cfe4e8e4cf9314c4f26a061def737ff9b36.png" class="latex" alt="$60 \times 60$" width="58" height="12" /> square and set the bottom-left point as the origin. Then, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> has vertices: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/3/993acd33bfbc3a0a8e736b60861a922ef8e52cf4.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(0,0), (60,0), (60,60), (0,60).\]" width="224" height="18" />
</p><p>Now, let a point in the square have coordinates <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/5/5855dbfeb3da9106ad08951ed047a41d9c278d9f.png" class="latex" alt="$(x, y).$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="45" height="18" />
</p><p>In order for the point to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/5/7c5709cab39ff8bcfc1f78441acb15dc9f65e979.png" class="latex" alt="$100-$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="13" /> ray partitional, we must be able to make <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> triangles with area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/c/fbcb52a3ec6ce049254791426b0d47fb5859adc7.png" class="latex" alt="$60^2/100 = 36.$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="107" height="19" /> For it to not be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" />-ray partitional, we cannot make <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" /> triangles with area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/0/120813d984b664daf519db47c06eb6e4d519e556.png" class="latex" alt="$60^2/60 = 60.$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="98" height="19" />
</p><p>When we draw such a triangle, it's base will always be on one of the sides of the square. If it is on the bottom side, then the height must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />. So, the base of each triangle must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/4/144c9a896bee8f2e809e4ec2e7b7861f2a52062c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{72}{y}.$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="25" height="40" /> So, there will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/1/921819583ea1c0b9e2a525d14273628607d1fc85.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{60}{\frac{72}{y}} = \frac{60y}{72}$" style="vertical-align: -21px" width="76" height="46" /> total triangles on that side.
</p><p>If the triangle is on the right side of the square, then the height has to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/9/089b71b50c845f743dc3d7e4deeada790a47acab.png" class="latex" alt="$60 - x.$" width="54" height="12" /> So, the base will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/3/d432f30d18ab387007cbf38655376f1787bc5cdf.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{72}{60-x}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="53" height="37" /> and there will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/7/3a753581f77dedd06421c4999742ff2aa9d8e6a0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{60 (60-x)}{72}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="85" height="38" /> triangles.
</p><p>If the triangle is on the top, the height will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/d/92d308a9fe2f707eb36b96a9251cbeb057571491.png" class="latex" alt="$60-y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="49" height="16" />, the base will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/9/db904bbdd40c13e629376a783afeb93c3832ab5d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{72}{60-y}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="52" height="40" /> and there will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc62fa877110d46ed2e3e9c97a100959ad1d12df.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{60 (60-y)}{72}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="84" height="38" /> triangles along this side.
</p><p>The triangles on the left will have height <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />, base <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/1/2c196ad4f009e3a7fe10324d8d08e497254a1abd.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{72}{x}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/0/7/c07da258f2af34e21d24c643e55fa4137f171676.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{60x}{72}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="31" height="37" /> such triangles must exist. 
</p><p>Simplifying, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/1/b31cfd7f3c03c74e2933ff763fdbf8ff8d7082ca.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5y}{6}, \frac{5 (60-x)}{6}, \frac{5(60-y)}{6}, \frac{5x}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="223" height="38" /> triangles on the respective sides of the square. 
</p><p>All of these numbers must be integers. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/6/4a63b3b95f1ea0f4e70bc290582181e88c7c32ae.png" class="latex" alt="$x = 60a$" width="62" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/9/f994f23d8576161b54c22dfd387d7215661d061d.png" class="latex" alt="$y = 60b$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="60" height="16" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/1/cd1eb8a5771361e198a3edf298953d31b36bd6de.png" class="latex" alt="$0 &lt; a, b &lt; 1.$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="95" height="16" />
</p><p>Then, the amounts become: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/1/c918bb50a57116b1ba5950e092e18927ebd125b1.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[50a, 50b,  50 - 50a, 50 - 50b.\]" width="215" height="16" />
</p><p>As long as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/8/4b8df722da4d825515ee8141d7a596f0e26a4622.png" class="latex" alt="$50a$" width="27" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/0/bc0e7c46d7bc8f8bbb611f48a8958934b70f757c.png" class="latex" alt="$50b$" width="26" height="12" /> are integers, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/c/cdcb2789cf8e11b908706160796f3810faaa50cb.png" class="latex" alt="$50 - 50a$" width="68" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/9/b394cb9ef16a16942b9b7c77aaa0333e4226fb25.png" class="latex" alt="$50 - 50b$" width="66" height="12" /> will also be integers. 
</p><p>For this to happen, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> must be of the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/3/b63492b8a2d321d43b61ed50f1f4004b9946fee4.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{x}{50}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="33" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/1/f11a36a100f9d11ffebe0f5978949cadae26ed14.png" class="latex" alt="$1 \le x \le 49.$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="89" height="15" /> So, we have a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/c/21c3e3592b6612d7d76150d31d631b422c08314d.png" class="latex" alt="$49^2 = 2401$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="85" height="15" /> points that are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />-ray partitional. 
</p><p>Now, we must calculate the number of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />- ray partitional points that are also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" />-ray partitional. 
</p><p>Using a method similar to before, if a point is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" />-ray partitional, then we must be able to make <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/e/96e49e61b416cbf43327179ce85b53720293ec7f.png" class="latex" alt="$30a, 30b, 30 - 30a, 30 -30b$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="212" height="16" /> triangles on the different sides. 
</p><p>So, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/3/e43866eaec3e9dcd80f20aa2de7aeee4b19b9911.png" class="latex" alt="$30a$" width="27" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/2/54270073a408b7f7e8cb07368e1df809138882fb.png" class="latex" alt="$30b$" width="26" height="12" /> must be integers. This means <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> have to be of the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/c/cbcd81e3baaba33c17221877111f3315bacebb9d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{y}{30}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="33" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/2/0c29e14769c013132d97921fdef8470c05c8b220.png" class="latex" alt="$0&lt;y&lt;30.$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="88" height="16" />
</p><p>If a point is both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />-ray partitional and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" />-ray partitional, then it can be written as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/d/dadc046843af25cdb699fc2e02459abf2e5117f7.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{x}{50} =  \frac{y}{30}\]" width="65" height="32" />. Note that whenever <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> is divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> will always exist to satisfy the above equation.
</p><p>So, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> has to be in the range <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/d/d5d695c0496cbb64ec9b849d8064756368167f2d.png" class="latex" alt="$(0, 50)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="47" height="18" /> and must be divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> possibilities, namely <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/8/788599046a7d6c295f7488badd762b24dafaa6be.png" class="latex" alt="$5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45.$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="222" height="16" /> 
</p><p>The <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-coordinate of the point and the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />-coordinate of the point can each use any of these <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> possibilities, giving <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/1/091a85fba0b5b9aecbf88d6ef3b8012db44f2432.png" class="latex" alt="$9^2 = 81$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="58" height="15" /> numbers that are both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />-ray partitional and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" />-ray partitional.
</p><p>Overall, we are left with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/8/ed8f717fa9e73fb2111244fe1caef26d644ae85e.png" class="latex" alt="$2401 - 81 = \boxed{2320}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="149" height="23" /> solutions.
</p>
