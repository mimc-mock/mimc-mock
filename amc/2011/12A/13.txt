<p>Triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> has side-lengths <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/d/c8d65241c0611ddc2de1b3aef77be2547c842620.png" class="latex" alt="$AB = 12, BC = 24,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="154" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/9/9195b8f3be608ad05e52dca2de7ab73df50198b9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC = 18.$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="73" height="13" /> The line through the incenter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> parallel to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/3/e33fe7d65facd8868f58b6e94ddc7f153a5a3f9f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BC}$" width="29" height="15" /> intersects <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/5/12517bbc31cb9026c71299b7a0d232d07e60f284.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AC}$" width="28" height="15" /> at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/4/0b4a6d41204181432504f1108ded79e4a49e0e03.png" class="latex" alt="$N.$" width="19" height="12" /> What is the perimeter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/a/f7aa3e04934d1b5c353b7e4edf3ef4a3a73de6e6.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AMN?$" width="74" height="13" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/2/4521b95ba2a5e4e9200da81da02d94a086742ae8.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 27 \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 30 \qquad \textbf{(C)}\  33 \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 36 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 42$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="394" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" /> be the incenter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/8/1f828f10cad9235a2c72c34369f5fd4a1179e579.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{ABC}$" width="58" height="13" />. Because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/d/b0d8f83bc78312dfba02faa80ee7558a951b0b9c.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{MO} \parallel \overline{BC}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="82" height="20" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/2/5d29ab3cedcc24c862f97e5d7f2bb1da18b7067e.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BO}$" width="29" height="15" /> is the angle bisector of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/0/0d089768b09c8f192985935b4efd1d34ab6094ca.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle{ABC}$" width="55" height="13" />, we have 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/0/4305bb6699bacdcba757e231d193c6641d876115.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\angle{MBO} = \angle{CBO} = \angle{MOB} = \frac{1}{2}\angle{MBC}\]" width="326" height="37" />
</p><p>It then follows due to alternate interior angles and base angles of isosceles triangles that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/e/55e4e088193854985a30e37cfca676f5a59a3540.png" class="latex" alt="$MO = MB$" width="92" height="12" />. Similarly, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/d/c4d2e382013fb4a007bc555a1e62f7d16c80c3a3.png" class="latex" alt="$NO = NC$" width="86" height="12" />. The perimeter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/2/39261b5a15ee2bfcd406ba74a71cbdb49f982568.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{AMN}$" width="66" height="13" /> then becomes
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/8/b38a9104edaff82037e5d5b8b9d34a0cb120970e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} AM + MN + NA &amp;= AM + MO + NO + NA \\ &amp;= AM + MB + NC + NA \\ &amp;= AB + AC \\ &amp;= 30 \rightarrow \boxed{(B)} \end{align*}" width="363" height="105" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" /> be the incenter. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c53cc93aa43cc55bea0dc2511833fe6bdda020f.png" class="latex" alt="$AO$" width="27" height="13" /> is the angle bisector of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/e/21e5827c549c40537c55853b73f1076a26ba7840.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle MAN$" width="62" height="13" />. Let the angle bisector of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/6/5862947b38a495ec9be80e5b9c7582de6cf0d269.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BAC$" width="55" height="13" /> meets <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" /> at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> and the angle bisector of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/a/a3ad57710b6810476e6017ea2c2cc6d41219c218.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle ABC$" width="55" height="13" /> meets <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/7/a179ff2638e4799cadd820db205c2beff6299ce9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC$" width="27" height="13" /> at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />. By applying both angle bisector theorem and Menelaus' theorem,
</p><p><br />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/7/db70e75e34fe0ea69193c554cb7c55d4df5f1f4f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AO}{OP} \times \frac{BP}{BC} \times \frac{CQ}{QA} = 1$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="175" height="41" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/f/c2fd48de464992c65d35b5d4d46fa31c5b60fbf0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AO}{OP} \times \frac{12}{30} \times \frac{24}{12} = 1$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="153" height="38" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/5/535d269952c7f47771da441f7d0950f52baa51b9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AO}{OP}=\frac{5}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="68" height="38" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/4/d7449425f5adea7bb5f5e5c59bc2b95f7532a8be.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AO}{AP}=\frac{5}{9}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="67" height="38" />
</p><p><br />
Perimeter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/a/24ae2d2fe892420b1221427e8165c3efaf8678d3.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AMN = \frac{12+24+18}{9} \times 5 = 30  \rightarrow \boxed{(B)}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="335" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Like in other solutions, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" /> be the incenter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c53cc93aa43cc55bea0dc2511833fe6bdda020f.png" class="latex" alt="$AO$" width="27" height="13" /> intersect <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" /> at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />. By the angle bisector theorem, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/0/760fa071996a15fd01cc234b03f03555580bdb0f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{BD}{DC} = \frac{AB}{AC} = \frac{12}{18} = \frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="173" height="38" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/d/40d03ae3adfa46ffb1a6a707ccc9d3b0cf67e61e.png" class="latex" alt="$BD+DC = 24$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="124" height="14" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/8/3a86b3505c21929447560d6d72a8bcf1de6f8310.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{BD}{24-BD} = \frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="110" height="38" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/4/d74fa0d3c9ee7dee3b28d30973ea674ffaf4eca7.png" class="latex" alt="$3BD = 48 - 2BD$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="143" height="13" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/f/78f6c0b0f95cd2c87d6d3c10c91b282f461e4c5a.png" class="latex" alt="$BD = \frac{48}{5} = 9.6$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="123" height="37" />. By the angle bisector theorem on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/5/f75d6d58b03031bd7cb52a7ca886a8ddf369d21c.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD$" width="59" height="13" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/6/db69fdc744b6899391040ed738f198d313a589ac.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{DO}{OA} = \frac{BD}{BA} = \frac{4}{5} = 0.8$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="176" height="37" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/0/1509630259e62e90ee9046547f894bb9719585f2.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{DA}{OA} = 1 + \frac{DO}{OA} = \frac{9}{5} = 1.8$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="206" height="38" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/c/37c2a01525edc5a617dc6925ed09ba9af4d2e4f5.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AO}{AD} = \frac{5}{9}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="69" height="38" />. Because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/1/5c1ef5c03ed500ff33e808ec4623df454f3c9ac5.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AMN \sim \triangle ABC$" width="149" height="13" />, the perimeter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/1/a718b55cf3d3cf2aa67b60f9a86c3bc135b584d6.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AMN$" width="66" height="13" /> must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/0/d50fc15baeb8166e3c6db2eb864f266ead803f80.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5}{9} (12 + 18 + 24) = 30$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="168" height="37" />, so our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/1/d712e44b2d06c4f9df372ce70a41b2c69d597ca1.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B)}\ 30}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="64" height="28" />.
</p><p>Another way to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/2/78293a2850c74680519bc2f2038034c665bba669.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AO}{OD}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="32" height="38" /> is to use mass points. Assign a mass of 24 to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, a mass of 18 to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, and a mass of 12 to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />. Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> has mass 30, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/0/b503f50439c79130dcf482f5d5b1b499f98d31cb.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AO}{OD} = \frac{30}{24} = \frac{5}{4} = 1.25$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="173" height="38" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>We know that the ratio of the perimeter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/1/a718b55cf3d3cf2aa67b60f9a86c3bc135b584d6.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AMN$" width="66" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> is the ratio of their heights, and finding the two heights is pretty easy. Note that the height from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/c/10c603896b1ed1fe34b3f76e856a34ff6f6f5154.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{9\sqrt{15}}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="44" height="41" /> from Herons and then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/1/af1d5787835368eb921fc4f52cb8ad97bf3de11a.png" class="latex" alt="$A=\frac{bh}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="58" height="37" /> and also that the height from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/c/bbcfabd65745f2b759dea1840f9a0753b2f992e8.png" class="latex" alt="$MN$" width="36" height="12" /> is simply the height from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" /> minus the inradius. We know the area and the semiperimeter so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/6/a76d98c72220b00b71f463f852b342702acb4c1d.png" class="latex" alt="$r=\frac{A}{s}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="49" height="38" /> which gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/7/6479fd1ed70a79f68e452297855e7bfc24a7a81d.png" class="latex" alt="$r=\sqrt{15}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="66" height="18" />. Now we know that the altitude from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/c/bbcfabd65745f2b759dea1840f9a0753b2f992e8.png" class="latex" alt="$MN$" width="36" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/2/d8218ec24a6d2ec9629654a737f9c665817f53de.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5\sqrt{15}}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="44" height="41" /> so the ratios of the heights from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/1/a718b55cf3d3cf2aa67b60f9a86c3bc135b584d6.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AMN$" width="66" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/c/e7cee4c1bc083c3979f7ac19ac9f8646a3d0a29e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5}{9}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. Thus the perimeter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/1/a718b55cf3d3cf2aa67b60f9a86c3bc135b584d6.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AMN$" width="66" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/3/3c398cd86502ea3796db2c1fe42e3ef43b0cd85c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5}{9} \times 54 = 30$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="95" height="37" /> so our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/e/f2e33ab9a00e021af49e888212645d4667903c49.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{B}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="26" height="24" />
</p><p>-srisainandan6
