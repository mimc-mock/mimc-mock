<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/c/cfc90becf098dcd7b505c70b0717df2a28e5ccdd.png" class="latex" alt="$P(z) = z^8 + \left(4\sqrt{3} + 6\right)z^4 - \left(4\sqrt{3} + 7\right)$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="311" height="32" />. What is the minimum perimeter among all the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" />-sided polygons in the complex plane whose vertices are precisely the zeros of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/0/9e048a9db04ffc89eec3e0d78777919f12df3aeb.png" class="latex" alt="$P(z)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="36" height="18" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/4/b84f4e7b3791d1b75100b2eb550a8bb7305713d1.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 4\sqrt{3} + 4 \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 8\sqrt{2} \qquad \textbf{(C)}\  3\sqrt{2} + 3\sqrt{6} \qquad \textbf{(D)}\  4\sqrt{2} + 4\sqrt{3} \qquad \textbf{(E)}\  4\sqrt{3} + 6$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="643" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Answer: (B)
</p><p>First of all, we need to find all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" />  such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/d/71dcb6eb815a761a204742591ffe23f28edf4877.png" class="latex" alt="$P(z) = 0$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="70" height="18" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/8/8781ed134ad638ea69e4c5e14f11d4c322a871f1.png" class="latex" alt="$P(z) = \left(z^4 - 1\right)\left(z^4 + \left(4\sqrt{3} + 7\right)\right)$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="272" height="32" />
</p><p>So <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/4/844533a1f0c664955217520b1f02c0e9d1b7803b.png" class="latex" alt="$z^4 = 1 \implies z =  e^{i\frac{n\pi}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="163" height="16" />
</p><p>or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/2/2d20e8f09874786c2d9b4909a698ce6a447f7275.png" class="latex" alt="$z^4 = - \left(4\sqrt{3} + 7\right)$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="141" height="32" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/7/29758780c55b6e9161bd614a00882257a50805f8.png" class="latex" alt="$z^2 = \pm i \sqrt{4\sqrt{3} + 7} = e^{i\frac{(2n+1)\pi}{2}} \left(\sqrt{3} + 2\right)$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="306" height="37" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/7/f877bb702c60d75538232145751bc3b20cd422ed.png" class="latex" alt="$z = e^{i\frac{(2n+1)\pi}{4}} \sqrt{\sqrt{3} + 2} = e^{i\frac{(2n+1)\pi}{4}} \left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{2}} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="359" height="53" />
</p><p><br />
</p><p>Now we have a solution at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/8/058e4191a627fa4f4397c9aa94dccad0c76dca84.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{n\pi}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="24" height="33" /> if we look at them in polar coordinate, further more, the 8-gon is symmetric (it is an equilateral octagon) . So we only need to find the side length of one and multiply by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" />.
</p><p>So answer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/8/cb8a2403c2901926422c536ac1fc058de52c5cd9.png" class="latex" alt="$= 8 \times$" width="39" height="12" /> distance from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/0/a709ea71c76fba586043f06e06c8b1aec0448088.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\right)\left(1 + i\right)$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="145" height="53" />
</p><p>Side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/c/89cb171a4d859bee111845bfc58e07fee34112af.png" class="latex" alt="$= \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} - \frac{1}{2}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\right)^2} = \sqrt{2\left(\frac{3}{4} + \frac{1}{4}\right)} = \sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -24px" width="436" height="64" />
</p><p>Hence, answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ada42b1f945fea827946e80d64ab5f27e7ba8c.png" class="latex" alt="$8\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Use the law of cosines.
We make <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> the distance.
Now, since the angle does not change the distance from the origin, we can just use the distance. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/4/e54643af2eea01a7ec6c7cc2d3e70bd135a3f2ae.png" class="latex" alt="$a^2 = (\frac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{2}} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}})^2 + 1^2 -2 \times \Big( \frac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{2}} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \Big)\times 1 \times \cos \frac{\pi}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="428" height="45" />, which simplifies to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/9/ef97fb70f4484d4df9e03ede91c26ae4f6100d0d.png" class="latex" alt="$a^2= 2 + \sqrt3 +1 - 1 - \sqrt3$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="206" height="18" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/b/35bea2ea08cd6bb94fcc17281ccd11aa5969282f.png" class="latex" alt="$a^2=2$" width="50" height="15" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/e/2be98244455ccefb960e5c13f409cca2cd5ecb64.png" class="latex" alt="$a=\sqrt2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="58" height="18" />.
Multiply the answer by 8 to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/1/f8160ee7ec6931a8af19e10d73ec41fc008ac64f.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{ (B) 8\sqrt2}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="74" height="33" />
</p>
