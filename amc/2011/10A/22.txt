<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>This problem is a direct application of the chromatic polynomial of a graph, represented by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/2/c82214fe68d50dd9cc4b4bb080cc1abe8651c6ee.png" class="latex" alt="$P_k(G)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="47" height="18" />, which returns a polynomial representing the number of ways to color a graph <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e28ce12d49d39f160d5a0ef54077fc98e4b9d2b.png" class="latex" alt="$G$" width="14" height="12" /> with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> colors such that no two adjacent vertices share the same color. In other words, it gives us a polynomial in terms of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> where we can plug in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/9/a295acb9662ee1bae4bb3aa92a1f42b2cfd83f10.png" class="latex" alt="$k=6$" width="43" height="12" /> to get our answer.   
</p><p>Well, if we want to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/e/76ebaba4ae3d5c911d6e31ad855d502ad88d48a6.png" class="latex" alt="$P_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> for a graph <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e28ce12d49d39f160d5a0ef54077fc98e4b9d2b.png" class="latex" alt="$G$" width="14" height="12" />, we should probably first draw the graph, right? We can first draw five vertices (without any edges) in the shape of a pentagon. After connecting the diagonals, we get a star. We attempt to shift the vertices around to simplify the graph, which we quickly realize is isomorphic (can be turned into) the five-cycle, or the pentagon.   
</p><p>Finding <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/e/76ebaba4ae3d5c911d6e31ad855d502ad88d48a6.png" class="latex" alt="$P_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> on a graph with a cycle of length at most 3 is straightforward -- we first pick a vertex v. It has 0 colored (visited) neighbors, so we can color it in k ways. We then move on to the vertices adjacent to v, etc, and at the end we multiply all these together. For example, the chromatic polynomial of a triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/0/050023e9db7d3263d14538fa776d6afe9ba8d498.png" class="latex" alt="$k(k-1)(k-2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="119" height="18" />. However, cycles with length &gt; 3 introduce ambiguity, and thus the above technique fails. Thus, we need to use the recursive formula <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/e/49e1bb4dd79a73653bbcf34f74509af1a759b7b7.png" class="latex" alt="$P_k(G) = P_k(G-e)-P_k(G*e)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="246" height="18" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/7/d171bc1a40cd874906a8922448ca8b8d63e19881.png" class="latex" alt="$G-e$" width="44" height="12" /> represents removing e and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/9/c2954b353714f9857809f8fbd737dedc0bb9e42c.png" class="latex" alt="$G*e$" width="39" height="12" /> represents contracting e, or collapsing the two endpoints of e into one. When we hit a graph where the longest cycle has length 3, we can use the first technique to quickly find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/e/76ebaba4ae3d5c911d6e31ad855d502ad88d48a6.png" class="latex" alt="$P_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" />. 
</p><p>After about four iterations of the algorithm, we get that the chromatic polynomial is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/4/d44faa70357a57c7fc500fe8e94279f5b7471254.png" class="latex" alt="$k(k-1)^4 - k(k-1)^3 + k(k-1)(k-2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="310" height="19" />. Plugging in 6 gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2552b97c927fc79e7b250e7646140a34efd8a6d3.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{3120 \ \mathbf{(C)}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="83" height="29" />. 
</p><p>~Helloworld1
