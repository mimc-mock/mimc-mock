<p>Two distinct regular tetrahedra have all their vertices among the vertices of the same unit cube.  What is the volume of the region formed by the intersection of the tetrahedra?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/7/dd7380e72ae60c9d9e79f4207019cc3d908b9895.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{(A)}\,\frac{1}{12} \qquad\text{(B)}\,\frac{\sqrt2}{12} \qquad\text{(C)}\,\frac{\sqrt3}{12} \qquad\text{(D)}\,\frac{1}{6} \qquad\text{(E)}\,\frac{\sqrt2}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="391" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/index.php/File:CubeAndStel.gif" class="image"><img alt="CubeAndStel.gif" src="https://wiki-images.artofproblemsolving.com//d/d3/CubeAndStel.gif" width="257" height="215" /></a>
</p><p>A regular unit tetrahedron can be split into eight tetrahedra that have lengths of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. 
The volume of a regular tetrahedron can be found using base area and height:
</p><p>For a tetrahedron of side length 1, its base area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/b/b9b30d7166f38d8f18e66dfd6e9473880efaca2e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="26" height="41" />, and its height can be found using Pythagoras' Theorem.  Its height is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/5/60511ca4b8d59cbf37984ed54d4a6d6980de97c3.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{1^2-\left(\frac{\sqrt3}{3}\right)^2}=\frac{\sqrt2}{\sqrt3}$" style="vertical-align: -24px" width="172" height="64" />.  Its volume is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/8/3a862a33aa1e2895b34c66b3ec12077936cd5add.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac13\times\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}\times\frac{\sqrt2}{\sqrt3}=\frac{\sqrt{2}}{12}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="165" height="45" />.
</p><p>The tetrahedron actually has side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/4/114e91200cd4c857d233c7fed6cbe7451f3a82fc.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" />, so the actual volume is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/a/7dab9d75db2a1381c333b99d961487d3786b12fb.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{2}}{12}\times\sqrt2^3=\frac13$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="118" height="41" />.
</p><p>On the eight small tetrahedra, the four tetrahedra on the corners of the large tetrahedra are not inside the other large tetrahedra.  Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/1/df17c90f6d0769161236b42a9ccb3198b407f161.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{4}{8}=\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="49" height="37" /> of the large tetrahedra will not be inside the other large tetrahedra.
</p><p>The intersection of the two tetrahedra is thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/7/4971110fa7e2eaaad0a7d99b9a59fc9321bcd0f0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac12\times\frac13=\frac{1}{6}=\boxed{\text{(D)}}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="147" height="37" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>The intersection of the two tetrahedra is an octahedron that has points touching the center of each face of the original cube. We can split the octahedron into two square pyramids, and from there we can find the volume of each pyramid. The sides of the square face of the pyramid will have lengths of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/6/1e61d03caa371f28c51f555bf685f1296ff8de8f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt2}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" />, so the volume of both pyramids (the whole octahedra) will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/b/5bbf764b52a0e6b103b40604e4696c6e51d41a31.png" class="latex" alt="$\left (\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\right )^2\times2\times\frac{1}{2}\times\frac{1}{3}=1/6=\boxed{\text{(D)}}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="278" height="56" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Notice that the intersection of the tetrahedra is one of the larger tetrahedra with the four smaller tetrahedra on its points cut off. Using the tetrahedron volume formula (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/f/47fd422e9ffcc1c0f68830404970a912a86bc71f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\text{x}^3}{6\sqrt2}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="35" height="43" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/8/168a42bb7f6e957bce663e82565257236f5ee5c0.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{x}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="8" /> is the length of a side), and using <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/4/114e91200cd4c857d233c7fed6cbe7451f3a82fc.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/6/1e61d03caa371f28c51f555bf685f1296ff8de8f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt2}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" /> as the side lengths, you get the volume of the intersection as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/3/533cc4790c8bd72b69b50ecdbb6431f36edb6632.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}-4\times\frac{1}{24}=\frac{1}{3}-\frac{1}{6}=\frac{1}{6}=\boxed{\text{(D)}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="260" height="37" />.
</p>
