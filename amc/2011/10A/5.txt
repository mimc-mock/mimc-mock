<p>At an elementary school, the students in third grade, fourth grade, and fifth grade run an average of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/9/f1965fae079a9ba2c0726c307070c2355dfcb213.png" class="latex" alt="$15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> minutes per day, respectively. There are twice as many third graders as fourth graders, and twice as many fourth graders as fifth graders. What is the average number of minutes run per day by these students?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/2/aa2d61042fdf08c0c6f84b28c7f345ac1b8cb4e8.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 12 \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ \frac{37}{3} \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ \frac{88}{7} \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 13 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 14$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="402" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Let us say that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/2/bb2c93730dbb48558bb3c4738c956c4e8f816437.png" class="latex" alt="$f$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="16" /> fifth graders. According to the given information, there must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/8/1283a855ba3a92fc352788ebd05646281230b745.png" class="latex" alt="$2f$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="19" height="16" /> fourth graders and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/f/35f6c7d9a6b65b70ee2d5d9fd0cc7a87f069359c.png" class="latex" alt="$4f$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="19" height="16" /> third graders. The average time run by each student is equal to the total amount of time run divided by the number of students. This gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/d/33d962bd4f95e4fb479c829d60be5e1f460b817c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{12\cdot 4f + 15\cdot 2f + 10\cdot f}{4f + 2f + f} = \frac{88f}{7f} = \frac{88}{7} \Rightarrow \boxed{C}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="351" height="41" />
</p><p><br />
</p><p>If you want to simplify the problem even more, just imagine/assume that only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> fifth grader existed. Then you can simply get rid of the variables.
</p>
