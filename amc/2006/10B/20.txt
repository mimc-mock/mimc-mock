<p>In rectangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/4/2b4551daab5e804bae3792dfe8a4e0bfd005cedf.png" class="latex" alt="$A=(6,-22)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="99" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/0/690336fe3d4bdc2cb18280ab846eb46c86c998d5.png" class="latex" alt="$B=(2006,178)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="123" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/f/affba931cf86ceea5c295017bb41871d0fb9b5e7.png" class="latex" alt="$D=(8,y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="79" height="18" />, for some integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />. What is the area of rectangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/9/c998e173b466b3623ae8cf05d51e695dd6bde265.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A) \ } 4000\qquad \mathrm{(B) \ } 4040\qquad \mathrm{(C) \ } 4400\qquad \mathrm{(D) \ } 40,000\qquad \mathrm{(E) \ } 40,400$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="512" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>Let the slope of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/d/25d659c8074c617549d61db9db0773e0fccad7b3.png" class="latex" alt="$m_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="21" height="10" /> and the slope of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc56862b1fdb71204894da278e42ef75de73a40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AD$" width="28" height="13" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/5/ee54e8432d4a60984309e86f32931b04c8e2f1da.png" class="latex" alt="$m_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="22" height="10" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/3/203815a361c27012193509278e3ce048ae6bdb1e.png" class="latex" alt="$m_1 = \frac{178-(-22)}{2006-6} = \frac{1}{10}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="192" height="39" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/3/fe35726ddde66934edaf6acad8e91451fc243669.png" class="latex" alt="$m_2 = \frac{y-(-22)}{8-6} = \frac{y+22}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="206" height="38" />
</p><p>Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc56862b1fdb71204894da278e42ef75de73a40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AD$" width="28" height="13" /> form a right angle: 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/2/6027fa608d6c4593280d6f5774fe9429dc841df0.png" class="latex" alt="$m_2 = -\frac{1}{m_1}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="87" height="39" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/c/06c5dc52f422632e8481d1c9fef008f111d394f3.png" class="latex" alt="$m_2 = -10$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="79" height="15" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/3/72301229ec6fc74ddabcb5a553accb5e774dc1c4.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{y+22}{2} = -10$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="109" height="37" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/1/9c194cd2c3a0fc30b97d46dc1ccecfe9f86d8623.png" class="latex" alt="$y = -42$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="65" height="15" />
</p><p>Using the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Distance_formula" title="Distance formula">distance formula</a>:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/4/0e43883c3f287eb680358e71bd2397174c5e956b.png" class="latex" alt="$AB = \sqrt{ (2006-6)^2 + (178-(-22))^2 } = \sqrt{ (2000)^2 + (200)^2 } = 200\sqrt{101}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="564" height="32" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/b/4/7b4800f568e870303bc5508587027de4821bc70e.png" class="latex" alt="$AD = \sqrt{ (8-6)^2 + (-42-(-22))^2 } = \sqrt{ (2)^2 + (-20)^2 } = 2\sqrt{101}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="503" height="32" />
</p><p>Therefore the area of rectangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/a/d4aa94d51f4aa2b53fd416b5aad5232280fd444c.png" class="latex" alt="$200\sqrt{101}\cdot2\sqrt{101} = 40,400 \Rightarrow E$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="254" height="20" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>This solution is the same as Solution 1 up to the point where we find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/e/d4eaaa38d59229958dec15b0edd9ae0c9eeb3025.png" class="latex" alt="$y=-42$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="65" height="15" />.
</p><p>We build right triangles so we can use the Pythagorean Theorem.  The triangle with hypotenuse <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> has legs <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/3/8f37626d49b4082c9102bf930bca51277ff2eb43.png" class="latex" alt="$200$" width="26" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/f/05f1ffc8a6829f25b0b0366087a70dfd4e06fef6.png" class="latex" alt="$2000$" width="35" height="12" />, while the triangle with hypotenuse <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc56862b1fdb71204894da278e42ef75de73a40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AD$" width="28" height="13" /> has legs <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/3/f8366cd6196dd8a9da6d38a3e9eafb109e99d53e.png" class="latex" alt="$20$" width="17" height="12" />.  Aha!  The two triangles are similar by SAS, with one triangle having side lengths <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> times the other!
</p><p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/9/849d923170c6cfab5b8b2d95ff5a7a1ede5b04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$AD=x$" width="63" height="13" />.  Then from our reasoning above, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/5/3a565d591982973504f91b58e3a24f38d4da38b1.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=100x$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="89" height="13" />.  Finally, the area of the rectangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/3/fe3a3a703d1d447af67d2f8fcfba3d5bbc9553e9.png" class="latex" alt="$100x(x)=100x^2=100(20^2+2^2)=100(400+4)=100(404)=\boxed{40400 \text{  (E)}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="590" height="28" />.
</p>
