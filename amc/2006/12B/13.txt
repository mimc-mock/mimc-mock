<p>Rhombus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> is similar to rhombus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/2/ac20853d56aed0edba1c70db16d44d3ca40402cd.png" class="latex" alt="$BFDE$" width="58" height="12" />.  The area of rhombus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> is 24, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/a/f7ad08337ee8955c04fd241de1518797a535258a.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BAD = 60^\circ$" width="105" height="13" />.  What is the area of rhombus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/2/ac20853d56aed0edba1c70db16d44d3ca40402cd.png" class="latex" alt="$BFDE$" width="58" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/e/43e4a11b576cfc60bb546ede67ebb9668484c340.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] defaultpen(linewidth(0.7)+fontsize(11)); pair A=origin, B=(2,0), C=(3, sqrt(3)), D=(1, sqrt(3)), E=(1, 1/sqrt(3)), F=(2, 2/sqrt(3)); pair point=(3/2, sqrt(3)/2); draw(B--C--D--A--B--F--D--E--B); label(&quot;$A$&quot;, A, dir(point--A)); label(&quot;$B$&quot;, B, dir(point--B)); label(&quot;$C$&quot;, C, dir(point--C)); label(&quot;$D$&quot;, D, dir(point--D)); label(&quot;$E$&quot;, E, dir(point--E)); label(&quot;$F$&quot;, F, dir(point--F)); [/asy]" width="668" height="402" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/9/4f9be258ec162f26eca09ca564a3b465aec8fbba.png" class="latex" alt="$\textrm{(A) } 6 \qquad \textrm{(B) } 4\sqrt {3} \qquad \textrm{(C) } 8 \qquad \textrm{(D) } 9 \qquad \textrm{(E) } 6\sqrt {3}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="379" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The ratio of any length on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> to a corresponding length on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/8/f08a1d43861a8d532b77db795d3244ccc5a6f6c0.png" class="latex" alt="$BFDE^2$" width="65" height="15" /> is equal to the ratio of their areas. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/c/fbc01c434328541e384c1c71c4e5ac63f1f43240.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BAD=60$" width="98" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/8/e08af8f3acc34996e3cba566dc970b7ed496ba4b.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ADB$" width="59" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/e/fee181f0e279c48906d10847f1986f902dc84efe.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DBC$" width="60" height="13" /> are equilateral. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/4/204a6f5a6757f5da4c1b3035ad8d806769744e63.png" class="latex" alt="$DB$" width="29" height="12" />, which is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" />, is the diagonal of rhombus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" />. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/e/bee5579525ec6bf6ba8f1daf8abf1fc9b1d7c7ac.png" class="latex" alt="$AC=\frac{DB(2)}{2\sqrt{3}}=\frac{DB}{\sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="166" height="43" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/4/204a6f5a6757f5da4c1b3035ad8d806769744e63.png" class="latex" alt="$DB$" width="29" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/7/a179ff2638e4799cadd820db205c2beff6299ce9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC$" width="27" height="13" /> are the longer diagonal of rhombuses <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/8/138dc12aed19ca7be5f5d3fe985631e408aefe7f.png" class="latex" alt="$BEDF$" width="58" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" />, respectively. So the ratio of their areas is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/4/b74019fcde4079f4588a9d2e9885dfa6e580458d.png" class="latex" alt="$(\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}})^2$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="48" height="41" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/1/4e1965f8d70832ded66434c75a7bd52b75090af0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. One-third the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" />. So the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/b/82b317654f901a9c8f56585721a617396d3b050c.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\text{C}}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="23" height="23" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>Draw the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/7/907e515c43197f174b604b7332f14b3f231d7ce1.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{DB}$" width="30" height="15" /> to form an equilateral triangle, since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/c/fbc01c434328541e384c1c71c4e5ac63f1f43240.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BAD=60$" width="98" height="13" />, and line segments 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/b/cfbb4c52e011ceed6e813118a7d59e931af444a2.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AD}$" width="29" height="15" /> are equal in length. To find the area of the smaller rhombus, we only need to find the value of any arbitrary base, then square the result. To find the value of the base, use the line we just drew and connect it to point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> at a right angle along line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/7/907e515c43197f174b604b7332f14b3f231d7ce1.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{DB}$" width="30" height="15" />. Call the connected point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />, with triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/0/8b0e475dc462398a8d537e8640542c80d22a8e98.png" class="latex" alt="$DPE$" width="44" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/8/9b8eb88b417a55dbc3c9a51ca6dfe9179f9966ae.png" class="latex" alt="$EPB$" width="42" height="12" /> being 30-60-90 triangles, meaning we can find the length of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/7/5f74106a4c831d948ec641e678edc348857426fc.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{ED}$" width="30" height="15" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/b/4ebfeb04023363364c27abf7ad4b73aee668fcfe.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{EB}$" width="29" height="15" />. The base of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/8/ed8dcdaee5c02f5afb2e8774644fc8fbc0b614f3.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{24}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />, and half of that length must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/f/0af834b481b16223a0f04fa9a05f4f8a581e0182.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{6}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" />(triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/0/8b0e475dc462398a8d537e8640542c80d22a8e98.png" class="latex" alt="$DPE$" width="44" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/8/9b8eb88b417a55dbc3c9a51ca6dfe9179f9966ae.png" class="latex" alt="$EPB$" width="42" height="12" /> are congruent by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/e/22e28d9bde02e365b67760c06b05e9003ca002c0.png" class="latex" alt="$SSS$" width="36" height="12" />). Solving for the third length yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/7/ca7b4acb8eeb90e37fa1434cb61472589e78a050.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{8}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" />, which we square to get the answer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/b/82b317654f901a9c8f56585721a617396d3b050c.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\text{C}}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="23" height="23" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h3>
<p>Draw line DB, cutting rhombus BFDE into two triangles which fit nicely into 2/3 of equilateral triangle ABD. Thus the area of BFDE is (2/3)*12=8.
</p>
