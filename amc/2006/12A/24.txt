<p>The expression 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/7/967fd0b1012b67df0bed030293f8a3ce757aaf56.png" class="latex" alt="$(x+y+z)^{2006}+(x-y-z)^{2006}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="250" height="19" />
</p><p>is simplified by expanding it and combining like terms. How many terms are in the simplified expression?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/1/c313505d434cd599eb56ffcd0db13e0d89f8556e.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A) \ } 6018\qquad \mathrm{(B) \ } 671,676\qquad \mathrm{(C) \ } 1,007,514\qquad \mathrm{(D) \ } 1,008,016\qquad\mathrm{(E) \ }  2,015,028$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="634" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>By the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Multinomial_Theorem" title="Multinomial Theorem">Multinomial Theorem</a>, the summands can be written as
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/b/7/7b78526602fd2bf277218a45fd8d1fdcc3efcdf4.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum_{a+b+c=2006}{\frac{2006!}{a!b!c!}x^ay^bz^c}\]" width="173" height="47" />
</p><p>and
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/5/1353e7455d59a32ac3de47ace211b252a9bd0cf6.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum_{a+b+c=2006}{\frac{2006!}{a!b!c!}x^a(-y)^b(-z)^c},\]" width="235" height="47" />
</p><p>respectively. Since the coefficients of like terms are the same in each expression, each like term either cancel one another out or the coefficient doubles. In each expansion there are:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/3/8d3ff6f9a5045933c1619fcab5e8bd4a48ee0457.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[{2006+2\choose 2} = 2015028\]" width="178" height="53" />
</p><p>terms without cancellation. For any term in the second expansion to be negative, the parity of the exponents of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> must be opposite. Now we find a pattern:
</p><p>if the exponent of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, the exponent of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> can be all even integers up to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/a/90ae760d1ea10eb7a90d22087b3c7e2393d02117.png" class="latex" alt="$2004$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="36" height="13" />, so there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/c/91c1231e0a53f15ef2e95c0afa2a2738e8f9a1ce.png" class="latex" alt="$1003$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="13" /> terms.
</p><p>if the exponent of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />, the exponent of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> can go up to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/e/96e470b7ce7a2f4d0c1ae064d2874ca377693897.png" class="latex" alt="$2002$" width="35" height="12" />, so there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/a/63a8720f439e8e8887a0746430412c323e81e13c.png" class="latex" alt="$1002$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="13" /> terms.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/c/69cdf7ec84ff918cbe7f21acf7543bf1bd243080.png" class="latex" alt="$\vdots$" width="3" height="16" />
</p><p>if the exponent of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/5/9f5b442c275b4324f100c365b774e872b6b21f1e.png" class="latex" alt="$2005$" width="35" height="12" />, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> can only be 0, so there is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> term.
</p><p>If we add them up, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/5/76597ea5bcd73e8e5a98311cb10311cba6ba1ad4.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1003\cdot1004}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="88" height="37" /> terms. However, we can switch the exponents of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> and these terms will still have a negative sign. So there are a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/4/c741cfeee5f1890b749bf1914a988a2d09fd1559.png" class="latex" alt="$1003\cdot1004$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="85" height="13" /> negative terms.
</p><p>By subtracting this number from 2015028, we obtain <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/0/9304770108cd71ca5c56514980daf0430e861031.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\mathrm{D}}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="25" height="24" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/e/66edfbf0a3ae65dfa4af8a9cc3c5c953507b337c.png" class="latex" alt="$1,008,016$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="79" height="16" /> as our answer.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Alternatively, we can use a <a href="/wiki/index.php/Generating_function" title="Generating function">generating function</a> to solve this problem. 
The goal is to find the generating function for the number of unique terms in the simplified expression (in terms of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />). In other words, we want to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c96dd6ec1dc4ad7520fbdc78fcdbec9edd068d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" /> where the coefficient of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/9/1e973203144975e93c31bdba139c105891889743.png" class="latex" alt="$x^k$" width="17" height="15" /> equals the number of unique terms in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/f/00f00acedcd3bbb02cf36fd2b46417908069d5ad.png" class="latex" alt="$(x+y+z)^k + (x-y-z)^k$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="213" height="20" />.
</p><p><br />
First, we note that all unique terms in the expression have the form, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/b/fcbd8eacfd5988c84397e9d7c782748dd9c3b7e1.png" class="latex" alt="$Cx^ay^bz^c$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="64" height="19" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/0/9b042058072cbe1f939573045d384b477c398bd1.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b+c=k$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="103" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> is some constant. Therefore, the generating function for the MAXIMUM number of unique terms possible in the simplified expression of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/f/00f00acedcd3bbb02cf36fd2b46417908069d5ad.png" class="latex" alt="$(x+y+z)^k + (x-y-z)^k$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="213" height="20" /> is
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/c/84c7d0006cfd2e05e461469355b98a403024b42c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(1+x+x^2+x^3\cdots)^3 = \frac{1}{(1-x)^3}\]" width="259" height="41" />
</p><p><br />
Secondly, we note that a certain number of terms of the form, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/b/fcbd8eacfd5988c84397e9d7c782748dd9c3b7e1.png" class="latex" alt="$Cx^ay^bz^c$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="64" height="19" />, do not appear in the simplified version of our expression because those terms cancel. Specifically, we observe that terms cancel when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/1/331a89bcb582e799d1315974cbe31c78adf9a9ba.png" class="latex" alt="$1 \equiv b+c\text{ (mod }2\text{)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="134" height="17" /> because every unique term is of the form:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/9/439e76ff20585a54ea8ac17bcc0f8263e4c81b56.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\binom{k}{a,b,c}x^ay^bz^c+(-1)^{b+c}\binom{k}{a,b,c}x^ay^bz^c\]" width="318" height="53" />
for all possible <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/b/a5b29b358e825defa3e11b7d903d43ec31e5909a.png" class="latex" alt="$a,b,c$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="41" height="16" />.
</p><p><br />
Since the generating function for the maximum number of unique terms is already known, it is logical that we want to find the generating function for the number of terms that cancel, also in terms of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />. With some thought, we see that this desired generating function is the following:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/6/ee6d5ceef465fdd6d7a1cb47f3c8363ae8b28f26.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[2(x+x^3+x^5\cdots)(1+x^2+x^4\cdots)(1+x+x^2+x^3\cdots) = \frac{2x}{(1-x)^3(1+x)^2}\]" width="531" height="41" />
</p><p><br />
Now, we want to subtract the latter from the former in order to get the generating function for the number of unique terms in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/f/00f00acedcd3bbb02cf36fd2b46417908069d5ad.png" class="latex" alt="$(x+y+z)^k + (x-y-z)^k$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="213" height="20" />, our initial goal:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/b/91b72d34758e7ed145a1c511bd2723978d2439a4.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{(1-x)^3}-\frac{2x}{(1-x)^3(1+x)^2} = \frac{x^2+1}{(1-x)^3(1+x)^2}\]" width="371" height="43" />
which equals
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/f/30f0bb48c16a88abe79887795d4c9a4cb7a2724c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(x^2+1)(1+x+x^2\cdots)^3(1-x+x^2-x^3\cdots)^2\]" width="361" height="19" />
</p><p><br />
The coefficient of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/a/a9a28c22d4a2bff38b552eeb06956089a0ab161a.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{2006}$" width="36" height="15" /> of the above expression equals
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/6/656d32098986fa900dc1f04828eb37888180a9bc.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum_{a=0}^{2006}\binom{2+a}{2}\binom{1+2006-a}{1}(-1)^a + \sum_{a=0}^{2004}\binom{2+a}{2}\binom{1+2004-a}{1}(-1)^a\]" width="555" height="53" />
</p><p><br />
Evaluating the expression, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/e/e2e2242eb748ddedf085140847e56c50936bc08a.png" class="latex" alt="$1008016$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="62" height="13" />, as expected.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Define <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/c/a6c3475766146c4c6afdfd16314cb46165519c2d.png" class="latex" alt="$P=y+z$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="78" height="16" />. Then the expression in the problem becomes:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/7/b87c748fa66e357cd2183bcaf011cad625f28fc3.png" class="latex" alt="$(x+P)^{2006}+(x-P)^{2006}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="197" height="19" />. 
</p><p>Expanding this using binomial theorem gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/2/a72dddf54e6c24c33f98a6a22135e291dc588fd7.png" class="latex" alt="$(x^n+Px^{n-1}+...+P^{n-1}x+P^n)+(x^n-Px^{n-1}+...-P^{n-1}x+P^n)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="545" height="19" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/c/a3c3f002f2d9116dc307409447177d0081edbcc7.png" class="latex" alt="$n=2006$" width="70" height="12" /> (we may omit the coefficients, as we are seeking for the number of terms, not the terms themselves). 
</p><p>Simplifying gives: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/0/830cc31427ba2a1a109f0c34ee76b4251955b27f.png" class="latex" alt="$2(x^n+x^{n-2}P^2+...+x^2P^{n-2}+P^n)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="284" height="19" />. Note that two terms that come out of different powers of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> cannot combine and simplify, as their exponent of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> will differ. Therefore, we simply add the number of terms produced from each addend. By the Binomial Theorem, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/9/3c95adb9981090d5cbb924dd376cc5432bb1c2e3.png" class="latex" alt="$P^k=(y+z)^k$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="108" height="20" /> will have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/5/f05f085f774fc8ab50676b778b86a1f1d1114fc8.png" class="latex" alt="$k+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="40" height="14" /> terms, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/6/206307c4db645758e6f96bee1e80e7720f6794a4.png" class="latex" alt="$1+3+5+...+2007=1004^2=1,008,016 \implies \boxed{D}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="416" height="24" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>Using stars and bars we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/5/885f148e92c5c714924fc2b8e80c1c88cee12bca.png" class="latex" alt="$(x+y+z)^{2006}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="113" height="19" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/f/c8f259bd43561904c57c32446445df0bef90d381.png" class="latex" alt="${2006+2\choose 2}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="91" height="53" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/9/aa955e4cd41f7611b0a9dabe07f035f3102faaf5.png" class="latex" alt="$2015028$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="62" height="13" /> different terms. Now we need to find out how many of these terms are canceled out by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/7/68726252923378ec4f565a169baed2780bfae8b5.png" class="latex" alt="$(x-y-z)^{2006}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="113" height="19" />. We know that for any term(let's say <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/4/4948e5dde4db14fe9176432066a0e8085b800711.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{a}(-y)^{b}(-z)^{c}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="106" height="20" />) where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/d/22d61749698f06d583904f9b6592020c685d91b7.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b+c=2006$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="129" height="14" /> of the expansion of  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/7/68726252923378ec4f565a169baed2780bfae8b5.png" class="latex" alt="$(x-y-z)^{2006}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="113" height="19" /> is only going to cancel out with the corresponding term <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/3/cd37e0ef92a9b51870667d66cc25445fc059821a.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{a}y^{b}z^{c}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="50" height="19" /> if only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> is odd and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> is even or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> is even and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> is odd. Now let's do some casework to see how many terms fit this criteria:
</p><p>Case 1:    <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is even  
</p><p>Now we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is even and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/d/22d61749698f06d583904f9b6592020c685d91b7.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b+c=2006$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="129" height="14" />. Thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/b/c9bbeb7caa1770d1d3cb842655f27e245b08188f.png" class="latex" alt="$b+c$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="38" height="14" /> is also even or both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> are odd or both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> are even. This case clearly fails the above criteria and there are 0 possible solutions.
</p><p>Case 2:    <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is odd
</p><p>Now we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is odd and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/d/22d61749698f06d583904f9b6592020c685d91b7.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b+c=2006$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="129" height="14" />. Thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/b/c9bbeb7caa1770d1d3cb842655f27e245b08188f.png" class="latex" alt="$b+c$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="38" height="14" /> is odd and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> is odd and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> is even or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> is even and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> is odd. All terms that have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> being odd work.
</p><p>We now need to figure out how many of the terms have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> as a odd number. We know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> can be equal to any number between 0 and 2006. There are 1003 odd numbers between this range and 2007 total numbers. Thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/1/03129dbc0986de892fbc26032082f7ac8814b980.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1003}{2007}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="38" height="37" /> of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/9/aa955e4cd41f7611b0a9dabe07f035f3102faaf5.png" class="latex" alt="$2015028$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="62" height="13" /> terms will cancel out and  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/9/6b93de132ac08d87bb603ab2404cbacef6b7a412.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1004}{2007}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="38" height="37" /> of the terms will work. Thus there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/4/e1493d6e676ef92bade52fe10fb0dbc27723ca00.png" class="latex" alt="$(\frac{1004}{2007})(2015028)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="130" height="37" /> terms. This number comes out to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/e/66edfbf0a3ae65dfa4af8a9cc3c5c953507b337c.png" class="latex" alt="$1,008,016$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="79" height="16" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/0/c10184ee1bf9d734669788d287ba350c73108f41.png" class="latex" alt="$\implies \boxed{D}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="71" height="24" />
(Author: David Camacho)
</p>
