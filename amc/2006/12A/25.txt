<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>This question can be solved fairly directly by casework and pattern-finding.  We give a somewhat more general attack, based on the solution to the following problem:
</p><p>How many ways are there to choose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> elements from an ordered <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> element <a href="/wiki/index.php/Set" title="Set">set</a> without choosing two consecutive members?
</p><p>You want to choose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> numbers out of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> with no consecutive numbers. For each configuration, we can subtract <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/5/27571bcad594106e5d9a023867301830b58f569e.png" class="latex" alt="$i-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="36" height="13" /> from the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/8/34857b3ba74ce5cd8607f3ebd23e9015908ada71.png" class="latex" alt="$i$" width="5" height="12" />-th element in your subset. This converts your configuration into a configuration with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> elements where the largest possible element is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/4/844de2793298415699edb274204c8798e09d0b05.png" class="latex" alt="$n-k+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="73" height="14" />, with no restriction on consecutive numbers. Since this process is easily reversible, we have a <a href="/wiki/index.php/Bijection" title="Bijection">bijection</a>.
Without consideration of the second condition, we have:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/2/fd2a5c75161d4b014fe90e81db553727bd689a68.png" class="latex" alt="${15 \choose 1} + {14 \choose 2} + {13 \choose 3} + ... + {9 \choose 7} + {8 \choose 8}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="335" height="53" />
</p><p>Now we examine the second condition. It simply states that no element in our original configuration (and hence also the modified configuration, since we don't move the smallest element) can be less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />, which translates to subtracting <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/c/efce61aab04f52f5d37708f357df021c8baa7434.png" class="latex" alt="$k - 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="13" /> from the "top" of each <a href="/wiki/index.php/Binomial_coefficient" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial coefficient">binomial coefficient</a>.
Now we have, after we cancel all the terms <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/d/44d91e7f8ccfc801e16fd929ae7af0811898b738.png" class="latex" alt="${n \choose k}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="35" height="53" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/7/ae7386d4b470aa726b7da3deb1a0bae50ded12b2.png" class="latex" alt="$n &lt; k$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="44" height="13" />, 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/e/cde53c76737164112f442dfc8293e62b619c5a7f.png" class="latex" alt="${15 \choose 1} + {13 \choose 2} + {11 \choose 3} + {9 \choose 4} + {7 \choose 5}=  15 + 78 + 165 + 126 + 21 = \boxed{405} \Longrightarrow \mathrm{(E)}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="651" height="53" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Another way of visualizing the solution above would be to use <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/3/a131e44938571300020512aceec1fee671ead5ef.png" class="latex" alt="$|$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="3" height="18" />'s and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/d/add22d9bda21e86390bc74fa4dde17730a442da7.png" class="latex" alt="$-$" style="vertical-align: 4px" width="13" height="1" />'s. Denote <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/3/a131e44938571300020512aceec1fee671ead5ef.png" class="latex" alt="$|$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="3" height="18" /> as the numbers we have chosen and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/d/add22d9bda21e86390bc74fa4dde17730a442da7.png" class="latex" alt="$-$" style="vertical-align: 4px" width="13" height="1" /> as other numbers. Taking an example, assuming we are picking two numbers, we imagine the shape <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/7/267e86dd5a8e523c20a43ec706a458a7042eb451.png" class="latex" alt="$| - |$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="30" height="18" />. This notation forces a number between the two chosen numbers, which blocks the two numbers we picked from being consecutive. Now we consider the orientations with this shape. We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/4/2d433751fb47c7d59c5be30274b0819a742db5de.png" class="latex" alt="$15 - 3 = 12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="91" height="13" /> remaining numbers.
</p><p>We need to find the number of ways to place the remaining <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/6/3462a1c3e35b92ab9bc32e070286c231ad3696c7.png" class="latex" alt="$12 -$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="31" height="12" />'s. We can find this by utilizing stars and bars, with the following marker being placed to represent groups: *| - *|*. Now, we have to place <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> numbers within <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> groups, which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/f/adf13b7345f8b06f5a4c701782ae297bd54a07b2.png" class="latex" alt="${14 \choose 2}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="42" height="53" />. The same concept can be used for the remaining numbers. The rest of the solution continues as above.
</p><p>Solution by: Everyoneintexas

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>We have the same setup as in the previous solution.
</p><p>Note that if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/e/51eeca74f45cab0ac751a1ead44293f57dfa5fc5.png" class="latex" alt="$n &lt; 2k - 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="84" height="13" />, the answer will be 0.  Otherwise, the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> elements we choose define <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/8/e18453e31b8b9f4f7722777b72a0eff030a17d1d.png" class="latex" alt="$k + 1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="40" height="14" /> boxes (which divide the nonconsecutive numbers) into which we can drop the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/9/779ac670ba70c351f02e70dd9cfc0a4991d56b0f.png" class="latex" alt="$n - k$" width="42" height="12" /> remaining elements, with the caveat that each of the middle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/c/efce61aab04f52f5d37708f357df021c8baa7434.png" class="latex" alt="$k - 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="13" /> boxes must have at least one element (since the numbers are nonconsecutive).  This is equivalent to dropping <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/e/08ed9de33e3fac3e09e640728030921ad843738b.png" class="latex" alt="$n - 2k + 1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="82" height="14" /> elements into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/8/e18453e31b8b9f4f7722777b72a0eff030a17d1d.png" class="latex" alt="$k + 1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="40" height="14" /> boxes, where each box is allowed to be empty.  And this is equivalent to arranging <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/f/74f5e3b588dc7c81e3c7dde4e47f9146e434b8a1.png" class="latex" alt="$n - k + 1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="73" height="14" /> objects, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> of which are dividers, which we can do in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/1/161b56ee5daf164ed9733b8feba6d77835081295.png" class="latex" alt="$F(n, k) = {n - k + 1 \choose k}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="180" height="53" /> ways.
</p><p>Now, looking at our original question, we see that the thing we want to calculate is just <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/8/0d824fe7286919c3a36990bed8a8c10715f71364.png" class="latex" alt="$F(15, 1) + F(14, 2) + F(13, 3) + F(12, 4) + F(11, 5) = {15\choose 1} + {13\choose2} + {11\choose 3} + {9\choose 4} + {7 \choose 5} = 15 + 78 + 165 + 126 + 21  = 405 \Longrightarrow \mathrm{(E)}$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="1075" height="55" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" /> be the numbers of elements in a subset. First we examine the second condition. No elements less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" /> can be put in a subset of size <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" />, therefore the "lowest" element that can go into the subset is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" />, whereas the "highest" element that can go into the subset is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/9/f1965fae079a9ba2c0726c307070c2355dfcb213.png" class="latex" alt="$15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. This is a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/1/a416ce3ae808ef0aa1e5fe3db6bb56d1cd08d58c.png" class="latex" alt="$15-s+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="79" height="14" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/8/16856f68e3b9641d2b790489909409f689b25a27.png" class="latex" alt="$16-s$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="48" height="13" /> possible elements. 
</p><p>Now we consider the first condition. No consecutive elements are allowed. This means that if an element <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/a/a3a59bb1293ee3f6dec19de4019a7178874219ae.png" class="latex" alt="$e$" width="8" height="8" /> is put into the subset, both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/0/6/c06153280c3b64278874a70aac5d4812bd8c252d.png" class="latex" alt="$e-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="38" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/3/5c31cbff23f0c4aff4659cbff65a00ccdac20862.png" class="latex" alt="$e+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="38" height="13" /> are no longer possibilities. Assume that all subsets are ordered from least to greatest (we are looking for the number of combinations, so we can order these combinations however we want). Then the first element will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" /> (as a reminder, the lowest possible element in a subset is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" />), the second element will be at least <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/2/1e2befa15a2ef30c53994455e1f3dcd36fbc30a7.png" class="latex" alt="$s+2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="42" height="15" />, and so on. After <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" /> elements are chosen, we will have skipped <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/4/f74d52f7378384fa067e7726071a01e3ee643edf.png" class="latex" alt="$s-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="38" height="12" /> elements (these are the elements that were "eliminated" as they were consecutive). Therefore, we ignore exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/4/f74d52f7378384fa067e7726071a01e3ee643edf.png" class="latex" alt="$s-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="38" height="12" /> elements (if we ignore more or less elements, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" /> changes) Since we must ignore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/4/f74d52f7378384fa067e7726071a01e3ee643edf.png" class="latex" alt="$s-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="38" height="12" /> elements, we can simply remove those beforehand. (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/9/299684999b316e9d38d480901d50d2222124cfa3.png" class="latex" alt="$16-s)-(s-1) = 17-2s$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="214" height="18" /> possible elements
</p><p>Now we look for the bounds of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" />. We are looking for non-empty subsets, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/5/9d54bd8a68e3de4c9a2ef7be7db72ba3edc374b3.png" class="latex" alt="$s\ge1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="41" height="14" />. If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" /> is too large, there will not be enough non-consecutive terms between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/9/f1965fae079a9ba2c0726c307070c2355dfcb213.png" class="latex" alt="$15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. Specifically, the highest element in a subset using "optimal" selection will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/b/4eb5c81c759b87bfa0724c249b9c2d8eaacf7df9.png" class="latex" alt="$s+2(s-1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="92" height="18" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/9/3e97c7258ee3fcc5c0b5c615f626829f598e3a6a.png" class="latex" alt="$3s-2$" width="48" height="12" />. If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/0/4503cef1f50bdb509e5237cb63bf0d2cee2ef9d0.png" class="latex" alt="$3s-1&gt;15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="90" height="13" />, that means s is too large. Therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/1/1f1819d83fe2d18b04559cb15c66eb6c3574aaba.png" class="latex" alt="$3s-2\le15$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="90" height="15" />; solving for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" /> yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/0/ec0d2615a6da751ddda36385592beaec24fe3f3a.png" class="latex" alt="$s\le5$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="41" height="15" />. Now we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/8/3b8147ec4d77799ab7085bc6f92cafe5cbbcc8cb.png" class="latex" alt="$1 \le s \le 5$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="74" height="15" />.
</p><p>We want to know the number of ways to arrange <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/1/f81c4d7aeab6ad5e22ffdd67e2b5abdeae8e0273.png" class="latex" alt="$17-2s$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="57" height="12" /> "balls" into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" /> identical "boxes" with at most <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> ball per box, for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/1/fb16e2177f55574e2538c0d9b240b1ee7d46d767.png" class="latex" alt="$s=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="41" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/9/a29de43d66d80fe04c93e2a579f05540926bab29.png" class="latex" alt="$s=5$" width="41" height="12" />. This is equivalent to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/c/8fc40f7a42079e25ddd827f6a48d2fc4a4a87970.png" class="latex" alt="$\sum_{s=1}^{5}{17-2s \choose s} = 405$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="165" height="53" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/3/d1302e73ece29d10218238af739577904f6f00a3.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\text{E}}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="23" height="23" />.
</p>
