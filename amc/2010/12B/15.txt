<p>For how many ordered triples <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/3/c73ed093cc73a8d9a14185fa525e7067e5c3725d.png" class="latex" alt="$(x,y,z)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="58" height="18" /> of nonnegative integers less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/3/f8366cd6196dd8a9da6d38a3e9eafb109e99d53e.png" class="latex" alt="$20$" width="17" height="12" /> are there exactly two distinct elements in the set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/c/1ac425ac9867b996bcf0d81f0b361b1ad9569f63.png" class="latex" alt="$\{i^x, (1+i)^y, z\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="117" height="18" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/9/75952e52a3dc061d24146c0df9062a54a5ce5a5e.png" class="latex" alt="$i=\sqrt{-1}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="69" height="18" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/6/b36e8f47029b215bf9116165e6e027d433adf2b9.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 149 \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 205 \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 215 \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 225 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 235$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="439" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>We have either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/e/e0ee3ad8264cbe46ea669b6d1e4e025a53dae95d.png" class="latex" alt="$i^{x}=(1+i)^{y}\neq z$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="131" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/b/dbbe734d47511c5ec9960a2b4c53077ac50e48b0.png" class="latex" alt="$i^{x}=z\neq(1+i)^{y}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="131" height="18" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/a/0fa083bc1924f738cd7b99200854cbf73a8b076e.png" class="latex" alt="$(1+i)^{y}=z\neq i^x$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="131" height="18" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/5/b856ce3d9538cc1a103fe79302330173dcffacf0.png" class="latex" alt="$i^{x}=(1+i)^{y}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="97" height="18" /> only occurs when it is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019681e77f8add1be13147eb8bcb18ead9a8625f.png" class="latex" alt="$(1+i)^{y}=1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="92" height="18" /> has only one solution, namely, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/8/ec89f09c84538ec43930ca5d8e5e0957e2000822.png" class="latex" alt="$y=0$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="16" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/b/90b46933216dfd6ede21a657946951aad63abbbb.png" class="latex" alt="$i^{x}=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="47" height="13" /> has five solutions between 0 and 19, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/6/126fc46064415e2ac8dbc79e00485543cd216d4a.png" class="latex" alt="$x=0, x=4, x=8, x=12$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="207" height="16" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/2/1628581d2c8bdf5dfbb9d5fafe360ec1773aaff6.png" class="latex" alt="$x=16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="52" height="13" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/7/e47303be5e142605b17e934b91e41ddaef143791.png" class="latex" alt="$z\neq 1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="41" height="17" /> has nineteen integer solutions between zero and nineteen. So for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/e/e0ee3ad8264cbe46ea669b6d1e4e025a53dae95d.png" class="latex" alt="$i^{x}=(1+i)^{y}\neq z$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="131" height="18" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/0/c20eef92d4246a49f01b92a4019ddeb1bafd2789.png" class="latex" alt="$5\cdot 1\cdot 19=95$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="104" height="13" /> ordered triples.
</p><p>For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/b/dbbe734d47511c5ec9960a2b4c53077ac50e48b0.png" class="latex" alt="$i^{x}=z\neq(1+i)^{y}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="131" height="18" />, again this only occurs at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/3/733ec74a06b35c1d79a535b0ee5b3001410b497d.png" class="latex" alt="$(1+i)^{y}\neq 1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="92" height="18" /> has nineteen solutions, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/b/90b46933216dfd6ede21a657946951aad63abbbb.png" class="latex" alt="$i^{x}=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="47" height="13" /> has five solutions, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/d/6bd3d68e77a39e1ea97e7009f70e12622fa76835.png" class="latex" alt="$z=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="41" height="12" /> has one solution, so again we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/0/c20eef92d4246a49f01b92a4019ddeb1bafd2789.png" class="latex" alt="$5\cdot 1\cdot 19=95$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="104" height="13" /> ordered triples.
</p><p>For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/a/0fa083bc1924f738cd7b99200854cbf73a8b076e.png" class="latex" alt="$(1+i)^{y}=z\neq i^x$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="131" height="18" />, this occurs at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/5/9a5b4928c8fe50ce3c2428da3bee3505e891b788.png" class="latex" alt="$16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019681e77f8add1be13147eb8bcb18ead9a8625f.png" class="latex" alt="$(1+i)^{y}=1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="92" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/d/6bd3d68e77a39e1ea97e7009f70e12622fa76835.png" class="latex" alt="$z=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="41" height="12" /> both have one solution while <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/6/9c63b52174e84d06cc8999d9ab744ed36297e11d.png" class="latex" alt="$i^{x}\neq 1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="47" height="17" /> has fifteen solutions. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/d/a5d43b03400fefba8d623a7a0ed05d8f9d86a522.png" class="latex" alt="$(1+i)^{y}=16$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="101" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/0/530de6734387250e89b61078342517676e2143dd.png" class="latex" alt="$z=16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="51" height="13" /> both have one solution, namely, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/4/8a4808fab877e290bdc0e68ce61cc8a852fd397b.png" class="latex" alt="$y=8$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/0/530de6734387250e89b61078342517676e2143dd.png" class="latex" alt="$z=16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="51" height="13" />, while <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/4/5f4414cbb065e7609e249095ebde07ef6b0acaea.png" class="latex" alt="$i^{x}\neq 16$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="56" height="17" /> has twenty solutions (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/c/d9c05382bed2cb37291348dff6dc10c063db6580.png" class="latex" alt="$i^x$" width="13" height="12" /> only cycles as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/b/48b20799b6d003f168e6a654eee904725050adb6.png" class="latex" alt="$1, i, -1, -i$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="82" height="16" />). So we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/1/5f13dfcec1a968f91d9af646e0574a7a47134b63.png" class="latex" alt="$15\cdot 1\cdot 1+20\cdot 1\cdot 1=35$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="189" height="14" /> ordered triples.
</p><p>In total we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/e/e8e6575f44c34c727195b5b99d4ac9dd3c27e388.png" class="latex" alt="${95+95+35=\boxed{\text{(D) }225}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="193" height="28" /> ordered triples
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Small_Clarification">Small Clarification</span></h2>
<p>To more clearly see why the reasoning above is true, try converting the complex numbers into exponential form. That way, we can more easily raise the numbers to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> respectively.
</p>
