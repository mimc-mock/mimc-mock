<p>Two circles lie outside regular hexagon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/2/8029902caf855d940e465d04ff40101d8850c3e0.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCDEF$" width="86" height="13" />. The first is tangent to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" />, and the second is tangent to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/9/699784c989c3db382520e6f92819e062d239f504.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{DE}$" width="30" height="15" />. Both are tangent to lines <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/3/193c5caa0ee1174e95025208873f5495493a2059.png" class="latex" alt="$FA$" width="27" height="13" />. What is the ratio of the area of the second circle to that of the first circle?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/5/e5538546185e3c7e7e07f11ecce2e64e3a3ad72c.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 18 \qquad  \textbf{(B)}\ 27 \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 36 \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 81 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 108$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="403" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>A good diagram is very helpful. 
</p>
<center> <a href="/wiki/index.php/File:Amc10B_2010.gif" class="image"><img alt="Amc10B 2010.gif" src="https://wiki-images.artofproblemsolving.com//d/d3/Amc10B_2010.gif" width="311" height="269" /></a> </center>
<p>The first circle is in red, the second in blue.
With this diagram, we can see that the first circle is inscribed in equilateral triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/6/d9620c6076e6a232da1c079e5c7ba595757254dd.png" class="latex" alt="$GBA$" width="42" height="13" /> while the second circle is inscribed in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/8/2f8390b0e73d2231db51f7b134dba39440528754.png" class="latex" alt="$GKJ$" width="43" height="12" />.
From this, it's evident that the ratio of the blue area to the red area is equal to the <b>ratio of the areas <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/a/1aa1faddcda00e126dbf90e08d6d800f99cd8a77.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle GKJ$" width="59" height="13" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/c/87cbb4501796cbc6dc2fdf82675b256ab6387998.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle GBA$" width="58" height="13" /></b>
</p><p>Since the ratio of areas is equal to the square of the ratio of lengths, we know our final answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/8/dd8abafec69234e3f2a58e209a422261bfc7540f.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{GK}{GB}\right)^2$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="67" height="46" />.
From the diagram, we can see that this is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/2/88272875b1ed1cfb6cf7682d2b90d5d0073fe36f.png" class="latex" alt="$9^2=\boxed{\textbf{(D)}\ 81}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="106" height="28" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>As above, we note that the first circle is inscribed in an equilateral triangle of sidelength 1 (if we assume, WLOG, that the regular hexagon has sidelength 1). The inradius of an equilateral triangle with sidelength 1 is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/d/2dd51255885db291e0e9a212d256ef893de5f44f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" />. Therefore, the area of the first circle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/1/ef17b6e26d81238df357087afc4c879318c7eae4.png" class="latex" alt="$(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{6})^2 \cdot \pi =\frac{\pi}{12}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="118" height="41" />.
</p><p><br />
Call the center of the second circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" />. Now we drop a perpendicular from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" /> to Circle O's point of tangency with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/1/901602020a2a613b9071b0e7175154040761855e.png" class="latex" alt="$GK$" width="31" height="12" /> and draw another line connecting O to G. Note that because triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/8/5789bc7b23fb2d2b88ff2808b33e0f558f60cb3f.png" class="latex" alt="$BGA$" width="42" height="13" /> is equilateral, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/a/5aa069e772cc7e3544b3c28896b7341dc4a2a3df.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BGA=60^{\circ}$" width="103" height="13" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/a/0/2a001d743af320a4435c551b34c5313c164adce4.png" class="latex" alt="$OG$" width="28" height="12" /> bisects <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/a/54a11c6d75bc3a6cc448a6b76acc418d0f573e26.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BGA$" width="55" height="13" />, so we have a 30-60-90 triangle. 
</p><p><br />
Call the radius of Circle O <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />.
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/7/e5715f3039d0ba3ac0865895457375edfa4e6bae.png" class="latex" alt="$OG=2r= \text{height of equilateral triangle} + \text{height of regular hexagon} + r$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="541" height="18" />
</p><p>The height of an equilateral triangle of sidelength 1 is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/9/4e9191a5007067cff8f6d73f4d3419e08238f00b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" />. The height of a regular hexagon of sidelength 1 is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/7/6c77cf4b63af80bfbc6a9ad9b4b9e8e549e77d78.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" />. Therefore,
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/d/68dbd055fc9ac3431fe78d19f3ade844b72765ae.png" class="latex" alt="$OG=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} + \sqrt{3} + r$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="158" height="41" />.
</p><p>We can now set up the following equation:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/1/711773d523dd73eae1daf2a23c7b40cc242efda1.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} + \sqrt{3} + r=2r$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="147" height="41" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/f/dff13685bcb3eaf3af6196bd6bb0645e7124a0ca.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} + \sqrt{3}=r$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="107" height="41" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/e/4beccd063cbc4747b0074beba9235ab372c47b0b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}=r$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="69" height="41" />
</p><p>The area of Circle O equals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/0/980ec1d15194d682e360a4a035d53fb88a3a0de6.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi r^2=\frac{27}{4} \pi$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="84" height="37" />
</p><p>Therefore, the ratio of the areas is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/d/dfd983bef73c3d9f3f20a9b8aba4b1b62f5564d4.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\frac{27}{4}}{\frac{1}{12}}=\boxed{\textbf{(D)}\ 81}$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="112" height="49" />
</p>

</p><p>~IceMatrix
