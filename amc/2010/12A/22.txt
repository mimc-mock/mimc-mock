<p>What is the minimum value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/2/1f2b592f9234b9ac891938b2a33510b16e7b179c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)=\left|x-1\right| + \left|2x-1\right| + \left|3x-1\right| + \cdots + \left|119x - 1 \right|$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="420" height="18" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/e/44e264741f7323ff8297178d0c27d3301de9d4aa.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 49 \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 50 \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 51 \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 52 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 53$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="394" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>If we graph each term separately, we will notice that all of the zeros occur at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/5/4659ee9ed51157f75502b0ceabc4b396eccd39ba.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{m}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="18" height="37" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> is any integer from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/5/2f5dc392ba2faf31608de0cc131ab8c7227b31dd.png" class="latex" alt="$119$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />, inclusive: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/d/48d1c9cbfb2c9930f3337c882bd91750ad38ecc6.png" class="latex" alt="$|mx-1|=0\implies mx=1\implies x=\frac{1}{m}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="316" height="38" />.
</p><p>The minimum value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c96dd6ec1dc4ad7520fbdc78fcdbec9edd068d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" /> occurs where the absolute value of the sum of the slopes is at a minimum <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/9/aa9c151a661a687f6e3e9b6e95ff672c231b6ef3.png" class="latex" alt="$\ge 0$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="27" height="15" />, since it is easy to see that the value will be increasing on either side. That means the minimum must happen at some <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/5/4659ee9ed51157f75502b0ceabc4b396eccd39ba.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{m}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="18" height="37" />.
</p><p>The sum of the slopes at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/2/3d2ed9039b779b42bac0e95d565ee180aa38c524.png" class="latex" alt="$x = \frac{1}{m}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="52" height="37" /> is
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/7/8978065756bf97c2302061def2dbcae2950643a9.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*}&amp;\sum_{i=m+1}^{119}i - \sum_{i=1}^{m}i\\ &amp;=\sum_{i=1}^{119}i - 2\sum_{i=1}^{m}i\\ &amp;=-m^2-m+7140\end{align*}" width="154" height="136" />
</p><p>Now we want to minimize <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/d/e8d47e92ac3323a6c4cc26e340eb57edcce7c33d.png" class="latex" alt="$-m^2-m+7140$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="133" height="16" />. The zeros occur at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/7/fd7b3df13bf29d1fa8ed22ba1ace6df8fa33d2f8.png" class="latex" alt="$-85$" width="31" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/3/4131457f67c5dbfd8b1c6be1f74a47595a9bd3be.png" class="latex" alt="$84$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="13" />, which means the slope is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/c/0bc72cd7a4226cc1324756f3dac913343e9334c4.png" class="latex" alt="$m = 84, 85$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="84" height="16" />.
</p><p>We can now verify that both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/3/48398ae9e387ea54a038a9ea14a28204c57ed8a2.png" class="latex" alt="$x=\frac{1}{84}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="55" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/7/d/67daf42f3f35346ac0a1ca9e05be66c43319fd71.png" class="latex" alt="$x=\frac{1}{85}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="55" height="37" /> yield <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/c/65c70e8a4890497383420353549f8931f84d6e9f.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{49\ \textbf{(A)}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="64" height="28" />.
</p><p>You can also think of the slopes playing 'tug of war', where the slope of each absolute function upon passing its <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-intercept is negated, positively tugging on the remaining negative slopes.
</p><p>The sum of the slopes is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/6/636058661025af5b6c5856e29386bb8bb12862f5.png" class="latex" alt="$1+2+3+4\dots 119=\sum_{m=1}^{119}m=\frac{119\cdot 120}{2}=60\cdot 119=7140$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="468" height="50" />
</p><p>So we need to find the least integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/5/c351489b09e12bab1cee4d8b4fb96e3271bf2045.png" class="latex" alt="$1+2+3+\dots a=\sum_{n=1}^an=\frac{a(a+1)}{2}\ge \frac{7140}{2}=3570:$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="418" height="48" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/e/e7ebfae02f14c600da13439a04fbebe93a1bedfd.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[a(a+1)\ge 7140\implies a^2+a-7140\ge 0\rightarrow a=84\text{ exactly!}\]" width="456" height="19" />
This "exactly" means that the slope is ZERO between the whole interval <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/1/611cd245e3280598f9002645b048116457704fba.png" class="latex" alt="$x\in\left(\frac{1}{85},\frac{1}{84}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="107" height="43" />. We can explicitly evaluate both to check that they are both equal to the desired minimum value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c96dd6ec1dc4ad7520fbdc78fcdbec9edd068d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" />:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/2/7a2b8ec05fb8707f154e253cce2cb3c47a46658a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{84+83+\dots+2+1+1+2+\dots+33+34}{85}=\frac{84(85)/2+34(35)/2}{85}=\frac{85(14+84)/2}{85}=49\]" width="720" height="38" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/8/4f8bef77e5fe21d0b3a3b733ce9e9236f12489a2.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{83+82+\dots+2+1+1+2+\dots+34+35}{84}=\frac{83(84)/2+35(36)/2}{84}=\frac{84(15+83)/2}{84}=49\]" width="720" height="39" />
</p><p>Thus the minimum value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c96dd6ec1dc4ad7520fbdc78fcdbec9edd068d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/c/34c8e6bf4d57cd6f2bb0d7d43e00f0f442f53b84.png" class="latex" alt="$49$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Rewrite the given expression as follows:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/0/220356eccd4b88b28a5dbb0f324fe152883a5e9f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[1|x-1| + 2\left|x-\frac 12\right| + \cdots + 119\left|x-\frac 1{119}\right|\]" width="320" height="43" />
Imagine the real line. For each <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/e/dde62e1a58a7aa195ec0bf712e6ecdabb10100b0.png" class="latex" alt="$n\in\{1,\dots,119\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="126" height="18" /> imagine that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> boys standing at the coordinate <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/e/0ee06e6776df89765d19b1644085a0100065f4b0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac 1n$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="13" height="37" />. We now need to place a donut on the real line in such a way that the sum of its distances from all the boys is minimal, and we need to compute this sum.
</p><p>Note that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/d/e7d37cd0646612106ad568b8d6ea79b7369c401b.png" class="latex" alt="$B=1+2+\cdots+119 = 119\cdot 60=7140$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="315" height="14" /> boys in total. Let's label them from 1 (the only boy placed at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />) to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> (the last boy placed at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/7/e4700cc8db3fee0d899ff15c47dd1a70a0ac3f5f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac 1{119}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="29" height="37" />.
</p><p>Clearly, the minimum sum is achieved if the donut's coordinate is the median of the boys' coordinates. To prove this, place the donut at the median coordinate. If you now move it in any direction by any amount <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" />, there will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/e/55e53d93c8f1ad6925c4aed4f056d9f1707e910f.png" class="latex" alt="$B/2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="32" height="18" /> boys such that it moves <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> away from this boy. For each of the remaining boys, it moves at most <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> closer, hence the total sum of distances does not decrease.
</p><p>Hence the optimal solution is to place the donut at the median coordinate. Or, more precisely, as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> is even, we can place it anywhere on the segment formed by boy <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/e/55e53d93c8f1ad6925c4aed4f056d9f1707e910f.png" class="latex" alt="$B/2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="32" height="18" /> and boy <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/7/f67fe791656d287507b07a3adfcc11b531cd3398.png" class="latex" alt="$(B/2)+1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="77" height="18" />: by extending the previous argument, anywhere on this segment the sum of distances is the same.
</p><p>By trial and error, or by solving the quadratic equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/4/5348800881c53017c9731d33d287848743bebbf6.png" class="latex" alt="$z(z+1)/2 = 7140/2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="160" height="18" /> we get that boy number <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/e/55e53d93c8f1ad6925c4aed4f056d9f1707e910f.png" class="latex" alt="$B/2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="32" height="18" /> is the last boy placed at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/a/dba614b9530f8c2f253e5e8dc4e5cecc009f63af.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac 1{84}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="37" /> and the next boy is the one placed at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/b/55bafd78d6b9a110c4fa235a3cf79b5d0db9d136.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac 1{85}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" />. Hence the given expression is minimized for any <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/9/fd9263d49f869f20521e9244c9ac8d87e6941105.png" class="latex" alt="$x\in\left[ \frac 1{85}, \frac 1{84} \right]$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="99" height="43" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_part_of_both_solutions">Common part of both solutions</span></h3>
<p>To find the minimum, we want to balance the expression so that it is neither top nor bottom heavy. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/d/8dde39f380651a6eb1a4e6f29d0cceabd9addcf8.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{119(120)}{2(2)}=\frac{7140}{2}=3570=\frac{84(85)}{2}=\frac{119(120)}{2}-\frac{84(85)}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="448" height="43" />.
</p><p>Now that we know that the sum of the first 84 <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />'s is equivalent to the sum of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />'s 85 to 119, we can plug either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/5/e953873e0cfc97a76b438dfd97a99b4d36778587.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{84}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="37" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/1/131f5aa6ec1aedfe8c2301e3fee5f5e1cdec52a9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{85}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" /> to find the minimum.
</p><p>Note that the terms <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/b/d4baf6d4b1cfa69b68b3204b5e2eba0c5755fe20.png" class="latex" alt="$x-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/f/75f695f62886f615296bc3e1527ba1ecd7eabac5.png" class="latex" alt="$83x-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="59" height="13" /> are negative, and the terms <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/f/0ff83a0c2c26088b07db8c1414c535dd77bc4f58.png" class="latex" alt="$85x-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="59" height="13" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/3/a23df583e73955b535cf16adce61340134c0da68.png" class="latex" alt="$119x-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="68" height="13" /> are positive. Hence we get:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/4/0d476b559f337f29a9d7c8d809011dd8cb1af728.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} &amp; |x-1| + |2x-1| + \cdots + |83x-1| \\ =~ &amp; (1-x) + (1-2x) + \cdots + (1-83x) \\ =~ &amp; 83 - x(1+2+\cdots+83) \\ =~ &amp; 83 - \frac 1{84} \cdot \frac{83\cdot 84}2 \\ =~ &amp; 83 - \frac{83}2 \\ =~ &amp; \frac{83}2 \end{align*}" width="305" height="199" />
and
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/5/bd5800a26de7a0d0efb06840f0cf2063e1fd1144.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} &amp; |85x-1| + |86x-1| + \cdots + |119x-1| \\ =~ &amp; (85x-1) + (86x-1) + \cdots + (119x-1) \\ =~ &amp; x(85+86+\cdots+119) - (119-84) \\ =~ &amp; \frac 1{84} \cdot \frac{84\cdot 85}2 - 35 \\ =~ &amp; \frac{85}2 - 35 \\ =~ &amp; \frac{15}2 \end{align*}" width="341" height="199" />
Hence the total sum of distances is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/9/f59b4c25061ef4d0f4f0a47fa69a20f8bd00317c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{83}2 + \frac{15}2 = 49$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="108" height="37" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>By the triangle inequality,<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/8/5f8fa2de4876f51ae29c327414205f4790ce825b.png" class="latex" alt="$|x-1|+|2x-1|+|3x-1|+\cdots + |119x-1| \geq |(x-1)+(2x-1)+\cdots+(119x)-1|.$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="692" height="18" />
However, we may change signs of some of these terms to cancel out the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />'s.
Since the minimum exists, we want all the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />s to cancel out. Thus, we want to find some <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/a/02a6b54943cc14ad2d45a56be7b057ba6638a72e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[1+2+3+...+n=(n+1)+(n+2)+(n+3)+...+119\]" width="464" height="18" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/8/858b800ffefb17d16ccb233a12863a1bd1e2eee5.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{n(n+1)}{2}=\frac{119\cdot120}{2}-\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\]" width="257" height="38" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/f/91fc75842e697e99622b5095de8a130b811419f6.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[n^2+n-7140=0\]" width="142" height="16" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/6/496996599a46b815c58314d97e413cc1a94ca204.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[n=84\]" width="52" height="13" />
</p><p>Then, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/4/bb462f0600b654c39ecbd483ceb54c38c751b02b.png" class="latex" alt="$x=\frac{1}{n}= \frac{1}{84}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="94" height="37" />. The answer(expression's value) is then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ff8e5ae34cf25ce54753fc36cd004e1a23f5241.png" class="latex" alt="$84*1+(119-85+1)*(-1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="232" height="18" /> , which becomes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/e/fee530ef38d73af4530b07c525ad06958dc4b045.png" class="latex" alt="$84-35=\boxed{49}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="112" height="23" />.
</p>
