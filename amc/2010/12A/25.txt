<p>Two quadrilaterals are considered the same if one can be obtained from the other by a rotation and a translation. How many different convex cyclic quadrilaterals are there with integer sides and perimeter equal to 32?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/d/a6de13207eb61c15ee427ae701485781561a9128.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 560 \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 564 \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 568 \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 1498 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 2255$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="457" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>It should first be noted that given any quadrilateral of fixed side lengths, there is exactly one way to manipulate the angles so that the quadrilateral becomes cyclic.
</p><p><b>Proof.</b> Given a quadrilateral <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> where all sides are fixed (in a certain order), we can construct the diagonal <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/7/4373e7b4be983c0315e82e1fda1df66ed34dd7fb.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BD}$" width="30" height="15" />. When <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/f/89fff82bb65d0215e49c8c91cb7c553da52205e2.png" class="latex" alt="$BD$" width="29" height="12" /> is the minimum allowed by the triangle inequality, one of the angles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/1/8b1fc15d59287d2ff7bf581a84f348b3207a0c64.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle DAB$" width="56" height="13" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/3/23386faeefe37629243fa264877b288499db5048.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BCD$" width="57" height="12" /> will be degenerate and measure <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/9/c79965629cf6c6f6e340be9c4b2384f5ef712316.png" class="latex" alt="$0^\circ$" width="15" height="13" />, so opposite angles will sum to less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/4/c4460b5100c3ce982ab5ebb20a8c3871e9cfdc54.png" class="latex" alt="$180^\circ$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="13" />. When <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/f/89fff82bb65d0215e49c8c91cb7c553da52205e2.png" class="latex" alt="$BD$" width="29" height="12" /> is the maximum allowed, one of the angles will be degenerate and measure <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/4/c4460b5100c3ce982ab5ebb20a8c3871e9cfdc54.png" class="latex" alt="$180^\circ$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="13" />, so opposite angles will sum to more than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/4/c4460b5100c3ce982ab5ebb20a8c3871e9cfdc54.png" class="latex" alt="$180^\circ$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="13" />. Thus, since the sum of opposite angles increases continuously as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/f/89fff82bb65d0215e49c8c91cb7c553da52205e2.png" class="latex" alt="$BD$" width="29" height="12" /> is lengthened from the minimum to the maximum values, there is a unique value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/f/89fff82bb65d0215e49c8c91cb7c553da52205e2.png" class="latex" alt="$BD$" width="29" height="12" /> somewhere in the middle such that the sum of opposite angles is exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/4/c4460b5100c3ce982ab5ebb20a8c3871e9cfdc54.png" class="latex" alt="$180^\circ$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="13" />.
</p><p>Denote <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> as the integer side lengths of the quadrilateral. Without loss of generality, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/8/3d875015c48a045e1e0413afee17383225bfc0fa.png" class="latex" alt="$a\ge b \ge c \ge d$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="107" height="15" />.
</p><p>Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/2/f528ecdad75e6d9255a91b98914730f8d6ff0d5b.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b+c+d = 32$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="143" height="14" />, the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Triangle_Inequality" title="Triangle Inequality">Triangle Inequality</a> implies that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/d/50dca7e372c2111c41703c7be9070e9b0479f879.png" class="latex" alt="$a \le 15$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="51" height="15" />.
</p><p><br />
We will now split into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> cases.
</p><p><br />
Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/3/49314d1fd2266b1a01085ede5b9e787845c53d1b.png" class="latex" alt="$a = b = c = d$" width="107" height="12" /> (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> side lengths are equal)
</p><p>Clearly there is only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> way to select the side lengths <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/a/1/2a1d73d927c15355a091409c0ecd4802cd456ff4.png" class="latex" alt="$(8,8,8,8)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="73" height="18" />, and no matter how the sides are rearranged only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> unique quadrilateral can be formed.
</p><p>Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/0/ca02295d74d8f21c5bf89078f81ac178b3d6a2c0.png" class="latex" alt="$a = b = c &gt; d$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="107" height="13" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/4/314aaade125ba8d92de4a39c8bf6d36c2c6ded63.png" class="latex" alt="$a &gt; b = c = d$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="107" height="13" /> (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> side lengths are equal)
</p><p>If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> side lengths are equal, then each of those side lengths can only be integers from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> except for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> (because that is counted in the first case). Obviously there is still only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> unique quadrilateral that can be formed from one set of side lengths, resulting in a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> quadrilaterals.
</p><p>Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/3/463c4d1325557f85d36390a6dc649d7a9048db7a.png" class="latex" alt="$a = b &gt; c = d$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="107" height="13" /> (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> pairs of side lengths are equal)
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> can be any integer from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/9/f1965fae079a9ba2c0726c307070c2355dfcb213.png" class="latex" alt="$15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />, and likewise <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> can be any integer from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" />. However, a single set of side lengths can form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> different cyclic quadrilaterals (a rectangle and a kite), so the total number of quadrilaterals for this case is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/e/9ee4f4454dd8f8a090d0efad55aee4ab42a695c6.png" class="latex" alt="$7\cdot{2} = 14$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="73" height="12" />.
</p><p>Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/7/1a7dafbb4090fcc13edd3ce6df2f1e0f04ae3a73.png" class="latex" alt="$a = b &gt; c &gt; d$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="107" height="13" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/b/35bc107bd63c887fb7c1b71350903bf630c5e00a.png" class="latex" alt="$a &gt; b = c &gt; d$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="107" height="13" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/3/f83d58a88f5bb66325360ad895f2d9a4f5aebbf4.png" class="latex" alt="$a &gt; b &gt; c = d$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="107" height="13" /> (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> side lengths are equal)
</p><p>If the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> equal side lengths are each <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, then the other <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> sides must each be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/9/f1965fae079a9ba2c0726c307070c2355dfcb213.png" class="latex" alt="$15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />, which we have already counted in an earlier case. If the equal side lengths are each <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, there is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> possible set of side lengths. Likewise, for side lengths of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> sets. Continuing this pattern, we find a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/e/3febb040a75dc6d5580470451e78659a66faa12b.png" class="latex" alt="$1+2+3+4+4+5+7+5+4+4+3+2+1 = 45$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="426" height="14" /> sets of side lengths. (Be VERY careful when adding up the total for this case!) For each set of side lengths, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> possible quadrilaterals that can be formed, so the total number of quadrilaterals for this case is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/1/d21677013de223d4e291464e30e61e259c4d4694.png" class="latex" alt="$3\cdot{45} = 135$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="91" height="13" />.
</p><p>Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/0/5d054dc8b366a8fcab4f1908771ea3a1edcfeaf3.png" class="latex" alt="$a &gt; b &gt; c &gt; d$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="107" height="13" /> (no side lengths are equal)
Using the same counting principles starting from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/1/8314bc82fa1289c9e190e2ab1251400475aedd6c.png" class="latex" alt="$a = 15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="51" height="13" /> and eventually reaching <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/c/9ac1354558266508b4407741ff1129cb2ab838a0.png" class="latex" alt="$a = 9$" width="42" height="12" />, we find that the total number of possible side lengths is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/0/a80952a9a250e7257f7c6d64e763dae189dbb271.png" class="latex" alt="$69$" width="17" height="12" />. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/a/2da866442e69c040daf44260b4728d37e98de313.png" class="latex" alt="$4!$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="12" height="13" /> ways to arrange the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> side lengths, but there is only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> unique quadrilateral for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> rotations, so the number of quadrilaterals for each set of side lengths is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/d/3cd5f09088ecea94515cd29b7669cb129ddeb7ff.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{4!}{4} = 6$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="51" height="38" />. The total number of quadrilaterals is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/9/b298456425ae6a070bdbb89e73629313c6bac7ab.png" class="latex" alt="$6\cdot{69} = 414$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="91" height="13" />.

And so, the total number of quadrilaterals that can be made is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/9/359d517dc04ecf259f662f7fd1946041bd8f16fa.png" class="latex" alt="$414 + 135 + 14 + 4 + 1 = \boxed{568\ \textbf{(C)}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="277" height="28" />.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>As with solution <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> we would  like to note that given any quadrilateral we can change its angles to make a cyclic one.
</p><p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/6/bf6e30d0963b0bdd4e4bcbd4bfb2b39d0d40b573.png" class="latex" alt="$a \ge b \ge c\ge d$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="107" height="15" /> be the sides of the quadrilateral.
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/e/e6ecbc3bd27e04a33b6a1fcb86229f19412979f6.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{31}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="42" height="53" /> ways to partition <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/4/4f4b8590f8aa9f9894b3891dd5dede0e354fa04e.png" class="latex" alt="$32$" width="17" height="12" />. However, some of these will not be quadrilaterals since they would have one side bigger than the sum of the other three.  This occurs when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/a/1/2a13bb1d6259372b3c495ac1c298a6a7bd3a5194.png" class="latex" alt="$a \ge 16$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="51" height="15" />.  For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/5/ad5bf11c474e7b22a77177f8bdcd8546e9a22e35.png" class="latex" alt="$a=16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="51" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/c/a0cdcc088659842a5dc221963feed74bc7863177.png" class="latex" alt="$b+c+d=16$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="111" height="14" />. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/d/08da0ef0ea782e41606454a7bf72b888621c8de0.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{15}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="42" height="53" /> ways to partition <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/5/9a5b4928c8fe50ce3c2428da3bee3505e891b788.png" class="latex" alt="$16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> could be any of the four sides, we have counted <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/f/dcfa2290fd44edbb904ddeba156ee8f6d8bc8ffe.png" class="latex" alt="$4\binom{15}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="51" height="53" /> degenerate quadrilaterals. Similarly, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5c1c1b77fc67f2be0839cd1ad76ecfe7cc8702.png" class="latex" alt="$4\binom{14}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="51" height="53" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/0/ff08686f240529f737850c069aa8cd9afa74fd4b.png" class="latex" alt="$4\binom{13}{2} \cdots 4\binom{2}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="125" height="53" /> for other values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" />.  Thus, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/6/406ceb115a6fe2257d05d3bd84191283687ae44e.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{31}{3} - 4\left(\binom{15}{2}+\binom{14}{2}+\cdots+\binom{2}{2}\right) = \binom{31}{3} - 4\binom{16}{3} = 2255$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="534" height="53" /> non-degenerate partitions of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/4/4f4b8590f8aa9f9894b3891dd5dede0e354fa04e.png" class="latex" alt="$32$" width="17" height="12" /> by the hockey stick theorem.  We then account for symmetry. If all sides are congruent (meaning the quadrilateral is a square), the quadrilateral will be counted once. If the quadrilateral is a rectangle (and not a square), it will be counted twice. In all other cases, it will be counted 4 times. Since there is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> square case, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" /> rectangle cases, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/2/9f2e68a0f6192a81b82fa97410f1db40e2d34329.png" class="latex" alt="$2255-1-2\cdot7=2240$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="181" height="13" /> quadrilaterals counted 4 times. Thus there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/2/1a23c2f030ade97a00edd3e92edde65b7e8b1cea.png" class="latex" alt="$1+7+\frac{2240}{4} = \boxed{568} = \boxed{\textbf{(C)}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="230" height="38" /> total quadrilaterals.
