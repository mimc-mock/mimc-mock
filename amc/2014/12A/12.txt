<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Let the radius of the larger and smaller circles be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />, respectively. Also, let their centers be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/1/3216e8846eb44f1055c322fb5bd30169d5d62cec.png" class="latex" alt="$O_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/e/5ee48e5d3968014ef1e27c31e56d2f7ea5f8cb1d.png" class="latex" alt="$O_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="20" height="15" />, respectively. Then the ratio we need to find is
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/f/0fffedcec747914069e4b8e8d009feee13957993.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\dfrac{\pi x^2}{\pi y^2} = \dfrac{x^2}{y^2}\]" width="75" height="42" />
Draw the radii from the centers of the circles to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />. We can easily conclude that the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/3/e73393067e6d6e401a5354accc7a730c7a2977aa.png" class="latex" alt="$30^{\circ}$" width="24" height="13" /> belongs to the larger circle, and the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" /> degree arc belongs to the smaller circle. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/0/280f3ddc401ea614c103ec6abdea078e6392a717.png" class="latex" alt="$m\angle AO_1B = 30^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="126" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/0/2f0ea2d89f238b5e51f75cb379657200b564f67e.png" class="latex" alt="$m\angle AO_2B = 60^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="126" height="15" />. Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/b/aeb5ef9f216c38f00cd0801c37cf970fd0deff45.png" class="latex" alt="$\Delta AO_2B$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="64" height="15" /> is equilateral, so when chord AB is drawn, it has length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />. Now, applying the Law of Cosines on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/5/9a5a9849b3c72955070a2bbb90337888a4552fbe.png" class="latex" alt="$\Delta AO_1B$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="64" height="15" />:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/0/0a0124afd9c63c7b4f90d33d060139a90fd51843.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[y^2 = x^2 + x^2 - 2x^2\cos{30} = 2x^2 - x^2\sqrt{3} = (2 - \sqrt{3})x^2\]" width="422" height="21" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/2/b82ce114dd7246cb6678171d472860ab8ac1c78c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\dfrac{x^2}{y^2} = \dfrac{1}{2 - \sqrt{3}} = \dfrac{2 + \sqrt{3}}{4-3} = 2 + \sqrt{3}=\boxed{\textbf{(D)}}\]" width="332" height="45" />

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Again, let the radius of the larger and smaller circles be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />, respectively, and let the centers of these circles be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/1/3216e8846eb44f1055c322fb5bd30169d5d62cec.png" class="latex" alt="$O_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/e/5ee48e5d3968014ef1e27c31e56d2f7ea5f8cb1d.png" class="latex" alt="$O_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="20" height="15" />, respectively.  Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> bisect segment <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" />.  Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/2/4425f8cb4a25a671b7c4682422d71373be10e7e1.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AXO_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="66" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/e/32e0a12ca519c62b1f37cee3eae43dcb553255d2.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AXO_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="66" height="16" /> are right triangles, with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/b/a6bcd9fc99441d12318ac2dc5996c2ad674e1cbf.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle AO_1X=15^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="112" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/7/787061ded7c8f373c47212af0ba4c208203e9156.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle AO_2X=30^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="112" height="15" />.  We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/d/8fd8191e82a705029789c0424a2ac4141731e6bb.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{15} = \dfrac{AX}{x}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="100" height="38" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/e/30ef2102ca55d06da2dbbee7b6ad155bec3ba96b.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{30} = \dfrac{AX}{y}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="100" height="41" />  and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/8/888c8b200071b92ea56f14d6767bad3c0becdec1.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{x}{y} = \dfrac{\sin{30}}{\sin{15}}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="84" height="41" />. Since the ratio of the area of the larger circle to that of the smaller circle is simply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/a/d1ab0cc2b022d5b2224566688237090f5a813828.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{\pi x^2}{\pi y^2} = \left(\dfrac{x}{y} \right)^2 = \left(\dfrac{\sin{30}}{\sin{15}} \right)^2$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="209" height="46" />, we just need to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/1/4912aa77a936f5380b692c69effb358e0c396eac.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{30}$" width="43" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/3/093625ad68c4f03b30dd272949d33a8890345b01.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{15}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="13" />. We know <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/0/6808c4f5a7ffda2d9ea60b1fcb071c8c6976b394.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{30} = \dfrac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="79" height="37" />, and we can use the angle sum formula or half angle formula to compute <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/2/b228d1defe5aff7d5e27d9d700afa13ba9101191.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{15} = \dfrac{\sqrt{6} - \sqrt{2}}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="141" height="41" />. Plugging this into the previous expression, we get: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/6/526b163fea8bde6d1c13a21a318363a0f2c3cca8.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\left(\dfrac {x}{y} \right)^2 = \left(\dfrac{\dfrac{1}{2}}{\dfrac{\sqrt{6} - \sqrt{2}}{4}} \right)^2 = \left(\dfrac{\sqrt{6} + \sqrt{2}}{2} \right)^2 = 2 + \sqrt{3} =\boxed{\textbf{(D)}}\]" width="465" height="90" />
(Solution by kevin38017)
