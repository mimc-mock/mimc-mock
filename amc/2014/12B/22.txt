<p>In a small pond there are eleven lily pads in a row labeled 0 through 10.  A frog is sitting on pad 1.  When the frog is on pad <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/8/c78f961646cafb20c51dc39f3c154fbf17265b4c.png" class="latex" alt="$0&lt;N&lt;10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="91" height="13" />, it will jump to pad <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/5/a256c70ad4c46ec1127c5be68f8bb3075e9ced31.png" class="latex" alt="$N-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="47" height="13" /> with probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/3/9c34b06249aac069179cec1997539a6c74799bba.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{N}{10}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="38" /> and to pad <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/f/a7fc8f668ffc0730528bca6447b71e28cebd0cf0.png" class="latex" alt="$N+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="47" height="14" /> with probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/9/0a92a16d0a76cb702e9c77ece07a2fd85d63c747.png" class="latex" alt="$1-\frac{N}{10}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="52" height="38" />.  Each jump is independent of the previous jumps.  If the frog reaches pad 0 it will be eaten by a patiently waiting snake.  If the frog reaches pad 10 it will exit the pond, never to return.  What is the probability that the frog will escape without being eaten by the snake?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/4/a64ac910622760606eba973537ab91fe236af0ba.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }\frac{32}{79}\qquad \textbf{(B) }\frac{161}{384}\qquad \textbf{(C) }\frac{63}{146}\qquad \textbf{(D) }\frac{7}{16}\qquad \textbf{(E) }\frac{1}{2}\qquad$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="426" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>A long, but straightforward bash:
</p><p>Define <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/a/74a3c6a3a3cae21e33f051e2e292b0bc344d576f.png" class="latex" alt="$P(N)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="43" height="18" /> to be the probability that the frog survives starting from pad N.
</p><p>Then note that by symmetry, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/3/b73903f4745f1074c59735ca1b097f504a51a93b.png" class="latex" alt="$P(5) = 1/2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="88" height="18" />, since the probabilities of the frog moving subsequently in either direction from pad 5 are equal.
</p><p>We therefore seek to rewrite <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/7/797a20647c81f3259c5069556cab51c94bf4b752.png" class="latex" alt="$P(1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="35" height="18" /> in terms of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/d/75d2840f4c4a51ffb604727078a50a895034a531.png" class="latex" alt="$P(5)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="35" height="18" />, using the fact that
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/0/d102691a3eb25222b388c725c17c6f0759171bce.png" class="latex" alt="$P(N) = \frac {N} {10}P(N - 1) + \frac {10 - N} {10}P(N + 1)$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="325" height="38" />
</p><p>as said in the problem.
</p><p>Hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/e/ccef8330954c5607d5e59e4da8ca04351c4548b2.png" class="latex" alt="$P(1) = \frac {1} {10}P(0) + \frac {9} {10}P(2) = \frac {9} {10}P(2)$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="285" height="38" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/c/58cb799beff88a0f046809b4ffc79e16febbbcdf.png" class="latex" alt="$\Rightarrow P(2) = \frac {10} {9}P(1)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="143" height="37" />
</p><p>Returning to our original equation:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/a/05af66a39fca6a403557ec1dc985744f761180d2.png" class="latex" alt="$P(1) = \frac {9} {10}P(2) = \frac {9} {10}\left(\frac{2} {10}P(1) + \frac{8} {10}P(3)\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="334" height="43" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/8/0f8b2b75dd538d85b2e9044771d48d0ff32aaf8d.png" class="latex" alt="$= \frac {9} {50}P(1) + \frac {18} {25}P(3) \Rightarrow P(1) - \frac {9} {50}P(1)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="306" height="37" /> 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/0/ed0094d53e75030e4e6e1101db7d82dd9b09b83b.png" class="latex" alt="$= \frac {18} {25}P(3)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="77" height="37" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/7/e7728de2981939d6cc4aa9512399b623a967451e.png" class="latex" alt="$\Rightarrow P(3) = \frac {41} {36}P(1)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="143" height="37" />
</p><p>Returning to our original equation:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/d/53d9c2adebd7c1185d76f589533dcccb76e7906b.png" class="latex" alt="$P(1) = \frac {9} {50}P(1) + \frac {18} {25}\left(\frac {3} {10}P(2) + \frac {7} {10}P(4)\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="332" height="43" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/6/f46275bcaaf6ec12ec0a32dcfaa16ef58099d63b.png" class="latex" alt="$= \frac {9} {50}P(1) + \frac {27} {125}P(2) + \frac {63} {125}P(4)$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="259" height="38" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/b/fcbe0f38bf5498db55874ff9733059ba84ea7dce.png" class="latex" alt="$= \frac {9} {50}P(1) + \frac {27} {125}\left(\frac {10} {9}P(1)\right) + \frac {63} {125}\left(\frac {4} {10}P(3) + \frac {6} {10}P(5)\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="441" height="43" />
</p><p>Cleaing up the coefficients, we have: 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/3/5539ccc2183448bfb86eb8f57ac261e43d594ca7.png" class="latex" alt="$= \frac {21} {50}P(1) + \frac {126} {625}P(3) + \frac {189} {625}P(5)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="259" height="37" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/6/776107c6add63d6b047b2ccd74522b045c2ea03c.png" class="latex" alt="$= \frac {21} {50}P(1) + \frac {126} {625}\left(\frac {41} {36}P(1)\right) + \frac {189} {625}\left(\frac {1} {2}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="313" height="43" />
</p><p>Hence, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/d/0dda986f99ad6efebab3e9d051d8ddcecd3d165d.png" class="latex" alt="$P(1) = \frac {525} {1250}P(1) + \frac {287} {1250}P(1) + \frac {189} {1250}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="300" height="38" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/3/103138e22f633b4661ddd39c6e030681fd976728.png" class="latex" alt="$\Rightarrow P(1) - \frac {812} {1250}P(1) = \frac {189} {1250} \Rightarrow P(1) = \frac {189} {438}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="344" height="38" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/0/40033c2a1cf5ac46f78ded6462bcd1212e9ab89e.png" class="latex" alt="$= \boxed{\frac {63} {146}\, (C)}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="93" height="48" />
</p><p>Or set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/d/26dc1fee3126f1cfa247981081b69d4fccc41a07.png" class="latex" alt="$P(1)=a,P(2)=b,P(3)=c,P(4)=d,P(5)=e=1/2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="434" height="18" />:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/c/b2c5d894909662c743bd8cc51b9f3fb826e03719.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[a=0.1\emptyset+0.9b,b=0.2a+0.8c,c=0.3b+0.7d,d=0.4c+0.6e\]" width="478" height="18" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/b/8ab0be04234eaf7cf5e39b42867e86027faba272.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[10a=\emptyset+9b,10b=2a+8c,10c=3b+7d,10d=4c+6e\]" width="447" height="18" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/0/8c05a46d83b68bceac26aca6051924152c138c55.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\implies b=\frac{10a-\emptyset}{9},c=\frac{5b-a}{4},d=\frac{10c-3b}{7},e=\frac{5d-2c}{3}=1/2\]" width="490" height="40" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/e/64e6766e648c5f17ff1a97cccfa073c57ba39d42.png" class="latex" alt="$b=\frac{10a}{9}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="62" height="37" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/d/82db557d2bf2d95837d8bbc1f36e2f89c8ee8164.png" class="latex" alt="$c=\frac{5\left(\frac{10a}{9}\right)-a}{4}=\frac{\frac{50a}{9}-a}{9}=\frac{41a}{36}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="259" height="42" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/b/1ab12aeba196236ecf3bcfc131b3c119549bff40.png" class="latex" alt="$d=\frac{10\left(\frac{41a}{36}\right)-3\left(\frac{30a}{9}\right)}{7}=\frac{\frac{205a}{18}-\frac{10a}{3}}{7}=\frac{145a}{126}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="344" height="42" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/6/d161be7f3b840a8b0b797d40b9193c55bc434332.png" class="latex" alt="$e=\frac{5\left(\frac{145a}{126}\right)-2\left(\frac{41a}{36}\right)}{3}=\frac{\frac{725a}{126}-\frac{41a}{18}}{3}=\frac{73a}{63}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="331" height="41" />
</p><p>Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/c/59cfed396a22c040f22cee3d77edfb04e58d9639.png" class="latex" alt="$e=\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="44" height="37" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/3/5b3918aa2e7e86ea58c170f49c0e7a5ca3017b00.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{73a}{63}=\frac{1}{2}\implies a=\boxed{\textbf{(C) }\frac{63}{146}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="230" height="48" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Notice that the probabilities are symmetrical around the fifth lily pad. If the frog is on the fifth lily pad, there is a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> chance that it escapes and a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> chance that it gets eaten. Now, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/e/76ebaba4ae3d5c911d6e31ad855d502ad88d48a6.png" class="latex" alt="$P_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> represent the probability that the frog escapes if it is currently on pad <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />. We get the following system of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> equations:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/2/9c2e4c28a45884b84086cb6eb0f167d4d3e69db5.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[P_1=\frac{9}{10}\cdot P_2\]" width="95" height="38" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/b/2bbb67cd6b1c50a72365ec5e6dde4a98db920cbf.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[P_2=\frac{2}{10}\cdot P_1 + \frac{8}{10}\cdot P_3\]" width="172" height="38" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/5/e65e1fbaa78bf606934653934fefca3a56818f16.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[P_3=\frac{3}{10}\cdot P_2 + \frac{7}{10}\cdot P_4\]" width="173" height="38" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/c/12ce201d1dc74685eaade813834f7619f9e33ac1.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[P_4=\frac{4}{10}\cdot P_3 + \frac{6}{10}\cdot P_5\]" width="172" height="38" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/c/98c1cdb7052a4bf6932e805224a4e3f9b036822b.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[P_5=\frac{5}{10}\]" width="63" height="38" />
We want to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/b/a5b0256a78d58b6d5f7a53400b19e7747d2983fa.png" class="latex" alt="$P_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="17" height="15" />, since the frog starts at pad <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. Solving the above system yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/d/efddbc2a71d6ee78ce7b8c7f301b691431500de3.png" class="latex" alt="$P_1=\frac{63}{146}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="73" height="38" />, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/b/6dbbac62a58811f44fda1df541fe2d2b7f43bc64.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="40" height="29" />.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Assign each lily pad a value, with pad <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> having value <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> and pad <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/d/25dfa5e1205b2ae736cbc053851e5518ca1d554e.png" class="latex" alt="$i &gt; 0$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="39" height="13" /> having value <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/b/24b53aa9144cae6739984cacd5bc03a461883a01.png" class="latex" alt="$\sum_{k=0}^{i-1}\frac{1}{\binom{9}{k}}$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="58" height="56" />. If we treat the process as a random walk <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/7/e57aa074447ecae8a2beedb51b2ff97e8c3c513d.png" class="latex" alt="$X_0, X_1, \cdots$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="87" height="18" /> over these values, note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/6/4967285270d715e70ef7576ce3d2b24791e748e9.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathbb{E}[X_{n+1}] = n$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="100" height="20" /> for all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/c/e2c7d62172af0e738dd12f47f3f8ad8ef70cd65f.png" class="latex" alt="$n \ge 0$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="43" height="15" />. This is purely by construction, as the expected gain at pad <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/9/d1933942c87799c99961e274e092da305e0b158e.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{9}{k}\cdot \frac{10-k}{10} - \binom{9}{k-1}\cdot \frac{k}{10} = 0$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="268" height="55" />. Therefore the process is a martingale. Since the stopping time has finite expectation and the increments are bounded, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optional_stopping_theorem">Optional Stopping Thoerem</a> applies and the expected final outcome is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />. This gives the probability of reaching safety as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/5/be51f9cae7ac181dfbc45d499dce3647603bfe79.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\sum_{k=0}^{9}\frac{1}{\binom{9}{k}}\right)^{-1} = \frac{63}{146}$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="160" height="59" />, which is choice <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/b/6dbbac62a58811f44fda1df541fe2d2b7f43bc64.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="40" height="29" />.
</p>
<h2><span id="Remark_(Markov_Chain)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Remark_.28Markov_Chain.29">Remark (Markov Chain)</span></h2>
<p>We can represent this problem with the following State Transition Digram of <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorbing_Markov_chain">Absorbing Markov Chain</a>. State <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> and state <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> are the absorbing states. This problem asks for the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorbing_Markov_chain#Absorbing_probabilities">Absorbing Probability</a> of state <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/index.php/File:Markov_Chain_Frog.png" class="image"><img alt="Markov Chain Frog.png" src="https://wiki-images.artofproblemsolving.com//thumb/0/03/Markov_Chain_Frog.png/950px-Markov_Chain_Frog.png" width="950" height="108" srcset="https://wiki-images.artofproblemsolving.com//thumb/0/03/Markov_Chain_Frog.png/1425px-Markov_Chain_Frog.png 1.5x, https://wiki-images.artofproblemsolving.com//thumb/0/03/Markov_Chain_Frog.png/1900px-Markov_Chain_Frog.png 2x" /></a></div></div>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/c/76ce4b055db58eaf041c6df81590b7303d886cf8.png" class="latex" alt="$p_{ij} = P(X_{n+1} = j | X_n = i)$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="209" height="20" />, the probability that state <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/8/34857b3ba74ce5cd8607f3ebd23e9015908ada71.png" class="latex" alt="$i$" width="5" height="12" /> transits to state <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/2/8122aa89ea6e80784c6513d22787ad86e36ad0cc.png" class="latex" alt="$j$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="8" height="16" /> on the next step. 
</p><p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/2/412787c048e28774dc63fc27db42dc52ca858de7.png" class="latex" alt="$a_i$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="14" height="10" /> be the absorbing probability of being absorbed in the absorbing state when starting from transient state <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/8/34857b3ba74ce5cd8607f3ebd23e9015908ada71.png" class="latex" alt="$i$" width="5" height="12" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/8/df8f3396927514bb3df77874fb9c6249d4157480.png" class="latex" alt="$a_i = \sum_{j} (p_{ij} \cdot a_{j})$" style="vertical-align: -25px" width="132" height="45" />
