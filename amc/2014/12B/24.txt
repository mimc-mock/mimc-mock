<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> denote the length of a diagonal opposite adjacent sides of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/0/820a20de2847dfdc1bc5cffc8d904933150032bd.png" class="latex" alt="$14$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> for sides <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/0/820a20de2847dfdc1bc5cffc8d904933150032bd.png" class="latex" alt="$14$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> for sides <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. Using Ptolemy's Theorem on the five possible quadrilaterals in the configuration, we obtain:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/6/1962458e45bc6dba8792d87cf96a526ea760b73a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align} c^2 &amp;= 3a+100 \\ c^2 &amp;= 10b+9 \\ ab &amp;= 30+14c \\ ac &amp;= 3c+140\\ bc &amp;= 10c+42 \end{align}" width="293" height="121" />
</p><p>Using equations <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/9/0d9c3b1c142cbd3f8217dabc160c57e4750df241.png" class="latex" alt="$(1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="21" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/f/62f62a19f0425d8e6121ca0c1cfd3ce96e43c9c5.png" class="latex" alt="$(2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="21" height="18" />, we obtain:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/1741a7cb15672aabf8a9e28c5bd6ad4011ac0bff.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[a = \frac{c^2-100}{3}\]" width="100" height="39" />
</p><p>and
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/8/b782ca88db9a5997d8183e2c6b3f53c3fd3648c7.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[b = \frac{c^2-9}{10}\]" width="80" height="39" />
</p><p>Plugging into equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/4/104992efa4deaa9a2dfeeec9b331ad0bf628f80a.png" class="latex" alt="$(4)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="21" height="18" />, we find that:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/f/85f84185bc8b0b957894a51615d721e9a6af412c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} \frac{c^2-100}{3}c &amp;= 3c + 140\\ \frac{c^3-100c}{3} &amp;= 3c + 140\\ c^3-100c &amp;= 9c + 420\\ c^3-109c-420 &amp;=0\\ (c-12)(c+7)(c+5)&amp;=0 \end{align*}" width="257" height="163" />
</p><p>Or similarly into equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/7/737a366a904e00f936f8728869ad8f2587dab46f.png" class="latex" alt="$(5)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="21" height="18" /> to check:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/5/a553be1ff95228fed2fbed507af968a2583d341d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} \frac{c^2-9}{10}c &amp;= 10c+42\\ \frac{c^3-9c}{10} &amp;= 10c + 42\\ c^3-9c &amp;= 100c + 420\\ c^3-109c-420 &amp;=0\\ (c-12)(c+7)(c+5)&amp;=0 \end{align*}" width="275" height="163" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" />, being a length, must be positive, implying that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/9/49986ad1ae5d88da95d611165eb5055d9ffcaa25.png" class="latex" alt="$c=12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="49" height="12" />. In fact, this is reasonable, since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/2/a821b1f9d0c53d73b5472e5488367d6912364387.png" class="latex" alt="$10+3\approx 12$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="91" height="14" /> in the pentagon with apparently obtuse angles. Plugging this back into equations <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/9/0d9c3b1c142cbd3f8217dabc160c57e4750df241.png" class="latex" alt="$(1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="21" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/f/62f62a19f0425d8e6121ca0c1cfd3ce96e43c9c5.png" class="latex" alt="$(2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="21" height="18" /> we find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/8/b585e5c7f5850fdd752d075ff859b25432afbafe.png" class="latex" alt="$a = \frac{44}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="54" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/8/c/18cb545dfde1f5bbeda99b1e0ccde882775506cd.png" class="latex" alt="$b= \frac{135}{10}=\frac{27}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="108" height="38" />.
</p><p>We desire <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/5/475db139d5cf51a72a2f5f3462f4041ad2166a92.png" class="latex" alt="$3c+a+b = 3\cdot 12 + \frac{44}{3} + \frac{27}{2} = \frac{216+88+81}{6}=\frac{385}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="422" height="37" />, so it follows that the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/d/b4de474c629d79eb01852c02dce57d26958f78d9.png" class="latex" alt="$385 + 6 = \fbox{\textbf{(D) }391}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="155" height="28" />
