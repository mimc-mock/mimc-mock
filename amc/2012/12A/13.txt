<p>Paula the painter and her two helpers each paint at constant, but different, rates. They always start at 8:00 AM, and all three always take the same amount of time to eat lunch. On Monday the three of them painted 50% of a house, quitting at 4:00 PM. On Tuesday, when Paula wasn't there, the two helpers painted only 24% of the house and quit at 2:12 PM. On Wednesday Paula worked by herself and finished the house by working until 7:12 P.M. How long, in minutes, was each day's lunch break?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/b/03b17a8afb947ab57b3df2849366ca05f8644053.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 30\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 36\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 42\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 48\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 60$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="394" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Let Paula work at a rate of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/f/36f73fc1312ee0349b3f3a0f3bd9eb5504339011.png" class="latex" alt="$p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" />, the two helpers work at a combined rate of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/8/8189a5b5a0917b8c93350827be4038af1839139d.png" class="latex" alt="$h$" width="10" height="12" />, and the time it takes to eat lunch be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/9/859ccf4cd60c7bc6b8fa1afc9a42dc811a826d6f.png" class="latex" alt="$L$" width="12" height="12" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/f/36f73fc1312ee0349b3f3a0f3bd9eb5504339011.png" class="latex" alt="$p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/8/8189a5b5a0917b8c93350827be4038af1839139d.png" class="latex" alt="$h$" width="10" height="12" /> are in house/hours and L is in hours. Then the labor on Monday, Tuesday, and Wednesday can be represented by the three following equations:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/b/a6b426a40f74b220a311ec1c88a811b4140cbbec.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(8-L)(p+h)=50\]" width="154" height="18" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/0/ca0bb9613fe2541315ddae223bb083c7ce71c150.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(6.2-L)h=24\]" width="123" height="18" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/4/c54ea3b1829b133d3a8c50813b201e554b787f2c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(11.2-L)p=26\]" width="130" height="18" />
</p><p>With three equations and three variables, we need to find the value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/9/859ccf4cd60c7bc6b8fa1afc9a42dc811a826d6f.png" class="latex" alt="$L$" width="12" height="12" />.
Adding the second and third equations together gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/2/5a27c0828e56071639b7e3330c84a39765509e98.png" class="latex" alt="$6.2h+11.2p-L(p+h)=50$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="229" height="18" />. Subtracting the first equation from this new one gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/3/273ddf25bf300d6a5d5770b741a06d4515d7d0a4.png" class="latex" alt="$-1.8h+3.2p=0$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="135" height="16" />, so we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/b/4/7b46db858d3594487f1eab4977c7c86e65dd5a33.png" class="latex" alt="$h=\frac{16}{9}p$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="66" height="37" />. 
Plugging into the second equation:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/3/0432514c9d8a2a0ebb087ffbae80de16d08ff8c1.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(6.2-L)\frac{16}{9}p=24\]" width="144" height="37" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/3/e4345c90982e72d78f0fb4e7b05f92af7c323bfc.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(6.2-L)p=\frac{27}{2}\]" width="124" height="37" />
</p><p>We can then subtract this from the third equation:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/6/8d69c9bb94b0f7f1f858955aa67404de7277fe40.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[5p=26-\frac{27}{2}\]" width="103" height="37" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/5/595d0f14b12bcd573f3b83d5026c5599072df59d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[p=\frac{5}{2}\]" width="46" height="37" />
Plugging <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/f/36f73fc1312ee0349b3f3a0f3bd9eb5504339011.png" class="latex" alt="$p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" /> into our third equation gives: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/5/b75a1c103aa62e4c825d52dcb8bcdd4351285201.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[L=\frac{4}{5}\]" width="47" height="37" />
</p><p>Converting <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/9/859ccf4cd60c7bc6b8fa1afc9a42dc811a826d6f.png" class="latex" alt="$L$" width="12" height="12" /> from hours to minutes gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/b/0bb8848314a86794818e72e24d1f610ec3183bfa.png" class="latex" alt="$L=48$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="54" height="13" /> minutes, which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/1/a310bc21c1c148d5b7b856f20b9f1b444b222987.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D)}\ 48}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="65" height="28" />.
</p>
