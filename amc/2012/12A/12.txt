<p>A square region <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> is externally tangent to the circle with equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/d/f1dd4184b561ea747be652fc3dee7fc5e1c5103f.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2+y^2=1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="89" height="18" /> at the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/1/be11a56a55425d6c6b69f70f5fa98eab95e8ad18.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> on the side <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/4/0948661e822f2a953c43a57ac9e40b2734476de4.png" class="latex" alt="$CD$" width="29" height="12" />.  Vertices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> are on the circle with equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/8/7a8b84ca3393ce6840ab42fe8edc44524e3c67e0.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2+y^2=4$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="90" height="18" />.  What is the side length of this square?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/5/4b588cafc447cba8405c7cdcaa13dd371f868a08.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{\sqrt{10}+5}{10}\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{2\sqrt{5}}{5}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{2\sqrt{19}-4}{5}\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \frac{9-\sqrt{17}}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="591" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/b/17b54bd31c2906599eee882f7b6902279c53365e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(15mm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt)+fontsize(10pt)); dotfactor=4;  real a=1; real b=2; pair O=(0,0); pair A=(-(sqrt(19)-2)/5,1); pair B=((sqrt(19)-2)/5,1); pair C=((sqrt(19)-2)/5,1+2(sqrt(19)-2)/5); pair D=(-(sqrt(19)-2)/5,1+2(sqrt(19)-2)/5); pair E=(-(sqrt(19)-2)/5,0); path inner=Circle(O,a); path outer=Circle(O,b); draw(outer); draw(inner); draw(A--B--C--D--cycle);  draw(O--D--E--cycle);  label(&quot;$A$&quot;,D,NW); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,E,SW); label(&quot;$O$&quot;,O,SE); label(&quot;$s+1$&quot;,(D--E),W); label(&quot;$\frac{s}{2}$&quot;,(E--O),S);  pair[] ps={A,B,C,D,E,O}; dot(ps); [/asy]" width="285" height="298" /></center>
<p>The circles have radii of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />. Draw the triangle shown in the figure above and write expressions in terms of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" /> (length of the side of the square) for the sides of the triangle. Because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c53cc93aa43cc55bea0dc2511833fe6bdda020f.png" class="latex" alt="$AO$" width="27" height="13" /> is the radius of the larger circle, which is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, we can write the Pythagorean Theorem.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/2/dd292321886aabfb5f25a00608daf9cdbbf10c26.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} \left( \frac{s}{2} \right) ^2 + (s+1)^2 &amp;= 2^2\\ \frac14 s^2 + s^2 + 2s + 1 &amp;= 4\\ \frac54 s^2 +2s - 3 &amp;= 0\\ 5s^2 + 8s - 12 &amp;=0 \end{align*}" width="175" height="146" />
</p><p>Use the quadratic formula.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/d/7ad6fcfea1fb8fd86a81479e331c734c0c97f65e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[s = \frac{-8+\sqrt{8^2-4(5)(-12)}}{10} = \frac{-8+\sqrt{304}}{10} = \frac{-8+4\sqrt{19}}{10} = \boxed{\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{2\sqrt{19}-4}{5}}\]" width="598" height="53" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Using the diagram above, we look at the top-right vertex of the square. Let us call this point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/3/e53b1fe25be1c679117fb44a6a886fe1247d189a.png" class="latex" alt="$(x,y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="40" height="18" />. Then, we that since the square is symmetrical over the y-axis, that the y value is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/f/8/6f8fc82e121c01034fef87ac28f73e7d4c0b465c.png" class="latex" alt="$2x+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="49" height="13" />, since we can multiply the x value(which is half of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" />) by two to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" />, and we add one since the square lies one unit above the origin. Now, all we must do is find the intersection of the larger circle, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/2/b02ae718756895bc1924d5290f0abd320bfa77ce.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2 + y^2 = 4$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="90" height="18" />, and the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/c/28cd6af3919e1c83ef4d64def64db549f5b37a01.png" class="latex" alt="$y=2x+1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="83" height="15" />. Substituting the second equation into the first, we get:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/f/40fb4749aaaae9ab63da7e6c64e54d88b6bc61c3.png" class="latex" alt="$5x^2 +4x -3 = 0$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="132" height="16" />
</p><p>Using the quadratic formula, we arrive with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/d/badfef1a2ae039582c470309eb782137d71c3b5b.png" class="latex" alt="$x=\frac{-4 \pm 2\sqrt{19}}{10}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="125" height="41" />. However, recall that the x value is only one half of the side length. Multiplying this value by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, then, and using only the positive root(since the top right vertex of the square has a positive x value), we get:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/b/19b25fa0a37b5f3aec6ffead763ee999a715b30d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{-4 + 2\sqrt{19}}{5} \Rightarrow \boxed{\textbf{(D)}}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="161" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Like the previous solutions, we can say that the square is symmetrical with respect to the y-axis.
</p>
<pre>Since CD passes through point (0,1), let C be (-x,1) and D be (x,1)
</pre>
<p>Point B is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/c/75c715ca3c7dc75557b0dd687d9026605666fc08.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(-x, \sqrt{4-x^2}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="115" height="32" /> and Point A is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/b/40b8b93add32c7cc8edce67889dcd7ce358d8e54.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(x,\sqrt{4-x^2}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="101" height="32" />. Now, we see that AB = 2x and BC = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/1/1b1092069a120f9478691f300e23c2f6acd16d21.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{4-x^2}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="64" height="18" />-1
</p><p>Setting those two equal, we get the same equation as the previous solutions: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/f/40fb4749aaaae9ab63da7e6c64e54d88b6bc61c3.png" class="latex" alt="$5x^2 +4x -3 = 0$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="132" height="16" /> and then solve the same way.
</p><p><br />
-Conantwiz2023
</p>
