<p>Distinct planes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/c/cacabfb69df0918fa06a56bd74694eb3b2bc9c70.png" class="latex" alt="$p_1,p_2,....,p_k$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="94" height="11" /> intersect the interior of a cube <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> be the union of the faces of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> and let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/8/6c87c0f0b8fc886ced9d7a999f4a76a9a2cd97cb.png" class="latex" alt="$P =\bigcup_{j=1}^{k}p_{j}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="79" height="53" />. The intersection of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> consists of the union of all segments joining the midpoints of every pair of edges belonging to the same face of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />. What is the difference between the maximum and minimum possible values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/8/168a44de56bbd22048dd76f1e5ab81bb979e3266.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 8\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 12\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 20\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 23\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 24$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="385" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/8/a28fc042669509f7c5de167812e8614ebaa1ccac.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pair A=(0,0), B=(2,0), C=(2,2), D=(0,2), E=(-1,3), F=(1,3), G=(1,1), H=(-1,1), I=(1,0), J=(2,1), K=(1,2), L=(0,1), M=(-0.5,0.5), N=(-1,2), O=(-0.5,2.5), P=(0,3), Q=(1.5,2.5), R=(1,2), S=(1.5,0.5), T=(0,1); draw(A--B--C--D--E--F); draw(H--A); draw(A--D); draw(H--E); draw(F--C); draw(H--G--F); draw(G--B); label(&quot;\(A\)&quot;,A,SW); label(&quot;\(B\)&quot;,B,SE); label(&quot;\(C\)&quot;,C,NE); label(&quot;\(D\)&quot;,D,SW); label(&quot;\(E\)&quot;,E,NW); label(&quot;\(F\)&quot;,F,NE); label(&quot;\(G\)&quot;,G,NE); label(&quot;\(H\)&quot;,H,SW); [/asy]" width="252" height="245" /></center>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/8/55848a80936c5f32b9dc873adb469a29ccd43034.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pair A=(0,0), B=(2,0), C=(2,2), D=(0,2), E=(-1,3), F=(1,3), G=(1,1), H=(-1,1), I=(1,0), J=(2,1), K=(1,2), L=(0,1), M=(-0.5,0.5), N=(-1,2), O=(-0.5,2.5), P=(0,3), Q=(1.5,2.5), R=(1,2), S=(1.5,0.5), T=(0,1); draw(A--B--C--D--E--F); draw(H--A); draw(A--D); draw(H--E); draw(F--C); draw(I--J--K--L--I); draw(I--K); draw(J--L); draw(K--Q--P--O--K); draw(K--P); draw(Q--O); draw(O--L--M--N--O); draw(O--M); draw(L--N); label(&quot;\(A\)&quot;,A,SW); label(&quot;\(B\)&quot;,B,SE); label(&quot;\(C\)&quot;,C,NE); label(&quot;\(D\)&quot;,D,SW); label(&quot;\(E\)&quot;,E,NW); label(&quot;\(F\)&quot;,F,NE); label(&quot;\(H\)&quot;,H,SW); [/asy]" width="252" height="245" /></center>
<p><br />
We need two different kinds of planes that only intersect <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> at the mentioned segments (we call them <i>traces</i> in this solution). These will be all the possible <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/a/f0a9cc9827a453a7403b1e5b13bf66527bdbf95b.png" class="latex" alt="$p_j$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="15" height="12" />'s.
</p><p>First, there are two kinds of segments joining the midpoints of every pair of edges belonging to the same face of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />: <i>long traces</i> are those connecting the midpoint of opposite sides of the same face of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />, and <i>short traces</i> are those connecting the midpoint of adjacent sides of the same face of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />.
</p><p>Suppose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/a/f0a9cc9827a453a7403b1e5b13bf66527bdbf95b.png" class="latex" alt="$p_j$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="15" height="12" /> contains a short trace <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/9/8b91b7245b0dd42b4f5585b97e323a8cfaa064a8.png" class="latex" alt="$t_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="14" /> of a face of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />. Then it must also contain some trace <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/0/a40dccc3bb61d55c03b7dafe15707eeef43885ab.png" class="latex" alt="$t_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="14" /> of an adjacent face of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/0/a40dccc3bb61d55c03b7dafe15707eeef43885ab.png" class="latex" alt="$t_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="14" /> share a common endpoint with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/9/8b91b7245b0dd42b4f5585b97e323a8cfaa064a8.png" class="latex" alt="$t_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="14" />. So, there are three possibilities for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/0/a40dccc3bb61d55c03b7dafe15707eeef43885ab.png" class="latex" alt="$t_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="14" />, each of which determines a plane <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/a/f0a9cc9827a453a7403b1e5b13bf66527bdbf95b.png" class="latex" alt="$p_j$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="15" height="12" /> containing both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/9/8b91b7245b0dd42b4f5585b97e323a8cfaa064a8.png" class="latex" alt="$t_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/0/a40dccc3bb61d55c03b7dafe15707eeef43885ab.png" class="latex" alt="$t_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="14" />.
</p><p><b>Case 1:</b> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/0/a40dccc3bb61d55c03b7dafe15707eeef43885ab.png" class="latex" alt="$t_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="14" /> makes an acute angle with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/9/8b91b7245b0dd42b4f5585b97e323a8cfaa064a8.png" class="latex" alt="$t_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="14" />. In this case, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/2/252693fea0f22ea5654679e7ee9129c6604a7ccf.png" class="latex" alt="$p_j \cap Q$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="51" height="17" /> is an equilateral triangle made by three short traces. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> of them, corresponding to the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> vertices.
</p><p><b>Case 2:</b> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/0/a40dccc3bb61d55c03b7dafe15707eeef43885ab.png" class="latex" alt="$t_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="14" /> is a long trace. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/4/284078eacb62d7373f69dbc2cb1958cd8a4aea10.png" class="latex" alt="$p \cap Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="16" /> is a rectangle. Each pair of parallel faces of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> contributes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> of these rectangles so there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> such rectangles.
</p><p><b>Case 3:</b> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/0/a40dccc3bb61d55c03b7dafe15707eeef43885ab.png" class="latex" alt="$t_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="14" /> is the short trace other than the one described in case 1, i.e. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/0/a40dccc3bb61d55c03b7dafe15707eeef43885ab.png" class="latex" alt="$t_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="14" /> makes an obtuse angle with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/9/8b91b7245b0dd42b4f5585b97e323a8cfaa064a8.png" class="latex" alt="$t_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="14" />. It is possible to prove that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/4/284078eacb62d7373f69dbc2cb1958cd8a4aea10.png" class="latex" alt="$p \cap Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="16" /> is a regular hexagon (See note #1 for a proof) and there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> of them.
</p><p><b>Case 4:</b> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/a/f0a9cc9827a453a7403b1e5b13bf66527bdbf95b.png" class="latex" alt="$p_j$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="15" height="12" /> contains no short traces. This can only make <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/2/252693fea0f22ea5654679e7ee9129c6604a7ccf.png" class="latex" alt="$p_j \cap Q$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="51" height="17" /> be a square enclosed by long traces.  There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> such squares.
</p><p>In total, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/5/f559c6d1271b94509442a6fe8cfdec6396fca382.png" class="latex" alt="$8+12+4+3=27$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="154" height="14" /> possible planes in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />. So the maximum of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/5/1051e7d41ec0b8bd688a872be17538413f4ee97b.png" class="latex" alt="$27$" width="18" height="12" />.
</p><p>On the other hand, the most economic way to generate these long and short traces is to take all the planes in case 3 and case 4. Overall, they intersect at each trace exactly once (there is a quick way to prove this. See note #2 below.) and also covered all the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/3/64301627985062dca9e305c72ad1279e1ed2e848.png" class="latex" alt="$6\times 4 + 4\times 3 = 36$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="145" height="14" /> traces. So the minimum of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" />. The answer to this problem is then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/e/4fe442cd39f0faba5002273bfc64a33459b3b5a2.png" class="latex" alt="$27-7=20$" width="91" height="12" /> ... <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/8/0b8c89e855982fd470ad44d61fdbd31f82c4b129.png" class="latex" alt="$\framebox{C}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="23" height="23" />.
</p>
<hr />
<p><b>Note 1</b>: Indeed, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/7/7d76c65c272c106fc6560adf58af045c60458078.png" class="latex" alt="$t_1=AB$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="66" height="15" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/e/ede703b1b14b84fecb9b82b9ac0435d4b36d8217.png" class="latex" alt="$B=t_1\cap t_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="85" height="15" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> be the other endpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/0/a40dccc3bb61d55c03b7dafe15707eeef43885ab.png" class="latex" alt="$t_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="14" /> that is not <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />. Draw a line through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> parallel to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/9/8b91b7245b0dd42b4f5585b97e323a8cfaa064a8.png" class="latex" alt="$t_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="14" />. This line passes through the center <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" /> of the cube and therefore we see that the reflection of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/8/a9874ea56cf8fec1bd246793224fbcfaad17ab3f.png" class="latex" alt="$A,B,C$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="58" height="16" />, denoted by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/1/ee149d65f55388735f7a78e7410c83ac70dfe67f.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;, B&#39;, C&#39;$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="71" height="17" />, respectively, lie on the same plane containing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/8/a9874ea56cf8fec1bd246793224fbcfaad17ab3f.png" class="latex" alt="$A,B,C$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="58" height="16" />. Thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/2/252693fea0f22ea5654679e7ee9129c6604a7ccf.png" class="latex" alt="$p_j \cap Q$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="51" height="17" /> is the regular hexagon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/6/af62869c71294325f0119f4e216c095ef5c03809.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCA&#39;B&#39;C&#39;$" width="98" height="14" />. To count the number of these hexagons, just notice that each short trace uniquely determine a hexagon (by drawing the plane through this trace and the center), and that each face has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> short traces. Therefore, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> such hexagons.
</p><p><b>Note 2</b>: The quick way to prove the fact that none of the planes described in case 3 and case 4 share the same trace is as follows: each of these plane contains the center and therefore the intersection of each pair of them is a line through the center, which obviously does not contain any traces.
</p>
