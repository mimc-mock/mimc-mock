<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> be the square one of whose diagonals has endpoints <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/2/862f201e958bfe26b708b25373fb46a0283c2a3e.png" class="latex" alt="$(1/10,7/10)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="93" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/4/534c45871d2d68ae93701b5f41d898adc5cb735b.png" class="latex" alt="$(-1/10,-7/10)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="121" height="18" />.  A point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/8/dd8c38453536d86b03e53eabc11c1048842a1ae4.png" class="latex" alt="$v=(x,y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="74" height="18" /> is chosen uniformly at random over all pairs of real numbers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/f/b3f583d5e3c39eadeb41d038a9dd9a1ab13cd843.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \le x \le 2012$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="103" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/3/dd33be2d2f455ae0913a0693aeb7a86ac811287b.png" class="latex" alt="$0\le y\le 2012$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="102" height="16" />.  Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/9/6d921d804d4021a8dc7930098b1c814d46143c77.png" class="latex" alt="$T(v)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="35" height="18" /> be a translated copy of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> centered at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/f/a9f23bf124b6b2b2a993eb313c72e678664ac74a.png" class="latex" alt="$v$" width="9" height="8" />.  What is the probability that the square region determined by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/9/6d921d804d4021a8dc7930098b1c814d46143c77.png" class="latex" alt="$T(v)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="35" height="18" /> contains exactly two points with integer coefficients in its interior?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/8/a288fb57379523d2b94bdd0107ae63cc1930ebf5.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{1}{8}\qquad\textbf{(B) }\frac{7}{50}\qquad\textbf{(C) }\frac{4}{25}\qquad\textbf{(D) }\frac{1}{4}\qquad\textbf{(E) }\frac{8}{25}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="398" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/f/f6f0e5566a32958540f7896563dc7761f10e4078.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pair A=(0.1,0.7), C=(-0.1,-0.7), B=(-0.7,0.1), D=(0.7,-0.1), X=(1,0), W=(-1,0), Y=(0,1), Z=(0,-1); draw (A--B--C--D--A); draw(A--C); draw(B--D); draw(W--X); draw(Y--Z); label(&quot;\((0.1,0.7)\)&quot;,A,NE); label(&quot;\((-0.1,-0.7)\)&quot;,C,SW); label(&quot;\(x\)&quot;,X,NW); label(&quot;\(y\)&quot;,Y,NE); [/asy]" width="242" height="252" /></center>
<p>The unit square's diagonal has a length of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/1/451a14f3bda6c95313d5010c95a7faedc2513d15.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{0.2^2 + 1.4^2} = \sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="147" height="19" />. Because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> square is not parallel to the axis, the two points must be adjacent.
</p><p>Now consider the unit square <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/b/e2bbebb3bd73f1ae5c64098ab0244f739abf7ca4.png" class="latex" alt="$U$" width="14" height="12" /> with vertices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/2/232822edbaeb07a6a3b98beb9204331f701874e2.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0), (1,0), (1,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="135" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/1/be11a56a55425d6c6b69f70f5fa98eab95e8ad18.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />. Let us first consider only two vertices, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/6/f9603ca3089464e548fc6f1366bc474e7efef8d9.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/a/25a306093bbd9e0699c65dba033c9483cd379dc4.png" class="latex" alt="$(1,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />. We want to find the area of the region within <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/b/e2bbebb3bd73f1ae5c64098ab0244f739abf7ca4.png" class="latex" alt="$U$" width="14" height="12" /> that the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/8/dd8c38453536d86b03e53eabc11c1048842a1ae4.png" class="latex" alt="$v=(x,y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="74" height="18" /> will create the translation of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/9/6d921d804d4021a8dc7930098b1c814d46143c77.png" class="latex" alt="$T(v)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="35" height="18" /> such that it covers both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/6/f9603ca3089464e548fc6f1366bc474e7efef8d9.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/a/25a306093bbd9e0699c65dba033c9483cd379dc4.png" class="latex" alt="$(1,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />. By symmetry, there will be three equal regions that cover the other pairs of adjacent vertices.
</p><p>For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/9/6d921d804d4021a8dc7930098b1c814d46143c77.png" class="latex" alt="$T(v)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="35" height="18" /> to contain the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/6/f9603ca3089464e548fc6f1366bc474e7efef8d9.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/f/a9f23bf124b6b2b2a993eb313c72e678664ac74a.png" class="latex" alt="$v$" width="9" height="8" /> must be inside square <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" />. Similarly, for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/9/6d921d804d4021a8dc7930098b1c814d46143c77.png" class="latex" alt="$T(v)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="35" height="18" /> to contain the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/a/25a306093bbd9e0699c65dba033c9483cd379dc4.png" class="latex" alt="$(1,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/f/a9f23bf124b6b2b2a993eb313c72e678664ac74a.png" class="latex" alt="$v$" width="9" height="8" /> must be inside a translated square <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> with center at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/a/25a306093bbd9e0699c65dba033c9483cd379dc4.png" class="latex" alt="$(1,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />, which we will call <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/3/bf344aa376979a8a81e8784f92fcb2381897ba65.png" class="latex" alt="$S&#39;$" width="15" height="14" />. Therefore, the area we seek is Area<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/e/8ceb8c713fd736cf3d29bb85ea7ced2dcc586d51.png" class="latex" alt="$(U \cap S \cap S&#39;)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="96" height="18" />.
</p><p>To calculate the area, we notice that Area<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/1/b91d8f02702e993c3989fa84ac78eb98204b93f6.png" class="latex" alt="$(U \cap S \cap S&#39;) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="139" height="37" /> Area<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/7/0a72d43ea62aa5a9b5adb3682971b0b025d362e4.png" class="latex" alt="$(S \cap S&#39;)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="62" height="18" /> by symmetry. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/e/14e3ffa8b16d443e8a0e0d23a5ffcc5bed072051.png" class="latex" alt="$S_1 = (0.1, 0.7), S_2 = (0.7, -0.1), S&#39;_1 = (1.1, 0.7), S&#39;_2 = (0.3, 0.1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="482" height="18" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/4/904ce32b8bf39c33f4bac46b6b7632ce18019469.png" class="latex" alt="$M = (0.7, 0.4)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="110" height="18" /> be the midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/e/88e5841d024a0d6c019780c5b632017ff20a7856.png" class="latex" alt="$S&#39;_1S&#39;_2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="35" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/b/6cbfb17351ed9d743ee7e4cefdff0ae19f10b062.png" class="latex" alt="$N = (0.7, 0.7)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="107" height="18" /> along the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/d/dedb482b99d6285a386052c6ed1adc1036685e76.png" class="latex" alt="$S_1S&#39;_1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="35" height="18" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/7/027f4a11d6090f9eac0ce2488df6384dad1263ea.png" class="latex" alt="$I$" width="9" height="12" /> be the intersection of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/3/bf344aa376979a8a81e8784f92fcb2381897ba65.png" class="latex" alt="$S&#39;$" width="15" height="14" /> within <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/b/e2bbebb3bd73f1ae5c64098ab0244f739abf7ca4.png" class="latex" alt="$U$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/b/abb5588023cfa1ac14643e9778699f03eecc57a3.png" class="latex" alt="$J$" width="12" height="12" /> be the intersection of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/3/bf344aa376979a8a81e8784f92fcb2381897ba65.png" class="latex" alt="$S&#39;$" width="15" height="14" /> outside <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/b/e2bbebb3bd73f1ae5c64098ab0244f739abf7ca4.png" class="latex" alt="$U$" width="14" height="12" />. Therefore, the area we seek is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/0/68001781d7eba567264d2f1f1fab55fed97988fa.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2} \cdot$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="16" height="37" /> Area<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/7/b270ea768e9f37d7e8beed6026eb9c14693d15ef.png" class="latex" alt="$(S \cap S&#39;) = \frac{1}{2} [IS&#39;_2JS_2]$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="167" height="37" />. Because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/0/dc08174b95d0904c6093ab0962101d4c8d287c85.png" class="latex" alt="$S_2, M, N$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="70" height="16" /> all have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> coordinate <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/5/a3588bb395f693f5fd014687a9b680837fd2dc6f.png" class="latex" alt="$0.7$" width="23" height="12" />, they are collinear. Noting that the side length of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/3/bf344aa376979a8a81e8784f92fcb2381897ba65.png" class="latex" alt="$S&#39;$" width="15" height="14" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> (as shown above), we also see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/9/fb9b0f3d196e057bd459f730be8d8acae4832359.png" class="latex" alt="$S_2M = MS&#39;_1 = 0.5$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="147" height="18" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/2/e12e578f1cf3c6a10b5c0e6e618a900027e77309.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{S&#39;_1NM} \cong \triangle{S_2IM}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="158" height="18" />. If follows that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/a/34a9aee7dce2596a7edc163dfc3bbf61f1de3cb3.png" class="latex" alt="$IS_2 = NS&#39;_1 = 1.1 - 0.7 = 0.4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="227" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/6/e26953162c997ceea64a987416a1ee93dcb6a218.png" class="latex" alt="$IS&#39;_2 = MS&#39;_2 - MI = MS&#39;_2 - MN = 0.5 - 0.3 = 0.2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="402" height="18" />. Therefore, the area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/0/68001781d7eba567264d2f1f1fab55fed97988fa.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2} \cdot$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="16" height="37" /> Area<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/f/a7ff9ff58cd7bae663d0d4221a9beb0f70610de1.png" class="latex" alt="$(S \cap S&#39;) = \frac{1}{2} [IS&#39;_2JS_2] = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 0.2 \cdot 0.4 = 0.04$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="335" height="37" />.
</p><p>Because there are three other regions in the unit square <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/b/e2bbebb3bd73f1ae5c64098ab0244f739abf7ca4.png" class="latex" alt="$U$" width="14" height="12" /> that we need to count, the total area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/f/a9f23bf124b6b2b2a993eb313c72e678664ac74a.png" class="latex" alt="$v$" width="9" height="8" /> within <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/b/e2bbebb3bd73f1ae5c64098ab0244f739abf7ca4.png" class="latex" alt="$U$" width="14" height="12" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/9/6d921d804d4021a8dc7930098b1c814d46143c77.png" class="latex" alt="$T(v)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="35" height="18" /> contains two adjacent lattice points is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/b/7cba831f2620ebfa1a19664a5e76a6a74d28c1cc.png" class="latex" alt="$0.04 \cdot 4 = 0.16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="110" height="13" />.
</p><p>By periodicity, this probability is the same for all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/f/b3f583d5e3c39eadeb41d038a9dd9a1ab13cd843.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \le x \le 2012$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="103" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/2/802e55c55867cc0bc6543ed44b9b9ae77abc7474.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \le y \le 2012$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="102" height="16" />. Therefore, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/1/941e318998397ee8371bd442e0298bfcf0fa0a3d.png" class="latex" alt="$0.16 = \boxed{\frac{4}{25} \textbf{(C)} }$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="119" height="48" />
</p>
