<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/3/903aad7ecd1bf5e4dd8c556473d5894c4c166e3e.png" class="latex" alt="$S=\{(x,y)&#160;: x\in \{0,1,2,3,4\}, y\in \{0,1,2,3,4,5\},\text{ and } (x,y)\ne (0,0)\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="545" height="18" />. 
Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" /> be the set of all right triangles whose vertices are in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" />. For every right triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/f/9bfaec0c14fc8da404cce61af690664f738551a3.png" class="latex" alt="$t=\triangle{ABC}$" width="89" height="13" /> with vertices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> in counter-clockwise order and right angle at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/e/3def3d21ca4449b1990e61e0163aea58bf358f74.png" class="latex" alt="$f(t)=\tan(\angle{CBA})$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="150" height="18" />. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/7/557dedb14b9ddd5d2ef6b6c8ff8260739c362351.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\prod_{t\in T} f(t)?\]" width="66" height="38" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/7/a479cb3ed3c6f87f54058d88565b9a4a9527e3df.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 1\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{625}{144}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{125}{24}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 6\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \frac{625}{24}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="414" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Consider reflections. For any right triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> with the right labeling described in the problem, any reflection <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/2/022948689ac03ea1705a6af78b86d32ae2a369eb.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;$" width="55" height="14" /> labeled that way will give us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/3/113d94a32df5c3a9e31ce38df659aec6191ae6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$\tan CBA \cdot \tan C&#39;B&#39;A&#39; = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="203" height="14" />. First we consider the reflection about the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/f/9cf93643d66b00e6ac489e35ecf1e29c83df230a.png" class="latex" alt="$y=2.5$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="56" height="16" />. Only those triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/4/0b45e24fd5bdbdcd91ad8e228df8967475cc09f1.png" class="latex" alt="$\subseteq T$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="32" height="15" /> that have one vertex at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/e/11e8da5448a5f378d5b8c253cdcd3cb49a90fa49.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,5)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> do not reflect to a traingle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/4/0b45e24fd5bdbdcd91ad8e228df8967475cc09f1.png" class="latex" alt="$\subseteq T$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="32" height="15" />. Within those triangles, consider a reflection about the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/8/e68741d15d63d34c3dc3d88a9d003e855a4b9e37.png" class="latex" alt="$y=5-x$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="75" height="16" />. Then only those triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/4/0b45e24fd5bdbdcd91ad8e228df8967475cc09f1.png" class="latex" alt="$\subseteq T$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="32" height="15" /> that have one vertex on the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/8/ec89f09c84538ec43930ca5d8e5e0957e2000822.png" class="latex" alt="$y=0$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="16" /> do not reflect to a triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/4/0b45e24fd5bdbdcd91ad8e228df8967475cc09f1.png" class="latex" alt="$\subseteq T$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="32" height="15" />. So we only need to look at right triangles that have vertices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/5/fe51e8b05cc9e9271ba8e86d53be1748da1dcf87.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,5), (*,0), (*,*)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="135" height="18" />. There are three cases:
</p><p>Case 1: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/b/66b7b001d61d5240d7a8eda64b20ffb77f786c6a.png" class="latex" alt="$A=(0,5)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="76" height="18" />. Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/c/d5cc7b65a744ad8d9e95e9e2cbb37aabcb3d2123.png" class="latex" alt="$B=(*,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="77" height="18" /> is impossible.
</p><p>Case 2: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/0/710cb6c49a1daf824059dce86e0f3054333f9ead.png" class="latex" alt="$B=(0,5)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="77" height="18" />. Then we look for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/8/f18cd7ca4a29cd012ac0aca9bd7372d22f7d605f.png" class="latex" alt="$A=(x,y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="78" height="18" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/9/5c99c9788acceb02c2c365409a0b2bd56c56fa98.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BAC=90^{\circ}$" width="103" height="13" /> and that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/7/477d200cca685f711f0917eadba419647fd2fbaf.png" class="latex" alt="$C=(*,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="77" height="18" />. They are: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/a/6da95ba604023aeaf255e1c83ea0d1d36f9e40a2.png" class="latex" alt="$(A=(x,5), C=(x,0))$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="180" height="18" />,  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/e/36e228824bc99381fc94f96c53a67093787fdf79.png" class="latex" alt="$(A=(3,2), C=(1,0))$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="177" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/0/9a0afee355b6f23cea14a4133590ff83e6d5e8d7.png" class="latex" alt="$(A=(4,1), C=(3,0))$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="177" height="18" />. The product of their values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/e/f6e891094852435c420c723456e2a0df6039ef75.png" class="latex" alt="$\tan \angle CBA$" width="84" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/9/c89d776eb2b038adc1a932e1e816826102c6c35b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5}{1}\cdot  \frac{5}{2} \cdot \frac{5}{3} \cdot \frac{5}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{2}{3} = \frac{625}{144}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="198" height="38" />.
</p><p>Case 3: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/f/50fc1e719c987e5c4c16f8565b44c6082e9710d5.png" class="latex" alt="$C=(0,5)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="77" height="18" />. Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/6/c8637e153edf6bb84fca1a9aded9f78614653dd9.png" class="latex" alt="$A=(*,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="76" height="18" /> is impossible.
</p><p>Therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/1/2d1b88fa82dc8dda85d0b46f697ba10a4081395f.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B)}  \ \frac{625}{144}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="77" height="48" /> is the answer.
</p><p><br />
</p>
