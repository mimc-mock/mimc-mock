<p>Let points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/3/8d3b562c2718f735ae9d455f8b1280a128824fc2.png" class="latex" alt="$(0 ,0 ,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="56" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/3/40351a54433aa452afdfc6195970641d012c2158.png" class="latex" alt="$(1, 0, 0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="56" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/0/8b00149795d04641995639bade885a65bff6cb2e.png" class="latex" alt="$(0, 2, 0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="56" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/c/49cffcb8ee93e317c6afd392be3a832c3b970a18.png" class="latex" alt="$(0, 0, 3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="56" height="18" />. Points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e28ce12d49d39f160d5a0ef54077fc98e4b9d2b.png" class="latex" alt="$G$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/9/b1902d279ba37d60bdce4e0e987b7cd19d48974e.png" class="latex" alt="$H$" width="16" height="12" /> are midpoints of line segments <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/c/0cc3986abcac5506776beeb9e8009c12069b2646.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BD},\text{ }  \overline{AB}, \text{ } \overline {AC},$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="118" height="19" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/b/63b7e5ec2db31e9aded4bb09881b42d1c439fce9.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{DC}$" width="30" height="15" /> respectively. What is the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/3/9735b37ac43861ef9e105bff1711f117917b09ec.png" class="latex" alt="$EFGH$" width="59" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/c/1bc135a8bdfcfba0945a9711466eb37b9c7b9bbc.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \sqrt{2}\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{2\sqrt{5}}{3}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{3\sqrt{5}}{4}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ \sqrt{3}\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \frac{2\sqrt{7}}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="462" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Consider a tetrahedron with vertices at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/5/8859dbfb7a9ec734e16f2a88fb5cf1104eb6a39b.png" class="latex" alt="$A,B,C,D$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="81" height="16" /> on the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/7/d57d8fef9329fabd73078ae824a034e8a00e332f.png" class="latex" alt="$xyz$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="28" height="11" />-plane. The length of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/2/c128243ea8125d0bb007b8132dcfa44461df73a7.png" class="latex" alt="$EF$" width="28" height="12" /> is just one-half of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc56862b1fdb71204894da278e42ef75de73a40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AD$" width="28" height="13" /> because it is the midsegment of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/5/94585e5639fd985bbe71e9039a8681af8e09d680.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD.$" width="63" height="13" /> The same concept applies to the other side lengths. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/8/6e867467073fae9550c977a21ee2cb19426c4f92.png" class="latex" alt="$AD=3$" width="62" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/e/69ea4bf2b461449a56d9291685e14b2240a423cb.png" class="latex" alt="$BC=\sqrt{1^2+2^2}=\sqrt{5}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="172" height="19" />. Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/1/7a1166ed355a00212e8fecdea367ea576ebfc1c0.png" class="latex" alt="$EF=HG=\frac32$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="119" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/1/d51ed7a7f9bfc1381437c2a66b2faa21b74ed317.png" class="latex" alt="$EH=FG=\frac{\sqrt{5}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="134" height="41" />. The line segments lie on perpendicular planes so quadrilateral <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/3/9735b37ac43861ef9e105bff1711f117917b09ec.png" class="latex" alt="$EFGH$" width="59" height="12" /> is a rectangle. The area is
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/c/0ac93aa0c3d28811407d96e8211f079322b5d869.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[EF \cdot FG = \frac 32 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{5}}{2} = \frac{3\sqrt 5} 4\implies \boxed{\textbf C}.\]" width="288" height="41" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/3/e93d5efaf2b0c198ac89b154f643a4524951d4ed.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] import three; draw((0,0,0)--(1,0,0)--(0,0,3)--cycle); draw((0,0,0)--(0,2,0)); draw((0,2,0)--(0,0,3)); //EFGH draw((0.5,0,1.5)--(0.5,0,0)--(0,1,0)--(0,1,1.5)--(0.5,0,1.5),red); //Points label(&quot;$E$&quot;,(0.5,0,1.5),NW); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,(0.5,0,0),S); label(&quot;$G$&quot;,(0,1,0),S); label(&quot;$H$&quot;,(0,1,1.5),NE); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,(0,0,0),NE); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,(1,0,0),S); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,(0,2,0),S); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,(0,0,3),N); [/asy]" width="155" height="242" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Computing the points of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/3/9735b37ac43861ef9e105bff1711f117917b09ec.png" class="latex" alt="$EFGH$" width="59" height="12" /> gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/a/4da86b672229a20c5a681da17a35c8cb6d8ec6a7.png" class="latex" alt="$E(0.5, 0, 1.5), F(0.5, 0, 0), G(0,1,0), H(0,1,1.5)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="369" height="18" />.  The vector <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/2/c128243ea8125d0bb007b8132dcfa44461df73a7.png" class="latex" alt="$EF$" width="28" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/5/305e230c3081a51a672ce9506092b3b5a1d735ed.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0,-1.5)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="84" height="18" />, while the vector <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/b/68bc6f8caca948dca03f953aee8ce093afad4d88.png" class="latex" alt="$HG$" width="30" height="12" /> is also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/5/305e230c3081a51a672ce9506092b3b5a1d735ed.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0,-1.5)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="84" height="18" />, meaning the two sides <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/2/c128243ea8125d0bb007b8132dcfa44461df73a7.png" class="latex" alt="$EF$" width="28" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/7/0a7a248cfd02095e2a009f811d5732a9bea9d595.png" class="latex" alt="$GH$" width="31" height="12" /> are parallel.  Similarly, the vector <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/5/3951902c11a1cd39a091ea15f0f06cbc79095de4.png" class="latex" alt="$FG$" width="28" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/4/65454cea29d3279ea9b214384bb8888229093084.png" class="latex" alt="$(-0.5, 1, 0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="84" height="18" />, while the vector <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/b/90b19c0bfdec36082e8da6e7ba3a3149b69fa63c.png" class="latex" alt="$EH$" width="31" height="12" /> is also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/4/65454cea29d3279ea9b214384bb8888229093084.png" class="latex" alt="$(-0.5, 1, 0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="84" height="18" />.  Again, these are equal in both magnitude and direction, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/5/3951902c11a1cd39a091ea15f0f06cbc79095de4.png" class="latex" alt="$FG$" width="28" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/b/90b19c0bfdec36082e8da6e7ba3a3149b69fa63c.png" class="latex" alt="$EH$" width="31" height="12" /> are parallel.  Thus, figure <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/3/9735b37ac43861ef9e105bff1711f117917b09ec.png" class="latex" alt="$EFGH$" width="59" height="12" /> is a parallelogram.
</p><p>Computation of vectors <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/2/c128243ea8125d0bb007b8132dcfa44461df73a7.png" class="latex" alt="$EF$" width="28" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/b/68bc6f8caca948dca03f953aee8ce093afad4d88.png" class="latex" alt="$HG$" width="30" height="12" /> is sufficient evidence that the figure is a parallelogram, since the vectors are not only point in the same direction, but are of the same magnitude, but the other vector <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/5/3951902c11a1cd39a091ea15f0f06cbc79095de4.png" class="latex" alt="$FG$" width="28" height="12" /> is needed to find the angle between the sides.
</p><p>Taking the dot product of vector <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/2/c128243ea8125d0bb007b8132dcfa44461df73a7.png" class="latex" alt="$EF$" width="28" height="12" /> and vector <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/5/3951902c11a1cd39a091ea15f0f06cbc79095de4.png" class="latex" alt="$FG$" width="28" height="12" /> gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/3/a93b5b54dc4931ec3cfd145c233d1dd0ff93b0ad.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \cdot -0.5 + 0 \cdot 1 + -1.5 \cdot 0 = 0$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="227" height="14" />, which means the two vectors are perpendicular.  (Alternately, as above, note that vector <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/2/c128243ea8125d0bb007b8132dcfa44461df73a7.png" class="latex" alt="$EF$" width="28" height="12" /> goes directly down on the z-axis, while vector <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/5/3951902c11a1cd39a091ea15f0f06cbc79095de4.png" class="latex" alt="$FG$" width="28" height="12" /> has no z-component and lie completely in the xy plane.)  Thus, the figure is a parallelogram with a right angle, which makes it a rectangle.  With the distance formula in three dimensions, we find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/e/5ae63c4c7f1be0a8afef79a155aab508dc600042.png" class="latex" alt="$EF = \frac{3}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="64" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/0/c208bcd21ea70067eaa1a334befaf0f00fa61349.png" class="latex" alt="$FG = \frac{\sqrt{5}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="79" height="41" />, giving an area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/5/a45eeff3a07782cc6a1eb90a23546ccf789ab816.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac32 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{5}}{2} = \boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{3\sqrt{5}}{4}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="162" height="53" />
</p>
