<p>A <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/7/d278803d72fcc493afb1559174a483aa7f41d143.png" class="latex" alt="$3 \times 3$" width="40" height="12" /> square is partitioned into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> unit squares. Each unit square is painted either white or black with each color being equally likely, chosen independently and at random. The square is then rotated <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/c/a4cd45b3e5e4114d6f850997684da2d285b19541.png" class="latex" alt="$90\,^{\circ}$" width="27" height="13" /> clockwise about its center, and every white square in a position formerly occupied by a black square is painted black. The colors of all other squares are left unchanged. What is the probability the grid is now entirely black?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/a/91af084b8ad58b37eb530c7acefea73bdbb16aa4.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{49}{512}\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{7}{64}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{121}{1024}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{81}{512}\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \frac{9}{32}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="449" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>First, look for invariants. The center, unaffected by rotation, must be black. So automatically, the chance is less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/6/616d5a21c98919a0a286960273edefb463314726.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="16" height="37" /> Note that a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/7/6a7dfa8a2a90d521b2b03f5b82a1fb9f85dce88c.png" class="latex" alt="$90^{\circ}$" width="24" height="13" /> rotation requires that black squares be across from each other across a vertical or horizontal axis. 
</p><p><br />
As such, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> squares directly across from each other must be black in the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> edge squares. Since there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> configurations for this to be possible (top and bottom, right and left), this is a chance of
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/5/375c1a49a50d57948aeacba351e6497f0f1b8667.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\left (\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \right )+ \left (\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \right )=\frac{1}{2}\]" width="185" height="43" /> 
</p><p><br />
However, by PIE, subtract the chance all 4 are black and both configurations are met: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/1/17182224e908a1f3a5018d60670835884900b97e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}- \left (\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \right ) \cdot \left (\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \right )=\frac{7}{16}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="224" height="43" />. Through symmetrical reasoning, the corners also have a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/4/bd4ea89e8a9b511a1fd1ba07cb76c32eaaa35a50.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{7}{16}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="37" /> chance of having a configuration that yields all black corners. 
</p><p>Then, the chance that all squares black is the union of all these probabilities: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/0/5f061964e94079bd70bcd38798f2fead1604f6e8.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2} \left (\frac{7}{16} \right ) \left (\frac{7}{16} \right ) = \boxed{\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{49}{512}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="217" height="48" /> 
</p><p><br />
</p>
<pre>Psst. Great article for PIE. -&gt; <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="https://artofproblemsolving.com/wiki/index.php/Principle_of_Inclusion-Exclusion">https://artofproblemsolving.com/wiki/index.php/Principle_of_Inclusion-Exclusion</a> 
</pre>
<p>~BJHHar
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>First, there is only one way for the middle square to be black because it is not affected by the rotation. Then we can consider the corners and edges separately. Let's first just consider the number of ways we can color the corners. There is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> case with all black squares. There are four cases with one white square and all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> work. There are six cases with two white squares, but only the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> with the white squares diagonal from each other work. There are no cases with three white squares or four white squares. Then the total number of ways to color the corners is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/c/5dc06609d59478c44777f33b4f1a13d812874380.png" class="latex" alt="$1+4+2=7$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="105" height="13" />. In essence, the edges work the same way, so there are also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" /> ways to color them. The number of ways to fit the conditions over the number of ways to color the squares is
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/2/d12be61744c1adb4e336be847dcbf4e9a9ea7d06.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{7\times7}{2^9}=\boxed{\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{49}{512}}\]" width="144" height="48" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>We proceed by casework.
Note that the middle square must be black because when rotated 90 degrees, it must keep its position. Now we have to deal with the following cases: <br />
Case 1: 0 white squares.
There is exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> way to color the grid this way. <br />
Case 2: 1 white square.
Note that the white square can be anywhere on the grid except for the middle square because when rotating 90 degrees it can never land on itself. Thus, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> cases.<br />
Case 3: 2 white squares. 
We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/5/e55072bfcfed692e524b9da0ab6c9c88adac19e8.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{8}{2}=28$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="79" height="53" /> ways to color two white squares without restrictions (the middle square must be black, giving us 8 squares to choose from). However, we must subtract the ways in which two white squares differ by a rotation of 90 degrees about the middle of the square. There are a total of 8 cases we must subtract (these are not too hard to see). Thus, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/3/f8366cd6196dd8a9da6d38a3e9eafb109e99d53e.png" class="latex" alt="$20$" width="17" height="12" /> ways from this.<br />
Case 4: 3 white squares.
Since we can not change the middle square, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/0/bd03425ac1672d23cc4a74638d4498a5d6410a85.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{8}{3}=56$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="79" height="53" /> ways to color this. However, we must consider the cases where at least two squares differ by a rotation of 90 degrees. We can count this with PIE: by the Principle of Exclusion, the number of cases we want to exclude is the number of cases where 2 squares differ by a rotation of 90 degrees and minus when there are 3 squares such that two of them differ by rotation of 90 and 1 of them differs by rotation of 180, because of the overcount from our first case. 
From case 2, there are 8 causes such that two squares differ by a rotation of 90. There are also 6 other places we can place the third square (it can't be the middle of the two that we already colored), for a total of 48 ways. We have to subtract the second case. Note that there are 8 ways in which we can arrange two squares differing by 180 degrees. Out of these, each one has two ways to put another square such that two differ by 90 degrees and 1 pair differs by 180. However, this is overcounted by a factor of 2, so there are actually 8*2/2=8 ways. 
Thus, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/7/1e712887c8dc9912614fc27c1782de59002177e6.png" class="latex" alt="$56-(48-8)=16$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="146" height="18" /> ways in this case.<br />
Case 5: 4 white squares.
Note that two of them have to be on one of the 4 corner squares, and two of them have to be on one of the 4 edge squares. Each solution yields two combinations, for a total of 2 * 2 = 4.
Adding up our cases yields 1+8+20+16+4=49 ways. There are 512 ways to color the square without restrictions. Thus, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/9/419f3a5d52ca5166f1645a1dad284cad28240c34.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(A)}\ 49/512}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="101" height="29" />.
</p>
