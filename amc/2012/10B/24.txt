<p>Amy, Beth, and Jo listen to four different songs and discuss which ones they like. No song is liked by all three. Furthermore, for each of the three pairs of the girls, there is at least one song liked by those two girls but disliked by the third. In how many different ways is this possible?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/6/f36eae8118b8bff53ffaf12e23f6e53bcecc7279.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 108\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 132\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 671\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 846\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 1105$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="448" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions">Solutions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>Let the ordered triple <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/d/f3d9e22097e20a1249b5332202b24941a4bf9e1f.png" class="latex" alt="$(a,b,c)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="54" height="18" /> denote that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> songs are liked by Amy and Beth, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> songs by Beth and Jo, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> songs by Jo and Amy. We claim that the only possible triples are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/3/573691f3d08c1d7999d47735cb962c56f5b35764.png" class="latex" alt="$(1,1,1), (2,1,1), (1,2,1)(1,1,2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="245" height="18" />. 
</p><p>To show this, observe these are all valid conditions. Second, note that none of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/b/a5b29b358e825defa3e11b7d903d43ec31e5909a.png" class="latex" alt="$a,b,c$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="41" height="16" /> can be bigger than 3. Suppose otherwise, that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/b/33b3c27171aba2140505212ce5b912115d1cdd69.png" class="latex" alt="$a = 3$" width="42" height="12" />. Without loss of generality, say that Amy and Beth like songs 1, 2, and 3. Then because there is at least one song liked by each pair of girls, we require either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> to be at least 1. In fact, we require either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> to equal 1, otherwise there will be a song liked by all three. Suppose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/1/851b03ce92c3ad8da97daf2785cf4e103c7a8d66.png" class="latex" alt="$b = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="13" />. Then we must have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/0/a80d7a3ca845e6cae14b9db710b64331219ec3f7.png" class="latex" alt="$c=0$" width="40" height="12" /> since no song is liked by all three girls, a contradiction.
</p><p><b>Case 1</b>: How many ways are there for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/d/f3d9e22097e20a1249b5332202b24941a4bf9e1f.png" class="latex" alt="$(a,b,c)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="54" height="18" /> to equal <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/d/35d43e6e252922c8df825114d48e1418c7516d6d.png" class="latex" alt="$(1,1,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="56" height="18" />? There are 4 choices for which song is liked by Amy and Beth, 3 choices for which song is liked by Beth and Jo, and 2 choices for which song is liked by Jo and Amy. The fourth song can be liked by only one of the girls, or none of the girls, for a total of 4 choices. So <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/e/31efc5f204e249dd6318a5c311e3e236aaf9c867.png" class="latex" alt="$(a,b,c)=(1,1,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="135" height="18" /> in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/f/7afa0c99ba3a5e101858d2442d3acc4c3cf4ea94.png" class="latex" alt="$4\cdot3\cdot2\cdot4 = 96$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="117" height="13" /> ways.
</p><p><b>Case 2</b>: To find the number of ways for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/d/c8d26b8c4f8d989b5ddb61c3c154067ef7cb09f8.png" class="latex" alt="$(a,b,c) = (2,1,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="135" height="18" />, observe there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/0/f401413a5807bd29f485f438f9b97173ecd58351.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{4}{2} = 6$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="70" height="53" /> choices of songs for the first pair of girls. There remain 2 choices of songs for the next pair (who only like one song). The last song is given to the last pair of girls. But observe that we let any three pairs of the girls like two songs, so we multiply by 3. In this case there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/0/6e080646c440477afe082ba2e9bdc570a2e006fe.png" class="latex" alt="$6\cdot2\cdot3=36$" width="95" height="12" /> ways for the girls to like the songs.
</p><p>That gives a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/7/35746ec4bc7aa6c7da1ae5f1d1a1b7fe4977dbc0.png" class="latex" alt="$96 + 36 = 132$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="109" height="14" /> ways for the girls to like the songs, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/b/1db03dcf7f1c2490afe28c2aae06fd0f8973f670.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{(\textrm{\textbf{B}})}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="39" height="29" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/9/c49e5f2d7548ae9523a5b697ad62d3ff5b4923cf.png" class="latex" alt="$AB, BJ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="63" height="16" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/9/a59b9a6c6724df7384848b8ea1772a120b9e2652.png" class="latex" alt="$AJ$" width="25" height="13" /> denote a song that is liked by Amy and Beth (but not Jo), Beth and Jo (but not Amy), and Amy and Jo (but not Beth), respectively. Similarly, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/8/f58fa41562657684d2b37768e7920e240cdc5f9c.png" class="latex" alt="$A, B, J,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="58" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" /> denote a song that is liked by only Amy, only Beth, only Jo, and none of them, respectively. Since we know that there is at least <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/8/b58bda21b18a1f1daa6a88b02f732074dc026cbb.png" class="latex" alt="$1\: AB, BJ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="76" height="16" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/9/a59b9a6c6724df7384848b8ea1772a120b9e2652.png" class="latex" alt="$AJ$" width="25" height="13" />, they must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> songs out of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> that Amy, Beth, and Jo listened to. The fourth song can be of any type <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/8/8e8181d5440670655baea87e27eb51200d9fb20e.png" class="latex" alt="$N, A, B, J, AB, BJ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="149" height="16" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/9/a59b9a6c6724df7384848b8ea1772a120b9e2652.png" class="latex" alt="$AJ$" width="25" height="13" /> (there is no <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/a/edaa303fbc13aa28173a4c10dafb8e0964bbfce3.png" class="latex" alt="$ABJ$" width="40" height="13" /> because no song is liked by all three, as stated in the problem.) Therefore, we must find the number of ways to rearrange <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/0/770d04a6fe11e1b71fbe274ed11189dd0bd3e90f.png" class="latex" alt="$AB, BJ, AJ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="94" height="16" />, and a song from the set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/c/84c23e24382f186fd0b6c0817c9d9337e61ea970.png" class="latex" alt="$\{N, A, B, J, AB, BJ, AJ\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="197" height="18" />.
</p><p>Case 1: Fourth song = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/d/d2d1c690eaf0a43803f7d0cfd40b4340bd381f30.png" class="latex" alt="$N, A, B, J$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="80" height="16" />
</p><p>Note that in Case 1, all four of the choices for the fourth song are different from the first three songs.
</p><p>Number of ways to rearrange = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/c/f8c361b245b38583db296fae626fadabe19547c1.png" class="latex" alt="$(4!)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" /> rearrangements for each choice <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/f/9cf3519dde83af0d49c57820cba9d8d53474385d.png" class="latex" alt="$*\: 4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="22" height="12" /> choices = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/7/367f6b15d47102fd8fdfc1bafed72c1daeadb35a.png" class="latex" alt="$96$" width="17" height="12" />.
</p><p>Case 2: Fourth song = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/0/770d04a6fe11e1b71fbe274ed11189dd0bd3e90f.png" class="latex" alt="$AB, BJ, AJ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="94" height="16" />
</p><p>Note that in Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, all three of the choices for the fourth song repeat somewhere in the first three songs.
</p><p>Number of ways to rearrange = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/1/1d10a278abcc372547e357e66a83f64b0c3ec01f.png" class="latex" alt="$(4!/2!)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="49" height="18" /> rearrangements for each choice <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/c/dacaa20f075a803c94cfa7551ea0afd02a531b0b.png" class="latex" alt="$*\: 3$" width="21" height="12" /> choices = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/5/ce5d3cbb9ab0a992ed503c2d4499e4f4af2f9d65.png" class="latex" alt="$36$" width="17" height="12" />.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h3>
<p>This is a bipartite graph problem, with the girls as left vertices and songs as right vertices. An edge connecting left vertex and right vertex means that a girl like a song.
</p><p>Condition 1: "No song is liked by all three", means that the degree of right vertices is at most 2. 
</p><p>Condition 2: "for each of the three pairs of the girls, there is at least one song liked by those two girls but disliked by the third", means that for any pair of left vertices, there is at least a right vertex connecting to them.
</p><p>To meet condition 2, there are at least 3 right vertices with 2 edges connecting to left vertices.  There are 2 cases:
</p><p>Case 1: there are only 3 such right vertices. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/0/7c00ef1e94f8d52073f1803861762b7f45a87597.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{4}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" /> such vertices, with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/5/6859713adc973d3fbe97fafef9ce829757e54b12.png" class="latex" alt="$3!$" width="12" height="13" /> ways of connections to the left vertices, total arrangements are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/3/03340fc16e8c033e154b7e92a57ffb641b81c16b.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{4}{3}\cdot3! = 24$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="109" height="55" />. The fourth right vertex either has no edge to the 3 left vertices, or 1 edge to 1 of the 3 left vertices.  So there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/b/efb2a73d1295444a8528b83466f20bf487367d91.png" class="latex" alt="$24\cdot(1+3) = 96$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="130" height="20" /> ways.
</p><p>Case 2: there are 4 such right vertices, 2 of them have edges to the same pair of left vertices.  There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/2/54216aeffa0fa278199cd3ceeb64cc8400c4a8cd.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{4}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" /> such vertices, with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/5/6859713adc973d3fbe97fafef9ce829757e54b12.png" class="latex" alt="$3!$" width="12" height="13" /> ways of connections.  So there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/4/10482dab2f368e85f24f3042cc7e7241c46126fc.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{4}{2}\cdot3! = 36$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="109" height="55" /> ways.
</p><p>Total ways are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/e/5ce68a0ae47ff8950cc7b8599762320baadfb2f9.png" class="latex" alt="$96+36=132$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="112" height="15" />.
</p><p>Another way is to overcount then subtract overlap ways.  Similar to previous case 1,  the fourth right vertex could have all possible connection to the left vertices except connecting to all 3, so it is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/3/303909e05ed67c452fd3f50ba48f490d0725701b.png" class="latex" alt="$2^3-1=7$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="83" height="18" /> ways, so the total ways are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/3/cf3ee529aa4b4c4e0b30252c9751e0a5f5835410.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{4}{3}\cdot3!\cdot7 = 24\cdot7 = 168$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="205" height="55" />.  But this overcounts the case 2 with 36 ways.  So total ways are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/0/fb0b377afbb309e25bee4bdf063752cef9478486.png" class="latex" alt="$168-36=132$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="121" height="15" />.
</p><p>-<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://artofproblemsolving.com/wiki/index.php/User:Junche">junche</a>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5">Solution 5</span></h2>
<p>Let's assign to each of the 3 pairs a song that they like, and that song is disliked by the other girl(satisfy the problem's 2nd condition). The first pair has 4 choice, 2nd has 3 choice, and last one has 2 choice. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/e/28e82d05bc57855b5d7b1a5906bd189b5ce3b479.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \cdot 3 \cdot 2$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="53" height="13" />. Then the last song can be liked/disliked freely, so there is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/1/591d9b115cf384bfadec71afd7ffa4397726d79a.png" class="latex" alt="$2^3 = 8$" width="49" height="15" /> total ways they can like/dislike the songs. Of these 8 cases, one of them is when they all like the song (which isn't allowed by the problem), so we subtract it away to have 7 cases. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/f/8af8c4d88e77ec13debe41a259418b926fd00973.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 7 = 168$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="129" height="14" />. But, we have overcounts when one pair of girls likes 2 songs, and the other girl dislikes those 2 songs. We can find the amount of cases that this happens by giving 1 pair of girls 2 songs that they like (and the other dislikes), and the other 2 pairs of girls 1 song that they like. There are 3 pairs of girls that we can choose to like 2 songs, so there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/5/5056d7596c3e516612e0b68b6514ade1ee2fa0b8.png" class="latex" alt="$3 \cdot \left(\binom {4}{2} \cdot 2 \cdot 1\right) = 36$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="176" height="55" /> ways. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/7/be7597ae50b274cf6d0d166442f1998efe17c688.png" class="latex" alt="$168 - 36 = \boxed {\textbf{(B) 132}}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="168" height="30" />.
</p><p>~heheman
~Arcticturn (minor fixes in LaTeX)
~EthanSpoon (minor fixes in LaTeX)
</p>
