<p>Three circles with radius 2 are mutually tangent. What is the total area of the circles and the region bounded by them, as shown in the figure?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/3/fd36c0563f1447ec842cc791ac398c4cd8f20685.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] filldraw((0,0)--(2,0)--(1,sqrt(3))--cycle,gray,gray); filldraw(circle((1,sqrt(3)),1),gray); filldraw(circle((0,0),1),gray); filldraw(circle((2,0),1),grey);[/asy]" width="252" height="235" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/3/eb3ede7f186b54f4037ca12bcaeccc42056a4392.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 10\pi+4\sqrt{3}\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 13\pi-\sqrt{3}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 12\pi+\sqrt{3}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 10\pi+9\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 13\pi$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="629" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>To determine the area of the figure, you can connect the centers of the circles to form an equilateral triangle with a side of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />. We must find the area of this triangle to include the figure formed in between the circles. Since the equilateral triangle has two 30-60-90 triangles inside, we can find the height and the base of each 30-60-90 triangle from the ratios: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/5/325593b1d7158d9991c84b4ac56b706296008ef8.png" class="latex" alt="$1: \sqrt{3}: 2.$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="76" height="18" /> The height is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/5/e455d80f4657b1112ba302a6e2c42e9c81f780ee.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" /> and the base is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />. Multiplying the height and base together with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/4/4a4a7df1100efe353cc612d5d1c289c22d5bf8c1.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/5/e455d80f4657b1112ba302a6e2c42e9c81f780ee.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />. Since there are two 30-60-90 triangles in the equilateral triangle, we multiply the area of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/c/47c78b85058ee5e17f88e2cde302e48aad826b35.png" class="latex" alt="$30-60-90$" width="98" height="12" /> triangle by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/2/0724eb2af08977be3554453187958c0bf167d5ad.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[2\cdot 2\sqrt{3} = 4\sqrt{3}.\]" width="116" height="18" />
</p><p>To find the area of the remaining sectors, which are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/1/f5181c4181c7caf8f1b006f55d5d5efccb3d122e.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{5}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> of the original circles once we remove the triangle, we know that the sectors have a central angle of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/6/d56e411bbbf7054a08a34cc6f5f0d1a73bafcf37.png" class="latex" alt="$300^\circ$" width="33" height="13" /> since the equilateral triangle already covered that area. Since there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/5/c55ed481f94445cc6cf731c6585a80ede1608cdc.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> pieces gone from the equilateral triangle, we have, in total, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/4/4a4a7df1100efe353cc612d5d1c289c22d5bf8c1.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> of a circle (with radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />) gone. Each circle has an area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/d/96dba061976eb9e3e38e0df9fc61e7471cf35518.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi r^2 = 4\pi$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="71" height="15" />, so three circles gives a total area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/6/ff63030fb1a38a8e170e4907219fd1eac6b0e072.png" class="latex" alt="$12\pi$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="28" height="12" />. Subtracting the half circle, we have: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/2/282489b5df7de97c913d0ddc6ad6f7c1c4b4c59a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[12\pi - \dfrac{4\pi}{2} = 12\pi - 2\pi = 10\pi.\]" width="227" height="37" /> 
</p><p>Summing the areas from the equilateral triangle and the remaining circle sections gives us: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/6/da6515abea39aa1f638ef297d3c355fee8f7e71e.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(A)} 10\pi + 4\sqrt3}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="125" height="33" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternate_Solution">Alternate Solution</span></h2>
<p>First, the area of the 3 circles is simply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/f/65ffff4ab1edf0f71a90df0e189d8cd46950dc3b.png" class="latex" alt="$3*\pi*2^2 = 12 \pi$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="123" height="15" />. Notice that the middle area is a little more than a rectangle formed by completely filling the rectangle formed by connecting two 90 degrees partial circles and then subtracting the two 90 degrees partial circles. 
The area of the rectangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/5/d8532fea6e90aca51de5a596c8914b62863d4407.png" class="latex" alt="$4*2=8$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="68" height="13" /> and the area of the 90 degrees partial circles are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/b/efb98405f55ef94898c85840cb2235908ee35e98.png" class="latex" alt="$2*(1/4)*\pi*2^2 = 2\pi$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="173" height="19" />. Therefore, the area of the shape in between the three circles is a little less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/a/b1a1f1b71cf34d6e43591494b7fc6147e20776da.png" class="latex" alt="$8 - 2\pi$" width="51" height="12" />. Summing up the 3 circles we got and the approximate area of the middle shape, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/0/3004cff2060229655e6104c0a6028c66a18cea07.png" class="latex" alt="$10\pi+8$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="60" height="14" />, which is a little more than what we want. We see that all answer choices except <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/6/da6515abea39aa1f638ef297d3c355fee8f7e71e.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(A)} 10\pi + 4\sqrt3}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="125" height="33" /> is greater than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/0/3004cff2060229655e6104c0a6028c66a18cea07.png" class="latex" alt="$10\pi+8$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="60" height="14" />, therefore it's the only answer. -dchang0524
</p>
