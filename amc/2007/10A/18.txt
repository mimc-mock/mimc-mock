<p>Consider the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" />-sided polygon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/0/f/c0fef2a783971fc08bd075a80546c6242e0ed9f3.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCDEFGHIJKL$" width="168" height="13" />, as shown. Each of its sides has length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, and each two consecutive sides form a right angle. Suppose that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/f/63f0f6aa257bd96696d97454bb777b1a283069e8.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AG}$" width="28" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/f/baf06e5af8cfb43ec7280a9e284f6a338d8b6175.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CH}$" width="31" height="15" /> meet at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" />. What is the area of quadrilateral <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/1/d4180c1fb3d692aaacf48a8f066c4f6fc1acb7bc.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCM$" width="61" height="13" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/9/1398362ab7277efd803b7326d6c586d92b193210.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(13mm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt)+fontsize(10pt)); dotfactor=4;  pair A=(1,3), B=(2,3), C=(2,2), D=(3,2), Ep=(3,1), F=(2,1), G=(2,0), H=(1,0), I=(1,1), J=(0,1), K=(0,2), L=(1,2); pair M=intersectionpoints(A--G,H--C)[0];  draw(A--B--C--D--Ep--F--G--H--I--J--K--L--cycle); draw(A--G); draw(H--C); dot(M);  label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,NW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,NE); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,NE); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,NE); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,Ep,SE); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,F,SE); label(&quot;$G$&quot;,G,SE); label(&quot;$H$&quot;,H,SW); label(&quot;$I$&quot;,I,SW); label(&quot;$J$&quot;,J,SW); label(&quot;$K$&quot;,K,NW); label(&quot;$L$&quot;,L,NW); label(&quot;$M$&quot;,M,W); [/asy]" width="228" height="218" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/0/a703ff8c8741ef37104a44da8142fe7070b322b3.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{(A)}\ \frac {44}{3}\qquad \text{(B)}\ 16 \qquad \text{(C)}\ \frac {88}{5}\qquad \text{(D)}\ 20 \qquad \text{(E)}\ \frac {62}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="386" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>We can obtain the solution by calculating the area of rectangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/c/b1c109a8ffe32014813d961109ba8b2ded63772d.png" class="latex" alt="$ABGH$" width="59" height="13" /> minus the combined area of triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/d/99dc01734e30b381a1aa488537a0388dbca88def.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AHG$" width="60" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/1/a013638eb175a47c098e47bd0a81080afda7da81.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle CGM$" width="64" height="13" />.
</p><p>We know that triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/d/57d081d595dc6773062577ae05f4ca20019b2d02.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AMH$" width="66" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/3/b6376ec20d31a8c7cc946d91ca27e4e5b5c9b2ae.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle GMC$" width="64" height="13" /> are similar because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/b/33b0b4a41229f41b7edd4adcbd3def0f8a008ab2.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AH} \parallel \overline{CG}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="78" height="20" />. Also, since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/c/b5c85382bd4ccb9b2c36d4ff7c6324e9834307f3.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AH}{CG} = \frac{3}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="70" height="38" />, the ratio of the distance from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/0/5605e7674254425ca5932c0a752f8a04b6b16770.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AH}$" width="30" height="15" /> to the distance from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/d/8ddc7b8c702ae12dacad9c833f53fc173066c622.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CG}$" width="29" height="15" /> is also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/d/cfdbd19877c13d1755e01b54ba640f512c883a34.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{3}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. Solving with the fact that the distance from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/0/5605e7674254425ca5932c0a752f8a04b6b16770.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AH}$" width="30" height="15" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/d/8ddc7b8c702ae12dacad9c833f53fc173066c622.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CG}$" width="29" height="15" /> is 4, we see that the distance from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/d/8ddc7b8c702ae12dacad9c833f53fc173066c622.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CG}$" width="29" height="15" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/8/778abab8ebf560b512a9a7e95a1c404065dc9cb0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{8}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />.
</p><p>The area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/d/99dc01734e30b381a1aa488537a0388dbca88def.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AHG$" width="60" height="13" /> is simply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/7/8b796eac2b0a571d7debceed0f344dde8c775a6d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2} \cdot 4 \cdot 12 = 24$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="109" height="37" />, the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/1/a013638eb175a47c098e47bd0a81080afda7da81.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle CGM$" width="64" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/7/8/6785373ed3261e0c907bd7fb414b9a4120883280.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{8}{5} \cdot 8 = \frac{32}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="106" height="37" />, and the area of rectangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/c/b1c109a8ffe32014813d961109ba8b2ded63772d.png" class="latex" alt="$ABGH$" width="59" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/8/d589e6009c41c09f064dadebe7ea6d2a53743c53.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \cdot 12 = 48$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="82" height="13" />.
</p><p>Taking the area of rectangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/c/b1c109a8ffe32014813d961109ba8b2ded63772d.png" class="latex" alt="$ABGH$" width="59" height="13" /> and subtracting the combined area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/d/99dc01734e30b381a1aa488537a0388dbca88def.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AHG$" width="60" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/1/a013638eb175a47c098e47bd0a81080afda7da81.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle CGM$" width="64" height="13" /> yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/337dfed808964b06c3f9f6873cfcdbbf7033b4a7.png" class="latex" alt="$48 - (24 + \frac{32}{5}) = \boxed{\frac{88}{5}}\ \text{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="204" height="48" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/7/64721f756f1c801d9696fe36e45507773be011b5.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(2cm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt)+fontsize(10pt)); dotfactor=4;  pair A=(1,3), B=(2,3), C=(2,2), D=(3,2), Ep=(3,1), F=(2,1), G=(2,0), H=(1,0), I=(1,1), J=(0,1), K=(0,2), L=(1,2); pair M=intersectionpoints(A--G,H--C)[0]; pair Z=(2.5,3);  draw(A--B--C--D--Ep--F--G--H--I--J--K--L--cycle); draw(A--G); draw(H--C); draw(B--Z--C); draw(C--F); dot(M);  label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,NW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,N); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,SE); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,NE); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,Ep,SE); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,F,SE); label(&quot;$G$&quot;,G,SE); label(&quot;$H$&quot;,H,SW); label(&quot;$I$&quot;,I,SW); label(&quot;$J$&quot;,J,SW); label(&quot;$K$&quot;,K,NW); label(&quot;$L$&quot;,L,NW); label(&quot;$M$&quot;,M,W); label(&quot;$N$&quot;,Z,NE); [/asy]" width="325" height="322" />
</p><p>Extend <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/e/20e8e26d580012b86d8fe55e70e5740908c35af0.png" class="latex" alt="$CH$" width="31" height="12" /> and call their intersection <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" />.
</p><p>The triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/6/576d595084e2a94ac3eb1c2ee067562030dd825d.png" class="latex" alt="$CBN$" width="45" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/0/e10d021bbf3c6a09ec084e2f20b3752a85aa4937.png" class="latex" alt="$CGH$" width="45" height="12" /> are clearly similar with ratio <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/e/32e1a1a7adf986532b7598e896785a56c785f779.png" class="latex" alt="$1:2$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="32" height="12" />, hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/d/76dbb36ee3e49fc6ad1e92e43bb98560193a7795.png" class="latex" alt="$BN=2$" width="64" height="12" /> and thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/2/3a28afe2cd30888e82cba0c28deb2d6df5e995e9.png" class="latex" alt="$AN=6$" width="63" height="13" />. The area of the triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/6/036bf5e5bf3938a62201c696cf6051b5509be494.png" class="latex" alt="$BCN$" width="45" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/b/50be8838823e86b768d2fd3aefa6093a51270e21.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2\cdot 4}2 = 4$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="68" height="37" />.
</p><p>The triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/c/7dcbd24f888801c82be1dd75b1cc4b0d7a160f0a.png" class="latex" alt="$MAN$" width="49" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/f/72f0a8fc3106b64b4666cfeb5c178dfbe4d35746.png" class="latex" alt="$MGH$" width="50" height="12" /> are similar as well, and we now know that the ratio of their dimensions is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/4/5d42d298f1005b627b53683d668a878a9b2c0499.png" class="latex" alt="$AN:GH = 6:4 = 3:2$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="191" height="13" />.
</p><p>Draw altitudes from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" /> onto <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/a/9aafc7e3cc1eafce5e16d3a45250eeda7b1a6526.png" class="latex" alt="$AN$" width="30" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/7/0a7a248cfd02095e2a009f811d5732a9bea9d595.png" class="latex" alt="$GH$" width="31" height="12" />, let their feet be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/6/3c6a4a0680d4e0668219585d856c7f109cd09f77.png" class="latex" alt="$M_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="23" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/7/c879631d01bcc51ec112e4a32a039f624d40c91c.png" class="latex" alt="$M_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="23" height="15" />. We get that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/8/378fcec1210f5749828cd5a0e8dac1f93dc9d400.png" class="latex" alt="$MM_1&#160;: MM_2 = 3:2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="161" height="15" />. Hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/a/d1aa20b2a7c167ab60d9fcd383d395a661238794.png" class="latex" alt="$MM_1 = \frac 35 \cdot 12 = \frac {36}5$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="158" height="37" />. (An alternate way is by seeing that the set-up AHGCM is similar to the 2 pole problem (<a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.artofproblemsolving.com/wiki/index.php/1951_AHSME_Problems/Problem_30">http://www.artofproblemsolving.com/wiki/index.php/1951_AHSME_Problems/Problem_30</a>). Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/0/460fdf918ae5a80d5aae3784ad89817d960499ff.png" class="latex" alt="$MM_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="43" height="15" /> must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/d/d4d8a87f47b20ac6b62efb935b3c0048a7fd143a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{\frac{1}{8}+\frac{1}{12}} = \frac{24}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="99" height="43" />, by the harmonic mean. Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/4/15458d6b968290feda8dfc4a1f8426dec75028f8.png" class="latex" alt="$MM_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="42" height="15" /> must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/d/01dd532bb72d26ccb1ac3325d7c6569e40977ca0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{36}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" />.)   
</p><p>Then the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/a/90aa302feed3983e7156a34b821ad877642919b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AMN$" width="50" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/9/4a90acda8c5e1953665558891984833e4aaeccf4.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac 12 \cdot AN \cdot MM_1 = \frac{108}5$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="168" height="37" />, and the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/1/d4180c1fb3d692aaacf48a8f066c4f6fc1acb7bc.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCM$" width="61" height="13" /> can be obtained by subtracting the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/6/036bf5e5bf3938a62201c696cf6051b5509be494.png" class="latex" alt="$BCN$" width="45" height="12" />, which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />. Hence the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/6/6b65e811774898d8347be11cd55a4ef6d6232bd1.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{108}5 - 4 =  \boxed{\frac{88}5}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="120" height="48" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/9/1398362ab7277efd803b7326d6c586d92b193210.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(13mm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt)+fontsize(10pt)); dotfactor=4;  pair A=(1,3), B=(2,3), C=(2,2), D=(3,2), Ep=(3,1), F=(2,1), G=(2,0), H=(1,0), I=(1,1), J=(0,1), K=(0,2), L=(1,2); pair M=intersectionpoints(A--G,H--C)[0];  draw(A--B--C--D--Ep--F--G--H--I--J--K--L--cycle); draw(A--G); draw(H--C); dot(M);  label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,NW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,NE); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,NE); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,NE); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,Ep,SE); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,F,SE); label(&quot;$G$&quot;,G,SE); label(&quot;$H$&quot;,H,SW); label(&quot;$I$&quot;,I,SW); label(&quot;$J$&quot;,J,SW); label(&quot;$K$&quot;,K,NW); label(&quot;$L$&quot;,L,NW); label(&quot;$M$&quot;,M,W); [/asy]" width="228" height="218" />
</p><p>We can use coordinates to solve this. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/d/e3d32e656f121ea92ac7c093fbb988905fca6bdb.png" class="latex" alt="$H=(0,0).$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="84" height="18" /> Thus, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc5121dc3b75fb4a457cfceb23c5c3c79d02fdcb.png" class="latex" alt="$A=(0,12), C=(4,8), G=(4,0).$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="265" height="18" /> Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/1/b11fca7fa0396390456782405b2897976a413f77.png" class="latex" alt="$AG$" width="27" height="13" /> has equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/0/1a0f3fac311230251de91efd99b500d3451b6718.png" class="latex" alt="$-3x+12=y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="107" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/b/bcb82bca7de59684e050803ba493e050e355614f.png" class="latex" alt="$HC$" width="30" height="12" /> has equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/8/cd873d4c595edc4ec5b30468f234d3e04cbc1903.png" class="latex" alt="$2x=y.$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="57" height="15" /> Solving, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/d/addef7b8d40ef7f6d3b49caccb09a579f6d67c72.png" class="latex" alt="$M=(\frac {12}{5},\frac {24}{5}).$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="117" height="38" /> Using the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Shoelace_Theorem" title="Shoelace Theorem">Shoelace Theorem</a> (or you could connect <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/0/290b753dd098f48a53d57bf3b755c9756e11c7e3.png" class="latex" alt="$LC$" width="26" height="12" /> and solve for the resulting triangle + trapezoid areas), we find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/8/1d8b0e62f4e452e57d1fb1ad55c9d4c49a0696da.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABCM]=\boxed{\mathrm{(C) \ }\dfrac{88}{5}}.$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="166" height="48" />
