<p>A circle passes through the three vertices of an isosceles triangle that has two sides of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> and a base of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />. What is the area of this circle?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/9/bc9d8b9f630c6970845b712342467cbba9955cf0.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 2\pi \qquad\textbf{(B) } \frac{5}{2}\pi \qquad\textbf{(C) } \frac{81}{32}\pi \qquad\textbf{(D) } 3\pi \qquad\textbf{(E) } \frac{7}{2}\pi$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="429" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions">Solutions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> have vertex <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and center <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" />, with foot of altitude from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> intersecting <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" /> at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />. 
</p>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/6/dd6654d76ebcb5aa78bf644545d9639c8fccd53a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] import olympiad; pair B=(0,0), C=(2,0), A=(1,3), D=(1,0); pair O=circumcenter(A,B,C); draw(A--B--C--A--D); draw(B--O--C); draw(circumcircle(A,B,C)); dot(O); label(&quot;\(A\)&quot;,A,N); label(&quot;\(B\)&quot;,B,S); label(&quot;\(C\)&quot;,C,S); label(&quot;\(D\)&quot;,D,S); label(&quot;\(O\)&quot;,O,W); label(&quot;\(r\)&quot;,(O+A)/2,SE); label(&quot;\(r\)&quot;,(O+B)/2,N); label(&quot;\(h\)&quot;,(O+D)/2,SE); label(&quot;\(3\)&quot;,(A+B)/2,NW); label(&quot;\(1\)&quot;,(B+D)/2,N); [/asy]" width="232" height="252" /></center>
<p>Then by the Pythagorean Theorem (with radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> and height <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/0/d008b0f638eecc580a199181c4bf3895784e726b.png" class="latex" alt="$OD = h$" width="64" height="12" />) on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/5/e75efb6abaae407233baf1ad552792b2982c9757.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle OBD$" width="60" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/5/f75d6d58b03031bd7cb52a7ca886a8ddf369d21c.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD$" width="59" height="13" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/2/e225bbbd6e79777bba07d4043109ece480d93a19.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} h^2 + 1 &amp; = r^2 \\ (h + r)^2 + 1 &amp; = 9 \end{align*}" width="132" height="47" />
</p><p>Substituting and solving gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/a/04a556d411a92237862585d419bf21a5691e694c.png" class="latex" alt="$r = \frac {9}{4\sqrt {2}}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="68" height="42" />. Then the area of the circle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/3/45343bca549eeb70cac8804857cab611f84c1351.png" class="latex" alt="$r^2 \pi = \left(\frac {9}{4\sqrt {2}}\right)^2 \pi = \boxed{\mathrm{(C) \ } \frac {81}{32} \pi}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="238" height="48" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>By <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/a/24ad33198cbeac568be32a33ad27d05575e38c58.png" class="latex" alt="$A = \frac {1}{2}Bh = \frac {abc}{4R}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="128" height="38" /> (or we could use <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/1/a91bbfd0ed8dd526d21ec9b36cc90c3f951a5103.png" class="latex" alt="$s = 4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="12" /> and <a href="/wiki/index.php/Heron%27s_formula" class="mw-redirect" title="Heron&#39;s formula">Heron's formula</a>),
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/6/3e633da80f20d181d823e5f1ae8ba9d76bad76fc.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[R = \frac {abc}{2Bh} = \frac {3 \cdot 3 \cdot 2}{2(2)(2\sqrt {2})} = \frac {9}{4\sqrt {2}}\]" width="244" height="43" />
and the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/d/d7dadc25488e10d48133f9982103e5c793868985.png" class="latex" alt="$R^2 \pi = \boxed{\mathrm{(C) \ } \frac {81}{32} \pi}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="134" height="48" />
</p><p>Alternatively, by the Extended <a href="/wiki/index.php/Law_of_Sines" title="Law of Sines">Law of Sines</a>,
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/c/e8c9e4437a60d324994ccf3463f040abe8b58da6.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[2R = \frac {AC}{\sin \angle ABC} = \frac {3}{\frac {2\sqrt {2}}{3}} \Longrightarrow R = \frac {9}{4\sqrt {2}}\]" width="301" height="47" />
Answer follows as above.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h3>
<p>Extend segment <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc56862b1fdb71204894da278e42ef75de73a40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AD$" width="28" height="13" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> on Circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" />.
</p>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/0/7609b3287774915ebf4e80694297138491851a07.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] import olympiad; pair B=(0,0), C=(2,0), A=(1,3), D=(1,0), R=(1,-0.35); pair O=circumcenter(A,B,C); draw(A--B--C--A--D--R--C); draw(B--O--C); draw(circumcircle(A,B,C)); dot(O); label(&quot;\(A\)&quot;,A,N); label(&quot;\(B\)&quot;,B,S); label(&quot;\(C\)&quot;,C,S); label(&quot;\(D\)&quot;,D,S); label(&quot;\(O\)&quot;,O,W); label(&quot;\(R\)&quot;,R,S); label(&quot;\(r\)&quot;,(O+A)/2,SE); label(&quot;\(r\)&quot;,(O+R)/2,SE); label(&quot;\(3\)&quot;,(A+C)/2,NE); label(&quot;\(1\)&quot;,(C+D)/2,N); [/asy]" width="208" height="252" /></center>
<p>By the Pythagorean Theorem <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/b/20bae5e12d2632fb54993e56b145e80866960791.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[AD^2 = 3^2 - 1^2\]" width="114" height="15" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/8/cf855c574c47d4adfe02415b542ec78c2305dcef.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[AD = 2\sqrt{2}\]" width="86" height="18" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/e/b8eabc87afe4c11a47e4aa27628d5517af4866b9.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ADC$" width="59" height="13" /> is similar to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/b/cbb5f81e8a4268637ec8f6cfeafffdbee5528060.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ACR$" width="58" height="13" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/b/2bb09cda9a14a319ee298f85108d1ccea8cb440a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac {2\sqrt{2}}{3} = \frac {3}{2r}\]" width="79" height="41" /> which gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/8/068ce0a056a24d56aaa2274b6a69d7e43d211949.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[2r = \frac {9}{2\sqrt{2}} = \frac {9\sqrt{2}}{4}\]" width="139" height="45" /> therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/9/6c965d62373a67169e4b33da037d9346e9d3ee72.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[r = \frac {9\sqrt{2}}{8}\]" width="68" height="41" />
</p><p>The area of the circle is therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/2/04250010557d0f1e76a6233cfe9b97291eb5d8fe.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi r^2 = \left(\frac {9\sqrt{2}}{8}\right)^2 \pi = \boxed{\mathrm{(C) \ } \frac {81}{32} \pi}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="240" height="56" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h3>
<p>First, we extend <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc56862b1fdb71204894da278e42ef75de73a40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AD$" width="28" height="13" /> to intersect the circle at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/0/710fb5fa3adedd7a16739e543d846747adbe05b5.png" class="latex" alt="$E.$" width="18" height="12" />
</p>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/5/315635f0b6afac6eba65f160b4765f8a089b242a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] import olympiad; pair B=(0,0), C=(2,0), A=(1,3), D=(1,0), E=(1,-(8^0.5)/8); pair O=circumcenter(A,B,C); draw(A--B--C--A--E); draw(circumcircle(A,B,C)); dot(O); dot(D); dot(B); dot(C); dot(A); dot(E); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,N); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,S); label(&quot;\(C\)&quot;,C,S); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,NE); label(&quot;$O$&quot;,O,W); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,E,S); label(&quot;$3$&quot;,(A+B)/2,NW); label(&quot;$1$&quot;,(B+D)/2,N); [/asy]" width="208" height="252" /></center>
<p>By the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Pythagorean_Theorem" title="Pythagorean Theorem">Pythagorean Theorem</a>, we know that 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/8/4/184993d354c51ec846b92ab7db734408bd7415c4.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[1^2+AD^2=3^2\implies AD=2\sqrt{2}.\]" width="254" height="18" />
We also know that, from the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Power_of_a_Point_Theorem" title="Power of a Point Theorem">Power of a Point Theorem</a>, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/4/df46c997de5643a2353c4ab9296ac2a4308d7af4.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[AD\cdot DE=BD\cdot DC.\]" width="172" height="13" /> 
We can substitute the values we know to get
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/d/5ed0635c1f7848d211d69de36918ec6a5ced3e2f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[2\sqrt{2}\cdot DE=1\]" width="108" height="18" />
We can simplify this to get that 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/9/c19312c2cd0459963595b99319c399fad66547f2.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[DE=\dfrac{2\sqrt{2}}{8}.\]" width="94" height="41" />
We add <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc56862b1fdb71204894da278e42ef75de73a40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AD$" width="28" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/5/6d5c9afa92ae3180f02754c177e530d9d0f8b4d4.png" class="latex" alt="$DE$" width="29" height="12" /> together to get the length of the diameter, and then we can find the area.
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/e/71ea1512510ba3cbcf0d77d0acf358f4bf87e702.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[AE=AD+DE=2\sqrt{2}+\dfrac{2\sqrt{2}}{8}=\dfrac{9\sqrt{2}}{4}.\]" width="315" height="41" />
Therefore, the radius is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/e/9fe258ed3bf0cdba0d2396b5b906308c53b40cf3.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{9\sqrt{2}}{8}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="35" height="41" />, so the area is 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/9/1f9d02075e4cc213c3fb5f56002933b9a1b1871e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\left(\dfrac{9\sqrt{2}}{8}\right)^2\pi=\boxed{(\text{C})\dfrac{81}{32}\pi.}\]" width="183" height="56" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5">Solution 5</span></h3>
<p>Another possible solution is to plot the circle and triangle on a graph with the circle having center <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/6/f9603ca3089464e548fc6f1366bc474e7efef8d9.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />. 
Let the radius of the circle = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />. 
Let the distance between the origin and the base of triangle = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" />.
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/b/47b06371a9052a4f43086e040471c8b28fe4e6a1.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[1 + a^2 = r^2\]" width="86" height="16" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/9/fb9965ccfd8130f05bb6033e814d3a4a08a0d5b2.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[r + a = 2\sqrt{2}\]" width="98" height="18" /> 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/c/f2ccbf8e73d27c08dcb264c65b7e5b45fd57f1ea.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[a = 2\sqrt{2} - r\]" width="97" height="18" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/1/511ac67b6010544f85b1dbbcd52c78b0e4a45ef5.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[9 - 4r\sqrt{2} = 0\]" width="105" height="18" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/3/953da2d9a76fd4c334a729e3a993df7cf7ba1e2a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[r = \frac{9\sqrt{2}}{8}\]" width="68" height="41" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/9/829a87e608cc795c1c9ab3bc2bad6c6eec088a24.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\pi r^2 = \boxed{(\text{C})\frac{81}{32}\pi}\]" width="122" height="48" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_6">Solution 6</span></h3>
<p>We can also use LOC to solve this problem. If O is the center of the circle, angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/3/14323858397d132fc0169507e1fb72c5ae7ef54d.png" class="latex" alt="$OBC$" width="43" height="12" /> is double angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/d/2edcdc0d73ef64ec441f60a3a8dccdbd10aa61a5.png" class="latex" alt="$BAC$" width="42" height="13" />. From law of cosines, we have that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/2/d420e194093e786fc5d37f30520ba030415eccc4.png" class="latex" alt="$\cos(\angle BAC) = \frac{7}{9}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="133" height="38" />. We now apply law of cosines on triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/3/14323858397d132fc0169507e1fb72c5ae7ef54d.png" class="latex" alt="$OBC$" width="43" height="12" /> to get that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/1/a11af5bf8eb8c2f697252bda707607f0b11b3e3b.png" class="latex" alt="$4 = r^2+r^2-2r^2\cos(2 \angle BAC)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="243" height="21" /> but we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/5/da5c5a40f3e30ab15b0e8e0ea59cd4732dba34a4.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cos (\angle BAC) = 2 \cdot (\frac{7}{9})^2 - 1 = \frac{17}{81}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="267" height="38" />. Plugging this in and simplifying, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/f/55fe2b0c7476d1f4fb6ce4cb4b60b7a63c465058.png" class="latex" alt="$r = \frac{9}{8} \cdot \sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="85" height="38" /> and thus the area of the circle is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/2/dd2414afec532275a8bdae0cc0b1d7ba4f096316.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{81}{32} \pi$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="35" height="38" />.
</p>
