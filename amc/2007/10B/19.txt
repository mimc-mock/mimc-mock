<p>The wheel shown is spun twice, and the randomly determined numbers opposite the pointer are recorded. The first number is divided by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/5/4054552638cf9f29e857a1306bd3ec31df3c19b6.png" class="latex" alt="$4,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="15" /> and the second number is divided by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/3/b835f2c6c592edc4583ce996f86bcc0d07ca8da5.png" class="latex" alt="$5.$" width="12" height="12" /> The first remainder designates a column, and the second remainder designates a row on the checkerboard shown. What is the probability that the pair of numbers designates a shaded square?
</p>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/6/d16f5082e7ba12a9e32d1bd5aad7b6620c1985ae.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(5mm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt)+fontsize(10pt)); dotfactor=4;  real r=2; pair O=(0,0); pair A=(0,2), A1=(0,-2); draw(A--A1); pair B=(sqrt(3),1), B1=(-sqrt(3),-1); draw(B--B1); pair C=(sqrt(3),-1), C1=(-sqrt(3),1); draw(C--C1); path circleO=Circle(O,r); draw(circleO); pair[] ps={O}; dot(ps); label(&quot;$6$&quot;,(-0.6,1)); label(&quot;$1$&quot;,(0.6,1)); label(&quot;$2$&quot;,(0.6,-1)); label(&quot;$9$&quot;,(-0.6,-1)); label(&quot;$7$&quot;,(1.2,0)); label(&quot;$3$&quot;,(-1.2,0));  label(&quot;$pointer$&quot;,(-4,0)); draw((-5.5,0.5)--(-5.5,-0.5)--(-3,-0.5)--(-2.5,0)--(-3,0.5)--cycle);  fill((4,0)--(4,1)--(5,1)--(5,0)--cycle,gray); fill((6,2)--(6,1)--(5,1)--(5,2)--cycle,gray); fill((6,0)--(6,-1)--(5,-1)--(5,0)--cycle,gray); fill((6,0)--(6,1)--(7,1)--(7,0)--cycle,gray); fill((4,-1)--(5,-1)--(5,-2)--(4,-2)--cycle,gray); fill((6,-1)--(7,-1)--(7,-2)--(6,-2)--cycle,gray); draw((4,2)--(7,2)--(7,-2)--(4,-2)--cycle); draw((4,1)--(7,1)); draw((4,0)--(7,0)); draw((4,-1)--(7,-1)); draw((5,2)--(5,-2)); draw((6,2)--(6,-2)); label(&quot;$1$&quot;,midpoint((4,-1)--(4,-2)),W); label(&quot;$2$&quot;,midpoint((4,0)--(4,-1)),W); label(&quot;$3$&quot;,midpoint((4,1)--(4,0)),W); label(&quot;$4$&quot;,midpoint((4,2)--(4,1)),W); label(&quot;$1$&quot;,midpoint((4,-2)--(5,-2)),S); label(&quot;$2$&quot;,midpoint((5,-2)--(6,-2)),S); label(&quot;$3$&quot;,midpoint((7,-2)--(6,-2)),S); [/asy]" width="298" height="115" /></center>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions">Solutions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>When dividing each number on the wheel by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/5/4054552638cf9f29e857a1306bd3ec31df3c19b6.png" class="latex" alt="$4,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="15" /> the remainders are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/2/4b25e18f21520c33cb4e71e9833192b08947b8cc.png" class="latex" alt="$1, 1, 2, 2, 3,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="81" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/0/93032f1ab8b685d84a767a2fde0d9006a464b5e4.png" class="latex" alt="$3.$" width="12" height="12" /> Each column on the checkerboard is equally likely to be chosen.
</p><p>When dividing each number on the wheel by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/4/21415a2eb558eb7c199a0382fcd50cbeda9299fb.png" class="latex" alt="$5,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> the remainders are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/2/4b25e18f21520c33cb4e71e9833192b08947b8cc.png" class="latex" alt="$1, 1, 2, 2, 3,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="81" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/1/cb15a16cca90d201094265ee529e9e2cfb113de8.png" class="latex" alt="$4.$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="12" height="12" />
</p><p>The probability that a shaded square in the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />st or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />rd row of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />st or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />rd column is chosen is
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/8/5085076484cb2b4c737dae329bacc077edc4922e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{2}{3} \times \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{3}\]" width="82" height="37" />
</p><p>The probability that a shaded square in the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />nd or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />th row of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />nd column is chosen is
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/d/76da33bad7bfda26ad29f4bbdf0278c7bde0d552.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{3} \times \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{6}\]" width="82" height="37" />
</p><p>Add those two together to get
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/1/94112e565703159cbc8443b1d20984d0a6e60d34.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{2}{6} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{3}{6} = \boxed{\textbf{(C)} \frac{1}{2}}\]" width="234" height="48" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>Alternatively, we may analyze this problem a little further. 
</p><p>First, we isolate the case where the rows are numbered 1 or 2. Notice that as listed before, the probability for picking a shaded square here is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/5/8f5f98380657e00df5d8281d93087ce901f6ec32.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{2}\]" width="9" height="37" /> because the column/row probabilities are the same, with the same number of shaded and non-shaded squares
</p><p><br />
Next we isolate the rows numbered 3 or 4. Note that the probability of picking the rows is same, because of our list up above. The columns, of course, still have the same probability. Because the number of shaded and non-shaded squares are equal, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/5/8f5f98380657e00df5d8281d93087ce901f6ec32.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{2}\]" width="9" height="37" />
Combining these we have a general probability of  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/f/bcfd67cc2601b2a56c84d402070819f0aa32614f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\boxed{\textbf{(C)} \frac{1}{2}}\]" width="53" height="48" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Similarly to Solution 1, we create two lists, one for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/0/4a008643cff8271116ac4264437602a0d0c4bdb3.png" class="latex" alt="$mod$" width="34" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> and one for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/0/4a008643cff8271116ac4264437602a0d0c4bdb3.png" class="latex" alt="$mod$" width="34" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />&#160;: (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/d/c4d1a182caf92cb8dd79baa41036257913dd2646.png" class="latex" alt="$1, 2, 3, 2, 3, 1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="94" height="16" />) and (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/0/6b0212072d6f2e1b419133f7821d6271e2bbbe49.png" class="latex" alt="$1, 2, 3, 1, 2, 4$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="95" height="16" />). Next, we make a list of the coordinates we want for the shaded boxes. (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/7/ad75edba938ba87ff4be905c48706b1f83e3e7dc.png" class="latex" alt="$1,1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="25" height="15" />), (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/4/6c44bd27931d72780474233e818b567de93a7ffa.png" class="latex" alt="$1,3$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="25" height="16" />), (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/6/176f88af23f69cae3be6b20a50f6f46a6c7875d9.png" class="latex" alt="$2,2$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="25" height="15" />), (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/e/16e9a931991c6c703de8abbc1f48a60bb6dfdaad.png" class="latex" alt="$3,1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="25" height="16" />), (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/9/15997e96256d403b8008c09b97e59e2417752569.png" class="latex" alt="$3,3$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="25" height="16" />), (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/2/502b2c2316cfc08a9a57d1232b6d1048e38dfbcb.png" class="latex" alt="$2,4$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="26" height="15" />). For each coordinate pair, we find the probability for it to occur. In order, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/5/d85352e655245178503272555de56e3bb0a7976d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{9}, \frac{1}{18}, \frac{1}{9}, \frac{1}{9}, \frac{1}{18}, \frac{1}{18}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="144" height="37" />. Sum these to get the answer of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/c/7fc2922f16df9d1668284d686b078c4f4e77f864.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C)} \frac{1}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="53" height="48" />
</p><p>~Dynosol
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>We take everything modulo <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> first. We get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> mod <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> mod <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> mod <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, twice. Therefore, the probability of any given column is equal. We take everything modulo <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> now. We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/0/6b0212072d6f2e1b419133f7821d6271e2bbbe49.png" class="latex" alt="$1, 2, 3, 1, 2, 4$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="95" height="16" /> mod <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> are different colored squares, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> are also different colored squares. Therefore, every single one of them has the same probability. 
</p><p>Therefore, our probability for a white square or a shaded square is equal, and therefore, the probability the square is shaded <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/5/ae53e810e832c865d14fb08ab0d71935f70c11a6.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed {(C)\frac {1}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="55" height="49" />
</p><p>~Arcticturn
</p>
