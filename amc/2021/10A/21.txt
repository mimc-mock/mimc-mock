<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/2/8029902caf855d940e465d04ff40101d8850c3e0.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCDEF$" width="86" height="13" /> be an equiangular hexagon. The lines <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/b/26b5cce32b3ba0fff61b9ce3db1da0146e9901d8.png" class="latex" alt="$AB, CD,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="70" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/2/c128243ea8125d0bb007b8132dcfa44461df73a7.png" class="latex" alt="$EF$" width="28" height="12" /> determine a triangle with area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/4/fe45e92ea72cdab15e186a2a5287a906fab52cc6.png" class="latex" alt="$192\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="52" height="18" />, and the lines <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/c/a4c4423fa03c28a09062170b6ba5cbc6b64d96f7.png" class="latex" alt="$BC, DE,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="70" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/3/193c5caa0ee1174e95025208873f5495493a2059.png" class="latex" alt="$FA$" width="27" height="13" /> determine a triangle with area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/b/92bd41cebf35cdd035b945481bb3925afc55e9fd.png" class="latex" alt="$324\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="52" height="18" />. The perimeter of hexagon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/2/8029902caf855d940e465d04ff40101d8850c3e0.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCDEF$" width="86" height="13" /> can be expressed as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/6/8063945efc80d504432f1ade6b77d4b899040215.png" class="latex" alt="$m +n\sqrt{p}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="73" height="18" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/4/5f4bf5eb49eb976370c998db03e26d528d21dd3e.png" class="latex" alt="$m, n,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="39" height="11" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/f/36f73fc1312ee0349b3f3a0f3bd9eb5504339011.png" class="latex" alt="$p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" /> are positive integers and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/f/36f73fc1312ee0349b3f3a0f3bd9eb5504339011.png" class="latex" alt="$p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" /> is not divisible by the square of any prime. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/2/8f23423034093484df72a3171da00744b1e11963.png" class="latex" alt="$m + n + p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="80" height="14" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/9/8d9ccc7cb043d1aa5c98b3a715e3e3fe6eb542ce.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)} ~47\qquad\textbf{(B)} ~52\qquad\textbf{(C)} ~55\qquad\textbf{(D)} ~58\qquad\textbf{(E)} ~63$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="394" height="17" />
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/f/21fbd1066ec981b0e6726b58fbbfecb6600411aa.png" class="latex" alt="$P,Q,R,X,Y,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="107" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/a/3ead47fb9fb4a4c273feee398f72ff2a09702b84.png" class="latex" alt="$Z$" width="13" height="12" /> be the intersections <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/4/ab4e98152e8d7cdff30e1d448c796c2bf0dc9604.png" class="latex" alt="$\overleftrightarrow{AB}\cap\overleftrightarrow{CD},\overleftrightarrow{CD}\cap\overleftrightarrow{EF},\overleftrightarrow{EF}\cap\overleftrightarrow{AB},\overleftrightarrow{BC}\cap\overleftrightarrow{DE},\overleftrightarrow{DE}\cap\overleftrightarrow{FA},$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="431" height="24" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/4/784cb64f62ae625be42a43c6115ce0e1d6f8572c.png" class="latex" alt="$\overleftrightarrow{FA}\cap\overleftrightarrow{BC},$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="84" height="24" /> respectively.
</p><p>The sum of the interior angles of any hexagon is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/f/dcffd42e375e1574d82f8f9d019a13abc9d0d27f.png" class="latex" alt="$720^\circ.$" width="38" height="13" /> Since hexagon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/2/8029902caf855d940e465d04ff40101d8850c3e0.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCDEF$" width="86" height="13" /> is equiangular, each of its interior angles is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/9/2c9f36a46476f04d37cc92446c1286914e82e8a6.png" class="latex" alt="$720^\circ\div6=120^\circ.$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="132" height="16" /> By angle chasing, we conclude that the interior angles of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/d/a0da861fbbfc24db3f7071eaa81112759818e924.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle PBC,\triangle QDE,\triangle RFA,\triangle XCD,\triangle YEF,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="336" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/8/b18faaad54ca91709598db459a1f49491f9ed08d.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ZAB$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="60" height="18" /> are all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/c/d7c2c21e4d6e19c4a002d68ad75ba8942318b14d.png" class="latex" alt="$60^\circ.$" width="29" height="13" /> Therefore, these triangles are all equilateral triangles, from which <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/a/b4a455d533200d7dfbd00731fa8bd9e8796fb803.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle PQR$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="59" height="17" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/3/3039f01ba9e41f5490b7130e344be554015a25b9.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle XYZ$" width="60" height="13" /> are both equilateral triangles.
</p><p>We are given that
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/5/5f5b55f5b4fa69ea2c5266b0c2cb7fc1f0ca9f17.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{alignat*}{8} [PQR]&amp;=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}\cdot PQ^2&amp;&amp;=192\sqrt3, \\ [XYZ]&amp;=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}\cdot YZ^2&amp;&amp;=324\sqrt3, \end{alignat*}" width="234" height="88" />
so we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/f/cdf53d9765ec8d47166bc42e47eceba71b72feef.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ=16\sqrt3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="97" height="23" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/b/a7ba3be640b89c1adc4eefbf47b76f445c225071.png" class="latex" alt="$YZ=36,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="78" height="18" /> respectively.
</p><p>By equilateral triangles and segment addition, we find the perimeter of hexagon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/f/5/6f56dd7984513e66daf1136818700f6ce3098d5e.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCDEF:$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="99" height="14" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/5/365eff60f48d230c4d5872a99c6da83a9eea73ea.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} AB+BC+CD+DE+EF+FA&amp;=AZ+PC+CD+DQ+YF+FA \\ &amp;=(YF+FA+AZ)+(PC+CD+DQ) \\ &amp;=YZ+PQ \\ &amp;=36+16\sqrt{3}. \end{align*}" width="619" height="94" />
Finally, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/8/7185aab1c2f20ffe6ce13eda715af78f61d1543b.png" class="latex" alt="$36+16+3=\boxed{\textbf{(C)} ~55}.$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="185" height="30" />
</p><p>~sugar_rush ~MRENTHUSIASM
</p>
