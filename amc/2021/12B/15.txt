<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" /> be the midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/4/0948661e822f2a953c43a57ac9e40b2734476de4.png" class="latex" alt="$CD$" width="29" height="12" />. Noting that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/f/1/6f1bf55dee68ba4123ca736e3b4b2121ffebc98a.png" class="latex" alt="$AED$" width="43" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/a/aaa4de95cc6371d125d5cf773cdd9f9913dd1d8e.png" class="latex" alt="$120$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> - <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/1/651ba418a810d971dbc8a326d49a71fe1541fae0.png" class="latex" alt="$30$" width="17" height="12" /> - <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/1/651ba418a810d971dbc8a326d49a71fe1541fae0.png" class="latex" alt="$30$" width="17" height="12" /> triangles because of the equilateral triangles, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/a/f3a4959d590e0b4b2e7b68607c169013d45852ee.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[AM=\sqrt{AD^2-MD^2}=\sqrt{12-1}=\sqrt{11} \implies [ACD]=\sqrt{11}.\]" width="484" height="21" /> Also, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/2/4c27a114deef1af31f794081d3a23b3667c22bea.png" class="latex" alt="$[AED]=2\cdot2\cdot\frac{1}{2}\cdot\sin{120^\circ}=\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="259" height="38" /> (or split <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/8/1/18167d788fd698322ba516cf8bfdba2c931fa17d.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AED$" width="59" height="13" /> into two <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/c/47c78b85058ee5e17f88e2cde302e48aad826b35.png" class="latex" alt="$30-60-90$" width="98" height="12" /> triangles) and so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/9/06996dd19c95a2accaf487e4b1c9dc965f0f47be.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[[ABCDE]=[ACD]+2[AED]=\sqrt{11}+2\sqrt{3}=\sqrt{11}+\sqrt{12} \implies \boxed{\textbf{(D)} ~23}.\]" width="563" height="28" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/2/fd2998f24f29241b10d5af45de73c7f2a207fda6.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] /* Made by samrocksnature, adapted by Tucker, then adjusted by samrocksnature again, then adjusted by erics118 xD*/ pair A=(-2.4638,4.10658); pair B=(-4,2.6567453480756127); pair C=(-3.47132,0.6335248637894945); pair D=(-1.464483379039766,0.6335248637894945); pair E=(-0.956630463955801,2.6567453480756127); pair F=(-1.85,2); pair G=(-3.1,2); draw(A--G--A--F, lightgray); draw(B--F--C, lightgray); draw(E--G--D, lightgray); dot(F^^G, lightgray); draw(A--B--C--D--E--A); draw(A--C--A--D); label(&quot;A&quot;,A,N); label(&quot;B&quot;,B,W); label(&quot;C&quot;,C,S); label(&quot;D&quot;,D,S); label(&quot;E&quot;,E,dir(0)); dot(A^^B^^C^^D^^E); [/asy]" width="225" height="252" />
</p><p>Draw diagonals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/7/a179ff2638e4799cadd820db205c2beff6299ce9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC$" width="27" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc56862b1fdb71204894da278e42ef75de73a40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AD$" width="28" height="13" /> to split the pentagon into three parts. We can compute the area for each triangle and sum them up at the end. For triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/4/70422100db8de33f8e5e0ab5062c5bb6a5ac969d.png" class="latex" alt="$ADE$" width="43" height="13" />, they each have area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/f/f2f96dac01d31840db9bd2f60aba4953e4d2a4e7.png" class="latex" alt="$2\cdot\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{4\sqrt{3}}{4}=\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="134" height="41" />. For triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/e/6ce2cd123db24a7b8373999aea7a98927e3b3c81.png" class="latex" alt="$ACD$" width="43" height="13" />, we can see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/e/78e294cf759352ae4a66e75d69132f0bbb4b86b5.png" class="latex" alt="$AC=AD=2\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="139" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/f/e2fb6f4572576df0b7fd91965771bba13959aeb3.png" class="latex" alt="$CD=2$" width="62" height="12" />. Using Pythagorean Theorem, the altitude for this triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/0/040e0891016ffa86cb136e30e6dcc4796f3c34f2.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{11}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />, so the area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/0/040e0891016ffa86cb136e30e6dcc4796f3c34f2.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{11}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />. Adding each part up, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/b/20bfa8544ca28820d6fdec2919b305c599c9bca3.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{3}+\sqrt{11}=\sqrt{12}+\sqrt{11} \implies \boxed{\textbf{(D)} ~23}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="316" height="28" />.
</p>
