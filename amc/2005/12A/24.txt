<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/5/c151ce8bc92075f81ed260702b548e5bb34168d5.png" class="latex" alt="$P(x)=(x-1)(x-2)(x-3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="229" height="18" />. For how many <a href="/wiki/index.php/Polynomial" title="Polynomial">polynomials</a> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/d/d9dfe7c7a0b887097fa2227a230fc915ba2e5477.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="37" height="18" /> does there exist a polynomial <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/e/a3eeefa367883375994fd2e1f575d088bce9d8b1.png" class="latex" alt="$R(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="37" height="18" /> of degree 3 such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/9/049eb754cfb7440116fe90a7fdfff9d7ac36e9e0.png" class="latex" alt="$P(Q(x))=P(x) \cdot R(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="180" height="18" />?
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/7/c1762798d43623fc8488246524853cf2dea6fb9a.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm {(A) } 19 \qquad \mathrm {(B) } 22 \qquad \mathrm {(C) } 24 \qquad \mathrm {(D) } 27 \qquad \mathrm {(E) } 32$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="359" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>We can write the problem as
</p>
<div style="text-align:center;">
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/8/a5868fd8e0c03148f154822e2fee074d9d4bc80b.png" class="latex" alt="$P(Q(x))=(Q(x)-1)(Q(x)-2)(Q(x)-3)=P(x)\cdot R(x)=(x-1)(x-2)(x-3)\cdot R(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="701" height="18" />.
</p>
</div>
<p><br />
Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/6/856bf449004225c6ac0a1a052eebec8f1fa5893c.png" class="latex" alt="$\deg P(x) = 3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="102" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/c/9fcc6e2072ce95a512dc83460f6894fd29fff055.png" class="latex" alt="$\deg R(x) = 3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="102" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/b/55bd492589fd991278636e37878139f8e5b0daf7.png" class="latex" alt="$\deg P(x)\cdot R(x) = 6$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="153" height="18" />. Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/a/fca84247d716035e9893800c0b6727ba12eced5d.png" class="latex" alt="$\deg P(Q(x)) = 6$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="130" height="18" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/8/5d88c03d793abf9e323e6f757491f16fb3938ebb.png" class="latex" alt="$\deg Q(x) = 2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="102" height="18" />.
</p>
<div style="text-align:center;">
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/8/eb86126cea2f82cdf2519a02613bcc63e7986b13.png" class="latex" alt="$P(Q(1))=(Q(1)-1)(Q(1)-2)(Q(1)-3)=P(1)\cdot R(1)=0,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="488" height="18" /><br /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/e/8ce4bc08e399565a7039ec3c889b91eb0c1cc72f.png" class="latex" alt="$P(Q(2))=(Q(2)-1)(Q(2)-2)(Q(2)-3)=P(2)\cdot R(2)=0,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="488" height="18" /><br /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/8/b382fca6a24197434b9f2bd8dc39e278bac4ae7c.png" class="latex" alt="$P(Q(3))=(Q(3)-1)(Q(3)-2)(Q(3)-3)=P(3)\cdot R(3)=0.$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="488" height="18" />
</p>
</div>
<p>Hence, we conclude <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/6/86660103953001c38b257a47ae96369ee8f2769a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="36" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/d/acdf1c49c6edbf6ffdf28065f6b71c1f0d4cc1c5.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="36" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/4/ab4fd9bf46d9d57b5b49c7c96485e858812c9ec4.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="36" height="18" /> must each be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />. Since a <a href="/wiki/index.php/Quadratic_equation" title="Quadratic equation">quadratic</a> is uniquely determined by three points, there can be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/0/8/c08be0a558bc90853ba9291ff77f861e557ff082.png" class="latex" alt="$3*3*3 = 27$" width="104" height="12" /> different quadratics <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/d/d9dfe7c7a0b887097fa2227a230fc915ba2e5477.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="37" height="18" /> after each of the values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/6/86660103953001c38b257a47ae96369ee8f2769a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="36" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/d/acdf1c49c6edbf6ffdf28065f6b71c1f0d4cc1c5.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="36" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/4/ab4fd9bf46d9d57b5b49c7c96485e858812c9ec4.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="36" height="18" /> are chosen.
</p><p><br />
However, we have included <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/d/d9dfe7c7a0b887097fa2227a230fc915ba2e5477.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="37" height="18" /> which are not quadratics: lines. Namely,
</p>
<div style="text-align:center;">
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/f/1df6884e06b66d683f8aafe5e5d3d28dc8344ebd.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(1)=Q(2)=Q(3)=1 \Rightarrow Q(x)=1,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="299" height="18" /><br /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/1/7918e1ab8053d67242115469954664fad31b0cfa.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(1)=Q(2)=Q(3)=2 \Rightarrow Q(x)=2,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="299" height="18" /><br /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/b/32b584e6ab95f71516cbd94d821b36d84b71a7a1.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(1)=Q(2)=Q(3)=3 \Rightarrow Q(x)=3,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="299" height="18" /><br /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/c/7cc88bf598b8a8519b72834d5a7beddaff03fa5f.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(1)=1, Q(2)=2, Q(3)=3 \Rightarrow Q(x)=x,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="335" height="18" /><br /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/3/983aad5b7418ed25229af39d2c5b5b37644966d6.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(1)=3, Q(2)=2, Q(3)=1 \Rightarrow Q(x)=4-x.$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="365" height="18" />
</p>
</div>
<p>Clearly, we could not have included any other constant functions. For any linear function, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/b/a0b5f7d81e9031cf6f8deaffa28565f7896c3905.png" class="latex" alt="$2\cdot Q(2) = Q(1) + Q(3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="180" height="18" /> because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/d/acdf1c49c6edbf6ffdf28065f6b71c1f0d4cc1c5.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="36" height="18" /> is y-value of the midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/c/d8cfb9899809d83893fe89503ac6e04e6186c882.png" class="latex" alt="$(1, Q(1))$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/3/1a3a2a0c900f650a6a8125e898c26656446874fb.png" class="latex" alt="$(3, Q(3))$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" />. So we have not included any other linear functions. Therefore, the desired answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/9/1a9422ef23438d007fada4493c72c19386d29fab.png" class="latex" alt="$27 - 5 = \boxed{\textbf{(B) }22}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="138" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quicker_Solution">Quicker Solution</span></h2>
<p>We see that 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/9/b6925b8e3251351a0599c70385f97012995ad295.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[P(Q(x))=(Q(x)-1)(Q(x)-2)(Q(x)-3)=P(x)\cdot R(x)=(x-1)(x-2)(x-3)\cdot R(x).\]" width="641" height="18" />
Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/c/89c9b2315aa14b7eb4c6b8dca73be75f18b2a676.png" class="latex" alt="$P(x) | P(Q(x))$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="109" height="18" />.  Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/0/3f0062c3634c2f0e4ab5902b13578109cc2bebe9.png" class="latex" alt="$\deg Q = 2,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="82" height="16" /> we must have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/0/0e0767e67bf74873a8827ad305a595995daa2e6c.png" class="latex" alt="$x-1, x-2, x-3$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="140" height="16" /> divide <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/9/a391549fa05320091d96f9251e4ac2e31376eb43.png" class="latex" alt="$P(Q(x))$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="65" height="18" />. So, we pair them off with one of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/3/d13314b6a51306958c363146a9042a0ee85cfb7c.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(x)-1, Q(x)-2,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="152" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/d/cbd92d752b24b83748c52c8c0d18d5e30b8d633c.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(x)-3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="69" height="18" /> to see that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/8/dc8831c7b0e610f9dfe634b5373ace5d1fdc7666.png" class="latex" alt="$3!+3 \cdot 2 \cdot \binom{3}{2} = 24$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="160" height="53" /> without restrictions. (Note that this count was made by pairing off linear factors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/f/6efaaf74e576202fad965134bc49c6a34aac4c30.png" class="latex" alt="$P(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="37" height="18" /> with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/3/d13314b6a51306958c363146a9042a0ee85cfb7c.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(x)-1, Q(x)-2,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="152" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/d/cbd92d752b24b83748c52c8c0d18d5e30b8d633c.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(x)-3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="69" height="18" />, and also note that the degree of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> is 2.) However, we have two functions which are constant, which are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/7/7e76ea667fa1d105e3f9f918726491df7f134cae.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(x) = x$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="73" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/4/f34b50eebf733e32320671565b37f752bdcab6da.png" class="latex" alt="$Q(x) = 4-x.$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="108" height="18" />  So, we subtract <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> to get a final answer of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/f/2bfc4a08efeb3d08a03e17ef9df2e8bf9dabc1cf.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{22} \implies \boxed{B}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="105" height="24" />. 
</p><p>~Williamgolly
</p><p><br />
</p>
