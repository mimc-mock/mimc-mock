<p>For how many positive integers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> less than or equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/9/e290f2cba0ba0e11c0b91630da15a408da2fcf1c.png" class="latex" alt="$24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/f/63f344e7d8b14a7886fc7fb72da53cd0a3271dcc.png" class="latex" alt="$n!$" width="14" height="13" /> evenly divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/5/1d5444a7b3d132eeed36b80590cb208a94d41ded.png" class="latex" alt="$1 + 2 + \cdots + n?$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="125" height="14" /> 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/7/bd7bb8bd0feb9ffd5c15911fef73ff0cff616ebb.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 8 \qquad \textbf{(B) } 12 \qquad \textbf{(C) } 16 \qquad \textbf{(D) } 17 \qquad \textbf{(E) } 21$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="392" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/f/7df640881b9090a4ae259e368af508b15994c990.png" class="latex" alt="$1 + 2 + \cdots + n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="210" height="38" />, the condition is equivalent to having an integer value for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/b/9/7b9845c6f149b9822eebc687628cb14605203b73.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{n!} {\frac{n(n+1)}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -21px" width="50" height="47" />. This reduces, when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/6/0965ac6a39c0b4d67771c31210b98312ae2e4f01.png" class="latex" alt="$n\ge 1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="43" height="14" />, to having an integer value for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/9/ae9dbe2dd195d0f68106d290405214dc882078b8.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2(n-1)!}{n+1}$" style="vertical-align: -14px" width="73" height="40" />. This fraction is an 
integer unless <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/3/0e3efd9b14723a92c2ae891fe27780d5f8e2b215.png" class="latex" alt="$n+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="41" height="13" /> is an odd prime. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> odd primes less than or equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/9/e290f2cba0ba0e11c0b91630da15a408da2fcf1c.png" class="latex" alt="$24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" />, so there 
</p><p>are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/a/adafee294ffd308a9016167895aab064467227ac.png" class="latex" alt="$24 - 8 =  \boxed{\textbf{(C) }16}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="140" height="30" /> numbers less than or equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/9/e290f2cba0ba0e11c0b91630da15a408da2fcf1c.png" class="latex" alt="$24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> that satisfy the condition.
</p>
