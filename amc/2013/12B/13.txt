<p>The internal angles of quadrilateral <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> form an arithmetic progression. Triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/a/a6adfd3ad27a2dddeb0af7131d73c33c86991e93.png" class="latex" alt="$ABD$" width="43" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/5/cf51b47182ee2e945de88c04f653d01d4919d104.png" class="latex" alt="$DCB$" width="44" height="12" /> are similar with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/d/52d190bc51c7eb705947bbd9259577d6eb54fe27.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle DBA = \angle DCB$" width="137" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/5/9f52b42a40f5576208b729abd4ecfe8af1da6d88.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle ADB = \angle CBD$" width="138" height="13" />. Moreover, the angles in each of these two triangles also form an arithmetic progression. In degrees, what is the largest possible sum of the two largest angles of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4ac89076a95bb9fc6666fea645a4019ba029dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 210 \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 220 \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 230 \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 240 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 250$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="439" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Since the angles of Quadrilateral <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> form an arithmetic sequence, we can assign each angle with the value <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/3/b83c9c3a8da8388f2c9107e68f8fe59683cd0cdc.png" class="latex" alt="$a+d$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="41" height="14" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/8/9b89e2225617aed18d1a3bb39906075c99699176.png" class="latex" alt="$a+2d$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="50" height="14" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/0/5/c05e574bb2b4ff8d69713613c435fabea7ec0d27.png" class="latex" alt="$a+3d$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="50" height="14" />. Also, since these angles form an arithmetic progression, we can reason out that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/6/4c6e0887b124ea770754a9601159fc3eae26f704.png" class="latex" alt="$(a)+(a+3d)=(a+d)+(a+2d)=180$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="327" height="18" />.
</p><p>For the sake of simplicity, lets rename the angles of each similar triangle. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e2724de42c158ed4ab1a2f3f013c2d1bef3b08d.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle ADB = \angle CBD = \alpha$" width="173" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/7/0a7094f57946f18772b51abdef9787c4e42f5112.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle DBA = \angle DCB = \beta$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="173" height="16" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/7/db7890dd0eaac39dc1bc296828754ea6304249d4.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle CDB = \angle BAD = \gamma$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="172" height="16" />.
</p><p>Now the four angles of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/b/fdb63b9e51abe6bbb16acfb5d7b773ddbb5bf4a8.png" class="latex" alt="$\beta$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="16" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/c/6cc5ed23e7b3ce8f560fe6e93ac88d6c9055900d.png" class="latex" alt="$\alpha + \beta$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="16" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/9/66981fa3920210c6ad8dbe5e968783d5dd7520c3.png" class="latex" alt="$\gamma$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/2/a42c68d7b4f92dc77928544d9a8cfcf2b03d0c2d.png" class="latex" alt="$\alpha + \gamma$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="14" />.
</p><p>As for the similar triangles, we can name their angles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/4/114a35d250b37e35346df2bab215b5ce2c18579e.png" class="latex" alt="$y+b$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="40" height="16" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/1/841a58c20dcb88f82768a4a219f6e56b894797af.png" class="latex" alt="$y+2b$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="49" height="16" />. Therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/b/13b7b3b6c2ba7f4e1324bf334d8e39bb75b6228d.png" class="latex" alt="$y+y+b+y+2b=180$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="193" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/8/a181a0f09482558879c23d2acfa83864948d47e1.png" class="latex" alt="$y+b=60$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="81" height="16" />. Because these 3 angles are each equal to one of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/b/7ebc9435cf037e230c3dea173d52a1c4a4172c10.png" class="latex" alt="$\alpha, \beta, \gamma$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="49" height="16" />, we know that one of these three angles is equal to 60 degrees.
</p><p>Now we we use trial and error. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/2/bc2034c50540db11bbaefc23f08bb3dfb60dbb5d.png" class="latex" alt="$\alpha = 60^{\circ}$" width="60" height="13" />. Then the angles of ABCD are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/b/fdb63b9e51abe6bbb16acfb5d7b773ddbb5bf4a8.png" class="latex" alt="$\beta$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="16" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/a/f/2af77b7cacf4f791e6bf8e9c274eefd8a05f6387.png" class="latex" alt="$60^{\circ} + \beta$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="58" height="16" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/9/66981fa3920210c6ad8dbe5e968783d5dd7520c3.png" class="latex" alt="$\gamma$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/9/cc95f39e8e8a70acd76cb3bf70cddaa707b9effe.png" class="latex" alt="$60^{\circ} + \gamma$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="58" height="16" />. Since these four angles add up to 360, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/2/632f48f0e86f6efcc0bcf8efe3ea9259b79362ce.png" class="latex" alt="$\beta + \gamma= 120$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="94" height="16" />. If we list them in increasing value, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/b/fdb63b9e51abe6bbb16acfb5d7b773ddbb5bf4a8.png" class="latex" alt="$\beta$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="16" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/9/66981fa3920210c6ad8dbe5e968783d5dd7520c3.png" class="latex" alt="$\gamma$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/a/f/2af77b7cacf4f791e6bf8e9c274eefd8a05f6387.png" class="latex" alt="$60^{\circ} + \beta$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="58" height="16" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/9/cc95f39e8e8a70acd76cb3bf70cddaa707b9effe.png" class="latex" alt="$60^{\circ} + \gamma$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="58" height="16" />. Note that this is the only sequence that works because the common difference is less than 45. So, this would give us the four angles 45, 75, 105, and 135. In this case, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/b/7ebc9435cf037e230c3dea173d52a1c4a4172c10.png" class="latex" alt="$\alpha, \beta, \gamma$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="49" height="16" /> also form an arithmetic sequence with 45, 60, and 75, and the largest two angles of the quadrilateral add up to 240 which is an answer choice.
</p><p>If we apply the same reasoning to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/b/fdb63b9e51abe6bbb16acfb5d7b773ddbb5bf4a8.png" class="latex" alt="$\beta$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/9/66981fa3920210c6ad8dbe5e968783d5dd7520c3.png" class="latex" alt="$\gamma$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" />, we would get the sum of the highest two angles as 220, which works but is lower than 240. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/f/3ffa2b9b315ee5a817cb9adeef77289ca65aeef7.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) }240}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="75" height="28" /> is the correct answer.
</p>
