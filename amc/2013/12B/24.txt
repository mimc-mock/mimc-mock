<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/6/d065bafb8e619f52de298260129fdbf274b9dd95.png" class="latex" alt="$BN=x$" width="65" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/1/5c1127d70d3933723ec8d29b3b7c3d3f98a6a1a9.png" class="latex" alt="$NA=y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="63" height="16" />. From the conditions, let's deduct some convenient conditions that seem sufficient to solve the problem.
</p><p><br />
</p><p><b><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" /> is the midpoint of side <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/7/a179ff2638e4799cadd820db205c2beff6299ce9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC$" width="27" height="13" />.</b>
</p><p>This implies that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/b/b2ba7dad4869237c73cb3dc4267313a3381a152f.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABX]=[CBX]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="132" height="18" />. Given that angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/5/c25098016dac91dc30e5febffb0d0e73a92f96e6.png" class="latex" alt="$ABX$" width="44" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" /> degrees and angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/9/4296dc042be00297e7a4f47ecaf47cc389b2a6eb.png" class="latex" alt="$BXC$" width="45" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/a/aaa4de95cc6371d125d5cf773cdd9f9913dd1d8e.png" class="latex" alt="$120$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> degrees, we can use the area formula to get
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/1/4d1bc5f216dc38fc5ebf0638b35a7e83cbfa6d1f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{2}(x+y)x \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = \frac{1}{2} x \cdot CX \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\]" width="224" height="41" />
</p><p>So, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/7/787e0c7c5f9f9f91e882eb9fa277f8586a4a5347.png" class="latex" alt="$x+y=CX$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="96" height="16" /> .....(1)
</p><p><br />
</p><p><b><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/3/ba369b33271c6fb52789f86292cc68559a2e0411.png" class="latex" alt="$CN$" width="31" height="12" /> is angle bisector.</b>
</p><p>In the triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" />, one has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/3/f43d7902cce8cfbbb354b81bfe830cb9fe4ebf37.png" class="latex" alt="$BC/AC=x/y$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="117" height="18" />, therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/e/ddea315f28eff111cc6f270f2ef9a374c439f15d.png" class="latex" alt="$BC=2x/y$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="90" height="18" />.....(2)
</p><p>Furthermore, triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/6/036bf5e5bf3938a62201c696cf6051b5509be494.png" class="latex" alt="$BCN$" width="45" height="12" /> is similar to triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/0/c7025468f3a21a83141e4a290bdd1c6bfde833f3.png" class="latex" alt="$MCX$" width="49" height="12" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/9/bd9c43252cf3710e19271be8a1bd91c87a21d38c.png" class="latex" alt="$BC/CM=CN/CX$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="164" height="18" />, therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/3/4a3e892b1505161773998503e7bdf2167b1cd513.png" class="latex" alt="$BC = (CX+x)/CX = (2x+y)/(x+y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="324" height="18" />....(3)
</p><p>By (2) and (3) and the subtraction law of ratios, we get 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/1/8d16b0be96cb7c1355717d1097b73b142a9750ac.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[BC=2x/y = (2x+y)/(y+x) = y/x\]" width="299" height="18" />
</p><p>Therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/5/5153a0fcff2b385ac51e194e5ceb0d52faaeeb45.png" class="latex" alt="$2x^2=y^2$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="66" height="18" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/f/1ef7914645b8f88450a98b52d9d0e8938d18c9a9.png" class="latex" alt="$y=\sqrt{2}x$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="68" height="20" />. So <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/f/a8fe551b0e08521b32270c63d6f7d58c6a1179b9.png" class="latex" alt="$BC = 2x/(\sqrt{2}x) = \sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="179" height="21" />.
</p><p>Finally, using the law of cosine for triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/6/036bf5e5bf3938a62201c696cf6051b5509be494.png" class="latex" alt="$BCN$" width="45" height="12" />, we get

</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/b/c5bbf4b26fd67dfacb45e0e5197c4c6d43764447.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x^2 = \frac{2}{5+3\sqrt{2}} = \boxed{\textbf{(A) }\frac{10-6\sqrt{2}}{7}}.\]" width="262" height="53" />
