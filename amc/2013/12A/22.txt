<p>A palindrome is a nonnegative integer number that reads the same forwards and backwards when written in base 10 with no leading zeros. A 6-digit palindrome <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> is chosen uniformly at random. What is the probability that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/d/4bd92e62d4bd9a3e01a01965d179d96759e92099.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{n}{11}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="33" /> is also a palindrome?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/b/e4bdd5ad1d2db90433aef2f8561466bef8191258.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)} \ \frac{8}{25} \qquad \textbf{(B)} \ \frac{33}{100} \qquad \textbf{(C)} \ \frac{7}{20} \qquad \textbf{(D)} \ \frac{9}{25} \qquad \textbf{(E)} \ \frac{11}{30}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="422" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>By working backwards, we can multiply 5-digit palindromes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/4/e2443b57033f09a4f1e044acee0d69a6bd1dce25.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCBA$" width="71" height="13" /> by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/8/c6878713578626763c38433b3f4c8c2205ad0c15.png" class="latex" alt="$11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" />, giving a 6-digit palindrome:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/f/17f0fb4bfcd854294b08dc9b466e53a6f0d49202.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{A (A+B) (B+C) (B+C) (A+B) A}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="287" height="21" />
</p><p>Note that if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/c/5ecae5e5c90b2fd0025fa5b041001bacd3e9d8a9.png" class="latex" alt="$A + B &gt;= 10$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="106" height="14" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/c/d8cbf3f8d39eea712c417895414cfff453da6ada.png" class="latex" alt="$B + C &gt;= 10$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="107" height="14" />, then the symmetry will be broken by carried 1s
</p><p>Simply count the combinations of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/d/59db3616e539878054b9dd007abeb26d5fcde87d.png" class="latex" alt="$(A, B, C)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="71" height="18" /> for which <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/d/e4d79377cad11754265a75378dd0291fa77f69f6.png" class="latex" alt="$A + B &lt; 10$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="92" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/a/7da1bc3fecd38c31a8c6a3c6cc19316ed2d13e13.png" class="latex" alt="$B + C &lt; 10$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="93" height="14" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/3/da3d600d21b2b1343040ecb85db0e354a60e96a5.png" class="latex" alt="$A = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="46" height="13" /> implies <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> possible <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> (0 through 8), for each of which there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/7/247b583c42638f0113eceefdc48335af1bcc373f.png" class="latex" alt="$10, 9, 8, 7, 6, 5, 4, 3, 2$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="155" height="16" /> possible C, respectively. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/c/b1cb5bdce2d06d2419066a2b55ab01d824217bd2.png" class="latex" alt="$54$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="13" /> valid palindromes when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/3/da3d600d21b2b1343040ecb85db0e354a60e96a5.png" class="latex" alt="$A = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="46" height="13" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/c/e1c13d77e2b5ede10a622c2229043191de82ce17.png" class="latex" alt="$A = 2$" width="46" height="13" /> implies <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> possible <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> (0 through 7), for each of which there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/9/859bba9a7fa86f1b8c63f963db3f04a2b07c219a.png" class="latex" alt="$10, 9, 8, 7, 6, 5, 4, 3$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="138" height="16" /> possible C, respectively. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/1/5e1d1611143f5792dc0f61f3b130af88cb790c04.png" class="latex" alt="$52$" width="17" height="12" /> valid palindromes when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/c/e1c13d77e2b5ede10a622c2229043191de82ce17.png" class="latex" alt="$A = 2$" width="46" height="13" />
</p><p>Following this pattern, the total is
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/1/751732fa5c87bc40f082fc9446a3aeb8de164ddf.png" class="latex" alt="$54 + 52 + 49 + 45 + 40 + 34 + 27 + 19 + 10 = 330$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="392" height="14" />
</p><p>6-digit palindromes are of the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/3/2c390e5afc9f65144e8e8b7ea9612b52665a56cf.png" class="latex" alt="$XYZZYX$" width="88" height="12" />, and the first digit cannot be a zero, so there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/1/281df5260bd50d9d56cd3a9c3067cb2e640316ce.png" class="latex" alt="$9 \cdot 10  \cdot 10 = 900$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="122" height="13" /> combinations of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/4/3e4510439cb4dbecbf5553774ee821efe36a8002.png" class="latex" alt="$(X, Y, Z)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="69" height="18" />
</p><p>So, the probability is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/c/abcf6d1d6581e735d88a24b860512fe0ec559fea.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{330}{900} = \frac{11}{30}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="76" height="37" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Note">Note</span></h3>
<p>You can more easily count the number of triples <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/d/59db3616e539878054b9dd007abeb26d5fcde87d.png" class="latex" alt="$(A, B, C)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="71" height="18" /> by noticing that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/1/e219e5da88ac8788b201ed76c9ce1a35521ec2cd.png" class="latex" alt="$9 - B$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="48" height="16" /> possible values for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/9/1290193b28ca9b897301be4a688949e49e38cbf6.png" class="latex" alt="$10 - B$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="57" height="16" /> possible values for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> once <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> is chosen. Summing over all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, the number is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/c/aaceb1f39d98f929ccb6fe93ca59808280f467d2.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[9\cdot 10 + 8\cdot 9 + \ldots + 1\cdot 2 = 2\left(\binom{2}{2} + \binom{3}{2} + \ldots + \binom{10}{2}\right).\]" width="470" height="53" />
By the hockey-stick identity, it is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/f/3af98bbf84dc20f8ebe4236c1b9d2f872c79c952.png" class="latex" alt="$2\binom{11}{3} = 330$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="109" height="55" />.
</p><p>~rayfish
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Let the palindrome be the form in the previous solution which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/3/2c390e5afc9f65144e8e8b7ea9612b52665a56cf.png" class="latex" alt="$XYZZYX$" width="88" height="12" />. It doesn't matter what <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/a/3ead47fb9fb4a4c273feee398f72ff2a09702b84.png" class="latex" alt="$Z$" width="13" height="12" /> is because it only affects the middle digit. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/c/bfcfee7bd8a5e15e5f6b7881c138de43651572a9.png" class="latex" alt="$90$" width="17" height="12" /> ways to pick <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/5/ce58e4af225c93d08606c26554caaa5ae32edeba.png" class="latex" alt="$Y$" width="14" height="12" />, and the only answer choice with denominator a factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/c/bfcfee7bd8a5e15e5f6b7881c138de43651572a9.png" class="latex" alt="$90$" width="17" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/a/9dac544706477520dbe9eb80a2200c319b59aad4.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(E)} \ \frac{11}{30}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="66" height="48" />.
</p>
