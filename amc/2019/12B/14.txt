<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> be the set of all positive integer divisors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/e/9ae4c9cacc6212f29f6454b88612f0a11694dea2.png" class="latex" alt="$100,000.$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="66" height="16" /> How many numbers are the product of two distinct elements of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/7/4d753026173a63cfed0ea918be191cc3b451677e.png" class="latex" alt="$S?$" width="20" height="12" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/f/a5fb2dde08302a76a0ca0e089f2527131bd523a8.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }98\qquad\textbf{(B) }100\qquad\textbf{(C) }117\qquad\textbf{(D) }119\qquad\textbf{(E) }121$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="434" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>The prime factorization of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/4/d545bb45d1f0199e4785046df6c11481c9164de4.png" class="latex" alt="$100,000$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="62" height="16" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/e/68ed7a132f8f6f0aaf5efec2bb6f3dd6757f282a.png" class="latex" alt="$2^5 \cdot 5^5$" width="44" height="15" />. Thus, we choose two numbers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/d/3ed0cc592e9b4e3a13523a53788ebd1384d07e42.png" class="latex" alt="$2^a5^b$" width="31" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/3/4033f34efdc5eaf6afcbbff57a987190f96eb915.png" class="latex" alt="$2^c5^d$" width="32" height="15" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/5/c65f47e94a6a83a3f8586cdb8f5b5b9ed9a679c4.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \le a,b,c,d \le 5$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="125" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/6/8d609893cbec7494d1fac548fc92368cc82d74c4.png" class="latex" alt="$(a,b) \neq (c,d)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="102" height="18" />, whose product is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/6/27655d0fd053ef0c4069636408ee9fea498cff0a.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{a+c}5^{b+d}$" width="65" height="15" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/9/589ae914e02768fb1be9202fb3dd647546d34683.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \le a+c \le 10$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="115" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/c/c8c30473996da68734363e05831ce8010cfaa677.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \le b+d \le 10$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="114" height="15" />.
</p><p>Notice that this is similar to choosing a divisor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/5/655c6f5a7b3a63584c89d950fbb459e7fef381cc.png" class="latex" alt="$100,000^2 = 2^{10}5^{10}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="139" height="18" />, which has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/6/3669cb2d22b01d324a66af6eeb543ca0e67c4a93.png" class="latex" alt="$(10+1)(10+1) = 121$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="177" height="18" /> divisors. However, some of the divisors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/f/8ff3da5186ba854e4a0c6cf6db8e2d901d153b70.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{10}5^{10}$" width="44" height="15" /> cannot be written as a product of two distinct divisors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/e/68ed7a132f8f6f0aaf5efec2bb6f3dd6757f282a.png" class="latex" alt="$2^5 \cdot 5^5$" width="44" height="15" />, namely: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/0/b80747c929cf8cf2a703a32c870e36dbc673ea80.png" class="latex" alt="$1 = 2^05^0$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="65" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/f/8ff3da5186ba854e4a0c6cf6db8e2d901d153b70.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{10}5^{10}$" width="44" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/b/b3b2422eb1e019642032c00b0c5d5982903fe45d.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{10}$" width="21" height="15" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/f/c5f680cdba5c60df18ad474bed509fa6e39a3759.png" class="latex" alt="$5^{10}$" width="21" height="15" />. The last two cannot be so written because the maximum factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/4/d545bb45d1f0199e4785046df6c11481c9164de4.png" class="latex" alt="$100,000$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="62" height="16" /> containing only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />s or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />s (and not both) is only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/7/5b7f45c960c681827d5d03d7b3c30811d3bfd77f.png" class="latex" alt="$2^5$" width="15" height="15" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/1/0e1baff6050db5d3e78ff320d0358d74adf9b153.png" class="latex" alt="$5^5$" width="15" height="15" />. Since the factors chosen must be distinct, the last two numbers cannot be so written because they would require <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/2/7f28e9466db13fec29ce218e06d00ffee6e668d5.png" class="latex" alt="$2^5 \cdot 2^5$" width="44" height="15" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/5/fe55235a2c6e912bb7ad9d5d533697ccb6aa84d4.png" class="latex" alt="$5^5 \cdot 5^5$" width="44" height="15" />. The first two would require <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/c/92c5770c5ac85900b35548289121eda8fbab3ad0.png" class="latex" alt="$1 \cdot 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="30" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/5/5c5d77a786e98c67bf839ea63f6967c6d30f33d7.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{5}5^{5} \cdot 2^{5}5^{5}$" width="77" height="15" />, respectively. This gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/3/af3bc80bc25f392bcbaee6368ed7e432060ab56e.png" class="latex" alt="$121-4 = 117$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="110" height="12" /> candidate numbers. It is not too hard to show that every number of the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/5/37583a9269a3aa87449a532c2ce1c4f9e0c1c354.png" class="latex" alt="$2^p5^q$" width="32" height="12" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/e/9de63f40c428c0fa3669d4c733778357a015013d.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \le p, q \le 10$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="101" height="16" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/7/f779d95c15f7e41de74cb767f52fe4e7b92c745e.png" class="latex" alt="$p,q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="26" height="11" /> are not both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />, can be written as a product of two distinct elements in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" />. Hence the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/1/261e5442a2352c402e97aa4749db407b13428e51.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) } 117}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="74" height="28" />.
</p>
