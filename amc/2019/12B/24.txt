<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/0/1d020174f178fd1697cc16033099d4bcc6730e07.png" class="latex" alt="$\omega=-\tfrac{1}{2}+\tfrac{1}{2}i\sqrt3.$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="128" height="23" /> Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> denote all points in the complex plane of the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/6/876b2135bc8f88b4597609080624e6a9042f90f4.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b\omega+c\omega^2,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="105" height="18" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/6/a3624c806aafa6e0ab9479030586ed99752a7421.png" class="latex" alt="$0\leq a \leq 1,0\leq b\leq 1,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="162" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/3/893be0caa16c25951a5cf0a4d7b8c6f1aa508248.png" class="latex" alt="$0\leq c\leq 1.$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="78" height="15" /> What is the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/0/3c080677594faf8b15ab300cac7e92f3f784a379.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } \frac{1}{2}\sqrt3 \qquad\textbf{(B) } \frac{3}{4}\sqrt3 \qquad\textbf{(C) } \frac{3}{2}\sqrt3\qquad\textbf{(D) } \frac{1}{2}\pi\sqrt3 \qquad\textbf{(E) } \pi$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="478" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/8/5/1859376c3119b46b0df33ad4782e7356a933798b.png" class="latex" alt="$\omega=e^{\frac{2i\pi}{3}}$" width="64" height="18" />, which is one of the cube roots of unity. We wish to find the span of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/8/08862f0282b094082fe81af6ed0687c59acf7ebb.png" class="latex" alt="$(a+b\omega+c\omega^2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="113" height="19" /> for reals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/a/33a368b5e696a651413ceaa56952d6fca1c1e18e.png" class="latex" alt="$0\le a,b,c\le 1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="107" height="16" />.
Observe also that if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/a/c7a0dc1d53202f0003849a81d76e5555b1ee251a.png" class="latex" alt="$a,b,c&gt;0$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="74" height="16" />, then replacing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/1/c618e67306eeac0e6fd82a5a01b6f1e01863fb18.png" class="latex" alt="$a-\min(a,b,c), b-\min(a,b,c),$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="246" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/2/c525a419a7d0997c0e7351acc2e6fd25b3f4c0dd.png" class="latex" alt="$c-\min(a,b,c)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="114" height="18" /> leaves the value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/c/a2c306cd41c785559b6f695c535388cc49fe37c5.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b\omega+c\omega^2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="99" height="16" /> unchanged. Therefore, assume that at least one of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/b/a5b29b358e825defa3e11b7d903d43ec31e5909a.png" class="latex" alt="$a,b,c$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="41" height="16" /> is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />. If exactly one of them is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />, we can form an equilateral triangle of side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> using the remaining terms. A similar argument works if exactly two of them are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />. In total, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/0/be022135ddf62a9681b4578d3b21cdf6bd50120c.png" class="latex" alt="$3+{3 \choose 2} = 6$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="101" height="53" /> equilateral triangles, whose total area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/3/8d3b4349c55465540850a1d2e14e655e67465aac.png" class="latex" alt="$6 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} = \boxed{\textbf{(C) } \frac{3}{2}\sqrt3}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="158" height="48" />.
</p><p><i>Note</i>: A diagram of the six equilateral triangles is shown below.
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/d/42dee9da6ce367a03187683e00b3061794ee81a5.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(200,200); draw((0,0)--(1,0)--(1/2,sqrt(3)/2)--cycle); draw((0,0)--(1/2,sqrt(3)/2)--(-1/2,sqrt(3)/2)--cycle); draw((0,0)--(-1/2,sqrt(3)/2)--(-1,0)--cycle); draw((0,0)--(-1,0)--(-1/2,-sqrt(3)/2)--cycle); draw((0,0)--(-1/2,-sqrt(3)/2)--(1/2,-sqrt(3)/2)--cycle); draw((0,0)--(1/2,-sqrt(3)/2)--(1,0)--cycle); draw((-2,0)--(2,0)); draw((0,-2)--(0,2)); [/asy]" width="335" height="335" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1_but_not_exactly">Solution 1 but not exactly</span></h2>
<p>WLOG <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> is the smallest of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />. Then the expression is equivalent to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/6/de62b5b56658b304af387a24c775513727ea0033.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b\omega$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="51" height="14" />. To find the area of the region, we need only consider the extremities (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/5/4559967057fa75539ffe05f49644a1d9248abc23.png" class="latex" alt="$a=0, a=1, b=0, b=1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="194" height="18" />), as they will form a polygon which contains all points. So, when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/7/85721af34098db413d50c6891430d81bf5b8663f.png" class="latex" alt="$a=0, b=0$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="91" height="16" /> we have the origin (diagram omitted). When <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/7/077804ce4e27bf17cd94bea69b102309859f90d3.png" class="latex" alt="$a=1, b=0$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="94" height="18" /> we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/8/bc8bfcec4e893fce5af5bd604020203451a13995.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(100,100); draw((-2,0)--(2,0), gray); draw((0,-2)--(0,2), gray); draw((0,0)--(1,0), red); [/asy]" width="168" height="168" />
</p><p>When <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/7/6a7372a2f6167ca6f7e7a2bb7d5327f13c40d1d3.png" class="latex" alt="$a=0, b=1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="91" height="16" /> we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/a/6daadc8f563d24f943f940aa7bff08e6d351e3a3.png" class="latex" alt="$-\frac{1}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="62" height="41" />:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/6/926457dae1b29c096cd5fd0ecfb5319a318347ec.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(100,100); draw((-2,0)--(2,0), gray); draw((0,-2)--(0,2), gray); draw((0,0)--(-1/2,sqrt(3)/2), red); draw((0,0)--(1,0), red); [/asy]" width="168" height="168" />
</p><p>When <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/c/63c4192938291ec8d8c0743ef6e5bab78415469f.png" class="latex" alt="$a=1, b=1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="91" height="16" /> we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/6/e36c583e5e885e56eacf8c989285ff116f21a374.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="51" height="43" />:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/6/8a62261bfd20fa77070b5f9733d01a63af411bf1.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(100,100); draw((-2,0)--(2,0), gray); draw((0,-2)--(0,2), gray); draw((0,0)--(-1/2,sqrt(3)/2), red); draw((0,0)--(1,0), red); draw((-1/2,sqrt(3)/2)--(1/2,sqrt(3)/2), red); draw((1,0)--(1/2,sqrt(3)/2), red); [/asy]" width="168" height="168" />
</p><p>The area of this is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/9/4e9191a5007067cff8f6d73f4d3419e08238f00b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" />. Multiply this by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We can add on each term one at a time. Firstly, the possible values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/d/1cde14c4fc4efc5fb89cb7fa564c49cdfa2b00aa.png" class="latex" alt="$\textstyle c\omega^2=c\left(-\frac{1}{2}-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i\right)$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="155" height="32" /> lie on the following line:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/2/8c2d83d684aa7536f13f6b53a82a041b1de44934.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(100,100); draw((0,0)--(-1/2,-sqrt(3)/2), blue); draw((-2,0)--(2,0)); draw((0,-2)--(0,2)); [/asy]" width="168" height="168" />
</p><p>For each point on the line, we can add <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/2/c522515d86502509bddfbeaaeb6c230a1c1177ef.png" class="latex" alt="$\textstyle b\omega=b\left(-\frac{1}{2}+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i\right)$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="148" height="32" />. This means that we can extend the area to
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/9/96983bc6c01c87d0af76515627bc8b76718003be.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(100,100); fill((0,0)--(-1/2,sqrt(3)/2)--(-1,0)--(-1/2,-sqrt(3)/2)--cycle, lightgray); draw((0,0)--(-1/2,sqrt(3)/2)--(-1,0)--(-1/2,-sqrt(3)/2)--cycle); draw((0,0)--(-1/2,sqrt(3)/2), red); draw((0,0)--(-1/2,-sqrt(3)/2), blue); draw((-2,0)--(2,0)); draw((0,-2)--(0,2)); [/asy]" width="168" height="168" />
</p><p>by "moving" the blue line along the red line. Finally, we can add <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> to every point, giving
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/9/b998688df0c98abb935726c2aeaf6d053c96694b.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(100,100); fill((-1/2,sqrt(3)/2)--(-1,0)--(-1/2,-sqrt(3)/2)--(1/2,-sqrt(3)/2)--(1,0)--(1/2,sqrt(3)/2)--cycle, lightgray); draw((-1/2,sqrt(3)/2)--(-1,0)--(-1/2,-sqrt(3)/2)--(1/2,-sqrt(3)/2)--(1,0)--(1/2,sqrt(3)/2)--cycle); draw((0,0)--(-1/2,sqrt(3)/2), red); draw((0,0)--(-1/2,-sqrt(3)/2), blue); draw((-2,0)--(2,0)); draw((0,-2)--(0,2)); draw((0,0)--(1,0), heavygreen); [/asy]" width="168" height="168" />
</p><p>by "moving" the previous area along the green line. This leaves us with a regular hexagon with side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, so, as in Solution 1, the total area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/2/8c20ce2dff8969f5a41fd05a37649001b5df5e3f.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) } \frac{3}{2}\sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="84" height="48" />.
</p>
