<p>For how many integral values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> can a triangle of positive area be formed having side lengths <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/c/a5c32e99d62f9862741ea8e4b5504604f26156a0.png" class="latex" alt="$\log_{2} x, \log_{4} x, 3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="113" height="17" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/9/529ec07cc85781dfd16ae43a56c75b109142bcfc.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 57 \qquad\textbf{(B) } 59 \qquad\textbf{(C) } 61 \qquad\textbf{(D) } 62 \qquad\textbf{(E) } 63$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="398" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>For these lengths to form a triangle of positive area, the Triangle Inequality tells us that we need <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/8/53816532ec0773f5ef34eaf982b0b4ec12f1b524.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\log_2{x} + \log_4{x} &gt; 3\]" width="143" height="17" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/b/d4b5db6cd5a9d33ce2efd052cc2ef64145c7808a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\log_2{x} + 3 &gt; \log_4{x}\]" width="143" height="17" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/4/4e419e588cbc0f1b20fda03fc9bca2372d54084f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\log_4{x} + 3 &gt; \log_2{x}.\]" width="147" height="17" /> The second inequality is redundant, as it's always less restrictive than the last inequality. 
</p><p>Let's raise the first inequality to the power of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />. This gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/1/f91d0ef3148951abb921a3cf296c47277ac08fea.png" class="latex" alt="$4^{\log_2{x}} \cdot 4^{\log_4{x}} &gt; 64 \Rightarrow \left(2^2\right)^{\log_2{x}} \cdot x &gt; 64 \Rightarrow x^2 \cdot x &gt; 64$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="415" height="25" />. Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/5/af505a7aebbcf901ae1ededba228f8acc738b337.png" class="latex" alt="$x &gt; 4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="12" />. 
</p><p>Doing the same for the second inequality gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/d/97d0bb85cedbbcf216463104f09d9f7c542c8adc.png" class="latex" alt="$4^{\log_4{x}} \cdot 64 &gt; 4^{\log_2{x}} \Rightarrow 64x &gt; x^2 \Rightarrow x &lt; 64$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="322" height="16" /> (where we are allowed to divide both sides by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> must be positive in order for the logarithms given in the problem statement to even have real values).
</p><p>Combining our results, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> is an integer strictly between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/e/05e228ac5bee7b9ffb60cacf50f3f2c7c31f8038.png" class="latex" alt="$64$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="13" />, so the number of possible values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/a/a8aff79b1e0b71ea41ecd3d7c69554c22c70f816.png" class="latex" alt="$64 - 4 - 1 = \boxed{\textbf{(B) } 59}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="169" height="28" />.
</p><p><br />
