<p>How many unordered pairs of edges of a given cube determine a plane?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/6/d46b2aea68248c7549a772340af1923cddc34a12.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 12 \qquad \textbf{(B) } 28 \qquad \textbf{(C) } 36\qquad \textbf{(D) } 42 \qquad \textbf{(E) } 66$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="398" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Without loss of generality, choose one of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> edges of the cube to be among the two selected. We now calculate the probability that a randomly-selected second edge makes the pair satisfy the condition in the problem statement.
</p><p>For two lines in space to determine a common plane, they must either intersect or be parallel (in other words, they cannot be skew lines). If all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> line segments are extended to lines, the first (arbitrarily chosen) edge's line intersects <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> lines and is parallel to another <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />. Thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/6/916792d3166c88332e80557114630afd438626b5.png" class="latex" alt="$4+3=7$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="73" height="14" /> of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/2/572ac27aab1a8abd8d9b533fafc987065820636b.png" class="latex" alt="$12-1=11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="90" height="12" /> remaining line segments (which could be chosen for the second edge) give a pair of lines determining a common plane. To see this, observe that in the diagram below, the red edge is parallel to the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> green edges and intersects with the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> blue edges.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/2/7d2fb38c071febb9479738c42fa5468680d9496e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] import three; import three; unitsize(1cm); size(200); currentprojection=perspective(-6/5,-8/5,7/8); draw((0,1,0)--(0,0,0)--(1,0,0), blue); draw((1,0,0)--(1,1,0)--(0,1,0)); draw((0,0,0)--(0,0,1), red); draw((0,1,0)--(0,1,1), green); draw((1,1,0)--(1,1,1), green); draw((1,0,0)--(1,0,1), green); draw((0,1,1)--(0,0,1)--(1,0,1), blue); draw((1,0,1)--(1,1,1)--(0,1,1)); [/asy]" width="335" height="302" />
</p><p>This means that the probability that a randomly-selected pair of edges determine a plane is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/d/86d2ae99ef937044429b0550afe4a0baf2db28a1.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{7}{11}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="37" />, and we calculate that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/3/0f363e54394035b19d42157360920421dd1f88b0.png" class="latex" alt="${12 \choose 2} = 66$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="88" height="53" /> total pairs of edges that could be chosen (without the restriction). Thus the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/7/d479c8f69b93cb6634c1c7506611769d4bfa2f75.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{7}{11} \cdot 66 =\boxed{\textbf{(D) }42}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="143" height="37" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>As in Solution 1, we observe that the two edges must either be parallel or intersect. Clearly the edges will intersect if and only if they are part of the same face. We can thus divide into two cases:
</p><p><b>Case 1</b>: The two edges are part of the same face. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> faces, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/5/c555de232a89fb53ada2f2bd60b778610fe08d53.png" class="latex" alt="${4 \choose 2}=6$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="70" height="53" /> ways to choose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> edges of the square, giving a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/9/999956907865d13963bffca0c70fb57df8f1f734.png" class="latex" alt="$6 \cdot 6 = 36$" width="73" height="12" /> possibilities.
</p><p><b>Case 2</b>: The two edges are parallel and not part of the same face. Observe that each of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> edges is parallel to exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> edge that is not part of its face. The edges can thus be paired up, giving <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/c/2ec956e0ab38c40e4aab6db21bffdfd13e2d1719.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{12}{2} = 6$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="55" height="37" /> possibilities for this case.
</p><p>Adding the two cases, the answer is hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/6/7665c99f159f2b3b7b261ce106e420b7a1027348.png" class="latex" alt="$36 + 6 = \boxed{\textbf{(D) }42}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="139" height="28" />.
</p>
