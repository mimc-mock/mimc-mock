<p>A rectangular floor that is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> feet wide and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/6/b7616a230c77f0d9a577e5bca01aa06f1c35c457.png" class="latex" alt="$17$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> feet long is tiled with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/6/8066ce3115c8a4f144f69b4cffd01d33c402419b.png" class="latex" alt="$170$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> one-foot square tiles. A bug walks from one corner to the opposite corner in a straight line. Including the first and the last tile, how many tiles does the bug visit?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/c/5fc9414598bfed7a80e804f8a3dc7cbd7effe95d.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 17 \qquad\textbf{(B) } 25 \qquad\textbf{(C) } 26 \qquad\textbf{(D) } 27 \qquad\textbf{(E) } 28$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="398" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The number of tiles the bug visits is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> plus the number of times it crosses a horizontal or vertical line.  As it must cross <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/5/9a5b4928c8fe50ce3c2428da3bee3505e891b788.png" class="latex" alt="$16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> horizontal lines and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> vertical lines, it must be that the bug visits a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/1/3817f29425eb5d8e95779d5aabf52a7f574b443c.png" class="latex" alt="$16+9+1 = \boxed{\textbf{(C) }26}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="170" height="28" /> squares.
</p><p><i>Note</i>: The general formula for this is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/b/aebd4da688cf43de4f47c98fdd6fe7f625187973.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b-\gcd(a,b)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="127" height="18" />, because it is the number of vertical/horizontal lines crossed minus the number of corners crossed (to avoid double counting). In this particular problem, it was <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/f/73fad5856df1b2eea8cc37ec399f44c27404d16b.png" class="latex" alt="$16 + 9 - 1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="79" height="14" /> (since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/b/4abf8b2c3c91feb5143577b293ab81b00404c3fb.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{gcd}(16,9) = 1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="106" height="18" />), which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/9/e290f2cba0ba0e11c0b91630da15a408da2fcf1c.png" class="latex" alt="$24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" />, but then you add <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> because the first tile and the last tile are counted, which in the general formula are not counted.
</p><p><br />
One can see why it is gcd(a,b) due to slope
~Williamgolly
