<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/f/36f73fc1312ee0349b3f3a0f3bd9eb5504339011.png" class="latex" alt="$p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/1/0615acc3725de21025457e7d6f7694dab8e2f758.png" class="latex" alt="$q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="8" height="11" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> be the distinct roots of the polynomial <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/e/a4ed9bcc72fc4555c3be2fcdcfc2316490cd8fbc.png" class="latex" alt="$x^3 - 22x^2 + 80x - 67$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="167" height="16" />. It is given that there exist real numbers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/4/ed44e7481e867b959ea84d896b30b222fe5efea3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\dfrac{1}{s^3 - 22s^2 + 80s - 67} = \dfrac{A}{s-p} + \dfrac{B}{s-q} + \frac{C}{s-r}\]" width="361" height="41" />for all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/7/917380dced84f6389978d355c92c4e040fea59cc.png" class="latex" alt="$s\not\in\{p,q,r\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="90" height="18" />. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/b/3db8f3332a5b70da4d7b3d9db6c0e6b9e73b6164.png" class="latex" alt="$\tfrac1A+\tfrac1B+\tfrac1C$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="85" height="21" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/7/0577863302f8209ff40dedef0539f6d1f79bfdfc.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }243\qquad\textbf{(B) }244\qquad\textbf{(C) }245\qquad\textbf{(D) }246\qquad\textbf{(E) } 247$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="444" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Multiplying both sides by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/d/e0dd86c1a1143adfa555374b2ac2842cf24edc91.png" class="latex" alt="$(s-p)(s-q)(s-r)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="159" height="18" /> yields
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/8/98876ccfdf56b419e524f7df285b0d3ec6b34d15.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[1 = A(s-q)(s-r) + B(s-p)(s-r) + C(s-p)(s-q)\]" width="440" height="18" />
As this is a polynomial identity, and it is true for infinitely many <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" />, it must be true for all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" /> (since a polynomial with infinitely many roots must in fact be the constant polynomial <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />). This means we can plug in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/f/1dfc20f5dcffba4a944c9744696cc4ec534cbff4.png" class="latex" alt="$s = p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="11" /> to find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/3/0d39cc2afcb31489f6bbaf2f61c0fc6fd8cc8877.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac1A = (p-q)(p-r)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="149" height="37" />.  Similarly, we can find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/7/af780f521a40ae8a18a64b2e945fe761c8486068.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac1B = (q-p)(q-r)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="149" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/e/ccec7eccb01fea2faa05838cf1e2d5deba6b3737.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac1C = (r-p)(r-q)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="149" height="37" />.  Summing them up, we get that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/f/b5f6ab443798a3cdbaf34f942a40bb85f346c112.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac1A + \frac1B + \frac1C = p^2 + q^2 + r^2 - pq - qr - pr\]" width="334" height="37" />
By Vieta's Formulas, we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/c/f1cfdc572860a37ac2943ac3756bce3dfa2a66d0.png" class="latex" alt="$p^2 + q^2 + r^2 = (p+q+r)^2 - 2(pq + qr + pr) = 324$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="405" height="19" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/1/0a1a9a9c52d2da2691c10dbd5cc9ee0f506c06ef.png" class="latex" alt="$pq + qr + pr = 80$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="140" height="16" />. Thus the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/1/6e14f77c967805c657eb538ac04e0dda1d998e6a.png" class="latex" alt="$324 -80 = \boxed{\textbf{(B) } 244}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="165" height="28" />.
</p><p><i>Note</i>: this process of substituting in the 'forbidden' values in the original identity is a standard technique for partial fraction decomposition, as taught in calculus classes.
