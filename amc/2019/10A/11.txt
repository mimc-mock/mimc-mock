<p>How many positive integer divisors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/2/5a221edbab19018a2e8a7836e2af02aa0d17d5ba.png" class="latex" alt="$201^9$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="15" /> are perfect squares or perfect cubes (or both)?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/0/4a0028c433bf3673f7cae9ace7f03b370ee77739.png" class="latex" alt="${\textbf{(A) }32} \qquad {\textbf{(B) }36} \qquad {\textbf{(C) }37} \qquad {\textbf{(D) }39} \qquad {\textbf{(E) }41}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="397" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Prime factorizing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/2/5a221edbab19018a2e8a7836e2af02aa0d17d5ba.png" class="latex" alt="$201^9$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="15" />, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/f/8efa9e6336fbc700994420227f67b290399d9715.png" class="latex" alt="$3^9\cdot67^9$" width="53" height="15" />. 
A perfect square must have even powers of its prime factors, so our possible choices for our exponents to get perfect square are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/1/ef1c69750366d38516dfda6352e5d1848e28a082.png" class="latex" alt="$0, 2, 4, 6, 8$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="77" height="16" /> for both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/a/7fa7247f4d29d95459cbf6f238007a1b1090ebea.png" class="latex" alt="$67$" width="18" height="12" />. This yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/2/5526f40171c32d7e131faf736257cfec1639c0a6.png" class="latex" alt="$5\cdot5 = 25$" width="73" height="12" /> perfect squares. 
</p><p>Perfect cubes must have multiples of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> for each of their prime factors' exponents, so we have either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/5/5253c12d0f9bd4a93fb23f43110033519746ad86.png" class="latex" alt="$0, 3, 6$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="43" height="16" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> for both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/a/7fa7247f4d29d95459cbf6f238007a1b1090ebea.png" class="latex" alt="$67$" width="18" height="12" />, which yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/a/88aefb0be2b6e340617ae3993d988dbb812a0bbb.png" class="latex" alt="$4\cdot4 = 16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="73" height="13" /> perfect cubes, for a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/2/702c3fc38a9824e964f5127fa6aad98efc61ac57.png" class="latex" alt="$25+16 = 41$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="100" height="14" />. 
</p><p>Subtracting the overcounted powers of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/c/c4c62c72c654ece2d2920b06ead5e1901b4d6827.png" class="latex" alt="$3^0\cdot67^0$" width="53" height="15" /> , <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/8/02884272ef8e92434ab7e4250be9975aca072032.png" class="latex" alt="$3^0\cdot67^6$" width="53" height="15" /> , <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/4/6d4b76219bca10c7220aeefa26f7f76e17a1d817.png" class="latex" alt="$3^6\cdot67^0$" width="53" height="15" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/2/1e23ac0c3561d4ea2c87e7d086d9644867482293.png" class="latex" alt="$3^6\cdot67^6$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="57" height="17" />), we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/e/77ede912a8f73024301638a5fd561455752c8a75.png" class="latex" alt="$41-4 = \boxed{\textbf{(C) }37}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="138" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Observe that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/9/ee9cf21262e37f327039191b30ea907f4e26045f.png" class="latex" alt="$201 = 67 \cdot 3$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="91" height="13" />. Now divide into cases:
</p><p><b>Case 1</b>: The factor is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/c/59c404d8a8901ace9c02b39cb10d8dd226134c19.png" class="latex" alt="$3^n$" width="17" height="12" />. Then we can have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/8/598a16d4b7dde3dec3a390109882d54bb0308ea5.png" class="latex" alt="$n = 2$" width="43" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" />. 
</p><p><b>Case 2</b>: The factor is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/1/f517edfd5583210bbd1fa2d6b930c2d2cb561b8f.png" class="latex" alt="$67^n$" width="26" height="12" />. This is the same as Case 1.
</p><p><b>Case 3</b>: The factor is some combination of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />s and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/a/7fa7247f4d29d95459cbf6f238007a1b1090ebea.png" class="latex" alt="$67$" width="18" height="12" />s.
</p><p>This would be easy if we could just have any combination, as that would simply give <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/4/d942d5198e2575401f3f645b6c7a4c89ca2279dc.png" class="latex" alt="$6 \cdot 6$" width="30" height="12" />. However, we must pair the numbers that generate squares with the numbers that generate squares and the same for cubes. In simpler terms, let's organize our values for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/8/598a16d4b7dde3dec3a390109882d54bb0308ea5.png" class="latex" alt="$n = 2$" width="43" height="12" /> is a "square" because it would give a factor of this number that is a perfect square. More generally, it is even.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c342b01f0cdef0f23d11a2bc9c5aa67f6d83ccb.png" class="latex" alt="$n = 3$" width="43" height="12" /> is a "cube" because it would give a factor of this number that is a perfect cube. More generally, it is a multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/1/5218af40079081e694d8b82eeac2d368ef6ea76a.png" class="latex" alt="$n = 4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="44" height="12" /> is a "square".
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/2/e326b48726d4c73525c3b980409600fd87ea9846.png" class="latex" alt="$n = 6$" width="43" height="12" /> is interesting, since it's both a "square" and a "cube". Don't count this as either because this would double-count, so we will count this in another case.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/8/26858c2becbfe024f2a38f506fce66d56f3e0b66.png" class="latex" alt="$n = 8$" width="43" height="12" /> is a "square"
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/8/1/1819f547a53f667e1a44a49ba2bec08c28d7f222.png" class="latex" alt="$n = 9$" width="43" height="12" /> is a "cube".
</p><p>Now let's consider subcases:
</p><p><b>Subcase 1</b>: The squares are with each other.
</p><p>Since we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> square terms, and they would pair with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> other square terms, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/8/a08f8b84240fbf97ba60819f475b53b1a2295b9e.png" class="latex" alt="$3 \cdot 3 = 9$" width="64" height="12" /> possibilities.
</p><p><b>Subcase 2</b>: The cubes are with each other.
</p><p>Since we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> cube terms, and they would pair with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> other cube terms, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/6/8b69aa4309847d47e4aed15a0b00891106fb1807.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot 2 = 4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="64" height="12" /> possibilities.
</p><p><b>Subcase 3</b>: A number pairs with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/2/2f2a6991d2108f237ee451bf37db91cacaf4134e.png" class="latex" alt="$n=6$" width="43" height="12" />.
</p><p>Since any number can pair with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/2/2f2a6991d2108f237ee451bf37db91cacaf4134e.png" class="latex" alt="$n=6$" width="43" height="12" /> (as it gives both a square and a cube), there would be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> possibilities. Remember however that there can be two different bases (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/a/7fa7247f4d29d95459cbf6f238007a1b1090ebea.png" class="latex" alt="$67$" width="18" height="12" />), and they would produce different results. Thus, there are in fact <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/5/785806dee2edf4f47b6b15f6f4beb572b97b9de3.png" class="latex" alt="$6 \cdot 2 = 12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="73" height="13" /> possibilities.
</p><p>Finally, summing the cases gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/b/32bed2411021a455be06690351237a9b2f2a6fba.png" class="latex" alt="$6+6+9+4+12 = \boxed{\textbf{(C) }37}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="232" height="28" />.
</p>
