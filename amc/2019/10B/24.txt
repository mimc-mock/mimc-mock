<p>Define a sequence recursively by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/4/7c46287124de82898a1f653997147758eef5d98b.png" class="latex" alt="$x_0=5$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="50" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/4/934a32343352988b8d8328a7ef93c7553a1ce642.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x_{n+1}=\frac{x_n^2+5x_n+4}{x_n+6}\]" width="163" height="41" /> for all nonnegative integers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/9/bd923e41635af40a1abe58f230732f2280b0723a.png" class="latex" alt="$n.$" width="14" height="8" /> Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> be the least positive integer such that
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/8/e889e3b4b1d11c0fd3ce0eda55858624351b69fd.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x_m\leq 4+\frac{1}{2^{20}}.\]" width="109" height="37" />
</p><p>In which of the following intervals does <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> lie?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/2/b4200e832f7695e2ceefdcf8621675991b8b23cb.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } [9,26] \qquad\textbf{(B) } [27,80] \qquad\textbf{(C) } [81,242]\qquad\textbf{(D) } [243,728] \qquad\textbf{(E) } [729,\infty)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="608" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We first prove that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/e/f3ee826367202841af58c001a41fbc78374c3be6.png" class="latex" alt="$x_n &gt; 4$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="52" height="14" /> for all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/c/e2c7d62172af0e738dd12f47f3f8ad8ef70cd65f.png" class="latex" alt="$n \ge 0$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="43" height="15" />, by induction. Observe that
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/6/806b143cf192ce65fba18d27c4cddc57b33f25d3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x_{n+1} - 4 = \frac{x_n^2 + 5x_n + 4 - 4(x_n+6)}{x_n+6} = \frac{(x_n - 4)(x_n+5)}{x_n+6}.\]" width="454" height="42" />
so (since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/5/195db3e04ee69d85a0aeaedbb1cf9afa97ff4546.png" class="latex" alt="$x_n$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="10" /> is clearly positive for all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, from the initial definition), <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/9/d492e9fc7679d5daf73a5d3b2e47b4ea3d66bd45.png" class="latex" alt="$x_{n+1} &gt; 4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="69" height="16" /> if and only if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/c/b1cff7fc1bc70c0a2f996bb1d848285f84e8f261.png" class="latex" alt="$x_{n} &gt; 4$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="52" height="14" />.
</p><p>We similarly prove that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/5/195db3e04ee69d85a0aeaedbb1cf9afa97ff4546.png" class="latex" alt="$x_n$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="10" /> is decreasing:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/9/229ef868eca3eb3a17ad0bbbe22a58705557a4ff.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x_{n+1} - x_n = \frac{x_n^2 + 5x_n + 4 - x_n(x_n+6)}{x_n+6} = \frac{4-x_n}{x_n+6} &lt; 0.\]" width="429" height="42" />
</p><p>Now we need to estimate the value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/e/1de7ff48b06fef6cfdde479edd79c0334987dd6d.png" class="latex" alt="$x_{n+1}-4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="16" />, which we can do using the rearranged equation:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/9/4d92acef32f1cd30af72c8e8d9fde263d3beb975.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x_{n+1} - 4 = (x_n-4)\cdot\frac{x_n + 5}{x_n+6}.\]" width="227" height="40" />
Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/5/195db3e04ee69d85a0aeaedbb1cf9afa97ff4546.png" class="latex" alt="$x_n$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="10" /> is decreasing, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/6/de626469842eb2023e96c0aef53b43cd2feb48b0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{x_n + 5}{x_n+6}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="53" height="40" /> is also decreasing, so we have
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/c/fcc1383d3329ce9633e8b64797b4b08c835823a0.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{9}{10} &lt; \frac{x_n + 5}{x_n+6} \le \frac{10}{11}\]" width="144" height="40" />
and
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/5/5357bc1feb3acd8fb1cd1fa7894931fce998ab95.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{9}{10}(x_n-4) &lt; x_{n+1} - 4 \le \frac{10}{11}(x_n-4).\]" width="291" height="37" />
</p><p>This becomes
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/c/ebc3e2580ccd11f785bed67674298209bbd275f3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\left(\frac{9}{10}\right)^n = \left(\frac{9}{10}\right)^n \left(x_0-4\right) &lt; x_{n} - 4 \le \left(\frac{10}{11}\right)^n \left(x_0-4\right) = \left(\frac{10}{11}\right)^n.\]" width="504" height="43" />
The problem thus reduces to finding the least value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> such that
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/8/71821f593e10dec8153e48c67209978f55322f32.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\left(\frac{9}{10}\right)^n &lt; x_{n} - 4 \le \frac{1}{2^{20}} \text{  and  }  \left(\frac{10}{11}\right)^{n-1} &gt; x_{n-1} - 4 &gt; \frac{1}{2^{20}}.\]" width="442" height="46" />
</p><p>Taking logarithms, we get
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/c/82c48e3034aa4fc81be9754e0b0574cd537bdd42.png" class="latex" alt="$n \ln \frac{9}{10} &lt; -20 \ln 2$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="140" height="38" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/d/9bdecf6b98799ffbc455b53aecc83f647c03a631.png" class="latex" alt="$(n-1)\ln \frac{10}{11} &gt; -20 \ln 2$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="186" height="38" />, i.e.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/d/b3d87b8431f57ad9bb111397a194aa610a34e008.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[n &gt; \frac{20\ln 2}{\ln\frac{10}{9}} \text{  and  }  n-1 &lt; \frac{20\ln 2}{\ln\frac{11}{10}} .\]" width="247" height="44" />
</p><p>As approximations, we can use <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/6/e36b5791edcdc38cf05b740161a5ef4298da47a1.png" class="latex" alt="$\ln\frac{10}{9} \approx \frac{1}{9}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="76" height="37" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/a/3baf0342773b9e993db976696d0861ee1cfb4657.png" class="latex" alt="$\ln\frac{11}{10} \approx \frac{1}{10}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="85" height="37" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/d/5bd8214f3ed73c9b3f1666f8853063a11262d20e.png" class="latex" alt="$\ln 2\approx 0.7$" width="74" height="12" />. These approximations allow us to estimate
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c968dcde0b1eee8fdafa429250a177dcf68be175.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[126 &lt; n &lt; 141,\]" width="116" height="15" />
which gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/4/744256a1c01f82683c66afa79fb03acd68310679.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) } [81,242]}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="110" height="29" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>The condition where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/f/04f6ac75e513c1df140cb64a15efe2e1b0aec203.png" class="latex" alt="$x_m\leq 4+\frac{1}{2^{20}}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="104" height="37" /> gives the motivation to make a substitution to change the equilibrium from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />. We can substitute <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/b/d4bb93e74011ca068b41baa2381140f77d64dfc3.png" class="latex" alt="$x_n = y_n + 4$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="93" height="15" /> to achieve that. Now, we need to find the smallest value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/9/e0975a57ea8840a15f22bb778e6e657ad2a46572.png" class="latex" alt="$y_m\leq \frac{1}{2^{20}}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="71" height="37" /> given that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/6/cb6cba0b5147a2a7509e70b1613f7cf93ff36936.png" class="latex" alt="$y_0 = 1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="48" height="15" />. 
</p><p><br />
</p><p>Factoring the recursion <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/8/928c4046177d2a9101a7b931a11216e54c23e11c.png" class="latex" alt="$x_{n+1} = \frac{x_n^2 + 5x_n+4}{x_n + 6}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="164" height="41" />, we get: 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/6/c563c9871e5ac11da731e091b5a0e4fe9f38656d.png" class="latex" alt="$x_{n+1}=\dfrac{(x_n + 4)(x_n + 1)}{x_n + 6} \Rightarrow y_{n+1}+4=\dfrac{(y_n+8)(y_n+5)}{y_n+10}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="442" height="42" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/0/f60f02e118d9e9216e66d7400077fe245df87bb2.png" class="latex" alt="$y_{n+1}+4=\dfrac{y_n^2+13y_n+40}{y_n+10} = \dfrac{y_n^2+9y_n +(4y_n+40)}{y_n+10}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="408" height="42" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/0/200f9bab250ffe515d5c8cd0b7817b981b6a7bad.png" class="latex" alt="$y_{n+1}+4=\dfrac{y_n^2+9y_n}{y_n+10} + 4$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="192" height="42" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/4/de414d94a0cc640964566ae34e7b07fd146cce5f.png" class="latex" alt="$y_{n+1}=\dfrac{y_n^2+9y_n}{y_n+10}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="128" height="42" />.
</p><p><br />
</p><p>Using wishful thinking, we can simplify the recursion as follows:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/6/d16213067694dfb0cfd20a267e0a9474ac43e55a.png" class="latex" alt="$y_{n+1} = \frac{y_n^2 + 9y_n + y_n - y_n}{y_n + 10}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="209" height="42" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/4/2c45c341ede1dc2f6ebe2592cf4a042824f520ec.png" class="latex" alt="$y_{n+1} = \frac{y_n(y_n + 10) - y_n}{y_n + 10}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="192" height="42" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/e/5be726334d1f0e15e12fe744de104513f9de5c83.png" class="latex" alt="$y_{n+1} = y_n - \frac{y_n}{y_n + 10}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="159" height="36" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/d/28d5f8a8749547f88188fba8ddf954fe913bdcd5.png" class="latex" alt="$y_{n+1} = y_n\left(1 - \frac{1}{y_n + 10}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="196" height="43" />.
</p><p><br />
The recursion looks like a geometric sequence with the ratio changing slightly after each term. Notice from the recursion that the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/2/3b23a00ac79117133abc0c4e699668642494fc66.png" class="latex" alt="$y_n$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="17" height="11" /> sequence is strictly decreasing, so all the terms after <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/1/721e40b7e0ac4359611aebe48cfb63122a347798.png" class="latex" alt="$y_0$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="15" height="11" /> will be less than 1. Also, notice that all the terms in sequence will be positive. Both of these can be proven by induction.
</p><p><br />
With both of those observations in mind, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/b/0cb5c00505fa6d673681bb9df45853112b8bf5da.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{9}{10} &lt; 1 - \frac{1}{y_n + 10} \leq \frac{10}{11}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="185" height="41" />. Combining this with the fact that the recursion resembles a geometric sequence, we conclude that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/7/c7739af3fec158ce3b11692c0069850fdf43accd.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{9}{10}\right)^n &lt; y_n \leq \left(\frac{10}{11}\right)^n.$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="187" height="44" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/b/f9b7bce0f53f67c1bcc64573cbad2562d02bdd31.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{9}{10}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="38" /> is approximately equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/9/cd92179163794b2abc012fbe4258ea5058518fe1.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{10}{11}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="38" /> and the ranges that the answer choices give us are generous, so we should use either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/b/f9b7bce0f53f67c1bcc64573cbad2562d02bdd31.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{9}{10}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="38" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/9/cd92179163794b2abc012fbe4258ea5058518fe1.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{10}{11}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="38" /> to find a rough estimate for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" />.
</p><p><br />
Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/1/ba17b925b0ef57f3dd32325bd20c1b55d6232f18.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}{2}=0.5$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="60" height="37" />, that means <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/f/42f050aa7469a4756adcef55423c6ace0229de5f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}=2^{-\frac{1}{2}} \approx 0.7$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="129" height="41" />. Additionally, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/4/5041d0d96a78cfc0321a1254e1bf11c663f5a124.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{9}{10}\right)^3=0.729$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="120" height="46" />
</p><p><br />
Therefore, we can estimate that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/b/76bdd1aa34bc635d40002df1c87f5821d7417cab.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{-\frac{1}{2}} &lt; y_3$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="69" height="21" />.
</p><p>Raising both sides to the 40th power, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/d/64d40f02dca2a43d1e5bb623f22cfab115a2f924.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{-20} &lt; (y_3)^{40}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="100" height="19" />
</p><p>But <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/d/c6d667d792f6a47b11b36155189b63755d0de9e9.png" class="latex" alt="$y_3 = (y_0)^3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="77" height="19" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/0/bb0ecd08034628a8343bc8970a4902c79852bcbe.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{-20} &lt; (y_0)^{120}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="107" height="19" /> and therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/9/eb95c673e62f05d08b710320ca52586a0d488f04.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{-20} &lt; y_{120}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="86" height="18" />.
</p><p>This tells us that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> is somewhere around 120, so our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/4/744256a1c01f82683c66afa79fb03acd68310679.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) } [81,242]}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="110" height="29" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Since the choices are rather wide ranges, we can use approximation to make it easier. Notice that
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/1/d51583ae3b9729a246e0a0b5df7579bbe1e916e5.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x_{n+1} - x_n = \frac{4-x_n}{x_n+6}\]" width="154" height="39" />
And <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/8/7d83295b8a458996634b9d037a5b78acd2a364b2.png" class="latex" alt="$x_0 =5$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="50" height="15" />, we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/5/195db3e04ee69d85a0aeaedbb1cf9afa97ff4546.png" class="latex" alt="$x_n$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="10" /> is a declining sequence, and as it get close to 4 its decline will slow, never falling below 4. So we'll use 4 to approximate <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/5/195db3e04ee69d85a0aeaedbb1cf9afa97ff4546.png" class="latex" alt="$x_n$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="10" /> in the denominator so that we have a solvable difference equation:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/d/08db0d71e3f31fce54f549e63a56323793bf74c9.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x_{n+1} - x_n = \frac{4-x_n}{10}\]" width="154" height="37" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/a/00ab424f9eab3edda63c41263b3774c8c3664d34.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x_{n+1} = \frac{9}{10}x_n + \frac{2}{5}\]" width="135" height="38" />
Solve it with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/4/91492b2fddeae4f9ffb7cd370c13308852f8dc8a.png" class="latex" alt="$x_0 = 5$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="50" height="15" />, we have
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/3/b93e157568819399286db8a464adbf1647fcb70d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x_n = 4 + (\frac{9}{10})^n\]" width="118" height="38" />
Now we wish to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> so that
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/6/b06965623625b9843b3a16c2d5c39d32b0ac23a2.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(\frac{9}{10})^n \approx \frac{1}{2^{20}}\]" width="93" height="38" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/e/aae4ea99b71df3b9e3399be7e37f7199068380ea.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[n \approx \frac{\log{2^{20}}}{\log{10}-\log9} \approx \frac{20*0.3}{0.05} = 120\]" width="278" height="42" />
Since 120 is safely within the range of [81,242], we have the answer.
