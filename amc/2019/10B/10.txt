<p>In a given plane, points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> units apart. How many points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> are there in the plane such that the perimeter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/6/906734e2f1010e9a171ffee8050cbc0649cea37c.png" class="latex" alt="$50$" width="17" height="12" /> units and the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> square units?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/b/f3bcf78a9dbfed08c8ffab5d6e1fc99caa27273c.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }0\qquad\textbf{(B) }2\qquad\textbf{(C) }4\qquad\textbf{(D) }8\qquad\textbf{(E) }\text{infinitely many}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="453" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Notice that whatever point we pick for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> will be the base of the triangle. Without loss of generality, let points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/6/f9603ca3089464e548fc6f1366bc474e7efef8d9.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/1/d41af465b6bc77419584c42e96e45efaa21e6782.png" class="latex" alt="$(10,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="48" height="18" />, since for any other combination of points, we can just rotate the plane to make them <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/6/f9603ca3089464e548fc6f1366bc474e7efef8d9.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/1/d41af465b6bc77419584c42e96e45efaa21e6782.png" class="latex" alt="$(10,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="48" height="18" /> under a new coordinate system. When we pick point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />, we have to make sure that its <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />-coordinate is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/6/d262843d63fe83802a905359e46bd1618b4c62f7.png" class="latex" alt="$\pm20$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="31" height="13" />, because that's the only way the area of the triangle can be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />. 
</p><p>Now when the perimeter is minimized, by symmetry, we put <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> in the middle, at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/b/7fb2bcf88c701b6cbd5fc7137012bb838b175fad.png" class="latex" alt="$(5, 20)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="47" height="18" />. We can easily see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/7/a179ff2638e4799cadd820db205c2beff6299ce9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC$" width="27" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" /> will both be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/0/370204c53a27ff6beef9e50a5b39770f4f9ee069.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{20^2+5^2} = \sqrt{425}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="146" height="19" />. The perimeter of this minimal triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/3/153182cb960109e93ae7adf70bd6e578d60f4119.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{425} + 10$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="91" height="18" />, which is larger than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/6/906734e2f1010e9a171ffee8050cbc0649cea37c.png" class="latex" alt="$50$" width="17" height="12" />. Since the minimum perimeter is greater than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/6/906734e2f1010e9a171ffee8050cbc0649cea37c.png" class="latex" alt="$50$" width="17" height="12" />, there is no triangle that satisfies the condition, giving us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/e/f1ea1f26eedb2d601fdd27e52e6dc0e5f4b4be7b.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(A) }0}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="56" height="28" />.
</p><p>~IronicNinja
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Without loss of generality, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> be a horizontal segment of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. Now realize that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> has to lie on one of the lines parallel to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> and vertically <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/3/f8366cd6196dd8a9da6d38a3e9eafb109e99d53e.png" class="latex" alt="$20$" width="17" height="12" /> units away from it. But <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/d/7ddab0075fd9120391073e4a4b0c325b3751e7ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10+20+20$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="98" height="14" /> is already 50, and this doesn't form a triangle. Otherwise, without loss of generality, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/3/963139fd0cf2b02108dd607f269ba99dcc846c51.png" class="latex" alt="$AC&lt;20$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="69" height="13" />. Dropping altitude <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/4/0948661e822f2a953c43a57ac9e40b2734476de4.png" class="latex" alt="$CD$" width="29" height="12" />, we have a right triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/e/6ce2cd123db24a7b8373999aea7a98927e3b3c81.png" class="latex" alt="$ACD$" width="43" height="13" /> with hypotenuse <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/3/963139fd0cf2b02108dd607f269ba99dcc846c51.png" class="latex" alt="$AC&lt;20$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="69" height="13" /> and leg <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/7/8f7dceaa33eb1c7e6f41dd12fe477aadde31fb75.png" class="latex" alt="$CD=20$" width="71" height="12" />, which is clearly impossible, again giving the answer as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/e/f1ea1f26eedb2d601fdd27e52e6dc0e5f4b4be7b.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(A) }0}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="56" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>We have:
</p><p>1. Area = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />
</p><p>2. Perimeter = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/6/906734e2f1010e9a171ffee8050cbc0649cea37c.png" class="latex" alt="$50$" width="17" height="12" />
</p><p>3. Semiperimeter <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/b/9dbd31b7e05d344a79807d369a7192bd0da2e153.png" class="latex" alt="$s = 50 \div 2 = 25$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="127" height="15" />
</p><p>We let:
</p><p>1. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/b/feb2e8bb2ec7f72ec7c7cee973542cff45925811.png" class="latex" alt="$z = \overline{AB} = 10$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="106" height="18" />
</p><p>2. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/1/321d4695d9c6c5d16877c6427c0530e76739bb9b.png" class="latex" alt="$x = \overline{AC}$" width="62" height="15" />
</p><p>3. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/e/aaea15e9a4194d016554335c4a68094cde91be85.png" class="latex" alt="$y = 50-10-x = 40-x$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="199" height="16" />.
</p><p><br />
Heron's formula states that for real numbers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" />, and semiperimeter <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" />, the area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/1/4614ad49b992dc5d9bc712588a87bfed5a114265.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{(s)(s-x)(s-y)(s-z)}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="205" height="32" />.
</p><p>Plugging numbers in, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a4b19cf26a26ae49b3bb15d81a5a8ce70c496f7.png" class="latex" alt="$100 = \sqrt{(25)(25-10)(25-x)(25-(40-x))} = \sqrt{(375)(25-x)(x-15)}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="572" height="32" />.
</p><p><br />
Square both sides, divide by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/1/531204a6ace18de0514d0adfd920358e6d0c2e2f.png" class="latex" alt="$375$" width="26" height="12" /> and expand the polynomial to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/5/5f563898b27c1c9d2148f3d167395644c2a1b2ab.png" class="latex" alt="$40x - x^2 - 375 = \frac{80}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="166" height="38" />.
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/b/78b1e86cb5f93616429bc5c203fbaf4e4fe12669.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2 - 40x + \left(375 + \frac{80}{3}\right) = 0$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="224" height="45" /> and the discriminant is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/f/4bf3e38ce1ea116858f0538a7d7aa4161d46a53e.png" class="latex" alt="$\left((-40)^2 - 4 \times 1 \times 401 \frac{2}{3}\right) &lt; 0$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="240" height="45" />. Thus, there are no real solutions.
</p>
