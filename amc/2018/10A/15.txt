<p>Two circles of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> are externally tangent to each other and are internally tangent to a circle of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/0/030e19e4e2f1c074127cb50581e8496470c5419f.png" class="latex" alt="$13$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> at points  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, as shown in the diagram. The distance <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> can be written in the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/5/c85ea1ad780a799bdaf8f327e664f65eb6f3546b.png" class="latex" alt="$\tfrac{m}{n}$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="14" height="18" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> are relatively prime positive integers. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/d/02dbab3601fa6cce490d54263017048f69e1a35d.png" class="latex" alt="$m+n$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="48" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/d/c1d7b33c122356459463ccf321031d04f8f53752.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] draw(circle((0,0),13)); draw(circle((5,-6.2),5)); draw(circle((-5,-6.2),5)); label(&quot;$B$&quot;, (9.5,-9.5), S); label(&quot;$A$&quot;, (-9.5,-9.5), S); [/asy]" width="252" height="252" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/1/d51239b5dd3cf63b3daced7294839cc563d1dcb7.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }   21   \qquad    \textbf{(B) }  29   \qquad    \textbf{(C) }  58   \qquad   \textbf{(D) } 69 \qquad  \textbf{(E) }   93$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="398" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/5/0f5cde9eec57d1be48c64499b92a5ed49bb52b18.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] draw(circle((0,0),13)); draw(circle((5,-6.25),5)); draw(circle((-5,-6.25),5)); label(&quot;$A$&quot;, (-8.125,-10.15), S); label(&quot;$B$&quot;, (8.125,-10.15), S); draw((0,0)--(-8.125,-10.15)); draw((0,0)--(8.125,-10.15)); draw((-5,-6.25)--(5,-6.25)); draw((-8.125,-10.15)--(8.125,-10.15)); label(&quot;$X$&quot;, (0,0), N); label(&quot;$Y$&quot;, (-5,-6.25),NW); label(&quot;$Z$&quot;, (5,-6.25),NE); [/asy]" width="252" height="252" />
</p><p>Let the center of the surrounding circle be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" />. The circle that is tangent at point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> will have point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/5/ce58e4af225c93d08606c26554caaa5ae32edeba.png" class="latex" alt="$Y$" width="14" height="12" /> as the center. Similarly, the circle that is tangent at point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> will have point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/a/3ead47fb9fb4a4c273feee398f72ff2a09702b84.png" class="latex" alt="$Z$" width="13" height="12" /> as the center. Connect <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/a/3aab17e2f2005ca643854186430099294ad84b85.png" class="latex" alt="$YZ$" width="28" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/3/0e35dd6d3c033f72a812728441ec7d87136db3c2.png" class="latex" alt="$XA$" width="30" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/9/03933a7bd92d2cf64844a19ce10785a23f497959.png" class="latex" alt="$XB$" width="30" height="12" />. Now observe that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/3/3039f01ba9e41f5490b7130e344be554015a25b9.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle XYZ$" width="60" height="13" /> is similar to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/9/859d04abfad1da7b886d652c4ce7de207589dcd5.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle XAB$" width="60" height="13" /> by SAS.
</p><p>Writing out the ratios, we get
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/e/5fe2512bf29a8a348577f7d3ab0ac8bfd57152c5.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{XY}{XA}=\frac{YZ}{AB} \Rightarrow \frac{13-5}{13}=\frac{5+5}{AB} \Rightarrow \frac{8}{13}=\frac{10}{AB} \Rightarrow AB=\frac{65}{4}.\]" width="453" height="37" />
Therefore, our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/4/3d46f24c31d8a834d3a48cec59b58b0a799f5079.png" class="latex" alt="$65+4= \boxed{\textbf{(D) } 69}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="141" height="30" />.
</p>
