<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We apply the Change of Base Formula, then rearrange:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/c/19cc17bc94de49d579c72b73d9bd0b23d7b818dc.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} \frac{\log_2{4}}{\log_2{(3x)}}&amp;=\frac{\log_2{8}}{\log_2{(2x)}} \\ \frac{2}{\log_2{(3x)}}&amp;=\frac{3}{\log_2{(2x)}} \\ 3\log_2{(3x)}&amp;=2\log_2{(2x)}. \\ \end{align*}" width="186" height="114" />
By the logarithmic identity <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/e/80e79229181adf9a8dae0bfebe43ba7805ef6331.png" class="latex" alt="$n\log_b{a}=\log_b{\left(a^n\right)},$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="154" height="20" /> it follows that
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/8/ba8aafd38ffd307e1e71c416b8af0e3dba6bdeda.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} \log_2{\left[(3x)^3\right]}&amp;=\log_2{\left[(2x)^2\right]} \\ (3x)^3&amp;=(2x)^2 \\ 27x^3&amp;=4x^2 \\ x&amp;=\frac{4}{27}, \end{align*}" width="200" height="120" />
from which the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/d/e3d8c31c97b34206508942d7fc695458d9fbfd14.png" class="latex" alt="$4+27=\boxed{\textbf{(D) } 31}.$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="146" height="30" />

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We will apply the following logarithmic identity:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/0/3/c033090df6e4f09f52a5d1d23776c07cddff6999.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\log_{p^n}{\left(q^n\right)}=\log_{p}{q},\]" width="145" height="20" />
which can be proven by the Change of Base Formula: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/b/21b50f64d9c9253a192a39087d5d243d628327cd.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\log_{p^n}{\left(q^n\right)}=\frac{\log_{p}{\left(q^n\right)}}{\log_{p}{\left(p^n\right)}}=\frac{n\log_{p}{q}}{n}=\log_{p}{q}.\]" width="324" height="46" />
We rewrite the original equation as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/1/e9154fd2c79108f64cb497587e05508ca352aea3.png" class="latex" alt="$\log_{(3x)^3} 64 = \log_{(2x)^2} 64,$" style="vertical-align: -8px" width="184" height="22" /> from which

Therefore, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/d/e3d8c31c97b34206508942d7fc695458d9fbfd14.png" class="latex" alt="$4+27=\boxed{\textbf{(D) } 31}.$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="146" height="30" />

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>By the logarithmic identity <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/e/80e79229181adf9a8dae0bfebe43ba7805ef6331.png" class="latex" alt="$n\log_b{a}=\log_b{\left(a^n\right)},$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="154" height="20" /> the original equation becomes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/5/1c5c70a897b56af2458b5b5b6f716c17faf110d8.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[2\log_{3x} 2 = 3\log_{2x} 2.\]" width="152" height="17" />
By the logarithmic identity <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/d/1ed091892c6770f5966b9dc45e56e253fab6fee7.png" class="latex" alt="$\log_b{a}\cdot\log_a{b}=1,$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="138" height="19" /> we multiply both sides by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/8/f48438914c307876b155509ebf645cea87a33b23.png" class="latex" alt="$\log_2{(2x)},$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="74" height="20" /> then apply the Change of Base Formula to the left side:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/8/f08819a5b13f334ac33caa20838dfcb62db2cdd6.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} 2\left[\log_{3x}2\right]\left[\log_2{(2x)}\right] &amp;= 3 \\ 2\left[\frac{\log_2 2}{\log_2{(3x)}}\right]\left[\frac{\log_2{(2x)}}{\log_2 2}\right] &amp;= 3 \\ 2\left[\frac{\log_2{(2x)}}{\log_2{(3x)}}\right] &amp;=3 \\ 2\left[\log_{3x}{(2x)}\right] &amp;= 3 \\ \log_{3x}{\left[(2x)^2\right]} &amp;= 3 \\ (3x)^3&amp;=(2x)^2 \\ 27x^3&amp;=4x^2 \\ x&amp;=\frac{4}{27}. \end{align*}" width="259" height="272" />
Therefore, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/d/e3d8c31c97b34206508942d7fc695458d9fbfd14.png" class="latex" alt="$4+27=\boxed{\textbf{(D) } 31}.$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="146" height="30" />

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>We can convert both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/a/32a35631e1e126934ead6d2af161e9d250827bbb.png" class="latex" alt="$2^2$" width="15" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/2/7d2e5497d2fd16a18da58f6475061f823c4ed9eb.png" class="latex" alt="$2^3,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="23" height="20" /> respectively: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/b/b7ba1c715fd314a99da595be6ae7329743cf6448.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[2\log_{3x} (2) = 3\log_{2x} (2).\]" width="174" height="18" />
Converting the bases of the right side, we get
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/4/e84996d8b1371d594baa855345c134f1d0a45155.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} \log_{2x} 2 &amp;= \frac{\ln 2}{\ln (2x)} \\ \frac{2}{3}\cdot\log_{3x} (2) &amp;= \frac{\ln 2}{\ln (2x)} \\ 2^\frac{2}{3} &amp;= (3x)^\frac{\ln 2}{\ln (2x)} \\ \frac{2}{3} \cdot \ln 2 &amp;= \frac{\ln 2}{\ln (2x)} \cdot \ln (3x). \end{align*}" width="228" height="170" />
Dividing both sides by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/b/14b252b34efe69da144b5c5da68ac27399c8a01a.png" class="latex" alt="$\ln 2,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" /> we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/d/6edcfd7e6c605fc41223a04e321b0ffe8ad48b1e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{3} = \frac{\ln (3x)}{\ln (2x)},$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="97" height="45" /> from which <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/4/b84f76257312229b7ea2c1460523b36684f0e43e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[2\ln (2x) = 3\ln (3x).\]" width="149" height="18" />
Expanding this equation gives
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/3/793a79c57882d4cd0275df6ad099f1831db77b35.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} 2\ln 2 + 2\ln (x) &amp;= 3\ln 3 + 3\ln (x) \\ \ln (x) &amp;= 2\ln 2 - 3\ln 3. \end{align*}" width="249" height="43" />
Thus, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/a/a9aab51673c3f854bdf34251bc2ea5aa3fc5e427.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x = e^{2\ln 2 - 3\ln 3} = \frac{e^{2\ln 2}}{e^{3\ln 3}} = \frac{2^2}{3^3} = \frac{4}{27},\]" width="273" height="39" />
from which the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/d/e3d8c31c97b34206508942d7fc695458d9fbfd14.png" class="latex" alt="$4+27=\boxed{\textbf{(D) } 31}.$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="146" height="30" />
</p><p>~lepetitmoulin (Solution)
