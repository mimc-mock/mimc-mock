<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We can start by finding the total volume of the parallelepiped. </p><p>Next, we can consider the wedge-shaped section made when the plane <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/c/88ca7901f3a38f980dd83e1b24a5f7dba0dd7e77.png" class="latex" alt="$BCHE$" width="59" height="12" /> cuts the figure. We can find the volume of the triangular pyramid with base <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/9/8c9c1db283a081d93c34b8f8be624033e15801a8.png" class="latex" alt="$EFB$" width="42" height="12" /> and apex <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" />. The area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/9/8c9c1db283a081d93c34b8f8be624033e15801a8.png" class="latex" alt="$EFB$" width="42" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/a/78a3004f895e946b5ad133c5bcdb0856047d306f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2} \cdot 2 \cdot 3 = 3$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="90" height="37" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/3/e33fe7d65facd8868f58b6e94ddc7f153a5a3f9f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BC}$" width="29" height="15" /> is given to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, we have that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/a/3bafb9f66f79fd3e846e2bff03bbc140fa6b0800.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{FM}$" width="34" height="15" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. Using the formula for the volume of a triangular pyramid, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/d/58d681dfa2abd8821ced5d97d1ca17588a914432.png" class="latex" alt="$V = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot 3 = \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="136" height="37" />. Also, since the triangular pyramid with base <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/6/d2675033f0b9487e4ffdafcca70961bd76dd72a5.png" class="latex" alt="$HGC$" width="45" height="12" /> and apex <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" /> has the exact same dimensions, it has volume <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> as well.
</p><p>The original wedge we considered in the last step has volume <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />, because it is half of the volume of the parallelepiped. We can subtract out the parts we found to have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/8/5b8d4e866fe7049717941b5db5d130bda2b03718.png" class="latex" alt="$3 - \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2 = 2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="99" height="37" />. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5">Solution 5</span></h2>
<p>We can start by identifying the information we need. We need to find the area of rectangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/2/6c2971e372d2cbb0037a4b42e8d365264aa40dc4.png" class="latex" alt="$EHCB$" width="59" height="12" /> and the height of rectangular prism <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/7/3e7e2f03e12e18c37a0e3f339650e632fcca05dc.png" class="latex" alt="$EHCBM$" width="79" height="12" />.
</p><p>In order to find the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/3/ee3ad769352583d01dc91a29977cd5c44a6859df.png" class="latex" alt="$EHCB,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="64" height="16" /> we can use the Pythagorean Theorem. We find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/8/df87ace0b84656d7d27a5456596350ec964b596e.png" class="latex" alt="$EB = \sqrt{13}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="87" height="18" />, so the area of rectangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/3/bc3cf268afbe6cb094c65bec3c9a962bb77611f4.png" class="latex" alt="$EHCB = \sqrt{13}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="118" height="18" />. We shall refer to this as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />.
</p><p>In order to find the height of rectangular prism <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/7/3e7e2f03e12e18c37a0e3f339650e632fcca05dc.png" class="latex" alt="$EHCBM$" width="79" height="12" />, we can examine triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/9/8c9c1db283a081d93c34b8f8be624033e15801a8.png" class="latex" alt="$EFB$" width="42" height="12" />. The answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/2/55206bce678ac0394d97dc04dd72398df2bdf572.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{E}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="26" height="24" />.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_6">Solution 6</span></h2>
<p>We start by setting the formula for the volume of a rectangular pyramid: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/1/e21ecee861d024e222191ccfb66124e06ae6234e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}Bh$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="38" height="37" />. By the Pythagorean Theorem, we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/1/3f133091672c4df1b9deee0754b2ec32d0cd3f48.png" class="latex" alt="$BE = \sqrt{BF^2 + EF^2} = \sqrt{13}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="221" height="19" />. Therefore, the area of the base is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/c/b9c86ed50a2e5c72b8e07bee98538cdbe1305f44.png" class="latex" alt="$1 \times \sqrt{13} = \sqrt{13}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="122" height="18" />. Next, we would like to know the height of the pyramid. We can observe that the altitude from point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/9/ae91ce43214ef7cd53d4ef57350933c86fa19909.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle EFB$" width="59" height="13" /> is parallel to the height of the pyramid and therefore congruent because those two altitudes are on the same plane of base <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/5/395a0a9ed507a1c875e84df828a4b98a32f655bc.png" class="latex" alt="$EBCH$" width="60" height="12" />. From this, we only need to find the altitude from point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/9/ae91ce43214ef7cd53d4ef57350933c86fa19909.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle EFB$" width="59" height="13" /> and plug it into our formula for the volume of a rectangular pyramid. This is easy because we already know the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/9/ae91ce43214ef7cd53d4ef57350933c86fa19909.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle EFB$" width="59" height="13" /> and the base from point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" />, so all we need to do is divide: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/9/9594533816f164b8a3bd97693151812f482a689b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2 \times 3}{\sqrt{13}} = \frac{6}{\sqrt{13}} = \frac{6\sqrt{13}}{13}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="175" height="45" />. Now all we need to do is plug in all our known values into the volume formula: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/f/e0fa5d64ca6431c9a8e6a186e1143e6f4a6655e9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}Bh = \frac{\sqrt{13} \times \frac{6\sqrt{13}}{13}}{3} = \boxed{(E) 2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="230" height="43" />
</p><p>~ellpet
</p>
