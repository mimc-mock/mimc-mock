<p>A box contains <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> chips, numbered <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />. Chips are drawn randomly one at a time without replacement until the sum of the values drawn exceeds <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />. What is the probability that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> draws are required?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/8/e987a6aadf83f1190380e2353a5b11f0568f6062.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)} \frac{1}{15} \qquad \textbf{(B)} \frac{1}{10} \qquad \textbf{(C)} \frac{1}{6} \qquad \textbf{(D)} \frac{1}{5} \qquad \textbf{(E)} \frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="358" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Notice that the only four ways such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> draws are required are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/3/2d338f393d100febd19e4284537ac40517b48340.png" class="latex" alt="$1,2$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="25" height="15" />; <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/4/6c44bd27931d72780474233e818b567de93a7ffa.png" class="latex" alt="$1,3$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="25" height="16" />; <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/8/a78ef4dbb3e6ba13dc2581d27e7d0e0c66c4a017.png" class="latex" alt="$2,1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="25" height="15" />; and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/4/6c44bd27931d72780474233e818b567de93a7ffa.png" class="latex" alt="$1,3$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="25" height="16" />. Notice that each of those cases has a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/4/ba4284d43bade818970a4516a2524f69ed9d299b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{5} \cdot \frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="37" height="37" /> chance, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/b/13b13a01b3e89cca14caefd13030ee353f4a1869.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{5} \cdot \frac{1}{4} \cdot 4 = \frac{1}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="97" height="37" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/b/55bdade53f471aab49f63d1a734979cc0c636640.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{D}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="27" height="24" />.
</p><p>Jonathan Xu (pi_is_delicious_69420)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We only have to analyze first two draws as that gives us insight on if third draw is necessary. Also, note that it is necessary to draw a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> in order to have 3 draws, otherwise <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> will be attainable in two or less draws. So the probability of getting a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/2/d129a7b3c0bf9fbd0693afdd426a66d2a0a8c3ae.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. It is necessary to pull either a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> on the next draw and the probability of that is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. But, the order of the draws can be switched so we get:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/9/3a95d1a33fcd51f5f29083cab5f0e2da3df77afb.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{5} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2 = \frac{1}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="97" height="37" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/5/3d543534cd2ead9a8dbdf64a3ff3beda948affc2.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed {D}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="27" height="24" />
</p><p>By: Soccer_JAMS
</p>
