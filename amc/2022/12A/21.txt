<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/6/c66458c6088164c71caaad852c39637d9cfb6740.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[P(x) = x^{2022} + x^{1011} + 1.\]" width="195" height="20" /> Which of the following polynomials is a factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/f/6efaaf74e576202fad965134bc49c6a34aac4c30.png" class="latex" alt="$P(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="37" height="18" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/6/eb645ff1b97eb975e6a64ab3dbe927742e856dc7.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)} \, x^2 -x + 1 \qquad\textbf{(B)} \, x^2 + x + 1 \qquad\textbf{(C)} \, x^4 + 1 \qquad\textbf{(D)} \, x^6 - x^3 + 1  \qquad\textbf{(E)} \, x^6 + x^3 + 1$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="684" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/f/b6f06aa3967638dd3b0d038f22c7db059eadcc49.png" class="latex" alt="$P(x) = x^{2022} + x^{1011} + 1$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="194" height="21" /> is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/4/a3456549c161082799fd75216f7930b8d0f74ca4.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{x^{3033}-1}{x^{1011}-1}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="75" height="42" /> by difference of powers.
</p><p>Therefore, the answer is a polynomial that divides <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/f/09f55b9e3311372fefcd82952d30a1775993764c.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{3033}-1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="71" height="18" /> but not <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/8/938e932d957a970d5a1aba60ca5adf8422985619.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{1011}-1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="71" height="18" />.
</p><p>Note that any polynomial <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/3/0f3e882e9bf05941af6acbf60a9d2278b0a9105b.png" class="latex" alt="$x^m-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="53" height="13" /> divides <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/a/f2addc206f2f79ece86f205af746ba29a8522627.png" class="latex" alt="$x^n-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="49" height="13" /> if and only if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> is a factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />.
</p><p>The prime factorizations of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/e/6ce7e7e796ef6786e471adc9586c4c0bbc1703cf.png" class="latex" alt="$1011$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="38" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/3/003e52e0dc1e958da2049dd2026f4ee4ff964c28.png" class="latex" alt="$3033$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="38" height="14" /> are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/2/662d0fdf9435b7671885efdc82156f52ffa6f139.png" class="latex" alt="$3*337$" width="53" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/2/b02e365c54e3d48049cecd230d495d5980eccdfd.png" class="latex" alt="$3^2*337$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="63" height="17" />, respectively.
</p><p>Hence, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/2/c4234cd0bad68d0c2a346bd80adbe46a8c9af137.png" class="latex" alt="$x^9-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="48" height="15" /> is a divisor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/f/09f55b9e3311372fefcd82952d30a1775993764c.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{3033}-1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="71" height="18" /> but not <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/8/938e932d957a970d5a1aba60ca5adf8422985619.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{1011}-1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="71" height="18" />.
</p><p>By difference of powers, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/6/7860a16988237b86708db7c0e6a88bc329df9015.png" class="latex" alt="$x^9-1=(x^3-1)(x^6+x^3+1)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="242" height="21" />.
Therefore, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/2/55206bce678ac0394d97dc04dd72398df2bdf572.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{E}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="26" height="24" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We simply test roots for each, as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/9/be9b4a4224c62d299eeaffc2fe50fa5e5d1de92d.png" class="latex" alt="$2022,1011$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="83" height="17" /> are multiples of three, we need to make sure the roots are in the form of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/a/6/2a6dd43eff8f41de4e726421bcddd025500b2dcf.png" class="latex" alt="$e^{i\frac{k\pi}{9}}$" width="29" height="17" />, so we only have to look at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/d/fed096cb12bb4627f07259bd946a58ff827aefa6.png" class="latex" alt="$D,E$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="37" height="16" />.
</p><p>If we look at choice <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/b/78b2ace6b1819fc277fbaf60da280d45e329c556.png" class="latex" alt="$x=e^{i\frac{\pm2\pi}{9}}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="76" height="20" /> which works perfectly, the answer is just <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" />
</p><p>~bluesoul
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/9/c19e2dad782d78c035a710bc9643b3bbef7e2141.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{1011} = u$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="74" height="17" />, now we can rewrite our polynomial as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/b/3eb3590858f295db620ecd9c219dd37b672e6fa3.png" class="latex" alt="$u^2+u+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="80" height="16" />. Using the quadratic formula to solve for the roots of this polynomial, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/a/baa714e9ca788c6c9a1fb3bb0b4f231a8be038eb.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x^{1011} = \frac{-1\pm i\sqrt{3}}{2}\]" width="140" height="41" /> Looking at our answer choices, we want to find a polynomial whose roots satisfy this expression. Since the expression <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/0/630812c47e96b214835deb16e51bfd8532dd6db1.png" class="latex" alt="$x^6+x^3+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="88" height="16" /> is in a similar form to our original polynomial, except with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/5/48524d3889ec8433cb03669176e738011df245b8.png" class="latex" alt="$x^3$" width="16" height="15" /> in place of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/c/15c3dfb16cb14f8581d36ec126d26e900b102bd3.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{1011}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="40" height="17" />, this would be a good place to start. Solving for the roots of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/5/48524d3889ec8433cb03669176e738011df245b8.png" class="latex" alt="$x^3$" width="16" height="15" /> in a similar fashion, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/1/5e1a03efb85bc4bfddd2819ccfca5e7e00335974.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x^3= \frac{-1\pm i\sqrt{3}}{2}\]" width="120" height="41" /> for the solution we are testing. Now notice that we can rewrite the roots of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/5/48524d3889ec8433cb03669176e738011df245b8.png" class="latex" alt="$x^3$" width="16" height="15" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/0/d40569f91729b626b08dd1a1d08cb3fc03b85e6b.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x^3 = \operatorname{cis}{\frac{2\pi}{3}}, \operatorname{cis}{\frac{4\pi}{3}}\]" width="143" height="37" /> Both of which are third roots of unity. We want to now check if this value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/5/48524d3889ec8433cb03669176e738011df245b8.png" class="latex" alt="$x^3$" width="16" height="15" /> satisfies <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/2/20217f37ad8209ba815b87a7b704129e0b73791a.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{1011} = \frac{-1\pm i\sqrt{3}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="144" height="43" />. Notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/3/bc3df1e119de29ec9d02d557f46005c629495eea.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{1011} = (x^{3})^{112\cdot3}\cdot x^3$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="158" height="21" />, and since both values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/5/48524d3889ec8433cb03669176e738011df245b8.png" class="latex" alt="$x^3$" width="16" height="15" /> are roots of unity, we can simplify the expression we want satisfiedto the expression to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/c/e4c95a71bc09338b76b826ca80d28b09663c740b.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{1011}=x^3$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="82" height="17" />. Since both values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/5/48524d3889ec8433cb03669176e738011df245b8.png" class="latex" alt="$x^3$" width="16" height="15" /> are also values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/c/15c3dfb16cb14f8581d36ec126d26e900b102bd3.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{1011}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="40" height="17" />, the roots for our <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/0/630812c47e96b214835deb16e51bfd8532dd6db1.png" class="latex" alt="$x^6+x^3+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="88" height="16" /> are also roots of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/9/53920ab88475d6af2e0ff7ea81909e2ddf7961b3.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{2022}+x^{1011}+1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="131" height="18" />, meaning that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/4/cf4a152021171080b28ca990bd705c93d856fe41.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x^6+x^3+1 | x^{2022}+x^{1011}+1\]" width="221" height="20" /> so Therefore, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/2/55206bce678ac0394d97dc04dd72398df2bdf572.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{E}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="26" height="24" />.
