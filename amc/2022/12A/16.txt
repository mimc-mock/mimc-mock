<p>A <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/9/fa9c60dfec433cfc429e75077f024649a7df64f4.png" class="latex" alt="$\emph{triangular number}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="133" height="17" /> is a positive integer that can be expressed in the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/5/9d5608b150a01243cf4bf6edb2073184ae30f5ab.png" class="latex" alt="$t_n = 1+2+3+\cdots+n$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="190" height="17" />, for some positive integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />. The three smallest triangular numbers that are also perfect squares are
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/c/f7c2bf9b74663cbbe1032a94b651160b2fb1231e.png" class="latex" alt="$t_1 = 1 = 1^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="90" height="20" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/f/4df1e503cf50b8eb2e3e107ab48553e2744b07af.png" class="latex" alt="$t_8 = 36 = 6^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="99" height="20" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/e/3be623973c32db9c582d58eb9abd8bf8e547d932.png" class="latex" alt="$t_{49} = 1225 = 35^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="133" height="20" />. What is the sum of the digits of the fourth smallest triangular number that is also a perfect square?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/8/938c4f1e2f7a266556ad6f2902560d33e5ba82cf.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 6 \qquad \textbf{(B) } 9 \qquad \textbf{(C) } 12 \qquad \textbf{(D) } 18 \qquad \textbf{(E) } 27$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="382" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/6/356a2cea8d91bb6d133df5c23e711ac93ec3d8e7.png" class="latex" alt="$t_n = \frac{n (n+1)}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="113" height="40" />.
If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/9/2e9bb94664d3e2d8c57a28afbd35f239ab99ef27.png" class="latex" alt="$t_n$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="14" height="14" /> is a perfect square, then it can be written as
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/9/c999603c6d0325812110bcb41831ca63d31418c9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{n (n+1)}{2} = k^2$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="115" height="40" />,
where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> is a positive integer.
</p><p>Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/f/d1f1b88a1f62ab9d28eea95385e71a91e2ea42f6.png" class="latex" alt="$n (n+1) = 2 k^2$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="120" height="21" />. Rearranging, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/f/2df343fc20b2cf867b9aed5e500b0976567229f4.png" class="latex" alt="$(2n+1)^2-2(2k)^2=1$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="180" height="21" />, a Pell equation. So <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/5/cc5e6711102ba644d139579312035c76089d15d0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2n+1}{2k}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="57" height="38" /> must be a truncation of the continued fraction for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/4/2c4dc1409208ff6a3039f4871d85c16763334983.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" />:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/5/6655077f7c06c45d1f53ef20296902ecbd0d0727.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{eqnarray*} 1+\frac12&amp;=&amp;\frac{2\cdot1+1}{2\cdot1}\\ 1+\frac1{2+\frac1{2+\frac12}}&amp;=&amp;\frac{2\cdot8+1}{2\cdot6}\\ 1+\frac1{2+\frac1{2+\frac1{2+\frac1{2+\frac12}}}}&amp;=&amp;\frac{2\cdot49+1}{2\cdot35}\\ 1+\frac1{2+\frac1{2+\frac1{2+\frac1{2+\frac1{2+\frac1{2+\frac12}}}}}}&amp;=&amp;\frac{2\cdot288+1}{2\cdot204} \end{eqnarray*}" width="298" height="240" />
</p><p>Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/c/07c2eccbf8bf6e984746a5512023890bf8afc16d.png" class="latex" alt="$t_{288} = \frac{288\cdot289}{2} = 204^2 = 41616$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="254" height="38" />, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/e/7ae344dde8b64f37737c7bb685d66594e984d34e.png" class="latex" alt="$4+1+6+1+6=\boxed{\textbf{(D) 18}}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="227" height="30" />.
