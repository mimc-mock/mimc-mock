<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The number <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/a/8ea972a3547d6e5b8c88bc77e0110bf571d75898.png" class="latex" alt="$I_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="15" /> can be written as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/b/cdbc4e27f3de15083a95942e285fa59203466513.png" class="latex" alt="$10^{k+2} + 64 = 5^{k+2}\cdot 2^{k+2} + 2^6$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="226" height="17" />.
</p><p>For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/4/064ef04cf06a910513d8b3d9b4a55248aee8759a.png" class="latex" alt="$k\in\{1,2,3\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="92" height="18" /> we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/c/0ec50ba8aa4dccec887528b7f2f7097a71472420.png" class="latex" alt="$I_k = 2^{k+2} \left( 5^{k+2} + 2^{4-k} \right)$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="181" height="22" />. The first value in the parentheses is odd, the second one is even, hence their sum is odd and we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/a/f9ae325aa59d9b79f634c5415bad4b3f536cf5e9.png" class="latex" alt="$N(k)=k+2\leq 5$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="139" height="18" />.
</p><p>For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/8/df8b57d9b5385406a194d393db9d6b7e1bc42ef4.png" class="latex" alt="$k&gt;4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="13" /> we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/0/540d90698e496753635c3e02ca3724b8678c3401.png" class="latex" alt="$I_k=2^6 \left( 5^{k+2}\cdot 2^{k-4} + 1 \right)$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="186" height="22" />. For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/8/df8b57d9b5385406a194d393db9d6b7e1bc42ef4.png" class="latex" alt="$k&gt;4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="13" /> the value in the parentheses is odd, hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/4/114dfa5407c666f41fd4dae15953796050a18fc7.png" class="latex" alt="$N(k)=6$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="73" height="18" />.
</p><p>This leaves the case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/a/eeae143c5c9dbd376ffa1ffa8bdb93a58e8c9889.png" class="latex" alt="$k=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="13" />. We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/4/b6433dff7dfe5c12787d9f82e8fbcc869d883605.png" class="latex" alt="$I_4 = 2^6 \left( 5^6 + 1 \right)$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="120" height="22" />. The value <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/5/285e3280d0a65093a3e94dd9adc36f55022bf43f.png" class="latex" alt="$5^6 + 1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="47" height="16" /> is obviously even. And as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/6/5463d6e14c5b1d8bffb9515cdf98e03b7e3e683b.png" class="latex" alt="$5\equiv 1 \pmod 4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="113" height="18" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/2/1622b9a318ff20133e0a7525a5d79a5d6c138f60.png" class="latex" alt="$5^6 \equiv 1 \pmod 4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="120" height="19" />, and therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/1/ac10d4b676c5fa69c9d7db386775cf111ee7c2ea.png" class="latex" alt="$5^6 + 1 \equiv 2 \pmod 4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="151" height="19" />. Hence the largest power of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> that divides <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/0/26078d10d25b9644e81c8d09a505dc4f5054eeca.png" class="latex" alt="$5^6+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="47" height="16" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/6/4e68ad31d12ba3d14b80a9341ee44e6238555124.png" class="latex" alt="$2^1$" width="14" height="15" />, and this gives us the desired maximum of the function <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" />: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/c/acc7e11db90d3495453e318edc4ce0a829d49a51.png" class="latex" alt="$N(4) = \boxed{7}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="85" height="23" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> is a prime factor of an integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> if and only if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> is even. Therefore, given any sufficiently high positive integral value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />, dividing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/a/8ea972a3547d6e5b8c88bc77e0110bf571d75898.png" class="latex" alt="$I_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="15" /> by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/1/a91d92ded08453fe4662a56223d382b4cba6aa35.png" class="latex" alt="$2^6$" width="15" height="15" /> yields a terminal digit of zero, and dividing by 2 again leaves us with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/e/40e1ca5e9c31a0314225351c99ca61ccb73e27a4.png" class="latex" alt="$2^7 \cdot a = I_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="78" height="17" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is an odd integer.
Observe then that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/7/c17f91eed0d0b7bbd17b6f319da9d5fdcf9ba2ce.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B)}7}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="49" height="28" /> must be the maximum value for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/2/a821d533038fe74e68da69b36840a2278bc2962e.png" class="latex" alt="$N(k)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="39" height="18" /> because whatever value we choose for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/2/a821d533038fe74e68da69b36840a2278bc2962e.png" class="latex" alt="$N(k)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="39" height="18" /> must be less than or equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" />.
</p><p>"Isn't this solution incomplete because we need to show that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/0/0f0c68edff0d9ec6000971c0c6395afcaa515f68.png" class="latex" alt="$N(k) = 7$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="74" height="18" /> can be reached?"
</p><p>An example of 7 being reached is 1000064. 1000064 divided by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/a/bdab99612d6ec89cecfa082adb0afdbf8ceee4ee.png" class="latex" alt="$2^7=128$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="67" height="15" /> is 7813.
</p><p>In fact, 1000064 is the ONLY <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/2/a821d533038fe74e68da69b36840a2278bc2962e.png" class="latex" alt="$N(k)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="39" height="18" /> that satisfies <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" />. All others are 6 or lower, because if there are more zeroes, to be divisible by 128 (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/c/afc77519ee6bab163b1aa195a3ba88718d414aa4.png" class="latex" alt="$2^7$" width="15" height="15" />), the last 7 digits must be divisible by 128, but 64 isn't. Meanwhile, if there are less zeroes, we can test by division that they do not work.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>As in the first solution, the number <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/a/8ea972a3547d6e5b8c88bc77e0110bf571d75898.png" class="latex" alt="$I_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="15" /> can be written as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/f/bafca4252546f2bea840e273fa95f4e832778c50.png" class="latex" alt="$10^{k+2} + 64 = 5^{k+2} 2^{k+2} + 2^6$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="213" height="17" />. Factor <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/1/a91d92ded08453fe4662a56223d382b4cba6aa35.png" class="latex" alt="$2^6$" width="15" height="15" /> out of the expression to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/1/73193193bfe255a2cb077d584b23df6b1a82b2a3.png" class="latex" alt="$I_k = 2^6(1+2^{k-4}5^{k+2})$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="169" height="20" />.
</p><p>You can now easily see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/a/8ea972a3547d6e5b8c88bc77e0110bf571d75898.png" class="latex" alt="$I_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="15" /> is divisible by at least 6 factors of two, from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/1/a91d92ded08453fe4662a56223d382b4cba6aa35.png" class="latex" alt="$2^6$" width="15" height="15" />. Any other factors of two will come from the expression <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/3/063b176a9f95ead3890a2d7afc48e86967a96f58.png" class="latex" alt="$(1+2^{k-4}5^{k+2})$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="112" height="20" />. 
</p><p>Make the substitution: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/d/74dbddd9bea7fff910794fa64e052ff212f0c60e.png" class="latex" alt="$x=k-4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="76" height="13" />.
You now have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/8/eb8c9a426504af36801b8e8990606b5c3dc7256e.png" class="latex" alt="$(1+2^{x}5^{x+6}) = (1+10^x 5^6)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="208" height="19" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/3/3b301e4b5383022bc349347f78436ff13e11851d.png" class="latex" alt="$5^6=15625$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="85" height="15" />, so the expression becomes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/0/6103440a736d345d6d2666533cf4686ab222e2bf.png" class="latex" alt="$(1+15625\cdot10^x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="129" height="18" /> This is valid when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/e/01e7c83cdff0da16def0ab5236a18d4159abf552.png" class="latex" alt="$x&gt;-4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="57" height="12" />. 
</p><p>Obviously, if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> is negative, the expression will be fractional and not contain any extra factors of two.
</p><p>If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/e/02e28efa00c4f2c66d1aa381c0cb1f8e0ef8d6e4.png" class="latex" alt="$x&gt;0$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="13" />, the value <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/f/81f4c414b52d36eea6569f2a432c32c1f18a3e0f.png" class="latex" alt="$15625\cdot10^x$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="83" height="13" /> will end in a zero. When 1 is added to the expression, the expression will end in 1. Since numbers divisible by 2 end in 0,2,4,6, or 8, the expression is not divisible by 2 and will not contribute to the total.
</p><p>If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/e/34ea5b6f6de584d56d19a89cc4923a9d9a5cfa41.png" class="latex" alt="$x=0$" width="43" height="12" />, the expression evaluates to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/2/5728b3410bdb6beef4e39adfa19bf2190b24434c.png" class="latex" alt="$15626$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="44" height="13" />. Dividing out twos, you find that this value is only divisible by one factor of 2.
</p><p>The six factors of two from earlier add to this factor of two to give <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/d/abd1547150201005422804dcf4b34e8618df63c4.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(B)}\ 7\qquad$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="43" height="17" />

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>Similar to the other solutions, notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/a/8ea972a3547d6e5b8c88bc77e0110bf571d75898.png" class="latex" alt="$I_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="15" /> can be written as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/d/82dc92db9a944efe67af7b5e8a8703d5721c6e7f.png" class="latex" alt="$10^{k+2}+64 \Rightarrow 2^{k+2}5^{k+2}+2^6$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="217" height="17" />. Factoring out <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/1/a91d92ded08453fe4662a56223d382b4cba6aa35.png" class="latex" alt="$2^6$" width="15" height="15" /> we see that
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/d/d0d93448c520dfc78370aed832a90502c3180871.png" class="latex" alt="$I_k = 2^6(2^{k-4}5^{k+2}+1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="169" height="20" />
</p><p>Notice that for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/e/54ec22546e712b50d52c0bd4e67a72a8a68a9740.png" class="latex" alt="$k &lt; 4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/0/d/c0d71d2dbe19950e06f78f1ecdc466fd6b53c6b9.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{k-4}$" width="33" height="15" /> will not be an integer, and will "steal" some <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />'s from the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/1/a91d92ded08453fe4662a56223d382b4cba6aa35.png" class="latex" alt="$2^6$" width="15" height="15" />. We don't want this to happen, since we want to maximize the exponent of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />. We start by considering <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/4/1b4096f1768d4b1191ccc0386de6b4cc16503a1e.png" class="latex" alt="$k = 4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="13" />. Then
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/7/f77fd0b7fdc5093264a21e6936789060d6b6469c.png" class="latex" alt="$I_k = 2^6(*5^6+1) \Rightarrow 2^6(5^6+1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="233" height="19" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/1/d414a9ee4aa06adfe1bd94c6a8d1ef59a7040a6f.png" class="latex" alt="$5^6$" width="15" height="15" /> is an odd number; more specifically, it ends in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/6/66644fd9f411299c3c1eabf151b5f601c9ef6c21.png" class="latex" alt="$25$" width="17" height="12" /> (all powers of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> after <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/f/50f9c72d91ec440826a71947c96d58c58318c97a.png" class="latex" alt="$5^1$" width="14" height="15" /> end in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/6/66644fd9f411299c3c1eabf151b5f601c9ef6c21.png" class="latex" alt="$25$" width="17" height="12" />). Therefore the value in the parentheses will be some large number that ends in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/a/bda00d9a38a0c6bd9aac2512c6d065581882d43c.png" class="latex" alt="$26$" width="17" height="12" />. Considering the rules of divisibility, we find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/0/26078d10d25b9644e81c8d09a505dc4f5054eeca.png" class="latex" alt="$5^6+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="47" height="16" /> is even, so it is divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />.
