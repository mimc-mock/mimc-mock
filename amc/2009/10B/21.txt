<p>What is the remainder when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/4/68434db7869612b348e56966f4ae2ffa786d788b.png" class="latex" alt="$3^0 + 3^1 + 3^2 + \cdots + 3^{2009}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="194" height="16" /> is divided by 8?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/2/eb29ca57051e7f39f8ade96c4576eb75817d49c8.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ 0\qquad \mathrm{(B)}\ 1\qquad \mathrm{(C)}\ 2\qquad \mathrm{(D)}\ 4\qquad \mathrm{(E)}\ 6$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="342" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>The sum of any four consecutive powers of 3 is divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/6/2668b54017f235efee8d36e015154b1ff46561b8.png" class="latex" alt="$3^0 + 3^1 + 3^2 +3^3 = 40$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="174" height="16" /> and hence is divisible by 8. Therefore
</p>
<dl><dd><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/d/87d1790e8db6e7e2c1e2f4df36c456acf04ee340.png" class="latex" alt="$(3^2 + 3^3 + 3^4 + 3^5) + \cdots + (3^{2006} + 3^{2007} + 3^{2008} + 3^{2009})$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="437" height="19" /></dd></dl>
<p>is divisible by 8.  So the required remainder is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/2/192f17bf83c572d3298ff161665f5b270d7dccb8.png" class="latex" alt="$3^0 + 3^1 = \boxed {4}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="100" height="23" />. The answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/b/bfbf4df7c25681bb178ddc50ee0c0d656aa25b5e.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(D)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/9/559a50826726cbb95c60c32517544bed2a954e42.png" class="latex" alt="$3^2 = 9 \equiv 1 \pmod 8$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="154" height="19" />. Hence for any <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/8/878f598749a91a7c88d72d686dd5ecd0b3e33c1d.png" class="latex" alt="$3^{2k}\equiv 1^k = 1 \pmod 8$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="169" height="20" />, and then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/6/96655ad2e9da41626471ce5e2045651eca786c35.png" class="latex" alt="$3^{2k+1} = 3\cdot 3^{2k} \equiv 3\cdot 1 = 3  \pmod 8$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="270" height="20" />.
</p><p>Therefore our sum gives the same remainder modulo <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/2/f825e3ce0f4942d027b5ec05ecbac8feb9708196.png" class="latex" alt="$1 + 3 + 1 + 3 + 1 + \cdots + 1 + 3$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="240" height="14" />. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/4/054090e60a5dcb8bc33213620b3af9278f9a3e12.png" class="latex" alt="$2010$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="13" /> terms in the sum, hence there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/a/5fa61da555b703baf7f541194b95d331878f9463.png" class="latex" alt="$2010/2 = 1005$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="114" height="18" /> pairs of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/3/193b9b8ef461adf0e723da43776cfb7386b5ebd1.png" class="latex" alt="$1+3$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="40" height="14" />, and thus the sum is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/f/45f2226320bf7847e220e7dda5bbba065d2b4350.png" class="latex" alt="$1005 \cdot 4 = 4020 \equiv 20 \equiv \boxed{4} \pmod 8$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="277" height="23" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h3>
<p>We have the formula <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/d/d5ddfa82b7af3f68e8b6a37031e1e107d82d44c2.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a(r^n-1)}{r-1}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="75" height="39" /> for the sum of a finite geometric sequence which we want to find the residue modulo 8.
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/b/6db10a23c131768960b3f0f57e141d4e9d2edcec.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1 \cdot (3^{2010}-1)}{2}\]" width="104" height="39" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/5/d05bd1b955c4f2eb083e52b012d3d33c86a94702.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{3^{2010}-1}{2} = \frac{9^{1005}-1}{2}\]" width="164" height="39" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/a/68a6dafa6a361a53ef0f2421e265eed91901085a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{9^{1005}-1}{2} \equiv \frac{1^{1005}-1}{2} \equiv \frac{0}{2} \pmod 8\]" width="283" height="39" />
Therefore, the numerator of the fraction is divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" />. However, when we divide the numerator by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, we get a remainder of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> modulo <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" />, giving us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/b/bfbf4df7c25681bb178ddc50ee0c0d656aa25b5e.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(D)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" />.
</p><p>Note: you need to prove that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/e/b9e6e42c3fae1e6d2f6a9b861c9e974a79a5cbd4.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{9^{1005}-1}{2}\]" width="68" height="39" /> is not congruent to 0 mod 16 because if so, then the whole thing would be congruent to 0 mod 8, even after dividing by 2 ~ ilikepi12
</p>
