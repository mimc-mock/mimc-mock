<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/e/8be90c4c2c281e2298292cbc415bcb6e08019586.png" class="latex" alt="$a + ar_1 + ar_1^2 + ar_1^3 + \cdots$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="195" height="19" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/f/4ff4c53a44b64ff99685cb7814fabc9abfac2828.png" class="latex" alt="$a + ar_2 + ar_2^2 + ar_2^3 + \cdots$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="195" height="19" /> be two different infinite geometric series of positive numbers with the same first term.  The sum of the first series is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/c/adc143c3f887fa7e92fd80d91bb22af51b4abd77.png" class="latex" alt="$r_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="13" height="10" />, and the sum of the second series is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/4/0b4f3f4b698ce35667432041e168f7653533fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$r_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="14" height="10" />.  What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/3/9338efce1e9efab973711535ac35105f76563762.png" class="latex" alt="$r_1 + r_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="52" height="13" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/c/56c539aef3d61e416b7c9cf880277f4998e9b379.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 0\qquad \textbf{(B)}\ \frac {1}{2}\qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 1\qquad \textbf{(D)}\ \frac {1 + \sqrt {5}}{2}\qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="403" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Using the formula for the sum of a geometric series we get that the sums of the given two sequences are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/9/6d949cab5e29cb9eacb58ef6fdaa5c105a015eb0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac a{1-r_1}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="49" height="35" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/5/de57026814801e0fbc038aaa54d16a6b9f70c058.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac a{1-r_2}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="49" height="35" />. 
</p><p>Hence we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/9/689e152b77560dd68e67e9dac8b5cf1fd819382e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac a{1-r_1} = r_1$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="88" height="35" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/0/53089e4f85f602d2d7f5b7fad83863be4cc9519b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac a{1-r_2} = r_2$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="89" height="35" />.
This can be rewritten as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/0/050a5a38993e4934023e4bffec0032bdc02de781.png" class="latex" alt="$r_1(1-r_1) = r_2(1-r_2) = a$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="211" height="18" />. 
</p><p>As we are given that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/c/adc143c3f887fa7e92fd80d91bb22af51b4abd77.png" class="latex" alt="$r_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="13" height="10" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/4/0b4f3f4b698ce35667432041e168f7653533fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$r_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="14" height="10" /> are distinct, these must be precisely the two roots of the equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/d/1ed95bf4e1196cb72ef474469448272dc6b9bd32.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2 - x + a = 0$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="115" height="16" />.
</p><p>Using <a href="/wiki/index.php/Vieta%27s_formulas" title="Vieta&#39;s formulas">Vieta's formulas</a> we get that the sum of these two roots is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/4/3547351abf03ab0b6cbda7b0b905b2e4f6220e1e.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{1}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="20" height="23" />.
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We basically have two infinite geometric series whose sum is equivalent to the common ratio. Let us have a geometric series: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/4/eb4a8e7fa93e80377aea2129c54b49fcc75444c0.png" class="latex" alt="$b, br, br^2.....$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="87" height="18" />. 
</p><p>The sum is: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/9/0596e08d84e91fbe79163af931c3a373bb566c67.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{b}{1-r} = r.$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="79" height="38" /> Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/c/7ec84c04fd03ad77cfaa76a379aa0541f86bc52c.png" class="latex" alt="$b = r-r^2$" width="77" height="15" /> and by Vieta's, the sum of the two possible values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/c/adc143c3f887fa7e92fd80d91bb22af51b4abd77.png" class="latex" alt="$r_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="13" height="10" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/4/0b4f3f4b698ce35667432041e168f7653533fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$r_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="14" height="10" />) is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />.
</p><p><br />
</p><p>~conantwiz2023
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternate_Solution">Alternate Solution</span></h2>
<p>Using the formula for the sum of a geometric series we get that the sums of the given two sequences are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/9/6d949cab5e29cb9eacb58ef6fdaa5c105a015eb0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac a{1-r_1}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="49" height="35" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/5/de57026814801e0fbc038aaa54d16a6b9f70c058.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac a{1-r_2}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="49" height="35" />. 
</p><p>Hence we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/9/689e152b77560dd68e67e9dac8b5cf1fd819382e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac a{1-r_1} = r_1$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="88" height="35" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/0/53089e4f85f602d2d7f5b7fad83863be4cc9519b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac a{1-r_2} = r_2$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="89" height="35" />.
This can be rewritten as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/0/050a5a38993e4934023e4bffec0032bdc02de781.png" class="latex" alt="$r_1(1-r_1) = r_2(1-r_2) = a$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="211" height="18" />.
</p><p>Which can be further rewritten as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/e/2cea29d8ae27b85213a563736776339ebc0869b4.png" class="latex" alt="$r_1-r_1^2 = r_2-r_2^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="131" height="19" />.
Rearranging the equation we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/1/fb19a3d00d52ea14fe0c8eda6915b2a6ce7a3231.png" class="latex" alt="$r_1-r_2 = r_1^2-r_2^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="131" height="19" />.
Expressing this as a difference of squares we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/3/be31e8842862a3c45543f522558f9d11a29d12d3.png" class="latex" alt="$r_1-r_2 = (r_1-r_2)(r_1+r_2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="211" height="18" />.
</p><p>Dividing by like terms we finally get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/9/a4916571b4c31c84c1226806fc695c2798677a26.png" class="latex" alt="$r_1+r_2 = \boxed{1}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="98" height="23" /> as desired.
</p><p><br />
</p><p><br />
Note: It is necessary to check that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/6/ab6aab31d9871356a02b0d6042cd8d67e500269f.png" class="latex" alt="$r_1-r_2\ne 0$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="86" height="17" />, as you cannot divide by zero. As the problem states that the series are different, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/7/527eb0f0e7b75dc304574944e8893beb189a8d51.png" class="latex" alt="$r_1 \ne r_2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="54" height="17" />, and so there is no division by zero error.
</p>
