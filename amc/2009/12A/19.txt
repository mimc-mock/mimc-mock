<p>Andrea inscribed a circle inside a regular pentagon, circumscribed a circle around the pentagon, and calculated the area of the region between the two circles.  Bethany did the same with a regular heptagon (7 sides).  The areas of the two regions were <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, respectively.  Each polygon had a side length of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />.  Which of the following is true?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/b/ecbf74be4f86a48d9cd51a33e1c077e933b2ddac.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ A = \frac {25}{49}B\qquad \textbf{(B)}\ A = \frac {5}{7}B\qquad \textbf{(C)}\ A = B\qquad \textbf{(D)}\ A$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="444" height="38" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/1/d41c27d7ed27f363bc7e6112eec93fa86bdcef77.png" class="latex" alt="$= \frac {7}{5}B\qquad \textbf{(E)}\ A = \frac {49}{25}B$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="187" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>In any regular polygon with side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, consider the isosceles triangle formed by the center of the polygon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> and two consecutive vertices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/5/ce58e4af225c93d08606c26554caaa5ae32edeba.png" class="latex" alt="$Y$" width="14" height="12" />. We are given that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/e/aae09d4486fc00fff609a7fe5436325c57e18b5a.png" class="latex" alt="$XY=2$" width="64" height="12" />. Obviously <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/c/dac5c1a27e11d7576cefe2973c935d5f82f6dafc.png" class="latex" alt="$SX=SY=r$" width="112" height="12" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> is the radius of the circumcircle. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" /> be the midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/7/2c7774174993ffa29a38cf577ae34d8e0a3a63cb.png" class="latex" alt="$XY$" width="30" height="12" />. Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/d/81d7eb63ca6ce45458627c306069aa41f0b98a8b.png" class="latex" alt="$XT=TY=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="114" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/1/b8124714d09a4b0ad62693c0062ab8f519a9bb41.png" class="latex" alt="$TS=\rho$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="59" height="16" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/2/0027034d8a10372a06deaf4f4084c01956587479.png" class="latex" alt="$\rho$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> is the radius of the incircle.
</p><p>Applying the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Pythagorean_theorem" class="mw-redirect" title="Pythagorean theorem">Pythagorean theorem</a> on the triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/5/4854f74585df7337efe7f0220f1f7f0cff6f52a9.png" class="latex" alt="$STX$" width="41" height="12" />, we get that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/f/78fe6b6bddb477e1c0ddaedda1757bff63f4a6cc.png" class="latex" alt="$\rho^2 + 1 = r^2$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="87" height="18" />.
</p><p>Then the area between the circumcircle and the incircle can be computed as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/0/570765cb6ee9ded33d0b18ec0b76385e52ef2364.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi r^2 - \pi \rho^2 = \pi r^2 - \pi (r^2 - 1) = \pi$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="258" height="19" />.
</p><p>Hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/c/43c0aa689c03f264dc37b4b2d79699793157f937.png" class="latex" alt="$A=\pi$" width="48" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/c/37cf18b1932461b8e7cdeda58f33158210528219.png" class="latex" alt="$B=\pi$" width="49" height="12" />, and therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/d/a5d1500269e237924cf5651bf6ec04f382a38403.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{A=B}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="64" height="24" />.
</p>
