Vertices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> are lattice points on the lines <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/0/a40fee4603b4536f856dde720ee499468ace443f.png" class="latex" alt="$y = x$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="11" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/a/52ab251aec37ec434a204cc59c2f7374421727c7.png" class="latex" alt="$y = kx$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="54" height="16" /> for some integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/1/411e8b2b30fbb26e55ed0d28303d10fa81aa5606.png" class="latex" alt="$k &gt; 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="13" />, respectively. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>The area of any parallelogram <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> can be computed as the size of the vector product of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/d/a8dfbb08d2b4dac0825dded0c20a373dd129bd35.png" class="latex" alt="$\overrightarrow{AB}$" width="27" height="22" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/1/4f1cfe1132bc1c6aaf9ab3fa99ce137532360136.png" class="latex" alt="$\overrightarrow{AD}$" width="28" height="22" />.
</p><p>In our setting where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/6/e56c2d75970eccaf7b305f042636bcebdb2a1cb0.png" class="latex" alt="$A=(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="76" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/9/f4964826732fa052019eb8bad4488db4d755201e.png" class="latex" alt="$B=(s,s)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="76" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/7/427800ef758510b7986317ba8e4945b5310841be.png" class="latex" alt="$D=(t,kt)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="83" height="18" /> this is simply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/c/e0cc3ff6a2e938d2219c366810d31f8256d839b0.png" class="latex" alt="$s\cdot kt - s\cdot t = (k-1)st$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="183" height="18" />. 
</p><p>In other words, we need to count the triples of integers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/a/3aa1f2fef22dbfcc135082dd0dc1b28e091f113d.png" class="latex" alt="$(k,s,t)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="54" height="18" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/f/08ff8ea020546fb6e8c90abbfb0b50802eee802d.png" class="latex" alt="$k&gt;1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/5/b15708c035601f339fb9bd91ffaa9cabf36581ba.png" class="latex" alt="$s,t&gt;0$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="56" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/9/9095a0d8f053339003c73a174c372c5c142d90a4.png" class="latex" alt="$(k-1)st = 1,\!000,\!000 = 2^6 5^6$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="224" height="19" />.
</p><p>These can be counted as follows: We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> identical red balls (representing powers of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />), <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> blue balls (representing powers of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />), and three labeled urns (representing the factors <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/c/7dc6fab4c2fdb4e9046030fbfdcf6ea721b05602.png" class="latex" alt="$k-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/d/e0d2bf360290fd61d1c1557e763f2622363b3d35.png" class="latex" alt="$t$" width="6" height="11" />). The red balls can be distributed in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/9/669bcc02211fba22e266e8f28a387a87c997d8a7.png" class="latex" alt="${8\choose 2} = 28$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="79" height="53" /> ways, and for each of these ways, the blue balls can then also be distributed in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/6/9265f5dd6dade78136a6be158ace46bf7c0f8ce3.png" class="latex" alt="$28$" width="17" height="12" /> ways. (See <a href="/wiki/index.php/Distinguishability" title="Distinguishability">Distinguishability</a> for a more detailed explanation.)
</p><p>Thus there are exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/7/407c7bfcc0be773985dfbf75feaaf67af2089057.png" class="latex" alt="$28^2 = 784$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="77" height="15" /> ways how to break <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/b/dbb4ab5e2bb1972223acd30cf80411d5aa12cd8c.png" class="latex" alt="$1,\!000,\!000$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="72" height="16" /> into three positive integer factors, and for each of them we get a single parallelogram. Hence the number of valid parallelograms is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/4/74434c300981a3d1684c6b6bc0f74747e73a1e78.png" class="latex" alt="$784 \longrightarrow \boxed{C}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="92" height="24" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>Without the vector product the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> can be computed for example as follows: If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/9/f4964826732fa052019eb8bad4488db4d755201e.png" class="latex" alt="$B=(s,s)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="76" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/7/427800ef758510b7986317ba8e4945b5310841be.png" class="latex" alt="$D=(t,kt)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="83" height="18" />, then clearly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/8/f4857652cc40de8c5a2f29e4fe39ad67ac5c1666.png" class="latex" alt="$C=(s+t,s+kt)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="144" height="18" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/4/52413f2445a949790c6ec387504c6c6a220e9d32.png" class="latex" alt="$B&#39;=(s,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="82" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/f/a/6fa98a511b079680fa9e522fce301a60ff832ff9.png" class="latex" alt="$C&#39;=(s+t,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="110" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/e/2fe47a1bff650f33f03e44e9d42d0a05c237291b.png" class="latex" alt="$D&#39;=(t,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="80" height="18" /> be the orthogonal projections of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> onto the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> axis. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/c/82c8af2036036acc93984fa2d3e7fac5cb926624.png" class="latex" alt="$[P]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="22" height="18" /> denote the area of the polygon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />. We can then compute:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/2/0e2371bad9d61eec15e73dc5d80907d7d6b38277.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} [ABCD] &amp; = [ADD&#39;] + [DD&#39;C&#39;C] - [BB&#39;C&#39;C] - [ABB&#39;] \\ &amp; = \frac{kt^2}2 + \frac{s(s+2kt)}2 - \frac{t(2s+kt)}2 - \frac{s^2}2 \\ &amp; = kst - st \\ &amp; = (k-1)st. \end{align*}" width="429" height="115" />
The remainder of the solution is the same as the above.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>

</p><p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/b/29b42f015c5ff4ae3bf1a017bdeec1574a3311d0.png" class="latex" alt="$2^x5^y$" width="33" height="12" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/5/32581661cddf01ad30c1e36efe72f99eb0a0adca.png" class="latex" alt="$x, y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="11" /> are integers on the interval <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/0/5e01eb697d66161f27846d4c0ad5475245aea55f.png" class="latex" alt="$[0, 6]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="34" height="18" />.
</p><p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/c/21c291117120a9dd386063ceb0165ba91e1f818f.png" class="latex" alt="$2^w5^z$" width="35" height="12" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/6/f0673d368732db7a8bd7e6c74d7be202360c1a35.png" class="latex" alt="$w, z$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="30" height="11" /> are integers, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/f/44f1458ebcb28eef0a9087b43afe6495ce21388c.png" class="latex" alt="$x+w \le 6$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="79" height="15" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/f/4ff4600709368c3c68da3026da2d87aa648bfe71.png" class="latex" alt="$y+z \le 6$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="74" height="16" />.
</p><p>For a pair <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/f/adfd9ae8a3fac031d3b8b470a52a709a23d4d6d2.png" class="latex" alt="$(x, y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="40" height="18" />, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/5/1f50ad524e676579df070adcb5377c8c3c494a01.png" class="latex" alt="$7-x$" width="41" height="12" /> possibilities for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/e/9ee4b825a2e36ae093ed7be5e4851ef453b34914.png" class="latex" alt="$w$" width="13" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/8/c58958911f044ed870b988b527491b031e7a476b.png" class="latex" alt="$7-y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="40" height="15" /> possibilites for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> doesn't have to be the co-factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/7/d0719671cbf653c7ab0101af730760c79f5ec972.png" class="latex" alt="$1,000,000$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="79" height="16" />, it just can't be big enough such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/b/0eb24e7aef380ad64a1713ce200eeb4ef9a11d60.png" class="latex" alt="$bd &gt; 1,000,000$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="120" height="16" />), for a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/a/5ba85bc57bae5d46de8f7b52524979e26cdd9e15.png" class="latex" alt="$(7-x)(7-y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="109" height="18" /> possibilities. So we want
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/5/4f5d0c64b61831ead0014867afd98d036e824f58.png" class="latex" alt="$\sum_{k=0, i=0}^6 (7-k)(7-i)$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="155" height="52" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/b/95bb6f4bfb1fa400f2c817d7cbff7ba5925c5523.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\text{the sum of the number of possible pairs}(w, z) \text{ for all pairs}(k , i)\text{ for } k[0, 6]\text{ and } i[0, 6]\right)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="631" height="18" />
</p><p><br />
Notice that if we "fix" the value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />, at, say <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />, then run through all of the values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/8/34857b3ba74ce5cd8607f3ebd23e9015908ada71.png" class="latex" alt="$i$" width="5" height="12" />, change the value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, and run through all of the values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/8/34857b3ba74ce5cd8607f3ebd23e9015908ada71.png" class="latex" alt="$i$" width="5" height="12" /> again, and so on until we exhaust all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/c/34c8e6bf4d57cd6f2bb0d7d43e00f0f442f53b84.png" class="latex" alt="$49$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> combinations of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/3/6c37980b1733b0f5af8b3078a2c42aebaac6e6f4.png" class="latex" alt="$(k, i)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="37" height="18" /> we get something like this:
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/2/11289e7452702a198aca08b6cefe3e2e7c6f62ed.png" class="latex" alt="$1*1 + 1*2 + ... + 1*6 + 1*7 + 2*1 + 2*2 + ... + 2*6 + 2*7 + ..... + 7*1 + 7*2 + ... + 7*6 + 7*7$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="828" height="14" />
</p><p><br />
which can be rewritten
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/7/9579ae03c0d91d7a13e6ce9f1b65959bca32cee0.png" class="latex" alt="$1(1+2+...+7)+2(1+2+...+7)+.....+7(1+2+...+7)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="487" height="18" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/5/44588feb548f490b52fd5e4787f444a4861fb625.png" class="latex" alt="$\Rightarrow (1+2+...+7)(1+2+...+7)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="268" height="18" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/2/b7278fc64f86733413e073e53dcb4d9f08b905ad.png" class="latex" alt="$\Rightarrow 28^2$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="47" height="15" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/a/12a6fa54abd4fba98b4e4dceddef6ab3ad9632ee.png" class="latex" alt="$\Rightarrow 784$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="50" height="13" />
</p><p><br />
So there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/c/a9c7cc583137cd786583f9df893ce7124f6b20e8.png" class="latex" alt="$784$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="27" height="13" /> possible sets of coordinates <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/e/ccedc2f7ab3f66b36d3e0cf1536a3c801f01f421.png" class="latex" alt="$B,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="18" height="16" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/b/e4b71b4d70045cace4c0a35b4c95a438181a4857.png" class="latex" alt="$D \Rightarrow \boxed{\text{C}}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="67" height="23" />.
</p><p>Note: this solution could be greatly simplified by using the Shoelace Formula on the triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/a/a6adfd3ad27a2dddeb0af7131d73c33c86991e93.png" class="latex" alt="$ABD$" width="43" height="13" />, which we know has half the area of the parallelogram. This eliminates the need to find the coordinates of point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />. 
</p><p>(Notational note: I'm not sure if the notation for double index summation is correct or even applicable in the context of this problem. If someone could fix the notation so that it is correct, or replace it without changing the general content of this solution, that would be great. If the notation is correct, then just delete this footnote)
</p>
