<p>For each positive integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/0/15023750e6756bd07d7ce22f7f9a167ab4dc8993.png" class="latex" alt="$f(n) = n^4 - 360n^2 + 400$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="195" height="19" />. What is the sum of all values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/f/eef0203e65664bd00b5f0325c5c5fcd81bcead8d.png" class="latex" alt="$f(n)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" /> that are prime numbers?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/b/d1b1c5fea192981f78c44ddb9327a9ed993d7496.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 794\qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 796\qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 798\qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 800\qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 802$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="439" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions">Solutions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>To find the answer it was enough to play around with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/2/bb2c93730dbb48558bb3c4738c956c4e8f816437.png" class="latex" alt="$f$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="16" />. One can easily find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/7/4/6744fe1984ffd23420a49bd669cdc4ae41596b82.png" class="latex" alt="$f(1)=41$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="75" height="18" /> is a prime, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/2/bb2c93730dbb48558bb3c4738c956c4e8f816437.png" class="latex" alt="$f$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="16" /> becomes negative for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/7/5e7a1b6098a8c98b8e4adae526aeef4b91712620.png" class="latex" alt="$18$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />, and then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/2/b02d809584ff4c5f4f886a0f09947ddd3da8a4e0.png" class="latex" alt="$f(19)=761$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="93" height="18" /> is again a prime number. And as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/d/56dfd30eb4dd9ef88cbf49dd1b2de7c541af8dfe.png" class="latex" alt="$41 + 761 = 802$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="118" height="14" /> is already the largest option, the answer must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/5/905f0e87cf9e9d5036a600e76f1c46504a16d6dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{802}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="39" height="23" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>We will now show a complete solution, with a proof that no other values are prime. 
</p><p>Consider the function <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/1/3f1e33555e018f969e8f0923060ab817b420288d.png" class="latex" alt="$g(x) = x^2 - 360x + 400$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="184" height="19" />, then obviously <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/9/da9e9d746cf1a74a8d7a3c8b1919012af7651c4c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x) = g(x^2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="99" height="19" />.
</p><p>The roots of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/1/311cabda3a9b09f0dde217303ca9d1cd9201dcf6.png" class="latex" alt="$g$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> are:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/6/bd611e70153e15741f627754fda139a655bfc44a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x_{1,2}  = \frac{ 360 \pm \sqrt{ 360^2 - 4\cdot 400 } }2  = 180 \pm 80 \sqrt 5\]" width="344" height="40" />
</p><p>We can then write <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/7/4e796510ac57a23e097d8c488d76443f2f9f087b.png" class="latex" alt="$g(x) = (x - 180 - 80\sqrt 5)(x - 180 + 80\sqrt 5)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="334" height="21" />, and thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/3/3832aff9f4be45f15ddd97e1992c176fcf2131a3.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x) = (x^2 - 180 - 80\sqrt 5)(x^2 - 180 + 80\sqrt 5)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="350" height="21" />.
</p><p>We would now like to factor the right hand side further, using the formula <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/e/11e905a5f7770ac96154a2a9602d8107a8b0c4aa.png" class="latex" alt="$(x^2 - y^2) = (x-y)(x+y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="206" height="19" />. To do this, we need to express both constants as squares of some other constants. Luckily, we have a pretty good idea how they look like.
</p><p>We are looking for rational <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/f/4ff81e88a3aca9be17e58febca8eef80dae52c22.png" class="latex" alt="$(a+b\sqrt 5)^2 = 180 + 80\sqrt 5$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="202" height="21" />. Expanding the left hand side and comparing coefficients, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/3/fa346c4048ee278b4becf31ee4e68c8a05ff1879.png" class="latex" alt="$ab=40$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="59" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/b/09b1c11eef04d5ce6161e50dd0c78bbec49e8e61.png" class="latex" alt="$a^2 + 5b^2 = 180$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="114" height="16" />. We can easily guess (or compute) the solution <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/b/32b95a5a1bf6f50913e72972a09d742814553958.png" class="latex" alt="$a=10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="51" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/a/fda5328bc4703ff88e62aa83ea58ae4e8361f5ee.png" class="latex" alt="$b=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="41" height="13" />.
</p><p>Hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/0/0402eeac3c36a4a9cfb723f692a2af6e4cac861e.png" class="latex" alt="$180 + 80\sqrt 5 = (10 + 4\sqrt 5)^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="210" height="21" />, and we can easily verify that also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/4/8b4315b38d82b667512ef7e967a875b4b1445294.png" class="latex" alt="$180 - 80\sqrt 5 = (10 - 4\sqrt 5)^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="210" height="21" />.
</p><p>We now know the complete factorization of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c96dd6ec1dc4ad7520fbdc78fcdbec9edd068d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" />: 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/5/e65b2163ba192ddf7afcfe37167b941d980c53a3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[f(x) = (x - 10 - 4\sqrt 5)(x + 10 + 4\sqrt 5)(x - 10 + 4\sqrt 5)(x + 10 - 4\sqrt 5)\]" width="478" height="21" />
</p><p>As the final step, we can now combine the factors in a different way, in order to get rid of the square roots. 
</p><p>We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/1/b91edb413631ca834d32a1bf1cd42c146f324970.png" class="latex" alt="$(x - 10 - 4\sqrt 5)(x - 10 + 4\sqrt 5) = (x-10)^2 - (4\sqrt 5)^2 = x^2 - 20x + 20$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="547" height="21" />,
and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/8/068c5c449db642b7b349ee52e04730eeff1080e1.png" class="latex" alt="$(x + 10 - 4\sqrt 5)(x + 10 + 4\sqrt 5) = x^2 + 20x + 20$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="373" height="21" />.
</p><p>Hence we obtain the factorization <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/8/3a8b463f97b4e8ab329fb73c91b81e709d417bb7.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x) = (x^2 - 20x + 20)(x^2 + 20x + 20)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="304" height="19" />.
</p><p>For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/0/3103474e7ab1ed2f518db200d1a5e78ef656cded.png" class="latex" alt="$x\geq 20$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="52" height="15" /> both terms are positive and larger than one, hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c96dd6ec1dc4ad7520fbdc78fcdbec9edd068d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" /> is not prime. For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/6/d86ec8c43f268ea0b453ecd831390766d05bc70c.png" class="latex" alt="$1&lt;x&lt;19$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="85" height="13" /> the second factor is positive and the first one is negative, hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c96dd6ec1dc4ad7520fbdc78fcdbec9edd068d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" /> is not a prime. The remaining cases are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/5/4c52e02a35db152495396584bfeae1d0599cd753.png" class="latex" alt="$x=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/3/5838ed5b3cc72d400e4e782e6def348bb1b362af.png" class="latex" alt="$x=19$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="52" height="13" />. In both cases, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c96dd6ec1dc4ad7520fbdc78fcdbec9edd068d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" /> is indeed a prime, and their sum is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/4/524883ac642d0b296857f1fd66723ef14f70466d.png" class="latex" alt="$f(1) + f(19) = 41 + 761 = \boxed{802}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="254" height="23" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h3>
<p>Instead of doing the hard work, we can try to guess the factorization. One good approach:
</p><p>We can make the observation that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c96dd6ec1dc4ad7520fbdc78fcdbec9edd068d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" /> looks similar to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/f/38fc830ad3c207223494aee79be8b1a6812b399e.png" class="latex" alt="$(x^2 + 20)^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="78" height="19" /> with the exception of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/b/76b2878564ab19b80abb21ba964abe90fe120846.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2$" width="16" height="15" /> term. In fact, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/3/ad309214ed2ee325104c20633b04978cb7d751b8.png" class="latex" alt="$(x^2 + 20)^2 = x^4 + 40x^2 + 400$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="229" height="19" />. But then we notice that it differs from the desired expression by a square: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/d/d6dec4489f71fac1f610bf00515eeae8836048a6.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x) = (x^2 + 20)^2 - 400x^2 = (x^2 + 20)^2 - (20x)^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="381" height="19" />.
</p><p>Now we can use the formula <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/e/11e905a5f7770ac96154a2a9602d8107a8b0c4aa.png" class="latex" alt="$(x^2 - y^2) = (x-y)(x+y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="206" height="19" /> to obtain the same factorization as in the previous solution, without all the work.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h3>
<p>After arriving at the factorization <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/8/3a8b463f97b4e8ab329fb73c91b81e709d417bb7.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x) = (x^2 - 20x + 20)(x^2 + 20x + 20)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="304" height="19" />, a more mathematical approach would be to realize that the second factor is always positive when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> is a positive integer. Therefore, in order for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c96dd6ec1dc4ad7520fbdc78fcdbec9edd068d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" /> to be prime, the first factor has to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />.
</p><p>We can set it equal to 1 and solve for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />: 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/5/525e975ddc117f1cbe0acf89d3d0f86a886c5cfa.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2-20x+20=1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="141" height="16" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/1/5b104fe90d2945f55d1a6bfe27d4437cdb7b9b9c.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2-20x+19=0$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="142" height="16" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/7/e175a6ff23252cf4da40e9a28e02405542a5d4f8.png" class="latex" alt="$(x-1)(x-19)=0$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="153" height="18" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/c/91c64d4424fb433ece1d9a8f81fecf5fac0104ad.png" class="latex" alt="$x=1, x=19$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="104" height="16" />
</p><p>Substituting these values into the second factor and adding would give the answer.
</p>
