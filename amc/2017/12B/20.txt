<p>Real numbers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> are chosen independently and uniformly at random from the interval <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/1/be11a56a55425d6c6b69f70f5fa98eab95e8ad18.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />. What is the probability that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/e/f0e01e434173f7f57359f245c95d61c233d41aaa.png" class="latex" alt="$\lfloor\log_2x\rfloor=\lfloor\log_2y\rfloor$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="141" height="19" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/c/02c729308fedefc623fb23a802778ba657698340.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{1}{8}\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{1}{6}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{1}{4}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{1}{3}\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="367" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>First let us take the case that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/b/45b89e9b9fa83206d336f200e5a414b629e91866.png" class="latex" alt="$\lfloor \log_2{x} \rfloor = \lfloor \log_2{y} \rfloor = -1$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="190" height="19" />. In this case, both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> lie in the interval <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/7/07752b32c11973528bf05da894bf141919fc49c6.png" class="latex" alt="$[{1\over2}, 1)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="40" height="37" />. The probability of this is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e5992bf70a872b6e72313fc370541ec9733bb784.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="75" height="37" />. Similarly, in the case that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/b/48b20079aadc7cdfb7837b4f918cb1a512f9376f.png" class="latex" alt="$\lfloor \log_2{x} \rfloor = \lfloor \log_2{y} \rfloor = -2$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="191" height="19" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> lie in the interval <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/b/40b1f6b7cfd2170891149701c45846a910cac70c.png" class="latex" alt="$[{1\over4}, {1\over2})$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="45" height="37" />, and the probability is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/b/bcb90c5b7b0a826471080ca25450574b4aec190a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{16}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="84" height="37" />. Recall that the probability that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> is the case, where case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> are mutually exclusive, is the sum of each individual probability. Symbolically that's <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/6/3763e4901c15aa22681514c165e2ce0eceeb3b68.png" class="latex" alt="$P(A \text{ or } B \text{ or } C...) = P(A) + P(B) + P(C)...$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="350" height="20" />. Thus, the probability we are looking for is the sum of the probability for each of the cases <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/6/f46754590b16cc74e47f4d93bea5ec9df8b67c64.png" class="latex" alt="$\lfloor \log_2{x} \rfloor = \lfloor \log_2{y} \rfloor = -1, -2, -3...$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="272" height="20" />. It is easy to see that the probabilities for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/6/37612d8071a351e9a78233c4327261ad2596511f.png" class="latex" alt="$\lfloor \log_2{x} \rfloor = \lfloor \log_2{y} \rfloor = n$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="179" height="19" /> for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/8/f989807735aed2fd04b7436cfe7ec5d10c03d8e9.png" class="latex" alt="$-\infty &lt; n &lt; 0$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="100" height="13" /> are the infinite geometric series that starts at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c339afa03e060d959e4942be7522c0c992f4c41c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="11" height="37" /> and with common ratio <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c339afa03e060d959e4942be7522c0c992f4c41c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="11" height="37" />. Using the formula for the sum of an infinite geometric series, we get that the probability is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/2/4d27d3800da3ba2e295f1b0de7c21681168d7a37.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\frac{1}{4}}{1 - \frac{1}{4}} = \boxed{\textbf{(D)}\frac{1}{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="124" height="49" />.
</p><p>Solution by: vedadehhc
\\ Edited by: jingwei325
</p>
