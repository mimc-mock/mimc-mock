<p>A circle has center <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/9/dd9a3fa3a414e936336f52d443eaf7a88700e3e2.png" class="latex" alt="$(-10, -4)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="76" height="18" /> and has radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/0/030e19e4e2f1c074127cb50581e8496470c5419f.png" class="latex" alt="$13$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. Another circle has center <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/d/48d906dea9f7181d8d225705494ff4fda235ef4b.png" class="latex" alt="$(3, 9)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> and radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/f/53fb48234e755a407618d6906fdfe1040800225a.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{65}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />. The line passing through the two points of intersection of the two circles has equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/e/44ea3216d3cb6a8de6ab1597b92e573f02bd1acc.png" class="latex" alt="$x+y=c$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="74" height="14" />. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/5/3759a30b166ce3611cfc045159e579d71a25be31.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 3\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 3\sqrt{3}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 4\sqrt{2}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 6\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \frac{13}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="409" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The equations of the two circles are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/f/03fbf29b76951436aab3ec381878ed88a3277700.png" class="latex" alt="$(x+10)^2+(y+4)^2=169$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="208" height="19" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/c/36c26242f5fd48ac76bc73ed0ff5b6a7e6f720da.png" class="latex" alt="$(x-3)^2+(y-9)^2=65$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="189" height="19" />. Rearrange them to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/e/a2ec7cc81156480b4063c1f70f0b292a3b4a31fe.png" class="latex" alt="$(x+10)^2+(y+4)^2-169=0$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="239" height="19" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/3/903fd3449b3adc7119186d8f86fff98a8f147ff3.png" class="latex" alt="$(x-3)^2+(y-9)^2-65=0$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="221" height="19" />, respectively. Their intersection points are where these two equations gain equality. The two points lie on the line with the equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/8/0d840c7b14905a5c4803c76fe0713fcb831d9c8f.png" class="latex" alt="$(x+10)^2+(y+4)^2-169=(x-3)^2+(y-9)^2-65$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="418" height="19" />. We can simplify this like the following. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/8/958ac2028bdaa9058b93126772c303678f561567.png" class="latex" alt="$(x+10)^2+(y+4)^2-169=(x-3)^2+(y-9)^2-65 \rightarrow (x^2+20x+100)+(y^2+8y+16)-(x^2-6x+9)-(y^2-18y+81)=104 \rightarrow 26x+26y+26=104 \rightarrow 26x+26y=78 \rightarrow x+y=3$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="1049" height="38" />. Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/6/116926a9361f70e7f543c14e1f23e4be91ef50a8.png" class="latex" alt="$c = \boxed{\textbf{(A)}\ 3}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="87" height="28" />.
</p><p>Solution by TheUltimate123
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2:_Shortcut_with_right_triangles">Solution 2: Shortcut with right triangles</span></h2>
<p>Note the specificity of the radii, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/0/030e19e4e2f1c074127cb50581e8496470c5419f.png" class="latex" alt="$13$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/f/53fb48234e755a407618d6906fdfe1040800225a.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{65}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />, and that specificity is often deliberately added to simplify the solution to a problem.
</p><p>One may recognize <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/0/030e19e4e2f1c074127cb50581e8496470c5419f.png" class="latex" alt="$13$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> as the hypotenuse of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/1/481e0f2720d510ab3f1ea8f9042837e257ae06cf.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{5-12-13}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="53" height="12" /> right triangle and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/f/53fb48234e755a407618d6906fdfe1040800225a.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{65}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" /> as the hypotenuse of the right triangle with legs <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" />. We can suppose that the legs of these triangles connect the circles' centers to their intersection along the gridlines of the plane.
</p><p>If we suspect that one of the intersections lies <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> units to the right of and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> units above the center of the first circle, we find the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/d/30dd9899db04c5f89432ab50fc919187420f0e11.png" class="latex" alt="$(-10 + 12,-4 + 5) = (2,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="212" height="18" />, which is in fact <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> unit to the left of and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> units below the center of the second circle at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/d/4fd0851e6c382a436dde70b469bb214ec9cc2137.png" class="latex" alt="$(3,9)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />.
</p><p>Plugging <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/6/aa6bb1c456d71ee24e7a0ee603d226a7992e5da3.png" class="latex" alt="$(2,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/5/a859acebdd07c34704648be45c0daf108e2a29d9.png" class="latex" alt="$x + y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="41" height="14" /> gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/0/45055d0f57c70f8ffed12e42c57f5fb546ba9bd4.png" class="latex" alt="$c = 2 + 1 = \boxed{\textbf{(A) } 3}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="153" height="28" />.
</p><p>A similar solution uses the other intersection point, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/a/b/2ab2fdfad0ad8c934758d113f39d2509e197b890.png" class="latex" alt="$(-5,8)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="53" height="18" />.
</p><p><br />
</p>
