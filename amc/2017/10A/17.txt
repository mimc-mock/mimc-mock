<p>Distinct points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> lie on the circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/c/c6c3975ec073ce47b2a783d376d4df4e2a0e4e72.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{2}+y^{2}=25$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="99" height="18" /> and have integer coordinates. The distances <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/3/7631e00d1deb6ad3d55301906009f15d342e412a.png" class="latex" alt="$RS$" width="26" height="12" />  are irrational numbers. What is the greatest possible value of the ratio <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/e/bce0e0f972aab68482774eb61d74fd64ca4e597a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{PQ}{RS}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="31" height="37" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/e/cfe908ebc16ea2a6e43df2f9ee0a33cdff6eebb7.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{\textbf{(A)}}\ 3\qquad\mathrm{\textbf{(B)}}\ 5\qquad\mathrm{\textbf{(C)}}\ 3\sqrt{5}\qquad\mathrm{\textbf{(D)}}\ 7\qquad\mathrm{\textbf{(E)}}\ 5\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="398" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> are lattice points, there are only a few coordinates that actually satisfy the equation. The coordinates are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840b9820619ff71910647d8920bdfa6efb652076.png" class="latex" alt="$(\pm 3,\pm 4), (\pm 4, \pm 3), (0,\pm 5),$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="212" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/2/e127dd54d7f9c88cd260573ba8249020f1f74e69.png" class="latex" alt="$(\pm 5,0).$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="58" height="18" /> We want to maximize <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" /> and minimize <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/f/a2f7efa4f1af8fa9b10b6c55ad680acd46bb2882.png" class="latex" alt="$RS.$" width="28" height="12" /> They also have to be non perfect squares, because they are both irrational. The greatest value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" /> happens when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> are almost directly across from each other and are in different quadrants. For example, the endpoints of the segment could be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/a/8baa0e2e8ffc7fa8d1879933bab7c27efc499060.png" class="latex" alt="$(-4,3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="53" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/8/d9833ea8293a1f5d61101830c6d64fb017224677.png" class="latex" alt="$(3,-4)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="52" height="18" /> because the two points are almost across from each other. Another possible pair could be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/a/8baa0e2e8ffc7fa8d1879933bab7c27efc499060.png" class="latex" alt="$(-4,3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="53" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/e/38ec6a35045b7cc0f04c4989a99570bcc29aee43.png" class="latex" alt="$(5,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />. To find out which segment is longer, we have to compare the distances from their endpoints to a diameter (which must be the longest possible segment). The closest diameter would be from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/a/8baa0e2e8ffc7fa8d1879933bab7c27efc499060.png" class="latex" alt="$(-4,3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="53" height="18" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/4/624f5a84a62d9c25cbb698e7a10fc99ba56508c6.png" class="latex" alt="$(4,-3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="52" height="18" />. The distance between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/8/d9833ea8293a1f5d61101830c6d64fb017224677.png" class="latex" alt="$(3,-4)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="52" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/a/8baa0e2e8ffc7fa8d1879933bab7c27efc499060.png" class="latex" alt="$(-4,3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="53" height="18" /> is greater than the distance between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/e/38ec6a35045b7cc0f04c4989a99570bcc29aee43.png" class="latex" alt="$(5,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/4/624f5a84a62d9c25cbb698e7a10fc99ba56508c6.png" class="latex" alt="$(4,-3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="52" height="18" />. Therefore, the segment from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/8/d9833ea8293a1f5d61101830c6d64fb017224677.png" class="latex" alt="$(3,-4)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="52" height="18" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/a/8baa0e2e8ffc7fa8d1879933bab7c27efc499060.png" class="latex" alt="$(-4,3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="53" height="18" /> is the longest attainable (the other possible coordinates for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/3/563948106c3332662355412547d99acab218c16f.png" class="latex" alt="$(4,3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/3/0f3c6178358be7c36c9ac2b95bbec0d05dafaa70.png" class="latex" alt="$(-3, -4)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/c/dcc1c720139325a2c3491ba41476d225311fcd1d.png" class="latex" alt="$(3, 4)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/8/a2813840663adc90f22b2db5d81f181af472f8fb.png" class="latex" alt="$(-4, -3)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="71" height="20" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/d/fad54ab653d3aa7be3416107a9e3ea47007d12f3.png" class="latex" alt="$(-3, 4)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="53" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/6/3467d14f7190c5178e35e312f9ead7be176cd250.png" class="latex" alt="$(4, -3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="52" height="18" />. The least value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/3/7631e00d1deb6ad3d55301906009f15d342e412a.png" class="latex" alt="$RS$" width="26" height="12" /> is when the two endpoints are in the same quadrant and are very close to each other. This can occur when, for example, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/f/97fadfe6bdbd1adb164f263b52f2a290723ad45c.png" class="latex" alt="$(3,4)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/2/542f4e987ffd0ff038e1dbb327a78e0444d906fe.png" class="latex" alt="$(4,3).$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="43" height="18" /> They are in the same quadrant and no other point on the circle with integer coordinates is closer to the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/f/97fadfe6bdbd1adb164f263b52f2a290723ad45c.png" class="latex" alt="$(3,4)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/3/563948106c3332662355412547d99acab218c16f.png" class="latex" alt="$(4,3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> and vice versa. Using the distance formula, we get that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/9/8697381e78460f079869f6744a1e21922ec3061a.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{98}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" /> and that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/3/7631e00d1deb6ad3d55301906009f15d342e412a.png" class="latex" alt="$RS$" width="26" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/4/eb485bd3ce593662cabace2375882fc28b737206.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{2}.$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="27" height="18" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/4/684afd1aa7f3807c60ca869c5bac0b5a00ffeec6.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{98}}{\sqrt{2}}=\sqrt{49}=\boxed{\mathrm{\textbf{(D)}}\ 7}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="175" height="45" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We can look at the option choices. Since we are aiming for the highest possible ratio, let's try using <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" />( though actually 5 <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/4/2c4dc1409208ff6a3039f4871d85c16763334983.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" /> is actually the highest ratio). Now, looking at the problem alone, we know that to have the largest ratio possible, we have to let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/3/7631e00d1deb6ad3d55301906009f15d342e412a.png" class="latex" alt="$RS$" width="26" height="12" /> be the minimum possible value while at the same time using integer coordinates. Thus, the smallest possible value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/3/7631e00d1deb6ad3d55301906009f15d342e412a.png" class="latex" alt="$RS$" width="26" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/d/38d98843e5f852e1d6888e80897e3203972d1452.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{1^{2}+1^{2}} = \sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="119" height="19" />. Assuming that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/7/be715064bfd7ad6bcbfb3203a05685d08b65de22.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{PQ}{RS} = 7$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="66" height="37" />, we plug in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/4/b64c6676c49a36039c5ed8ea1b981ee24a25852b.png" class="latex" alt="$RS = \sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="75" height="18" /> and solve for PQ: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/6/9168f373f95ee7ea576f6eaed047587d1964b224.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ=7\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="86" height="20" />. Remember, we don't know if this is possible yet, we are only trying to figure out if it is. But for what values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> does <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/1/211b9a4e2ebba756d26ee6a0512c2b2eb857af12.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}=7\sqrt2$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="133" height="22" />? We see that this can easily be made into a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/2/22231a5cdd4fe05a11f4b577e3b81221f3017a85.png" class="latex" alt="$45-45-90$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="98" height="13" /> triangle. But, instead of substituting <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/1/741a74d86df899c10724dc2f80638f5e7f599194.png" class="latex" alt="$y=x$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="11" /> into the equation and then using a whole lot of algebra, we can save time and use the little trick, that if in a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/2/22231a5cdd4fe05a11f4b577e3b81221f3017a85.png" class="latex" alt="$45-45-90$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="98" height="13" /> triangle, the two <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/7/f27da4fca6f3fecb215974023aad210b70ec3857.png" class="latex" alt="$45$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> degree sides have side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" />, then the hypotenuse is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/d/89d9e7f681b1b83dc4b8fbed7682abfbaf0d4f40.png" class="latex" alt="$s\sqrt2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />. Using this, we can see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/0/4c041840be872323a1bcee0674150c3a2a33246f.png" class="latex" alt="$s=7$" width="42" height="12" />, and since our equation does in fact yield a sensible solution, we can be assured that our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/6/8768e711dbe2db58dc614123b3fc0e813cbc2c80.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\mathrm{\textbf{(D)}}\ 7}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="55" height="28" />.
</p><p>Quality Control by fasterthanlight
</p><p>(Note by Carrot_Karen: We tried 7, but some might be confused why we concluded that it was the answer after verifying without trying the others, like why wasn't option <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/e/75e41e3cd48cee2c6866cfbb4386a8e99d3482f5.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(E)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="17" /> tried? This is because the problem can only have one correct answer, so if we have an option that already works, we can conclude that none of the others work and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/0/b305929a95b53f14a0ad53eba1d8560626e1f28b.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(D)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="28" height="17" /> is the answer.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>By inspection, R is (3, 4) and S is (4, 3), making RS as small as possible, and the greatest possible value of PQ arises when P is at (-3, 4) and Q is at (4, -3) giving a value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/f/80f89eb48e3b562eda706c7a522eff5c99426e3b.png" class="latex" alt="$7\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="35" height="21" /> for PQ. The requested fraction is then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/6/8768e711dbe2db58dc614123b3fc0e813cbc2c80.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\mathrm{\textbf{(D)}}\ 7}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="55" height="28" />.
</p>
