<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>There are 81 multiples of 11 between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/0/0705411d92671faf1fe5602cfef8f353c7f0f83d.png" class="latex" alt="$999$" width="26" height="12" /> inclusive. Some have digits repeated twice, making 3 permutations.
</p><p>Others that have no repeated digits have 6 permutations, but switching the hundreds and units digits also yield a multiple of 11. Switching shows we have overcounted by a factor of 2, so assign  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/3/703f2ef5a9d708c7fc8a407124312f5d68afdb12.png" class="latex" alt="$6 \div 2 = 3$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="73" height="13" /> permutations to each multiple.
</p><p>There are now 81*3 = 243 permutations, but we have overcounted*. Some multiples of 11 have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> as a digit. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> cannot be the digit of the hundreds place, we must subtract a permutation for each. 
</p><p>There are 110, 220, 330 ... 990, yielding 9 extra permutations
</p><p>Also, there are 209, 308, 407...902, yielding 8 more permutations.
</p><p>Now, just subtract these 17 from the total (243) to get 226. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/e/68e3817859ed7b4de4aaf4f79c8aba608e9f1f78.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(A) } 226}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="74" height="28" />
</p>
<ul><li>If short on time, observe that 226 is the only answer choice less than 243, and therefore is the only feasible answer.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>We can first overcount and then subtract.
We know that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/e/87edbdda753b32453027dc7cab023c0743b6b243.png" class="latex" alt="$81$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> multiples of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/8/c6878713578626763c38433b3f4c8c2205ad0c15.png" class="latex" alt="$11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" />.
</p><p>We can then multiply by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> for each permutation of these multiples. (Yet some multiples do not have six distinct permutations.)
