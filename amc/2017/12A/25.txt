<p>The vertices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/d/12d58aa29201da09d8e620f8698e3a37547f6b4a.png" class="latex" alt="$V$" width="14" height="12" /> of a centrally symmetric hexagon in the complex plane are given by
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/5/245a3e5ef97921f9aacfa8b49f29b5ba1a176939.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[V=\left\{   \sqrt{2}i,-\sqrt{2}i, \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}(1+i),\frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}(-1+i),\frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}(1-i),\frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}(-1-i) \right\}.\]" width="534" height="43" />
For each <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/2/8122aa89ea6e80784c6513d22787ad86e36ad0cc.png" class="latex" alt="$j$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="8" height="16" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/b/b2b075cbc15f7e9f8409eb095331b66b47c2c30d.png" class="latex" alt="$1\leq j\leq 12$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="83" height="16" />, an element <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/2/762a43137e1d61e8a958446459e86ac7ed0022d8.png" class="latex" alt="$z_j$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="14" height="12" /> is chosen from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/d/12d58aa29201da09d8e620f8698e3a37547f6b4a.png" class="latex" alt="$V$" width="14" height="12" /> at random, independently of the other choices. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/e/5fe777878a970043907cb696473f9d721e3103ad.png" class="latex" alt="$P={\prod}_{j=1}^{12}z_j$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="99" height="34" /> be the product of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> numbers selected. What is the probability that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/9/089ba6a71635f96bdde9a1e35cbffd9e405e77d5.png" class="latex" alt="$P=-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="60" height="13" />?

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>It is possible to solve this problem using elementary counting methods. This solution proceeds by a cleaner generating function.
</p><p>We note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/3/9534a248a304a7e5124757ad0431e8f0942fc83d.png" class="latex" alt="$\pm \sqrt{2}i$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="44" height="18" /> both lie on the imaginary axis and each of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/d/17d5f08ccb096de94129c72ed6329df13d1828de.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}(\pm 1\pm i)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="93" height="41" /> have length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> and angle of odd multiples of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/2/2023fd6335b423b0090fdb6999a68778fcaa3ee5.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi/4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="29" height="18" />, i.e. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57edb00bf7f4fd9aa0b7343f73aa5c6d062c1c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi/4,3\pi/4,5\pi/4,7\pi/4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="168" height="18" />. When we draw these 6 complex numbers out on the complex plane, we get a crystal-looking thing. Note that the total number of ways to choose 12 complex numbers is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/4/e945e3f5452694a771938590ca9bfb4dc881ded3.png" class="latex" alt="$6^{12}$" width="21" height="15" />. Now we count the number of good combinations.
</p><p>We first consider the lengths. When we multiply 12 complex numbers together, their magnitudes multiply. Suppose we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> of the numbers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/3/9534a248a304a7e5124757ad0431e8f0942fc83d.png" class="latex" alt="$\pm \sqrt{2}i$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="44" height="18" />; then we must have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/5/c652f57ac0f1684e0491a837cdb3515746f7568e.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\sqrt{2}\right)^n\cdot\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{12-n}=1 \Longrightarrow n=8$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="256" height="46" />. Having <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/4/1d4f171162b090bab7ce5dfa1c58715850530ba5.png" class="latex" alt="$n=8$" width="43" height="12" /> will take care of the length of the product; now we need to deal with the angle.
</p><p>We require <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/9/3293fce3a415558df6f324625fa924be0749cccb.png" class="latex" alt="$\sum\theta\equiv\pi \mod 2\pi$" style="vertical-align: -8px" width="145" height="25" />. Letting <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/9/a191179cb0b5ce4263de662ac6220a470ec67538.png" class="latex" alt="$e^{i\pi/4}$" width="35" height="16" />, we see that the angles we have available are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/b/69b28bd70004f294d5551ee6cbff1141f8d773cc.png" class="latex" alt="$\{z^1,z^2,z^3,z^5,z^6,z^7\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="157" height="19" />, where we must choose exactly 8 angles from the set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/8/6d8336f8a84ad4a9dfedc18894e51269c2e9fc90.png" class="latex" alt="$\{z^2,z^6\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="58" height="19" /> and exactly 4 from the set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/7/bc7220285d187967a4988173b3852f1d890de68d.png" class="latex" alt="$\{z^1,z^3,z^5,z^7\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="108" height="19" />. If we found a good combination where we had <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/2/412787c048e28774dc63fc27db42dc52ca858de7.png" class="latex" alt="$a_i$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="14" height="10" /> of each angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/c/a7c4e41f35e4d07774dcb0ba0b74e80193a8bd94.png" class="latex" alt="$z^i$" width="14" height="15" />, then the amount this would contribute to our count would be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/1/8c10f0af2a2e3929b7cc00515c9f0cb0166b221f.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{12}{4,8}\cdot\binom{8}{a_2,a_6}\cdot\binom{4}{a_1,a_3,a_5,a_7}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="267" height="53" />. We want to add these all up. We proceed by generating functions.
</p><p>Consider <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/6/336ec65b772ba632cfef90d2557c14a59833fb98.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(t_2x^2+t_6x^6)^8(t_1x^1+t_3x^3+t_5x^5+t_7x^7)^4.\]" width="325" height="19" /> The expansion will be of the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/3/ef35e84ea35f65558c9d7ec13131265af70a0816.png" class="latex" alt="$\sum_i\left(\sum_{\sum a=i} \binom{8}{a_2,a_6}\binom{4}{a_1,a_3,a_5,a_7}{t_1}^{a_1}{t_2}^{a_2}{t_3}^{a_3}{t_5}^{a_5}{t_6}^{a_6}{t_7}^{a_7}x^i \right)$" style="vertical-align: -27px" width="474" height="64" />. Note that if we reduced the powers of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> mod <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> and fished out the coefficient of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/0/2f0078b54474db2dbf6219e8386b9b62c4405c94.png" class="latex" alt="$x^4$" width="17" height="15" /> and plugged in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/9/d294f2d67464d26d17a8466216aa2ddb0c34c7e4.png" class="latex" alt="$t_i=1\ \forall\,i$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="69" height="15" /> (and then multiplied by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/5/6b527c4e8c45afb0cde3f67619f62123e942cc38.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{12}{4,8}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="50" height="53" />) then we would be done. Since plugging in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/2/6229bfc3813ec3a9551228ba9dfa9376fa6d38db.png" class="latex" alt="$t_i=1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="44" height="14" /> doesn't affect the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />'s, we do that right away. The expression then becomes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/3/a63f393e310a1c7d181eb02c172ec0465c28d18c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x^{20}(1+x^4)^8(1+x^2+x^4+x^6)^4=x^{20}(1+x^4)^{12}(1+x^2)^4=x^4(1+x^4)^{12}(1+x^2)^4,\]" width="572" height="19" /> where the last equality is true because we are taking the powers of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> mod <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/b/72badcf6375433c3e4d32281ba82dd91cf995196.png" class="latex" alt="$[x^n]f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="63" height="18" /> denote the coefficient of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/7/8c72f3acf1f7744cfe76edaf47e45d62432cf28b.png" class="latex" alt="$x^n$" width="18" height="12" /> in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c96dd6ec1dc4ad7520fbdc78fcdbec9edd068d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" />. Note <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/8/cd832a4ec4e3ba9054bd10499b42aa5867e05c5f.png" class="latex" alt="$[x^4] x^4(1+x^4)^{12}(1+x^2)^4=[x^0](1+x^4)^{12}(1+x^2)^4$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="393" height="20" />. We use the roots of unity filter, which states <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/a/30aac70e67ce9932ccd1be4244f231ee4ac39fec.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\text{terms of }f(x)\text{ that have exponent congruent to }k\text{ mod }n=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{m=1}^n \frac{f(z^mx)}{z^{mk}},\]" width="552" height="47" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/d/b8d1c9cf2f1101307ac42c83ce48d75a9195723e.png" class="latex" alt="$z=e^{i\pi/n}$" width="70" height="16" />. In our case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/d/badf0ecd1ef8289387d8462393438c6e2710ff28.png" class="latex" alt="$k=0$" width="43" height="12" />, so we only need to find the average of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/f/95f1a793972a0220c27965ffb9a75d951f6a4189.png" class="latex" alt="$f(z^mx)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="56" height="18" />'s.
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/5/1c58b0915f5adca8be90c04afa463d5178cc3394.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} z^0 &amp;\Longrightarrow (1+x^4)^{12}(1+x^2)^4,\\ z^1 &amp;\Longrightarrow (1-x^4)^{12}(1+ix^2)^4,\\ z^2 &amp;\Longrightarrow (1+x^4)^{12}(1-x^2)^4,\\ z^3 &amp;\Longrightarrow (1-x^4)^{12}(1-ix^2)^4,\\ z^4 &amp;\Longrightarrow (1+x^4)^{12}(1+x^2)^4,\\ z^5 &amp;\Longrightarrow (1-x^4)^{12}(1+ix^2)^4,\\ z^6 &amp;\Longrightarrow (1+x^4)^{12}(1-x^2)^4,\\ z^7 &amp;\Longrightarrow (1-x^4)^{12}(1-ix^2)^4. \end{align*}" width="214" height="213" />
We plug in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/5/4c52e02a35db152495396584bfeae1d0599cd753.png" class="latex" alt="$x=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="12" /> and take the average to find the sum of all coefficients of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/6/1a611340236790959c02dce68cf43c2b41b24502.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{\text{multiple of 8}}$" width="83" height="15" />. Plugging in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/5/4c52e02a35db152495396584bfeae1d0599cd753.png" class="latex" alt="$x=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="12" /> makes all of the above zero except for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/5/435a6bd3b5d041cac588c584b4ab6fc32c9905d3.png" class="latex" alt="$z^0$" width="15" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/b/77bd83e3db31d9e4cb73848472df29edaec2582c.png" class="latex" alt="$z^4$" width="16" height="15" />. Averaging, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/8/1f89817feaf52cc84e051c2ccce9df80257e4e72.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{14}$" width="22" height="15" />. Now the answer is simply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/7/4279710ac71dd63ad6845a305d507295a774787c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{\binom{12}{4,8}}{6^{12}}\cdot 2^{14}=\boxed{\textbf{(E)} ~\frac{2^2\cdot 5\cdot 11}{3^{10}}}.\]" width="217" height="50" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>By changing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/a/9ea31e5c5613f025b5be8aca425d318168491d89.png" class="latex" alt="$z_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="14" height="10" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/6/1a6b3c91ab6410d432f2307dda780f7680c4b9c4.png" class="latex" alt="$-z_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="28" height="10" />, we can give a bijection between cases where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/9/089ba6a71635f96bdde9a1e35cbffd9e405e77d5.png" class="latex" alt="$P=-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="60" height="13" /> and cases where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/9/6d99d5ffc20f99bef98a1861496b6758fa9541d2.png" class="latex" alt="$P=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="46" height="13" />, so we'll just find the probability that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/8/e484a7fe4842455d44f77772ba94ca0571363a40.png" class="latex" alt="$P=\pm 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="60" height="13" /> and divide by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> in the end. Multiplying the hexagon's vertices by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/8/34857b3ba74ce5cd8607f3ebd23e9015908ada71.png" class="latex" alt="$i$" width="5" height="12" /> doesn't change <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />, and switching any <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/2/762a43137e1d61e8a958446459e86ac7ed0022d8.png" class="latex" alt="$z_j$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="14" height="12" /> with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/3/7c3579f73434c6af9b18d4645717ed1fc1bc5f0d.png" class="latex" alt="$-z_j$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="28" height="12" /> doesn't change the property <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/8/e484a7fe4842455d44f77772ba94ca0571363a40.png" class="latex" alt="$P=\pm 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="60" height="13" />, so the probability that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/d/fcd0ca267a847c32561d053606652edf2c1b3288.png" class="latex" alt="$P=\pm1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="60" height="13" /> remains the same if we only select our <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/2/762a43137e1d61e8a958446459e86ac7ed0022d8.png" class="latex" alt="$z_j$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="14" height="12" />'s at random from
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/f/c4f1b8efef54bc5c1ed62f20f2b88bed43f5a667.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\left\{a= \sqrt 2,\quad b=\frac1{\sqrt{8}}(1+i),\quad c=\frac1{\sqrt{8}}(1-i)\right\}.\]" width="362" height="43" />

To ensure <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> is real, we must either choose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> times, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> times, or both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> times.
