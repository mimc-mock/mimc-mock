<p>Quadrilateral <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> is inscribed in circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" /> and has side lengths <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/8/2f8056479593935efd2a6478b077a748ce8ad40f.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=3, BC=2, CD=6$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="203" height="16" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/3/d73e9c9486954873b6a269cd3c4aaa53c97f4f91.png" class="latex" alt="$DA=8$" width="62" height="13" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/5/ce58e4af225c93d08606c26554caaa5ae32edeba.png" class="latex" alt="$Y$" width="14" height="12" /> be points on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/7/4373e7b4be983c0315e82e1fda1df66ed34dd7fb.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BD}$" width="30" height="15" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/b/dcb342c5e58a66e0e506e16d591c961a76944370.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{DX}{BD} = \frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="72" height="38" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/4/72480147975c8e4f661735140db11d8b6e6f485b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{BY}{BD} = \frac{11}{36}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="79" height="37" />.
Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> be the intersection of line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/8/2d857b2c804701b561a5c8c90c400f360d27232c.png" class="latex" alt="$AX$" width="29" height="13" /> and the line through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/5/ce58e4af225c93d08606c26554caaa5ae32edeba.png" class="latex" alt="$Y$" width="14" height="12" /> parallel to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/b/cfbb4c52e011ceed6e813118a7d59e931af444a2.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AD}$" width="29" height="15" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> be the intersection of line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/2/73205379672e860eb250c541187600fae3a3e225.png" class="latex" alt="$CX$" width="30" height="12" /> and the line through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> parallel to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/5/12517bbc31cb9026c71299b7a0d232d07e60f284.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AC}$" width="28" height="15" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e28ce12d49d39f160d5a0ef54077fc98e4b9d2b.png" class="latex" alt="$G$" width="14" height="12" /> be the point on circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" /> other than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> that lies on line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/2/73205379672e860eb250c541187600fae3a3e225.png" class="latex" alt="$CX$" width="30" height="12" />. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/e/85e579e484ef043e8e5adb6cfff36b0e42487e19.png" class="latex" alt="$XF\cdot XG$" width="74" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/6/de6983ea990c8e8151fdc0e0e9417fe992890d3a.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }17\qquad\textbf{(B) }\frac{59 - 5\sqrt{2}}{3}\qquad\textbf{(C) }\frac{91 - 12\sqrt{3}}{4}\qquad\textbf{(D) }\frac{67 - 10\sqrt{2}}{3}\qquad\textbf{(E) }18$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="595" height="41" />

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Using the given ratios, note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/0/410f417f9d9688008d872841cfb3469ad9b7885d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{XY}{BD} = 1 - \frac{1}{4} - \frac{11}{36} = \frac{4}{9}.$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="189" height="38" />
</p><p>By AA Similarity, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/9/8790933e50c3df2d83cd1289861b00fb078d92ae.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AXD \sim \triangle EXY$" width="147" height="13" /> with a ratio of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/a/c9aa2b3fb5f152edf3e4f39f827911e1c763158c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{DX}{XY} = \frac{9}{16}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="81" height="38" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/6/fe62973349e7d700817905b0aa3d4ff68d12c848.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ACX \sim \triangle EFX$" width="145" height="13" /> with a ratio of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/5/b95d9fb9b0fff1675642ce08b32283d58a838318.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AX}{XE} = \frac{DX}{XY} = \frac{9}{16}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="140" height="38" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/d/c9df2c8009faca3288d3ffc5cafda386d92267e6.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{XF}{CX} = \frac{16}{9}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="79" height="37" />.
</p><p>Now we find the length of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/f/89fff82bb65d0215e49c8c91cb7c553da52205e2.png" class="latex" alt="$BD$" width="29" height="12" />. Because the quadrilateral is cyclic, we can simply use the Law of Cosines. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/5/29542c351a72781439e8e161820a35b031f8615b.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[BD^2=3^2+8^2-48\cos\angle BAD=2^2+6^2-24\cos (180-\angle BAD)=2^2+6^2+24\cos\angle BAD\]" width="661" height="19" /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/4/b34cf2d3001695d3062ac78f2394da65b086811f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\rightarrow \cos\angle BAD = \frac{11}{24}\]" width="151" height="37" /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/8/f4808bd72cb90bda57cb7d70cfb0314568b0874a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\rightarrow BD=\sqrt{51}\]" width="110" height="18" />
By Power of a Point, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/8/558d647c33323a5ed43a0538142c432b7351aefc.png" class="latex" alt="$CX\cdot XG = DX\cdot XB = \frac{\sqrt{51}}{4} \frac{3\sqrt{51}}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="281" height="41" />. Thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/4/224767b8c0318c94d2b9f80b9176ecfdc5a5b9d9.png" class="latex" alt="$XF\cdot XG = \frac{XF}{CX} CX\cdot XG = \frac{51}{3} = \boxed{\textbf{(A)}\ 17}.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="347" height="37" />
</p><p>-solution by FRaelya
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We shall make use of the pairs of similar triangles present in the problem, Ptolemy's Theorem, and Power of a Point. 
Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/a/3ead47fb9fb4a4c273feee398f72ff2a09702b84.png" class="latex" alt="$Z$" width="13" height="12" /> be the intersection of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/7/a179ff2638e4799cadd820db205c2beff6299ce9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC$" width="27" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/f/89fff82bb65d0215e49c8c91cb7c553da52205e2.png" class="latex" alt="$BD$" width="29" height="12" />. First, from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> being a cyclic quadrilateral, we have that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/8/e085ba8a93ed5f25197654f5b18f1cfc700790a1.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BCZ \sim \triangle AZD$" width="141" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/0/f606ba420c0b87da8f9549c292a69641046d3fc4.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BZA \sim \triangle CDZ$" width="142" height="13" />. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/6/2d6f5a22a58b6a68df67628ba91a9bc3b67dbf80.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{BZ} = \frac{8}{AZ}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="86" height="37" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/5/705eb231622ec6d9c8d40ee8f7fa1a2ea2482f36.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{6}{CZ} = \frac{3}{BZ}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="86" height="37" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/1/fe16be2ca71fcd688d532a0b388a45e88bc18a2d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{CZ} = \frac{8}{DZ}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="87" height="37" />, so we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/5/f85ffda427fb55c41b535c67ad8b429fad371a1c.png" class="latex" alt="$BZ = \frac{1}{2}CZ$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="93" height="37" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/8/4887cc2c1a7989f7fc6b3fa8654baa4c898988c5.png" class="latex" alt="$AZ = 2CZ$" width="88" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/b/21bb4370f96512ab9aed940cf56eb394dda146a4.png" class="latex" alt="$DZ = 4CZ$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="90" height="13" />. By Ptolemy's Theorem, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/c/3ac19df806076cff70169140e4f7a4ae29fbd762.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(AB)(CD) + (BC)(DA) = (AC)(BD) = (AZ + ZC)(BZ + ZD)\]" width="488" height="18" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/a/f6ac3080949ef44eba9b1c1272c073a84886ba6c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\rightarrow 3 \cdot 6 + 2 \cdot 8 = 34 = \left(2CZ + ZC\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}CZ + 4CZ\right) = \frac{27}{2}CZ^2.\]" width="477" height="43" /> Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/5/6a502dc2423db389d29891c9cb03ee7766a1144c.png" class="latex" alt="$CZ^2 = \frac{68}{27}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="80" height="37" />. Then, by Power of a Point, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/b/deb0f522c992181e902584fc36ddfe4041b80294.png" class="latex" alt="$GX \cdot XC = BX \cdot XD = \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{4}BD^2 = \frac{3}{16} \cdot \left(\frac{9}{2}CZ\right)^2 = \frac{9 \cdot 17}{16}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="477" height="46" />. So, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/1/b718e0b1264057ba08e209f63b1e7edf95aac45a.png" class="latex" alt="$XG = \frac{9 \cdot 17}{16XC}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="106" height="38" />.
Next, observe that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/6/fe62973349e7d700817905b0aa3d4ff68d12c848.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ACX \sim \triangle EFX$" width="145" height="13" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/7/a573185a6614f1e962c860fb7dc023967a526a23.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{XE}{XF} = \frac{AX}{XC}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="92" height="38" />. Also, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/8/d38a23a7e24ce36c468da2d44d2ca3d526524645.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{AXD} \sim \triangle{EXY}$" width="147" height="13" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/1/2f18eb609724e0a8da516548394719e48c948238.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{8}{AX} = \frac{EY}{XE}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="91" height="37" />. We can compute <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/a/fdabdb3e888bb42480ff436d2e6f86bfc5d683d1.png" class="latex" alt="$EY = \frac{128}{9}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="83" height="37" /> after noticing that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/e/15e712bd449396ea63dc918c5b8ab3e825c46a13.png" class="latex" alt="$XY = BD - BY - DX = BD - \frac{11}{36}BD - \frac{1}{4}BD = \frac{4}{9}BD$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="453" height="37" /> and that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/c/ddc18816353eef90bb21de6a73901964558a0d58.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{8}{DX} = \frac{32}{BD} = \frac{EY}{XY} = \frac{EY}{\frac{4}{9}BD}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="221" height="44" />. So, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/d/99d988cb104b3f4651f82fdae07622c719601e98.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{8}{AX} = \frac{128}{9XE}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="101" height="37" />. Then, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/0/5d0baa5b36ca137763c808173b090ace1303c193.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{XE}{AX} = \frac{XF}{XC} = \frac{16}{9} \rightarrow XF = \frac{16}{9}XC$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="276" height="37" />. 
</p><p>Multiplying our equations for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/4/684ddc7ebf8968e63d288df2b06a1871d585a764.png" class="latex" alt="$XF$" width="30" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/8404db180a248e3e2cbbdde9c3b5cf31a2bb27e5.png" class="latex" alt="$XG$" width="30" height="12" /> yields that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/c/97c41da87e2ed41d171f4a1a03fc90e96b45c64c.png" class="latex" alt="$XF \cdot XG = \frac{9 \cdot 17}{16XC} \cdot \frac{16}{9}XC = \boxed{\textbf{(A)}\ 17}.$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="310" height="38" />
</p>
