<p>An integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" /> is selected at random in the range <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/9/a89a36515599ac5c978b807b70b1995c3fdc6128.png" class="latex" alt="$1\leq N \leq 2020$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="109" height="15" /> . What is the probability that the remainder when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/5/875cf99b5ee880c799fa36962a7ef33e6c47d431.png" class="latex" alt="$N^{16}$" width="29" height="15" /> is divided by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/f/04f253f0d6e2c3c987bceae84cf4aa7ecd812550.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{1}{5}\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{2}{5}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{3}{5}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{4}{5}\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 1$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="364" height="37" />

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Notice that we can rewrite <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/5/875cf99b5ee880c799fa36962a7ef33e6c47d431.png" class="latex" alt="$N^{16}$" width="29" height="15" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/a/5/2a5f214878aa03ae316eaf6843a7cfc8dcc57bbd.png" class="latex" alt="$(N^{4})^4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="44" height="19" />. By <a href="/wiki/index.php/Fermat%27s_Little_Theorem" title="Fermat&#39;s Little Theorem">Fermat's Little Theorem</a>, we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/7/0172e926918a45e2b0fcc8b91fe4183611f313f6.png" class="latex" alt="$N^{(5-1)} \equiv 1 \pmod {5}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="155" height="20" />  if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/1/a9102a4825353bede872afe3f050981ce353b2e7.png" class="latex" alt="$N \not \equiv 0 \pmod {5}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="120" height="18" />. Therefore for all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/1/a9102a4825353bede872afe3f050981ce353b2e7.png" class="latex" alt="$N \not \equiv 0 \pmod {5}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="120" height="18" /> we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/9/2399ae7c092ecc31788bd16b52a246886fef93e7.png" class="latex" alt="$N^{16} \equiv (N^{4})^4 \equiv 1^4 \equiv 1 \pmod 5$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="245" height="19" />. Since  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/9/a89a36515599ac5c978b807b70b1995c3fdc6128.png" class="latex" alt="$1\leq N \leq 2020$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="109" height="15" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/9/509a9369d5c55fd98dc99cb2938fb44f0101f90a.png" class="latex" alt="$2020$" width="35" height="12" /> is divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/2/d129a7b3c0bf9fbd0693afdd426a66d2a0a8c3ae.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> of the possible <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" /> are divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/d/fbdf454286562ea5de535378a3729ff52914c663.png" class="latex" alt="$N^{16} \equiv 1 \pmod {5}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="134" height="19" /> with probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/f/fbf90f96861234df5ac91133e597048430290205.png" class="latex" alt="$1-\frac{1}{5},$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="48" height="37" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/e/f2e87d25d200a3d327db7610992a100867086c73.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) } \frac 45}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="60" height="48" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Note that the patterns for the units digits repeat, so in a sense we only need to find the patterns for the digits <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/4/53458ebc12bfd4c66c5cad57012a1200803297e1.png" class="latex" alt="$0-9$" width="40" height="12" /> .
The pattern for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />, no matter what power, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> doesn't work. Likewise, the pattern for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> is always <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />. Doing the same for the rest of the digits, we find that the units digits of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/e/90efb7927361f42cf3c6450845f6ba95e2d103d6.png" class="latex" alt="$1^{16}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="21" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/f/ebfc79bbd3ccc9d92286e70e51d81a3a9648d5d4.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{16}$" width="21" height="15" /> ,<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/5/1f5144af4d364d9becc73bb56899fb9cfd50027b.png" class="latex" alt="$3^{16}$" width="21" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/8/cc8106079bfc59044fbc1793ecf32061cf92c8cf.png" class="latex" alt="$4^{16}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="21" height="15" /> ,<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/b/9eb2e83cf346933bdf92c66d69961638d657bf1d.png" class="latex" alt="$6^{16}$" width="21" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/5/e25b4dd6f560e8035aeb245611417a1fd1c2e02b.png" class="latex" alt="$7^{16}$" width="21" height="15" /> ,<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/9/649e170c0d808401bd328cfddc874d14edd81ab4.png" class="latex" alt="$8^{16}$" width="21" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/0/390ce7a84d7533c9987f94f4fd76e1045b48d660.png" class="latex" alt="$9^{16}$" width="21" height="15" /> all have the remainder of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> when divided by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/e/f2e87d25d200a3d327db7610992a100867086c73.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) } \frac 45}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="60" height="48" />.
</p><p><br />
Explanation:
Since the remainder is 1, the units digit is either 1 or 6. Since we only care about the units digits, we don't need to think about anything beyond units digits.
We count in modulus ten (fancy word of saying ignore everything except the units digit)
For 1:
1, 1, 1, 1... anything with a units digit one will always have powers that have a units digit one.
For 2:
2, 4, 8, 6, 2, 4, 8, 6... we see that it repeats with a cycle of 4. Since we are looking for the 16th power, the units digit will be 6, and this works.
For 3:
3, 9, 7, 1, 3, 9, 7, 1... similarly to 2, a cycle of 4 and the 16th power has a units digit of 1. This works.
For 4:
4, 6, 4, 6... a cycle of 4, 16th power has a units digit of 6. This works.
For 5:
5, 5, 5, 5... the units digit will always be 5, and this doesn't work.
For 6:
6, 6, 6, 6... the units digit will always be 6, and this works.
For 7:
7, 9, 3, 1, 7, 9, 3, 1... a cycle of 4, 16th power has a units digit of 1. This works.
For 8:
8, 4, 2, 6, 8, 4, 2, 6... a cycle of 4, 16th power has a units digit of 6. This works.
For 9:
9, 1, 9, 1... a cycle of 2, 16th power has a units digit of 1. This works.
Last but not least, 0:
0, 0, 0... the units digit will always be 0, and this does not work.
To sum up: There are ten choices for the units digit. Of these ten choices, 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 7, 8, 9 all work, and the probability is therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/c/f4c433ef8fb27d63ea8d953df90b31e99ae4a833.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{8}{10}=\frac{4}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="61" height="38" /> which is answer choice <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/9/e696fd64ff84fd71c3892fc3cffd386dda181311.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{4}{5}}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="61" height="49" />.
~Explanation by JH. L
