<p>A radio program has a quiz consisting of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> multiple-choice questions, each with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> choices. A contestant wins if he or she gets <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> or more of the questions right. The contestant answers randomly to each question. What is the probability of winning?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/a/efa3e8d86d99e078dd84c3fb72eb41b537169082.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{1}{27}\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{1}{9}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{2}{9}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{7}{27}\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="385" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>There are two ways the contestant can win.
</p><p>Case 1: The contestant guesses all three right. This can only happen <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/e/0ce226964c3acfea452ce576691b106c262ee195.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3} * \frac{1}{3} * \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{27}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="118" height="37" /> of the time.
</p><p>Case 2: The contestant guesses only two right. We pick one of the questions to get wrong, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />, and this can happen <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/1/6911cf09b4bf25b759bdaa7997f09973b4c65f80.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3} * \frac{1}{3} * \frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="72" height="37" /> of the time. Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/a/b7ade541a81cd42318d42ccf3e6ca416f5963627.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{27} * 3$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="48" height="37" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/7/1b72412a5837fac77bb0abdc0fd19757deeb70c6.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{6}{27}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" />.
</p><p>So, in total the two cases combined equals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/2/b62a8f231b68594b017c437f572cb52af9a77aee.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{27} + \frac{6}{27}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="65" height="37" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/a/55a6e7992e89abeac4bacb2103f0c0358b386b87.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{7}{27}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="69" height="48" />.
</p><p><br />
More detailed explanation: For case 1, the contestant must guess all three correctly. The probability of guessing one problem right is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/1/4e1965f8d70832ded66434c75a7bd52b75090af0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />, so the probability of getting all three right is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/1/40199d7158a8e6e5399c36ad26ae5a6bfe4f1d5e.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^{3}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="49" height="48" />. 
For case 2: we must choose one of the problems to answer correctly and two to answer incorrectly. The probabilities for guessing correctly and incorrectly are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/1/4e1965f8d70832ded66434c75a7bd52b75090af0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/4/7547747b133453d3c209cff6cfbf129aa67a6947.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />, respectively. So we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/2/5c281589010cf984feb73a6458645373af9b9015.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^{2}\cdot\frac{2}{3}\cdot3$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="97" height="48" />. The answer is the sum of probabilities of case 1 and 2, since there are no overcounts. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/b/5bb036ec0a4e5907491d60d7891b43fac23dd50e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{27}+\frac{6}{27}=\frac{7}{27}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="115" height="38" />.
</p>
