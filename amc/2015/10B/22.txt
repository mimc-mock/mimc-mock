<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/e/5ee220f278903ae2cff38105900cc272ba3898c9.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pair A=(cos(pi/5)-sin(pi/10),cos(pi/10)+sin(pi/5)), B=(2*cos(pi/5)-sin(pi/10),cos(pi/10)), C=(1,0), D=(0,0), E1=(-sin(pi/10),cos(pi/10)); //(0,0) is a convenient point //E1 to prevent conflict with direction E(ast) pair F=intersectionpoints(D--A,E1--B)[0], G=intersectionpoints(A--C,E1--B)[0], H=intersectionpoints(B--D,A--C)[0], I=intersectionpoints(C--E1,D--B)[0], J=intersectionpoints(E1--C,D--A)[0]; draw(A--B--C--D--E1--A); draw(A--D--B--E1--C--A); draw(F--I--G--J--H--F); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,N); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,E); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,SE); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,SW); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,E1,W); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,F,NW); label(&quot;$G$&quot;,G,NE); label(&quot;$H$&quot;,H,E); label(&quot;$I$&quot;,I,S); label(&quot;$J$&quot;,J,W); [/asy]" width="252" height="235" />
</p><p>Triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/0/8600e69a900b0105eb63e8712ffca58544004c00.png" class="latex" alt="$AFG$" width="42" height="13" /> is isosceles, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/9/769ea896cde59eb210cdffebdd13bd7d65c1c012.png" class="latex" alt="$AG=AF=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="112" height="13" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/2/a72360ee95c803f33d3b7c505c6ebf6fd8039bd2.png" class="latex" alt="$FJ = FG$" width="78" height="12" /> since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/e/7fed50822f1aaf9524609df18bf85675cacf5bfd.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle FGJ$" width="57" height="13" /> is also isosceles. Using the symmetry of pentagon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/0/3e0d5ac375e8138d63b1458a2b1866bf06b01ac4.png" class="latex" alt="$FGHIJ$" width="66" height="12" />, notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/e/01e6af7186c14f9ec3e58155ad4c357f3560af12.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle JHG \cong \triangle AFG$" width="141" height="13" />. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/6/fb67bf202a6500f894b38a2396193de2bab32ec2.png" class="latex" alt="$JH=AF=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="113" height="13" />.
</p><p>Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/0/cb0a44c0c0712b654ec012abe4521e015bdf13e9.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AJH \sim \triangle AFG$" width="140" height="13" />, 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/c/7ccfe2091576cd90b1fbfbfd50fd66cf8a02e211.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{JH}{AF+FJ}=\frac{FG}{FA}\]" width="133" height="38" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/3/b63c9ad2e1356848d37b6beed2a845aab1170faf.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{1+FG} = \frac{FG}1\]" width="117" height="38" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/4/f549ac301cd30a95e9c29eb84f98669e5e7b355d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[1 = FG^2 + FG\]" width="118" height="16" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/9/dd97e45bcd6eafbf2a191413fb99284e7a5fe931.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[FG^2+FG-1 = 0\]" width="150" height="16" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/3/533655eb8fb1bbbe21c3932038fcdae9af54451b.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[FG = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{5} }{2}\]" width="124" height="41" />
</p><p>So, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/7/c/67c4b624949fbe0885686cb1caff59a62666f0d5.png" class="latex" alt="$FG=\frac{-1 + \sqrt{5}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="125" height="41" /> since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/5/3951902c11a1cd39a091ea15f0f06cbc79095de4.png" class="latex" alt="$FG$" width="28" height="12" /> must be greater than 0.
</p><p>Notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/0/2e018e71aa9e59095817a0c52b3b9fa9e80f76af.png" class="latex" alt="$CD = AE = AJ = AF + FJ = 1 + \frac{-1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} = \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="443" height="41" />.
</p><p>Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/c/b5c22f3757d8ec773ec929b4776ddf9adc9e6dc4.png" class="latex" alt="$FG+JH+CD=\frac{-1+\sqrt5}2+1+\frac{1+\sqrt5}2=\boxed{\mathbf{(D)}\ 1+\sqrt{5}\ }$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="475" height="41" />
</p><p><br />
Note by Fasolinka: Alternatively, extend <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/4/fb47261bdfc683fe04c09798101fd8ae59c5ece4.png" class="latex" alt="$FI$" width="23" height="12" /> and call its intersection with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/e/27e525b776c49bbe7d0ae66cfc7ab8f58f40624e.png" class="latex" alt="$DC$" width="29" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/b/dfb064112b6c94470339f6571f69d07afc1c024c.png" class="latex" alt="$K$" width="16" height="12" />. It is not hard to see that quadrilaterals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/b/d0b61c6e447ee95fcdd9b10fcb25012b3f330b68.png" class="latex" alt="$FGCK$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="61" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/f/0ef2a75aee9415ab55e76c2440f3de15b5e07e38.png" class="latex" alt="$JHKD$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="63" height="14" /> are parallelograms, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/f/2ef8747386dd0bf253731641f19ea3a91e777d1e.png" class="latex" alt="$DC=DK+KC=JH+FG=1+\frac{-1+\sqrt{5}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="374" height="43" />, and the same result is achieved.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/6/d16dc9a18dda1d87a60aa7e4f0fa8579047f28dc.png" class="latex" alt="$JH=BH=BG=AG=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="221" height="13" />. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>When you first see this problem you can't help but see similar triangles. But this shape is filled with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/0/960b5b3a73164fc617b6609c8e126da7028ad4f9.png" class="latex" alt="$36 - 72 - 72$" width="98" height="12" /> triangles throwing us off. First, let us write our answer in terms of one side length. I chose to write it in terms of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/5/3951902c11a1cd39a091ea15f0f06cbc79095de4.png" class="latex" alt="$FG$" width="28" height="12" /> so we can apply similar triangles easily. To simplify the process lets write <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/5/3951902c11a1cd39a091ea15f0f06cbc79095de4.png" class="latex" alt="$FG$" width="28" height="12" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />.
</p><p>First what is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/c/a9c0038c64a84032c5ca301c21f6c333aedc15b6.png" class="latex" alt="$JH$" width="28" height="12" /> in terms of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />, also remember <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/8/4b87f9e6a7437726fa40afe8a6dd11ccb1b3e165.png" class="latex" alt="$AJ = 1+x$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="91" height="14" />: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/7/bc713cece138178971e638fa0725ff856a8bd1fd.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{JH}{1+x}=\frac{x}{1}\]" width="80" height="38" /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/f/d7fd77ef768016b348d1c1a3afa0c00f8fb2eaf5.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[JH = {x}^2+x\]" width="101" height="16" />
</p><p>Next, find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/e/27e525b776c49bbe7d0ae66cfc7ab8f58f40624e.png" class="latex" alt="$DC$" width="29" height="12" /> in terms of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />, also remember <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/b/1/7b111182675f04d2215e36772a09076fdd90eacf.png" class="latex" alt="$AD = 2+x$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="94" height="14" />: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/3/78345b6e15c17b0fc12f685f7825de64ff004b79.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{DC}{2+x}=\frac{x}{1}\]" width="80" height="38" /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/3/e13a82ed629a42e44c48d4ff56b6baa0533bdc9c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[DC = {x}^2+2x\]" width="113" height="16" />
</p><p>So adding all the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/f/78fdf32a562f21e3aecdf5ecd43f27e25c7e5176.png" class="latex" alt="$FG + JH + CD$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="130" height="14" /> we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/8/0889cae8e016b49b92a5c4be89d08903d2c5044c.png" class="latex" alt="$2{x}^2+4x$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="68" height="16" />. Now we have to find out what x is. For this, we break out a bit of trig. Let's look at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/4/9e4cae83c0fa7a0b33f58a5ec88e27c870c017a3.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AFG$" width="58" height="13" />. By the law of sines:  
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/5/525077bd89c2d3237a97092c0d4ec11ff336be8d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{x}{\sin(36)}=\frac{1}{\sin(72)}\]" width="138" height="41" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/6/f46681f0790e9b6469df5f6d3372c68ab4931fb0.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x=\frac{\sin(36)}{\sin(72)}\]" width="91" height="43" />
</p><p>Now by the double angle identities in trig. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/2/8a291b047ed38ef0847e2876e97641818a49e27c.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin(72) = 2\sin(36)\cos(36)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="203" height="18" /> 
substituting in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/a/b1a7bcc4f450a2ef63ab7ea064ae052da0fddfca.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x=\frac{1}{2\cos(36)}\]" width="105" height="41" /> 
</p><p>A good thing to memorize for AMC and AIME is the exact values for all the nice sines and cosines. You would then know that:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/f/f2f315afbe69112bc4e480224fd44a6b9f1c0e7d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\cos(36)= \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{4}\]" width="138" height="41" />

<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/4/8349ec30a20c03d16b171f3173f7a0a33f302ed5.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x = \frac{2}{1+\sqrt{5}} = \frac{-1+\sqrt{5}}{2}\]" width="190" height="45" />
</p><p>Substituting back into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/8/0889cae8e016b49b92a5c4be89d08903d2c5044c.png" class="latex" alt="$2{x}^2+4x$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="68" height="16" /> we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/8/408d8e062015b2d033e1897601482b939cb3c88f.png" class="latex" alt="$FG+JH+CD=\boxed{\mathbf{(D)}\ 1+\sqrt{5}\ }$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="264" height="33" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>Notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/1/be1e1d6e33092797b7f296f74512ca76768c9f57.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle AFG=\angle AFB$" width="134" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/c/75cb35df2eacc6903cdef61704bd66e23d74d5e3.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle FAG=\angle ABF$" width="134" height="13" />, so we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/3/b732572d378629148af36196a6f3e38c0756f831.png" class="latex" alt="$\bigtriangleup AFG=\bigtriangleup BAF$" width="141" height="13" />. Thus
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/0/e00557d319f27d7c030aa8ed0eeb0b8c5b750d66.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{AF}{FG}=\frac{FB}{JH}\]" width="86" height="37" />

<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/d/a5dd88c740df07d9be0f32d176f26be3d30ca4c9.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{AF}{FG}=\frac{AJ}{JH}\]" width="85" height="37" />

</p><p>Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/a/37a69801d21ce6b248b0fa7db2440d33f9bd8ec5.png" class="latex" alt="$\bigtriangleup AFG=\bigtriangleup ADC$" width="142" height="13" />

<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/5/f3584cce2cd3c1f7110f362e96c09164b8d732ed.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{AF}{FG}=\frac{2AF+FG}{DC}\]" width="145" height="37" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/2/f42a6e78bcc80c16d406bcabbade292b45625353.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{\frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{2}}=\frac{2+\frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{2}}{DC}\]" width="137" height="53" />
Solving the equation gets <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/2/73269ecc65a738018d84ef65e52513412fe9b31b.png" class="latex" alt="$DC=\frac{\sqrt{5}+1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="112" height="41" />
</p><p>Finally adding them up gets <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/f/77f78402801676b9d9576f4969a0edde95138e3c.png" class="latex" alt="$FG+JH+DC=\frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{2}+1+\frac{\sqrt{5}+1}{2}=1+\sqrt{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="408" height="41" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/3/d53b37edf32cfbc0179e6e6cd9b2ff4d482b6634.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\mathrm{(D)}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="39" height="29" />
</p><p>Note: this solution might be a bit complicated but it definitely works when none of the cleverer symmetries in Solution 1 is noticed.
</p><p>~ Nafer
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5">Solution 5</span></h2>
<p>Note that: 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/0/1d0d6543cd5edd55c069d99ce1d1f5f9555ee8a8.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{FG}{DC}=\frac{AG}{AC}\]" width="86" height="37" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/b/16bda1e1f2e3a433385df89bbeaa30d2989be23e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{JH}{DC}=\frac{AH}{AC}\]" width="88" height="37" />
Summing the equations, we have:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/9/4a93c94eaa862c3ea621d224505f34e6cc2a1f8c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{FG + JH + CD}{CD} - 1 = \frac{2 + GH}{AC}\]" width="253" height="37" />
We know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/7/5c799e9977234aeb7be124fc6cb90b8953b3f8b2.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[AC = 2 + GH\]" width="113" height="14" />
So we have
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/5/095f579fba32fefa9f9832601423bed84f59b190.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{FG+JH+CD}{CD} - 1 = 1 \Rightarrow FG + JH + CD = 2 \cdot CD\]" width="433" height="37" />
All that remains is to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/c/79c918bfa9b7b699552d7779d1de346b73d75010.png" class="latex" alt="$CD.$" width="33" height="12" /> By the law of cosines, we have
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/c/2ece69d7a91cc453669ce5ceb249a8ca4a6ab380.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} CD^2 &amp;= 2 - 2 \cos 108\\ &amp;= 2 - \frac{1 - \sqrt{5}}{2} \\ &amp;= \frac{3 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \end{align*}" width="158" height="117" />
</p><p>Thus, 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/0/4a029fdc4506f2da6ab5d018601782e08921e698.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} 2 \cdot CD &amp;= 2 \cdot \sqrt{\frac{3 + \sqrt{5}}{2}} \\ &amp;= 2 \cdot \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \\ &amp;= \boxed{\mathbf{(D)}\ 1 + \sqrt{5}} \end{align*}" width="179" height="141" />
</p><p>I'll leave you to convince yourself about certain facts that were deduced in order to obtain these equations.
</p><p>~mathboy282
</p>
