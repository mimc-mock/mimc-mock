<p>An unfair coin lands on heads with a probability of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/4/c84a414e9655bf25b41aebc8c9f7bcb651acf2fb.png" class="latex" alt="$\tfrac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="9" height="21" />. When tossed <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/2/65262bf247c4a3c6114a8223d249dc9702619d3e.png" class="latex" alt="$n&gt;1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="12" /> times, the probability of exactly two heads is the same as the probability of exactly three heads. What is the value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/f/74fc23bda9af8b1480f23e8d4dafc8674d4ca02a.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\; 5 \qquad\textbf{(B)}\; 8 \qquad\textbf{(C)}\; 10 \qquad\textbf{(D)}\; 11 \qquad\textbf{(E)}\; 13$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="373" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>When tossed <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> times, the probability of getting exactly 2 heads and the rest tails is
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc6405c602dee5d12cb017541fb674a4f3bbb8e8.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\dbinom{n}{2} {\left( \frac{1}{4} \right)}^2 {\left( \frac{3}{4} \right) }^{n-2}.\]" width="150" height="53" />
</p><p>Similarly, the probability of getting exactly 3 heads is
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/c/92c95422814935e2a1744e40bb8ff525c4db41a3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\dbinom{n}{3}{\left( \frac{1}{4} \right)}^3 {\left( \frac{3}{4} \right) }^{n-3}.\]" width="150" height="53" />
</p><p>Now set the two probabilities equal to each other and solve for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/e/deed9cb1861f787abde4c577294d240a24de5890.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\dbinom{n}{2}{\left( \frac{1}{4} \right)}^2 {\left( \frac{3}{4} \right) }^{n-2}=\dbinom{n}{3}{\left( \frac{1}{4} \right)}^3 {\left( \frac{3}{4} \right) }^{n-3}\]" width="320" height="53" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/f/07f1b2a26ad277631bd46c1314db7092bcd944c6.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{n(n-1)}{2!} \cdot \frac{3}{4} = \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}  \cdot \frac{1}{4}\]" width="272" height="39" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/4/5d414de3ecbe06f23e64517629c32e1a0023e6d3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[3 = \frac{n-2}{3}\]" width="77" height="37" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/d/1cd6ebfe15d5b338b44028394dc9b9543fe488f7.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[n-2 = 9\]" width="74" height="12" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/7/c27f5da250dd157f54a2a7e3c2e4eb2eb7e4ed48.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[n = \fbox{\textbf{(D)}\; 11}\]" width="102" height="28" />
</p><p><br />
Note: the original problem did not specify <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/2/65262bf247c4a3c6114a8223d249dc9702619d3e.png" class="latex" alt="$n&gt;1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="12" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/9/1d96ecc15129c5631ba4a2c6578e91e3d13e3baf.png" class="latex" alt="$n=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="12" /> was a solution, but this was fixed in the Wiki problem text so that the answer would make sense. <a href="/wiki/index.php/User:Adihaya" title="User:Adihaya">— @adihaya</a> (<a href="/wiki/index.php/User_talk:Adihaya" title="User talk:Adihaya">talk</a>) 15:23, 19 February 2016 (EST)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Bash it out with the answer choices! (not really a rigorous solution)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2.5">Solution 2.5</span></h2>
<p>In order to test the answer choices efficiently, realize that the probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> flips yielding two heads is of the form:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/7/e57badeeab7becbe678e1f6ad2c296667e1d2529.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{n}{2}{\left(\frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{4}\right)}{\left(\frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{3}{4} \ldots \right)} = \dbinom{n}{2}{\left(\frac{3^{n-2}}{4^n}\right)}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="345" height="53" />
</p><p>Similarly, the form for the probability of three heads is:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/c/c8ca0831d7944482c2537575a113ce88ee8c628e.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{n}{3}{\left(\frac{3^{n-3}}{4^n}\right)}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="100" height="53" />
</p><p>The probability of getting three heads (comapred to the probability of getting two) from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> flips is missing a factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> in the numerator. Thus, we need <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/3/a437de5bd6a33f09d120fb93f3ac1b82a8d1d816.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{n}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="35" height="53" /> to add a factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> to the numerator of the probability of getting three heads.
Our testing equation becomes
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/4/514dbaae76ec78e6d85fbe7314205aa254e62f61.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\dbinom{n}{2} \times 3 = \dbinom{n}{3}\]" width="125" height="53" />
</p><p>since after factoring out the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/3/a437de5bd6a33f09d120fb93f3ac1b82a8d1d816.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{n}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="35" height="53" />, the remaining factorizations should be equal. 
</p><p>The only answer choice satisfying this condition is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/9/e69d72d953cec5d6a759b9a16540394c9c38e263.png" class="latex" alt="$\fbox{\textbf{(D)}\;11}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="62" height="28" />.
</p><p>-Solution by Joeya
</p>
