<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>First, note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" /> lies on the radical axis of any of the pairs of circles. Suppose that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/1/3216e8846eb44f1055c322fb5bd30169d5d62cec.png" class="latex" alt="$O_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/e/5ee48e5d3968014ef1e27c31e56d2f7ea5f8cb1d.png" class="latex" alt="$O_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="20" height="15" /> are the centers of two circles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/b/70b97eb4ad6e4e7a5a97e2547d9ed6e965960d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$C_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/5/b65e6facfe122c182fe14d503aeace2c52a9739d.png" class="latex" alt="$C_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" /> that intersect exactly at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />, with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/1/3216e8846eb44f1055c322fb5bd30169d5d62cec.png" class="latex" alt="$O_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/e/5ee48e5d3968014ef1e27c31e56d2f7ea5f8cb1d.png" class="latex" alt="$O_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="20" height="15" /> lying on the same side of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/2/d52ea5ba2fd8e98f44b501c9ad8d43edc1aa7d40.png" class="latex" alt="$O_1 O_2=39$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="84" height="15" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/3/d73a924915d4574231fca799cc615f8b228c76d2.png" class="latex" alt="$x=O_1 R$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="70" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/f/cff9f35b680f7d49f0e3fc0e88c96a07ca94b661.png" class="latex" alt="$y=O_2 R$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="69" height="16" />, and suppose that the radius of circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/b/70b97eb4ad6e4e7a5a97e2547d9ed6e965960d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$C_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> and the radius of circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/5/b65e6facfe122c182fe14d503aeace2c52a9739d.png" class="latex" alt="$C_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/9/24929cc9cb511c6e92ca4ea7f1cd8ba54a483bbd.png" class="latex" alt="$\tfrac{5}{8}r$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="18" height="21" />.
</p><p>Then the power of point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> with respect to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/b/70b97eb4ad6e4e7a5a97e2547d9ed6e965960d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$C_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> is
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/d/12da2e08523757516e0c19547c8d7dbcc52bd383.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(r+x)(r-x) = r^2 - x^2 = 24^2\]" width="238" height="19" />
</p><p>and the power of point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> with respect to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/5/b65e6facfe122c182fe14d503aeace2c52a9739d.png" class="latex" alt="$C_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" /> is
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/6/8e6f2e3f3013f9bf4868bef2f88da91e61dae1ab.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\left(\frac{5}{8}r + y\right) \left(\frac{5}{8}r - y\right) = \frac{25}{64}r^2 - y^2 = 24^2.\]" width="314" height="43" />
</p><p>Also, note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/6/7d66facb91ae067e27bd59f62537087bba5e5092.png" class="latex" alt="$x-y=39$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="84" height="16" />.
</p><p>Subtract the above two equations to find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/4/4348bea1e2ca0af5b1dff296dd95ff463050be56.png" class="latex" alt="$\tfrac{39}{64}r^2 - x^2 + y^2 = 0$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="145" height="21" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/9/9b9163bed76567e6145306a5a535805645dd05f2.png" class="latex" alt="$39 r^2 = 64(x^2-y^2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="146" height="19" />. As <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/6/7d66facb91ae067e27bd59f62537087bba5e5092.png" class="latex" alt="$x-y=39$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="84" height="16" />, we find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/c/2bc53f605ff3a386f4731f8bb0aa33c6d0533e93.png" class="latex" alt="$r^2=64(x+y) = 64(2y+39)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="229" height="19" />. Plug this into an earlier equation to find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/0/2f0b9ef4daeaa892f33fed1ee00c309b77528fd1.png" class="latex" alt="$25(2y+39)-y^2=24^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="179" height="19" />. This is a quadratic equation with solutions <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/e/72e2df6e68010947005a8ebd8ef9e6ac7cfb7f68.png" class="latex" alt="$y=\tfrac{50 \pm 64}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="73" height="21" />, and as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> is a length, it is positive, hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/a/3ca2f64a782ebe6b16835ab40db7ba00eaeb910f.png" class="latex" alt="$y=57$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="52" height="16" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/4/5449b29cad0509516545f530c7eaca0f157b6e47.png" class="latex" alt="$x=y+39=96$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="126" height="16" />. This is the only possibility if the two centers lie on the same side of their radical axis.
</p><p>On the other hand, if they lie on opposite sides, then it is clear that there is only one possibility, and then it is clear that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/a/f7ae02532f149a1695c5fd35022e0261786e6a1a.png" class="latex" alt="$O_1 R + O_2 R = O_1 O_2 = 39$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="201" height="15" />. Therefore, we obtain exactly four possible centers, and the sum of the desired lengths is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/d/3ddc460ee1a8b318cb78d869cc0e2dd459d61942.png" class="latex" alt="$57+96+39 = \boxed{\textbf{(D)}\; 192}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="196" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Note that if a circle passes through a pair of points, the center of the circle is on the perpendicular bisector of the line segment between the pair of points. This means that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> are all on the perpendicular bisector of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" />. Let us say the distance from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> to the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" /> is some <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" />. Therefore, the distance from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> to the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/d/f0d38344f2252dfe9521b6c13fe6fa26e6a3d22a.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{(\frac{8}{5}\sqrt{a^2 + 24^2})^2 - 24^2}$" style="vertical-align: -14px" width="181" height="43" />, which comes out to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/a/74a0b2b42b793ce1ee45bd31cc4b4ab7475f5542.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{\frac{64}{25}a^2 + \frac{39}{25}\cdot 576}$" style="vertical-align: -14px" width="142" height="43" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/f/63feee87303d41c4c2337f8c50e66b188aae1fc5.png" class="latex" alt="$AB = 39$" width="70" height="13" />, we have one of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/e/edeec0ead7d95bed9d44bbdf412f9c470f9b2990.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{\frac{64}{25}a^2 + \frac{39}{25}\cdot 576} \pm a$" style="vertical-align: -14px" width="173" height="43" /> to be equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/5/e058d401a1b942f106f081ff1fcca8a8b1d42a7e.png" class="latex" alt="$39$" width="17" height="12" />. We can solve both equations to get that out of the four possible solutions, and only two are positive: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/1/4b14ef560acadba8244952e9a8d5481f494445dc.png" class="latex" alt="$57$" width="18" height="12" />. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Number-Intensive_Solution">Number-Intensive Solution</span></h2>
<p>Start by drawing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" /> first, because trying to get all four circles down will take you a few years. Next, because all circles have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> on them, and since all points on a circle are equidistant from the center, all circle centers lie on the perpendicular bisector of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" />, and point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> is on this bisector.
</p><p>In order for all the circle radii to be different (because the circles can't be congruent), two circle centers are on the same side of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" />, and two are straddling it. For the latter two circles- just call them <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />- clearly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/2/1a23d2aff965ab809972bb30bb90c51cdb5bf0fa.png" class="latex" alt="$AR+BR$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="78" height="14" /> is 39.
</p><p>Now, let's take the next case. Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> lie on the same side. Construct the triangles from your picture, and use the Pythagorean Theorem (maybe divide all lengths by 3 to lessen big numbers) and then you get that the distance from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> to the closest circle center is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/1/4b14ef560acadba8244952e9a8d5481f494445dc.png" class="latex" alt="$57$" width="18" height="12" />. Therefore, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/2/5923ec7122326296a4215a16b812d45cf1f57d34.png" class="latex" alt="$39+2*57+39=192 \boxed{(D)}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="218" height="29" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pythagorean_Theorem_Solution">Pythagorean Theorem Solution</span></h2>
<p>Since the radical axis <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" /> is perpendicular to the line connecting the center of the circles, we have that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/4/29486ec44bf1cfe3c8029a18874a9160430a5980.png" class="latex" alt="$A,B,C,D,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="85" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> are collinear.  WLOG, assume that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> are on the same side of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/c/15cf7f44e4585279a4894d62a8f4e1fb7ba31803.png" class="latex" alt="$AR=y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="61" height="16" /> and let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/b/9bb6d51dfeef42d2ffda457e32099b4b8c38977f.png" class="latex" alt="$BP=x$" width="63" height="12" /> so that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/0/db0f53244bfeab7db33db38d8449af38cf96e382.png" class="latex" alt="$AP=\frac{5}{8}x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="75" height="37" />.  Then, we have in right triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/6/636defc88bf9618a149d99dd3d03c0b2f82531b6.png" class="latex" alt="$PBR$" width="43" height="12" />  and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/9/5d9159f8dcc1c1518aafe312b212b907cc12674e.png" class="latex" alt="$PAR$" width="41" height="13" />,  
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/f/00f77252671e07af406c20fd95e58f1d3d42cfe1.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(39+y)^2+24^2=x^2\]" width="159" height="19" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/4/3b4529b3d6ca740d53ccf89c294d36856c2aa996.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[y^2+24^2=\frac{25}{64}x^2\]" width="127" height="38" /> Subtracting the second from the first gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/1/f3167e9426acdb31b3bbb3c4193e991053f05eda.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2=64(2y+39)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="131" height="19" /> and substituting this in the second equation gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/2/44215088a9a74e5c4b6000e14f8513f5e7cd7ab6.png" class="latex" alt="$y^2-50y-399=0=(y-57)(y+7).$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="296" height="19" />  Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/9/e19e1ccc11a468abd9bd2fdcea82dbe2d537cf50.png" class="latex" alt="$y&gt;0, y=57$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="103" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/1/bb194e144a6b800942e72703eabe719bfe5edc17.png" class="latex" alt="$AR=57$" width="70" height="13" /> while <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/0/6d0979a27ecea93edbca41c83f456beecaef3de5.png" class="latex" alt="$BR=AR+39=96.$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="166" height="14" /> Since none of the circles are congruent, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> must be on the opposite side of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/2/0f2a51b23a72c3a9be02c55c9b87fd54e7657548.png" class="latex" alt="$CR+DR=CD=39.$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="180" height="14" />  Hence, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/1/8c143fcaaf4b8492d77b628a9eb2a2b3a00d4ab8.png" class="latex" alt="$AR+BR+CR+DR=57+96+39=192,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="359" height="16" /> which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/b/55bdade53f471aab49f63d1a734979cc0c636640.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{D}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="27" height="24" />.
</p>
