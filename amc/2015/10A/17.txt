<p>A line that passes through the origin intersects both the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/3/34310f2f36ed6d724838edb08788ee62acb33386.png" class="latex" alt="$x = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="12" /> and the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/5/5c5d0774fabfa66742a85a953f5c0c8a90afc01f.png" class="latex" alt="$y=1+ \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3} x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="103" height="41" />. The three lines create an equilateral triangle. What is the perimeter of the triangle?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/b/12be0a6f2cf2144f67b0b2d9dbbafbbf213b7a2d.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 2\sqrt{6} \qquad\textbf{(B)} \ 2+2\sqrt{3} \qquad\textbf{(C)} \ 6 \qquad\textbf{(D)} \ 3 + 2\sqrt{3} \qquad\textbf{(E)} \ 6 + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="533" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Since the triangle is equilateral and one of the sides is a vertical line, the triangle must have a horizontal line of symmetry, and therefore the other two sides will have opposite slopes. The slope of the other given line is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/c/e5cb6e7c42d6bf0c332b448f0d28fff6c0aaaef1.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" /> so the third must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/e/9ee4f620cb0047a60cbfff5c979bdb5a528939e4.png" class="latex" alt="$-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="41" height="41" />. Since this third line passes through the origin, its equation is simply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/2/b52e445b5152a10f3fbc40809ba0db887e5a4923.png" class="latex" alt="$y = -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="86" height="41" />. To find two vertices of the triangle, plug in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/5/4c52e02a35db152495396584bfeae1d0599cd753.png" class="latex" alt="$x=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="12" /> to both the other equations. 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/7/db73a7e4ddec3c87a183b809b9284622f01c639f.png" class="latex" alt="$y = -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="74" height="41" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/9/699d303935f2bd03b54f2d56129ea225b19cf028.png" class="latex" alt="$y = 1 + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="92" height="41" />
</p><p>We now have the coordinates of two vertices, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/7/aa78892576d1846de9b191eeca4f169b31051dbb.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(1, -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="83" height="53" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/2/ce2889bd7b10e0adba5330b14079874f4520b3c5.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(1, 1 + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="101" height="53" />. The length of one side is the distance between the y-coordinates, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/3/e8355d01fa2acf82aa8078a6f17800f0cd84cf92.png" class="latex" alt="$1 +  \frac{2\sqrt{3}}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="67" height="41" />.
</p><p>The perimeter of the triangle is thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/5/6456240e20556fde9f490fb2ad6515c1ffbd8c5b.png" class="latex" alt="$3\left(1 +  \frac{2\sqrt{3}}{3}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="105" height="53" />, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/4/5449d48993b0316a29237b1e741e74b65c1d29c6.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) }3 + 2\sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="112" height="33" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Draw a line from the y-intercept of the equation  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/5/5c5d0774fabfa66742a85a953f5c0c8a90afc01f.png" class="latex" alt="$y=1+ \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3} x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="103" height="41" /> perpendicular to the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/5/4c52e02a35db152495396584bfeae1d0599cd753.png" class="latex" alt="$x=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="12" />. There is a square of side length 1 inscribed in the equilateral triangle. The problem becomes reduced to finding the perimeter of an equilateral triangle with a square of side length 1 inscribed in it. The side length is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/b/08b496cce5d3378b354c9de77af9321fd94ef76e.png" class="latex" alt="$2\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\right) + 1$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="96" height="43" />. After multiplying the side length by 3 and rationalizing, you get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/4/5449d48993b0316a29237b1e741e74b65c1d29c6.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) }3 + 2\sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="112" height="33" />.
</p>
