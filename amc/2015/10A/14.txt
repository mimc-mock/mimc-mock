<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The circumference of the clock is twice that of the disk. So, a quarter way around the clock (3:00), the point halfway around the disk will be tangent. The arrow will point to the left. We can see the disk made a 75% rotation from 12 to 3, and 3 is 75% of 4, so it would make 100% rotation from 12 to 4. The answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/7/dc79a4029bc276aa6f6c2c9b994ff6d42778718e.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) }4 \ \text{o&#39; clock}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="116" height="28" />. Similarly, the arrow would be pointing downward at 6:00. It would already have completed three 180 degree turns. Therefore, two 180 degree turns would be completed at 4:00.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>The rotation factor of the arrow is the sum of the rates of the regular rotation of the arrow (360° every 360° rotation = 1) and the rotation of the disk around the clock with twice the circumference (360° every 180° = 2). Thus, the rotation factor of the arrow is 3, and so our answer corresponds to 360°/3 = 120°, which is 4 o' clock. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/7/dc79a4029bc276aa6f6c2c9b994ff6d42778718e.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) }4 \ \text{o&#39; clock}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="116" height="28" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>The arrow travels a path of radius 30 (20 from the interior clock and 10 from the radius of the disk itself). We note that 1 complete rotation of 360 degrees is needed for the arrow to appear up again, so, therefore, the disk must travel its circumference before the arrow goes up. Its circumference is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/f/9cf5d779e3740a6916b73852f858ab4a72c82e92.png" class="latex" alt="$20\pi$" width="28" height="12" />, so that is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/f/9cf5d779e3740a6916b73852f858ab4a72c82e92.png" class="latex" alt="$20\pi$" width="28" height="12" /> traveled on a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/3/853d147eb41b0b558ab393d6095d6f1d2fe2ebc5.png" class="latex" alt="$60\pi$" width="28" height="12" /> arrow path. This is a ratio of 1/3, so the angle it carves is 120 degrees, which leads us to the correct answer of  4 o' clock. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/7/dc79a4029bc276aa6f6c2c9b994ff6d42778718e.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) }4 \ \text{o&#39; clock}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="116" height="28" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>

<p>We unfold the big clock and draw the small clock. We know that the small clocks arrow will be facing up again at 6 o clock on the unfolded big clock. We now draw corresponding arrows at each hour and fold it back again.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_6">Solution 6</span></h2>
<p>We can approach this problem with angle measures. As the circumference of the disk is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/9/4e98c51e58fe2f353a6811264d4cac079b7ad5bf.png" class="latex" alt="$10\pi,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="33" height="16" /> and the clock <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/8/fe8f3fb7f6b71d4ade5323708fad26880cb0034b.png" class="latex" alt="$20 \pi,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="33" height="16" /> we have that 30 degrees, or the angular measure between hours, of the disk is only 15 degrees of the clock. This yields that every two hour ticks that the clock rotates, on the third one, the ticks will meet. However, the disk must rotate 360 degrees in order to come back to its original position, so the angular measure that the disk has covered relative to the clock is simply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/4/6047f3fd6644adbdf5c4059ef2402ba64280bbb3.png" class="latex" alt="$12 \cdot 15 \cdot \frac{2}{3},$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="79" height="37" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/f/f3fd424e953ac76b50bf6103875acc049d21d3c2.png" class="latex" alt="$120^\circ$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="13" /> from the 12 starting point, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/7/dc79a4029bc276aa6f6c2c9b994ff6d42778718e.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) }4 \ \text{o&#39; clock}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="116" height="28" />.
</p><p>~ab2024

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_7">Solution 7</span></h2>
<p>If the big clock were a flat plane, then the smaller clock could travel <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/6/b869eced65278c414ded7b9ae21fd60c255b67f0.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{40\pi}{20\pi}=2$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="68" height="38" /> full revolutions.
</p><p>But we also need to account for rotation. If we mark a red dot on the bottom of the small clock/bottom of the arrow, and then drag it around the clock, the direction of the arrow would still change. After traveling around the big clock, the small clock would travel <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> full rotation.
</p><p>Considering these two movements, the small clock travels 3 full rotations around the big clock so the arrow is next pointing upwards at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/2/3c2fa57402e4b0b941e1132a26afed4c04083708.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{12 \text{ o&#39;clock}}{3}=\boxed{\textbf{(C) }4 \ \text{o&#39; clock}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="219" height="38" />.
</p><p>~BakedPotato66

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_8">Solution 8</span></h2>
<p>The center of rotation is in the center of the smaller circle, but extends to the center of the larger circle. That means the circumference of the circle in relation to the arrow is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/5/f8545123b36df7206dd273b0012b9f062f4f7b65.png" class="latex" alt="$60 \pi$" width="28" height="12" />. The other circle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/1/ad1b502ff80a0471cf3dd21e6fb7dd9bee7fad6c.png" class="latex" alt="$20 \pi$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="31" height="14" /> and so that is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/1/4e1965f8d70832ded66434c75a7bd52b75090af0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. So <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/5/235820ed89f9ed54954565f6f7e529e091874bde.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{12}{3} = 4$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="55" height="37" /> which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/7/dc79a4029bc276aa6f6c2c9b994ff6d42778718e.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) }4 \ \text{o&#39; clock}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="116" height="28" />.
