<p>Distinct points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> lie on a line, with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/e/8ee42ac86b004fcc822d941be77bddff14611d46.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=BC=CD=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="168" height="13" />. Points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> lie on a second line, parallel to the first, with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/7/a47e2091638649c63c5133fd16b46968879020b1.png" class="latex" alt="$EF=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="61" height="13" />. A triangle with positive area has three of the six points as its vertices. How many possible values are there for the area of the triangle?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/a/eda8c52b3f22156d30098c4a07f397f920f0bf63.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{(A) } 3 \qquad \text{(B) } 4 \qquad \text{(C) } 5 \qquad \text{(D) } 6 \qquad \text{(E) } 7$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="330" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Consider the classical formula for triangle area: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/9/669642065b537f251677134ade7a0a45e235dffc.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac 12 \cdot b \cdot h$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="57" height="37" />. 
Each of the triangles that we can make has exactly one side lying on one of the two parallel lines. If we pick this side to be the base, the height will always be the same - it will be the distance between the two lines.
</p><p>Hence each area is uniquely determined by the length of the base. And it can easily be seen, that the only possible base lengths are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />. Therefore there are only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/2/c9213ec13639db3b17d05ae9d87dfdc090c5a4ef.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{(A)3}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="48" height="29" /> possible values for the area.
</p><p>(To be more precise in the last step, the possible base lengths are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/e/c8e34334267c9dcff39eab33f1b83a44b5646062.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=BC=CD=EF=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="221" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/f/dafdeb67ed69bfb9af6d138517d62b2bc9427deb.png" class="latex" alt="$AC=BD=2$" width="115" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/8/6e867467073fae9550c977a21ee2cb19426c4f92.png" class="latex" alt="$AD=3$" width="62" height="13" />.)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>No matter what how we draw a triangle by selecting three non-linear points, its height will always remain the same. Therefore, we will only get different areas with different base-lengths. The possibilities are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> units for a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/5/ab5717420b6a232a4bacc1fe67661ab1a76b0169.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\text{(A) } 3}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="53" height="30" />.
</p><p>~MathFun1000
</p>
