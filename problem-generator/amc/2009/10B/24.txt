<p>The keystone arch is an ancient architectural feature. It is composed of congruent isosceles trapezoids fitted together along the non-parallel sides, as shown. The bottom sides of the two end trapezoids are horizontal. In an arch made with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> trapezoids, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> be the angle measure in degrees of the larger interior angle of the trapezoid. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />? 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/c/dec895c3ce8d2cb187b83306a03e47ef6abc8f75.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(4mm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt)); int i; real r=5, R=6;  path t=r*dir(0)--r*dir(20)--R*dir(20)--R*dir(0); for(i=0; i&lt;9; ++i) { draw(rotate(20*i)*t); } draw((-r,0)--(R+1,0)); draw((-R,0)--(-R-1,0)); [/asy]" width="268" height="115" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/b/b4b3480273496fadbc81016c1c5978a7f2450f48.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{(A) } 100 \qquad \text{(B) } 102 \qquad \text{(C) } 104 \qquad \text{(D) } 106 \qquad \text{(E) } 108$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="421" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Extend all the legs of the trapezoids. They will all intersect in the middle of the bottom side of the picture, forming the situation shown below.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/8/fd8cd92c472bdeba95f38b0309708383757b3551.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(6mm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt)); int i; real r=5, R=6;  path t=r*dir(0)--r*dir(20)--R*dir(20)--R*dir(0); for(i=0; i&lt;9; ++i) { draw(rotate(20*i)*t); } draw((-r,0)--(R+1,0)); draw((-R,0)--(-R-1,0));  for (int i=1; i&lt;9; ++i) draw( (0,0) -- (rotate(20*i)*(r,0)), dotted );  label(&quot;$X$&quot;,(0,0),S); label(&quot;$Y$&quot;,(R,0),SE); label(&quot;$Z$&quot;,rotate(20)*(R,0),ENE);  draw( arc( (0,0), (1.5,0), rotate(20)*(1.5,0) ) ); label(&quot;$20^\circ$&quot;, rotate(10)*(1.75,0), E ); [/asy]" width="398" height="192" />
</p><p>Each of the angles at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/3/d03421cfd2273a966d98f778465237ef3378211b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{180^\circ}9 = 20^\circ$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="87" height="37" />. From <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/3/3039f01ba9e41f5490b7130e344be554015a25b9.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle XYZ$" width="60" height="13" />, the degree measure of the smaller interior angle of the trapezoid is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/0/8e00302c1df47bfe4fcca5a9bd28ac899d90be48.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{180^\circ - 20^\circ}2 = 80^\circ$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="135" height="37" />, hence the degree measure of the larger interior angle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/9/8896e588f37d8ca2faac9f29ff5f6c2051a5512c.png" class="latex" alt="$180^\circ - 80^\circ = \boxed{100^\circ \Longrightarrow A}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="206" height="24" />.
</p><p>Proof that all the extended trapezoid legs intersect at the same point: It is sufficient to prove this for any pair of neighboring trapezoids. For two neighboring trapezoids, the situation is symmetric according to their common leg, therefore the extensions of both outside legs intersect the extension of the common leg at the same point, Q.E.D.
</p><p>Knowing this, we can now easily see that the intersection point must be on the bottom side of our picture, as it lies on the bottom leg of the rightmost trapezoid. And by symmetry the point must be in the center of this side.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>A decagon can be formed from the trapezoids and the base. The sum of the decagon's angles is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/d/35d7a73a9df398a966b792ad458b3a013e8ee6af.png" class="latex" alt="$180(10-2)=1440^\circ$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="157" height="18" />. Letting the larger angle in each trapezoid be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />, the two angles formed by the line each measures <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/9/4e902510dbcdccbf0d866093439244571b6ca910.png" class="latex" alt="$(180-x)^\circ$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="80" height="18" />. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> congruent angles left. Each of those angles measures <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/1/7f1978a1bad0a2d20ac27755ff0af3ee11cda98a.png" class="latex" alt="$(360-2x)^\circ$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="89" height="18" />.  Putting it all together: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/f/f9ffad168a18e251ec5af8ec18e82df120b30a6c.png" class="latex" alt="$8(360-2x)+2(180-x)=1440\implies x=\boxed{\mathrm{(A)\ }100^\circ}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="422" height="29" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>If we reflect the arch across the line, we form an 18-gon. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/1/95176df4f25afe29e71949311a5319c60105445e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{180*(18-2)}{18} = 160^\circ$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="169" height="39" /> so each interior angle of the 18-gon is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/b/63be0ca7bec77e7bfa985d3b42861959609e23f4.png" class="latex" alt="$160^\circ$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="13" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> be the degree measure of the larger interior angle of a trapezoid. From the diagram, we see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/4/404ff5678c02526a4180373ad452018421e11040.png" class="latex" alt="$2x + 160 = 360$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="119" height="14" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/1/6a1d42035a5da86f1a517feb1ae796490220daf4.png" class="latex" alt="$2x = 200$" width="70" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/b/d6bb5e1c9a17bddebd0a3a1dff2cddca20768d13.png" class="latex" alt="$x = 100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="61" height="13" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/e/f6eb65962292d994ce2f9a8af63728883f256b18.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{A}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="25" height="24" />.
</p>
