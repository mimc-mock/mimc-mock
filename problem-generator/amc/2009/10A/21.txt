<p>Many Gothic cathedrals have windows with portions containing a ring of congruent circles that are circumscribed by a larger circle, In the figure shown, the number of smaller circles is four. What is the ratio of the sum of the areas of the four smaller circles to the area of the larger circle? 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/c/a1c9cf43e5cccc1df5df071a00ccfbc0434fb45e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(6mm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt));  draw(Circle((0,0),1+sqrt(2))); draw(Circle((sqrt(2),0),1)); draw(Circle((0,sqrt(2)),1)); draw(Circle((-sqrt(2),0),1)); draw(Circle((0,-sqrt(2)),1)); [/asy]" width="138" height="138" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/a/dbaeda9ef2d3f46fb63d792a4e3f94d0eb73e82b.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ 3-2\sqrt2 \qquad \mathrm{(B)}\ 2-\sqrt2 \qquad \mathrm{(C)}\ 4(3-2\sqrt2) \qquad \mathrm{(D)}\ \frac12(3-\sqrt2) \qquad \mathrm{(E)}\ 2\sqrt2-2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="652" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Draw some of the radii of the small circles as in the picture below.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/1/941a0066171cfafbcecb4ec6ce35e0f2fc15db0b.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(12mm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt));  draw(Circle((0,0),1+sqrt(2))); draw(Circle((sqrt(2),0),1)); draw(Circle((0,sqrt(2)),1)); draw(Circle((-sqrt(2),0),1)); draw(Circle((0,-sqrt(2)),1)); draw( (sqrt(2),0) -- (0,sqrt(2)) -- (-sqrt(2),0) -- (0,-sqrt(2)) -- cycle ); draw( (0,sqrt(2)) -- (0,1+sqrt(2)) ); draw( (0,-sqrt(2)) -- (0,-1-sqrt(2)) ); draw( (0,sqrt(2)) -- (0,-sqrt(2)), dashed ); [/asy]" width="278" height="278" />
</p><p>Out of symmetry, the quadrilateral in the center must be a square. Its side is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/5/0a5745df50c8607bdc9b8de4968315633a3047dc.png" class="latex" alt="$2r$" width="17" height="12" />, and therefore its diagonal is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e21412832d95377643b75c0237ccc9f9828c944.png" class="latex" alt="$2r\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="42" height="18" />. We can now compute the length of the vertical diameter of the large circle as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/4/d04206d065e79ee3f05c717db625ee0a56cf4e42.png" class="latex" alt="$2r + 2r\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="82" height="18" />. Hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/f/58ffa8ac5bdc10ea774c55da723776a775c23a3e.png" class="latex" alt="$2R=2r + 2r\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="129" height="18" />, and thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/0/a80522710738811f1ab077d2ef176023399d4bf5.png" class="latex" alt="$R=r+r\sqrt{2}=r(1+\sqrt{2})$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="203" height="21" />.
</p><p>Then the area of the large circle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/c/63ceb9ebfe0600252b064143b9e11a1fa42981a1.png" class="latex" alt="$L = \pi R^2 = \pi r^2 (1+\sqrt 2)^2 = \pi r^2 (3+2\sqrt 2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="326" height="21" />.
The area of four small circles is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/a/29ab15020e6c60c817026fccec6d0354e587181e.png" class="latex" alt="$S = 4\pi r^2$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="71" height="15" />. Hence their ratio is:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/1/591c9365dd35ebdb87ba9f4566b29c7991b11173.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} \frac SL  &amp; = \frac{4\pi r^2}{\pi r^2 (3+2\sqrt 2)} \\ &amp; = \frac 4{3+2\sqrt 2} \\ &amp; = \frac 4{3+2\sqrt 2} \cdot \frac{3-2\sqrt 2}{3 - 2\sqrt 2} \\ &amp; = \frac{4(3 - 2\sqrt 2)}{3^2 - (2\sqrt 2)^2} \\ &amp; = \frac{4(3 - 2\sqrt 2)}1 \\ &amp; = \boxed{4(3 - 2\sqrt 2)} \end{align*}" width="187" height="287" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Draw the center of the large circle, and connect it with two adjacent centers of the smaller circles. Draw the line between the centers. Then, let the line connecting the center of the smaller circle to the center of the larger circle be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/3/fc3be04db73828946870d3ccc6d122395a4bacf6.png" class="latex" alt="$x.$" width="14" height="8" />
</p><p>By the Pythagorean Theorem, we have that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/f/93f6e39a56aaf2398e0706a74abb169d7515bc98.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x^2 + x^2 = (1+1)^2\]" width="142" height="20" />
</p><p>Simplifying, we have 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/6/a1642bc6ea36b925ab17178875b8bb22f1efa8e9.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} x &amp;= \sqrt{\frac{(1+1)^2}{2}} \\ &amp;= \sqrt{\frac{4}{2}} \\ &amp;= \sqrt{2} \end{align*}" width="117" height="118" />
</p><p>Thus, the radius is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/2/4a27d93e5c976d5687421abf367f03213611fe83.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[1 + x = 1 + \sqrt{2}\]" width="120" height="18" />
</p><p>The answer we desire is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/f/a9f49c7ac2e42402772cde85a60b9fcc5e446255.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{4 \cdot \left(\pi \cdot 1^2 \right)}{\pi \cdot (1+\sqrt{2})^2}\]" width="101" height="46" />
</p><p>Simplifying, we have that 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/9/80937231d13aae3bf0f6442e5e0043762950b9b7.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} \frac{4 \cdot \left(\pi \cdot 1^2 \right)}{\pi \cdot (1+\sqrt{2})^2} &amp;= \frac{4 \cdot \pi}{\pi \cdot (1 + \sqrt{2})^2} \\ &amp;= \frac{\pi \cdot 4}{\pi \cdot (1+\sqrt{2})^2} \\ &amp;= \frac{4}{(1+\sqrt{2})^2} \\ &amp;= \frac{4}{1^2 + 2 \cdot 1 \cdot \sqrt{2} + (\sqrt{2})^2} \\ &amp;= \frac{4}{1 + 2 \sqrt{2} + 2} \\ &amp;= \frac{4}{3 + 2 \sqrt{2}} \\ &amp;= \frac{4 (3 - 2\sqrt{2})}{(3 + 2 \sqrt{2})(3 - 2\sqrt{2})} \\ &amp;= \frac{4 (3 - 2\sqrt{2})}{3^2 - (2\sqrt{2})^2} \\ &amp;= \frac{4 (3 - 2\sqrt{2})}{9 - 8} \\ &amp;= \frac{4 (3 - 2\sqrt{2})}{1} \\ &amp;= \boxed{\textbf{(C) } 4 (3 - 2\sqrt{2})} \end{align*}" width="305" height="535" />
</p><p>~mathboy282
</p>
