<p>Three distinct vertices of a cube are chosen at random. What is the probability that the plane determined by these three vertices contains points inside the cube?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/6/d96cf89630705289bfceb5d7b36aae407ed2ae41.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ \frac{1}{4} \qquad \mathrm{(B)}\ \frac{3}{8} \qquad \mathrm{(C)}\ \frac{4}{7} \qquad \mathrm{(D)}\ \frac{5}{7} \qquad \mathrm{(E)}\ \frac{3}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="361" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions">Solutions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>First of all, number of planes determined by any three vertices of a cube is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/3/f8366cd6196dd8a9da6d38a3e9eafb109e99d53e.png" class="latex" alt="$20$" width="17" height="12" /> (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> surface, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> opposing parallel edges, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> points cut by three remote vertices). Among these <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/3/f8366cd6196dd8a9da6d38a3e9eafb109e99d53e.png" class="latex" alt="$20$" width="17" height="12" /> planes only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> surfaces will not cut into the cube.
Secondly, to choose three vertices randomly, the four vertices planes each will be chosen <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> times, while the three vertices planes each will be chosen once.
To conclude, the probability of a cutting in plane is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/9/d29e9433ac128d738c3b8a97a2b7a1f7fb0f8413.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {(6\cdot 4+8\cdot 1)}{(12\cdot 4+8\cdot 1)} = \frac {32}{56}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="160" height="45" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/6/0f67216cdaafcae2d4c5189d48ddac2da2b677ad.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed {\textbf{(C)}}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="42" height="30" />.
</p><p>-Vader10,Oct.6 2020-
</p><p>Minor LaTeX edit by Arcticturn
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>We will try to use symmetry as much as possible.
</p><p>Pick the first vertex <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, its choice clearly does not influence anything.
</p><p>Pick the second vertex <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />. With probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/6014d4569997566bef1041782ba39547ace0301a.png" class="latex" alt="$3/7$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="27" height="18" /> vertices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> have a common edge, with probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/6014d4569997566bef1041782ba39547ace0301a.png" class="latex" alt="$3/7$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="27" height="18" /> they are in opposite corners of the same face, and with probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/f/b3f4e9ccf77d551cda645e6a7d3c68c27dc1aea7.png" class="latex" alt="$1/7$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="27" height="18" /> they are in opposite corners of the cube. We will handle each of the cases separately.
</p><p>In the first case, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> faces that contain the edge <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" />. In each of these faces there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> other vertices. If one of these <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> vertices is the third vertex <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />, the entire triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> will be on a face. On the other hand, if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> is one of the two remaining vertices, the triangle will contain points inside the cube. Hence in this case the probability of choosing a good <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/a/63a49aeac29fbaf7cea3185325b0d752b08c5cc1.png" class="latex" alt="$2/6 = 1/3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="78" height="18" />.
</p><p>In the second case, the triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> will not intersect the cube if point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> is one of the two points on the side that contains <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" />. Hence the probability of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> intersecting the inside of the cube is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/b/38bd36e353df831303c0de895a9ec614cf3e7202.png" class="latex" alt="$2/3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" />.
</p><p>In the third case, already the diagonal <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> contains points inside the cube, hence this case will be good regardless of the choice of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />.
</p><p>Summing up all cases, the resulting probability is:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/3/4b390fbd82b19144e4b78ab84832109a96ca7bb0.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac 37\cdot\frac 13 + \frac 37\cdot \frac 23 + \frac 17\cdot 1 = \boxed{\frac 47}\]" width="205" height="48" />
</p>
<h3><span id="Solution_3_(Cheap_solution,_same_approach_as_Solution_2)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3_.28Cheap_solution.2C_same_approach_as_Solution_2.29">Solution 3 (Cheap solution, same approach as Solution 2)</span></h3>
<p>This problem can be approached the same way, by picking vertices, but with a much faster and kind of cheap solution: Pick any vertex A and a face it touches. For vertex B, out of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" /> remaining vertices, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> of them aren't on the same face as the one chosen for vertex A, so vertex C can be placed anywhere and the plane will no matter what be in the cube. Therefore, the probability of choosing a valid vertex B is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/b/07b007731c8aa702e6c3bdbb06ec8e415ab7dc92.png" class="latex" alt="$4/7$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="27" height="18" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h3>
<p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/0/bd03425ac1672d23cc4a74638d4498a5d6410a85.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{8}{3}=56$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="79" height="53" /> ways to pick three vertices from eight total vertices; this is our denominator. In order to have three points inside the cube, they cannot be on the surface. Thus, we can use complementary probability.
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/5/6e5cd3ae46a937d51bc177caf31a4147709ce168.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{4}{3}=4$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="70" height="53" /> ways to choose three points from the vertices of a single face. Since there are six faces, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/e/96ee0bd227e61a497248ead0ce1f35642f69061d.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \times 6 = 24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="82" height="13" />.
</p><p>Thus, the probability of what we don't want is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/0/f40843118cae7dcbf5d68637cdfc23792e410b3c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{24}{56} = \frac{3}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="58" height="37" />. Using complementary probability, 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/6/286a9362a3af3eeb6be682a1195a7352922c7174.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[1- \frac 37 = \boxed{\frac 47}\]" width="92" height="48" />
</p>
<h3><span id="Solution_5_(Casework)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5_.28Casework.29">Solution 5 (Casework)</span></h3>
<p>This problem is fairly simple. Start with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> points WLOG. 
</p><p>Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />: You pick 2 points that are diagonally across from each other but still on the same face. This happens with probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/e/b7ed81e4a72a137a0ebb143a06424e3998de0e75.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {3}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. We notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> out of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> possible outcomes include a point inside the cube. Therefore, the probability of this happening is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/c/49c1dbf42c67e0a2006b490b177c51279bff5035.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {2}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />.
</p><p>Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />: You pick 2 points that a directly across from each other. This happens with probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/e/b7ed81e4a72a137a0ebb143a06424e3998de0e75.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {3}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. We notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> out of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> possible outcomes include a point inside the cube. Therefore, the probability of this happening is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/4/344227d860e5273831bced0b2f048204df91b559.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {1}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="15" height="38" />.
</p><p>Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />: You pick 2 points that is a space diagonal of the cube. This happens with probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/4/344227d860e5273831bced0b2f048204df91b559.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {1}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="15" height="38" />. Clearly, all of the points contain a point inside the cube, so our probability is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/4/344227d860e5273831bced0b2f048204df91b559.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {1}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="15" height="38" />. 
</p><p>Adding these probabilities gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/7/837dbe5bb4df6a5f19b9b75a6d7c461146314031.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed {\frac {4}{7}}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="26" height="49" />
</p><p>~Arcticturn
</p>
