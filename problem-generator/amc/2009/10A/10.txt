<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>It is a well-known fact that in any right triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> with the right angle at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> the foot of the altitude from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> onto <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/7/a179ff2638e4799cadd820db205c2beff6299ce9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC$" width="27" height="13" /> we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d2d19c5505eb7d4b3aab96dc698513fc7b272d.png" class="latex" alt="$BD^2 = AD\cdot CD$" width="133" height="15" />. (See below for a proof.) Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/7/627521f6bdf67881bac53de7248cfdfcc426a1a4.png" class="latex" alt="$BD = \sqrt{ 3\cdot 4 } = 2\sqrt 3$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="159" height="18" />, and the area of the triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/4/304a28b6d9fb08e18d9be194210ac216103b631a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AC\cdot BD}2 = 7\sqrt3\Rightarrow\boxed{\text{(B)}}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="197" height="38" />.
</p><p><i>Proof</i>: Consider the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Pythagorean_theorem" class="mw-redirect" title="Pythagorean theorem">Pythagorean theorem</a> for each of the triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/a/a6adfd3ad27a2dddeb0af7131d73c33c86991e93.png" class="latex" alt="$ABD$" width="43" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/d/87da4992deed43fafdcdf6879ffb693fd6414995.png" class="latex" alt="$CBD$" width="44" height="12" />. We get:
</p>
<ol><li><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/4/bc494b1a58fcb99daf899fbc33970323ace95c40.png" class="latex" alt="$AB^2 + BC^2 = AC^2 = (AD+DC)^2 = AD^2 + DC^2 + 2 \cdot AD \cdot DC$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="518" height="19" />.</li>
<li><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/b/9fb0cda767c629c57b8249bde890e88f19a4fb5e.png" class="latex" alt="$AB^2 = AD^2 + BD^2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="155" height="16" /></li>
<li><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/c/65c3dcc2ac96bf87b9feac4826d6e671714f7f41.png" class="latex" alt="$BC^2 = BD^2 + CD^2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="156" height="16" /></li></ol>
<p>Substituting equations 2 and 3 into the left hand side of equation 1, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/d/7dd83dfa272752c4cb79f45a8fcbbd2b47ffef8f.png" class="latex" alt="$BD^2 =  AD \cdot DC$" width="134" height="15" />. 
</p><p>Alternatively, note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/a/5ea76aedc9f58e12b5819eeb9bb8311fff027487.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD \sim \triangle BCD \Longrightarrow \frac{AD}{BD} = \frac{BD}{CD}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="275" height="38" />.
</p>
