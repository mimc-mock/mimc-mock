<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Consider another sequence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/8/1f8bd6d6bf4cdf5413cfa3ad81bf986890dc91c5.png" class="latex" alt="$\{\theta_1, \theta_2, \theta_3...\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="96" height="18" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/f/fbf17f1628983567847ae1b8be137aad8c629a57.png" class="latex" alt="$a_n = \tan{\theta_n}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="88" height="15" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/a/4fa7c6e31b237e595a11f970a74ba297e5700e3e.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \leq \theta_n &lt; 180$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="101" height="15" />.
</p><p>The given recurrence becomes
</p>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/a/dba24d7ce865f07f6f06ea651e5b966734f2ea11.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} a_{n + 2} &amp; = \frac {a_n + a_{n + 1}}{1 - a_na_{n + 1}} \\ \tan{\theta_{n + 2}} &amp; = \frac {\tan{\theta_n} + \tan{\theta_{n + 1}}}{1 - \tan{\theta_n}\tan{\theta_{n + 1}}} \\ \tan{\theta_{n + 2}} &amp; = \tan(\theta_{n + 1} + \theta_n) \end{align*}" width="235" height="112" /></center>
<p>It follows that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/6/5e69edcab455a39685d62ad83e2f27e95ba5bd29.png" class="latex" alt="$\theta_{n + 2} \equiv \theta_{n + 1} + \theta_{n} \pmod{180}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="221" height="18" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/0/0909483e88d45c383415019dd949d928caab3654.png" class="latex" alt="$\theta_1 = 45, \theta_2 = 30$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="124" height="16" />, all terms in the sequence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/8/1f8bd6d6bf4cdf5413cfa3ad81bf986890dc91c5.png" class="latex" alt="$\{\theta_1, \theta_2, \theta_3...\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="96" height="18" /> will be a multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/9/f1965fae079a9ba2c0726c307070c2355dfcb213.png" class="latex" alt="$15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />.
</p><p>Now consider another sequence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/1/801225427c0eac417f28188192609a2bc4e9a135.png" class="latex" alt="$\{b_1, b_2, b_3...\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="94" height="18" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/2/c5262d42f58fa090c2412c8fc81fca09ce7bdb36.png" class="latex" alt="$b_n = \theta_n/15$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="85" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/0/960a3499acc4e69994c72f46529dff40d5ffc63b.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \leq b_n &lt; 12$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="92" height="15" />. The sequence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/f/8ffd01cdc48042ce7c67d8edcd4904809831cfe4.png" class="latex" alt="$b_n$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="16" height="15" /> satisfies <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/2/5928db5619836f868ad75dc2badd1adf7b776b00.png" class="latex" alt="$b_1 = 3, b_2 = 2, b_{n + 2} \equiv b_{n + 1} + b_n \pmod{12}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="323" height="18" />.
</p><p>As the number of possible consecutive two terms is finite, we know that the sequence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/f/8ffd01cdc48042ce7c67d8edcd4904809831cfe4.png" class="latex" alt="$b_n$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="16" height="15" /> is periodic. Write out the first few terms of the sequence until it starts to repeat.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/1/43182043e3e668c705132dac35f2d11a46004a22.png" class="latex" alt="$\{b_n\} = \{3,2,5,7,0,7,7,2,9,11,8,7,3,10,1,11,0,11,11,10,9,7,4,11,3,2,5,7,...\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="636" height="18" />
</p><p>Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/5/45512e08c5e21b91fbea56ea052e1f1ee628d182.png" class="latex" alt="$b_{25} = b_1 = 3$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="94" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/f/d0f218462e1093f94fc7e210e16700e4b0bf70f0.png" class="latex" alt="$b_{26} = b_2 = 2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="94" height="15" />. Thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/6/646c0524996280f8ceebcf176c24cfed07dc3935.png" class="latex" alt="$\{b_n\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" /> has a period of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/9/e290f2cba0ba0e11c0b91630da15a408da2fcf1c.png" class="latex" alt="$24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" />: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/3/7c31a9d4814393428c088ecaf86a3bc14d44dee0.png" class="latex" alt="$b_{n + 24} = b_n$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="80" height="16" />.
</p><p>It follows that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/1/3b1ec80c0f46f795e8fb2858c2aca281c09eeb47.png" class="latex" alt="$b_{2009} = b_{17} = 0$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="114" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/2/f62c7fd3bafd39d59dc362eeb2c11bf744c74a4b.png" class="latex" alt="$\theta_{2009} = 15 b_{2009} = 0$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="146" height="15" />. Thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/1/8018f34b4ab0b77c0d63842c3a11bb1ed1f1aa16.png" class="latex" alt="$a_{2009} = \tan{\theta_{2009}} = \tan{0} = 0.$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="225" height="15" />
</p><p>Our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/5/165a4cf084bf3c76a0ba442e91ff8d47e252652a.png" class="latex" alt="$|a_{2009}| = \boxed{\textbf{(A)}\ 0}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="127" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>First, some intuition. The given recurrence relation: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/f/a7fa26a9edd8dda91971491a82434efed4b8fe29.png" class="latex" alt="$a_{n + 2} = \frac {a_n + a_{n + 1}}{1 - a_na_{n + 1}}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="147" height="39" /> looks much like the tangent addition formula. So, we let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/6/95678e74b967599e696fa36d5c3d403a8ac1393d.png" class="latex" alt="$a_n=\tan{\theta_n}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="88" height="15" />. Then, we get:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/7/137e588aafc22e64141bfba03305eb72c16f351c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\tan{\theta_n}=\tan{(\theta_{n-1}+\theta_{n-2})}\]" width="204" height="18" />
This gives us:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/6/d06184d6facf706103e974a3ea5d1b2c323052bf.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\theta_n \equiv \theta_{n-1}+\theta_{n-2} \pmod{180}\]" width="231" height="18" />
Now, observe that 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/c/40c810722a009b877f1dff6f2526adb6974173c7.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\theta_1=45\]" width="57" height="16" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/9/ec9eee654b87dc2a42ccdd2824f5ce604262d3e8.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\theta_2=30\]" width="57" height="16" />
We know that this sequence of values for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/7/9f721fffde8982c65baf9862f0c755887099e238.png" class="latex" alt="$\theta_n$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="16" height="15" /> will repeat eventually because it is mod <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/b/5cb1b41850ab676e4d1cf15c69b1609af4befbc5.png" class="latex" alt="$180$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />.
It is just a matter of when, so we start bashing:

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clarification">Clarification</span></h2>
<p>(While the above solution is quite intuitive, it's kinda hard for people who don't know many proofing symbols to understand.)
</p><p><br />
Notice that the formula given in the problem looks incredibly similar to the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Trigonometric_identities#Angle_Addition.2FSubtraction_Identities" title="Trigonometric identities">Tangent Addition Formula</a>.
Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/1/b1171ce641826e9aca894162f9c15a57f6b46e09.png" class="latex" alt="$a_1 = 1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="49" height="14" />, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/b/1ab39d761413804680d26d972381f028001562f5.png" class="latex" alt="$a_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="10" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/b/cdbe09f5486f14717cd767feefbf6e7ede611ee6.png" class="latex" alt="$\tan{45}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="47" height="13" />. Similarly, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/9/5e97a8af68fbc8e357d3ee0eba452022b06c1875.png" class="latex" alt="$a_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="10" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/0/c8069cb18c6e389991bf08777b41da7f948146bf.png" class="latex" alt="$\tan{30}$" width="47" height="12" />. Then the formula for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/2/572b65ebc3f438b176b4ca4a890799f2f72564af.png" class="latex" alt="$a_3$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="10" /> reads
</p><p><br />
</p>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/3/613b85153dc8a3aac4677a85d7ebbd9dcc1cf88a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[a_3 = \frac {\tan{45} + \tan{30} }{1 - \tan{45}*\tan{30}}.\]" width="192" height="38" /></center>
<p><br />
But from the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Trigonometric_identities#Angle_Addition.2FSubtraction_Identities" title="Trigonometric identities">Tangent Addition Formula</a> we know that this is just the formula for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/e/35e3aee112087cce579537c3d590d7a360ebc649.png" class="latex" alt="$\tan{(45+30)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="100" height="18" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/e/dae664c0653c427f008970a6b5a4dc6f7313765e.png" class="latex" alt="$\tan{75}$" width="47" height="12" />, meaning <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/9/ae95ae77c967807867e0c29f8577821b4ce16c40.png" class="latex" alt="$a_3 = \tan{75}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="88" height="15" />. So, the sequence becomes a sort of <a href="/wiki/index.php/Fibonacci_sequence" title="Fibonacci sequence">Fibonacci sequence</a>  with angle measures. We continue to sum angle measures, like so:
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/7/667b2355502d88798515036f20a7b7644d1d84fa.png" class="latex" alt="$a_4 = \tan{(75+30)} = \tan{105}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="223" height="18" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/b/a2bd754d08db279f77d61d919354de85d56c9016.png" class="latex" alt="$a_5 = \tan{(105+75)} = \tan{180}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="232" height="18" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/7/ab7dcd09c503704124d05951bf76b5232cc2e742.png" class="latex" alt="$a_6 = \tan{(180+105)} =$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="180" height="18" /> ... wait a minute! <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/5/1e59c06064c1adc0f5c5ce1fe636a4f3d2f624be.png" class="latex" alt="$\tan{180} = \tan{0}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="118" height="13" />!
</p><p><br />
So now we have
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/2/2c25fb256d40e55ed63fb3fcb38bd98cb16592c8.png" class="latex" alt="$a_5 = \tan{0}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="79" height="15" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/6/956dab15ed01a66c76491a44a1f5d6bcceef13fa.png" class="latex" alt="$a_6 = \tan{(105+0)} = \tan{105}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="223" height="18" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/7/ed7ba8750a230c4132518183eef91fa08a13a846.png" class="latex" alt="$a_7 = \tan{(105+105)} = \tan{210}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="241" height="18" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/7/f27ea8dba35eabe1ea941917c778f8c3188ac660.png" class="latex" alt="$a_8 = \tan{(210+105)} =$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="180" height="18" /> .... Ok this is getting tiring. Let's stop and think about whether we can simplify this. Notice that all of the angle measures are a multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/9/f1965fae079a9ba2c0726c307070c2355dfcb213.png" class="latex" alt="$15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. So, let's express the angle measures as multiples of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/9/f1965fae079a9ba2c0726c307070c2355dfcb213.png" class="latex" alt="$15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />.
</p><p><br />
Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/b/30b948768aa2e8e8ef1319a7632a44cbbe3d7b39.png" class="latex" alt="$b_n = \frac{\arctan{(a_n)}}{15}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="129" height="39" />.
</p><p><br />
(Basically, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/f/8ffd01cdc48042ce7c67d8edcd4904809831cfe4.png" class="latex" alt="$b_n$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="16" height="15" /> is the angle measure of the corresponding <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/3/cb3bfec0678c4469260d15f649d94088bffca95b.png" class="latex" alt="$a_n,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="22" height="11" /> divided by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/9/f1965fae079a9ba2c0726c307070c2355dfcb213.png" class="latex" alt="$15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />)
</p><p>Now we have
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/0/320cd1ba1cbf20e8c03878bcebc24c4b50870f02.png" class="latex" alt="$b_1 = 3$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="48" height="15" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/b/85b0a2ba0313bce65af0e4add1b8375e1e757545.png" class="latex" alt="$b_2 = 2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="48" height="15" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/e/5de999e9fb2045b88eab0a5f3e3b7ff0698c2339.png" class="latex" alt="$b_3 = 5$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="48" height="15" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/f/15f12b0df9ba9b2455556cab696e757f723212d0.png" class="latex" alt="$b_4 = 7$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="48" height="15" />
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/8/10859cd3b120069f13009366da84a9347be835b2.png" class="latex" alt="$b_5 = 12$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="57" height="15" />
</p><p><br />
But wait... we're dealing with the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/b/75b04387daddfdad2ef55b5a9dfec552ea41db50.png" class="latex" alt="$\tan$" width="26" height="11" /> function, which has a period (recurrence rate) of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/9/709acef3551b886e6754de72d59a88f00660f0d3.png" class="latex" alt="$2\pi$" width="19" height="12" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/0/3f0cd95f95238159d02dbcea0aca632c2928991c.png" class="latex" alt="$180^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="13" />. Since we divided the angle measures by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/9/f1965fae079a9ba2c0726c307070c2355dfcb213.png" class="latex" alt="$15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />, the period is now <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> (which aligns with what we got earlier: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/3/4b3418e1194e237d9e6a4b0b5b991cbafbd52608.png" class="latex" alt="$a_5 = 0$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="50" height="15" />). This means that we can reduce the terms of the sequence based on the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/e/fee9189c7228dbcb16db6594c1a4d90e358a2103.png" class="latex" alt="$\bmod$" width="36" height="12" /> function, which returns the remainder after dividing by a certain amount. So now we can say <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/e/bbe3425580ffb1d9ecc0cd4f2a270eb5eb3f559a.png" class="latex" alt="$b_n \equiv b_n \bmod{12}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="120" height="15" /> (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/6/1165e91155febc4bc88785c5504eb52efe4f8c51.png" class="latex" alt="$\equiv$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="13" height="8" /> denotes modular equivalence in this context). Now we continue our sequence:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/2/ef2bf99f692e31ff42f803a6f0e72a38a402bc7c.png" class="latex" alt="$3, 2, 5, 7, 0, 7, 7, 2, 9, 11, 8, 7, 3, 10, 1, 11, 0, 11, 11, 10, 9, 7, 4, 11, 3, 2, 5$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="518" height="16" /> (It's starting to repeat now)
</p><p>So <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/d/c8d58ba63442c7d42b624ef6fddc38c3cd9d0606.png" class="latex" alt="$a_1  = 3, a_{25} = 3, a_{49} = 3$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="180" height="16" />, and so on. The sequence repeats every <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/9/e290f2cba0ba0e11c0b91630da15a408da2fcf1c.png" class="latex" alt="$24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> terms. The problem asks us for the value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/d/3ede96fb56e2c1cb0ffd5ae4e4d22972234e437a.png" class="latex" alt="$a_{2009}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="35" height="10" />. Let's whip out the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/e/fee9189c7228dbcb16db6594c1a4d90e358a2103.png" class="latex" alt="$\bmod$" width="36" height="12" /> function again.
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/6/87600a1d9c5a6609b837e0b81fb15300528676e2.png" class="latex" alt="$2009 \bmod 24 = 17$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="141" height="13" />
</p><p><br />
So we want the value of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/6/b7616a230c77f0d9a577e5bca01aa06f1c35c457.png" class="latex" alt="$17$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" />th number in the sequence, and looking back at it (hopefully you wrote it down somewhere) we find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/0/890401890e6cd4ae610813ad55d63d74b0299a92.png" class="latex" alt="$b_{17} = 0 \Rightarrow \boxed{\text{A}}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="107" height="23" />.
</p>
