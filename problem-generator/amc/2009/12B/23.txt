<p>A region <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> in the complex plane is defined by
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/1/1f183c6f855fb40cc0df24c6d24b6a99b1218b0d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[S = \{x + iy: - 1\le x\le1, - 1\le y\le1\}.\]" width="310" height="18" />
A complex number <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/0/5600c4ebdb9f6c9949c1486075fd832e16b295f9.png" class="latex" alt="$z = x + iy$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="81" height="16" /> is chosen uniformly at random from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" />. What is the probability that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/f/bcfe040e6a7a03be317a8fd349c739cb4ef11687.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac34 + \frac34i\right)z$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="92" height="43" /> is also in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/d/84dfeae3dd14ea07db37e2c23c91f60f979aeba4.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac12\qquad \textbf{(B)}\ \frac23\qquad \textbf{(C)}\ \frac34\qquad \textbf{(D)}\ \frac79\qquad \textbf{(E)}\ \frac78$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="367" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>First, turn <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/c/28ce6d1af182c6d61a6ed0d18778091ee8b7c98a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac34 + \frac34i$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="54" height="38" /> into polar form as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/2/f92a27362061195f7f7e451a373201a97be5834e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{3\sqrt{2}}{4}e^{\frac{\pi}{4}i}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="61" height="41" />. Restated using geometric probabilities, we are trying to find the portion of a square enlarged by a factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/4/4a4d3f9edd3ad697793fd21fb98d36d41c8156af.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{3\sqrt{2}}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="35" height="41" /> and rotated <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/7/f27da4fca6f3fecb215974023aad210b70ec3857.png" class="latex" alt="$45$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> degrees that lies within the original square. This skips all the absolute values required before. Finish with the symmetry method stated above.
</p><p>-asdf334
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We multiply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/6/276cd53bcaec852f2960b6096541f32e837bee4a.png" class="latex" alt="$(\frac{3}{4}+\frac{3}{4}i)$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="67" height="38" /> to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/3/df372c2f4d1a3cec657afbf57fd48dd98c06d344.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(\frac{3}{4}x-\frac{3}{4}y)+(\frac{3}{4}xi+\frac{3}{4}yi).\]" width="202" height="38" /> Since we want to find the probability that this number is in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" />, we need the real and complex coefficients of this number to be less than or equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> or greater than or equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/3/09352e06cfa1651aa82e838b91724b60b068d41d.png" class="latex" alt="$-1.$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="12" /> This gives us the equations <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/5/8f5b87c094344b5099bc45d300a7fd2cf17cecda.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[-1\le \frac{3}{4}x-\frac{3}{4}y \le 1\]" width="146" height="38" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/1/6411ad90105848d1a572c97e7dde878d52cf6a73.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[-1\le \frac{3}{4}x+\frac{3}{4}y\le 1.\]" width="151" height="38" /> Now, we see that we can solve this by graphing. We can graph our barriers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/8/74806ecebe302af1759af46a3c250369711db172.png" class="latex" alt="$-1\le x\le 1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="90" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/3/fa37bb66f0361f790d18d2dade7d229e49d7648f.png" class="latex" alt="$-1\le y\le 1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="89" height="15" /> to form a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> square centered at the origin. Graphing our two equations gives us the four lines <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/a/a1a1ec652ccebc89ff0f37eeb22f8ca04e85c1ec.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x-y=\frac{4}{3},\]" width="83" height="37" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/5/69564b0d4bf7bda9ba0a3eb6493b00d71953ae28.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x-y=-\frac{4}{3},\]" width="97" height="37" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/d/38d4d3f24ea42d139ecdd6e2c486aa578aed9648.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x+y=\frac{4}{3},\]" width="83" height="37" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/b/8fbf7f5ef56ede769c975f5aa626e1df255f3067.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x+y=-\frac{4}{3}.\]" width="96" height="37" /> The square that is formed is the region that satisfies these four equations. Now, the barriers and this square gives us an octagon as the desired region. The area of this octagon is the total area of the square minus the 4 small triangles on each corner, each with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/1/041a0b37ca609642fb34c7c855b9d67ed498a560.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{9}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> area. Therefore, the octagon has area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/7/df7e93072bd8354b2727fc2448a0b2ce398143e0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{28}{9}.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="25" height="37" /> Finally, to find the probability of it working, we find the area of the octagon divided by the area of the entire square which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/3/2639a8a3e1d5ce76c8377fe39c05d1d329ccaf5e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\frac{28}{9}}{4}=\frac{7}{9}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="57" height="41" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/c/a2c09ebd455d05463a4f9f15c952df1ce7f6065d.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{D}.$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="30" height="24" />
</p><p>-jeteagle
</p>
