<p>A convex polyhedron <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> has vertices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/3/4e30c2d7dac2dcc50dca9fff6e71d80c372edb0a.png" class="latex" alt="$V_1,V_2,\ldots,V_n$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="103" height="16" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> edges. The polyhedron is cut by planes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/9/0b9824caec77ced15e97fb784e77ee597b609eed.png" class="latex" alt="$P_1,P_2,\ldots,P_n$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="106" height="16" /> in such a way that plane <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/e/76ebaba4ae3d5c911d6e31ad855d502ad88d48a6.png" class="latex" alt="$P_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> cuts only those edges that meet at vertex <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/b/40b514f3d50ab12888fa4886e266bb6a1e8a7e9f.png" class="latex" alt="$V_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="17" height="15" />. In addition, no two planes intersect inside or on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />. The cuts produce <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> pyramids and a new polyhedron <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" />. How many edges does <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> have?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/0/4d02f74bda7f885638bf6b39eb7e59eedb5cd4dc.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ 200\qquad \mathrm{(B)}\ 2n\qquad \mathrm{(C)}\ 300\qquad \mathrm{(D)}\ 400\qquad \mathrm{(E)}\ 4n$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="418" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions">Solutions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>Each edge of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> is cut by two planes, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/3/8f37626d49b4082c9102bf930bca51277ff2eb43.png" class="latex" alt="$200$" width="26" height="12" /> vertices. Three edges of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> meet at each vertex, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/2/0f2a0da7f1431ae8e38acdf2fde4fe7a8b85fd6d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac 12 \cdot 3 \cdot 200 = \boxed {300}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="139" height="37" /> edges.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>At each vertex, as many new edges are created by this process as there are original edges meeting at that vertex.  Thus the total number of new edges is the total number of endpoints of the original edges, which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/3/8f37626d49b4082c9102bf930bca51277ff2eb43.png" class="latex" alt="$200$" width="26" height="12" />.  A middle portion of each original edge is also present in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/f/a8f665312ca427956aaafb4a8717cee65639803e.png" class="latex" alt="$100 + 200 = \boxed {300}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="140" height="23" /> edges.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h3>
<p>Euler's Polyhedron Formula applied to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/7/c570fb8b08aaa1dcc57b158859438836ac8881eb.png" class="latex" alt="$n - 100 + F = 2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="129" height="14" />, where F is the number of faces of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />.  Each edge of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> is cut by two planes, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/3/8f37626d49b4082c9102bf930bca51277ff2eb43.png" class="latex" alt="$200$" width="26" height="12" /> vertices.  Each cut by a plane <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/e/76ebaba4ae3d5c911d6e31ad855d502ad88d48a6.png" class="latex" alt="$P_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> creates an additional face on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" />, so Euler's Polyhedron Formula applied to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/9/8994fe8b8d2ca65eb7f2a2bb5d2ad7dbec37da49.png" class="latex" alt="$200 - E + (F+n) = 2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="180" height="18" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> is the number of edges of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" />.  Subtracting the first equation from the second gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/1/f71bd77a004874d45c5c2486adf407e46dde676a.png" class="latex" alt="$300 - E = 0$" width="96" height="12" />, whence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/3/ba376c1fe5ae127cf985072c605b0baa8e2e488c.png" class="latex" alt="$E = \boxed {300}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="77" height="23" />.  The answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/8/2484f88154fadcf9c781bba19becdae88696b77f.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="25" height="18" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h3>
<p>Each edge connects two points. The plane cuts that edge so it splits into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> at each end (like two legs) for a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> new edges.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/2/c429923367bfcc2936a5777fa8fe3ffe3edc70ef.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pair A,B,C,D,E,F; A=(0,50); B=(0,-50); C=(86.602540378443864676372317075294,0); D=(300,0); E=(370.7,70.7); F=(370.7,-70.7); draw(A--C--B); draw(C--D); draw(E--D--F); [/asy]" width="252" height="98" />
</p><p>But because each new edge is shared by an adjacent original edge cut similarly, the additional edges are overcounted <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/6/e163633a9718e9fc13fbe29b05d3a4ade9130f99.png" class="latex" alt="$\times 2$" width="22" height="12" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/a/b/2ab30e019dba055eed52896031a0c8a82bb68ceb.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pair A,B,C,D,E,F,G,H,I,J,K; A=(0,50); B=(0,-50); C=(86.602540378443864676372317075294,0); D=(300,0); E=(370.7,70.7); F=(370.7,-70.7);  G=(-107.5,236.2); H=(-107.5,-236.2); I=(370.7,285); J=(370.7,-285); K=(441.4,0);  draw(A--C--B); draw(C--D); draw(E--D--F);  draw(G--A, red); draw(H--B, red); draw(I--E, red); draw(J--F, red);  draw(A--B, dashed); draw(E--K, dashed); draw(F--K, dashed);  [/asy]" width="242" height="252" />
</p><p>Since there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> edges to start with, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/5/6e5fccef45bad4430128e68751ae877652b9e23c.png" class="latex" alt="$400/2=200$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="96" height="18" /> new edges result. So there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/5/105320aded457a1aac1414bb4755aeae3a63cc9b.png" class="latex" alt="$100+200=\boxed{\textbf{(C) }300}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="175" height="28" /> edges in the figure.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5">Solution 5</span></h3>
<p>The question specifies the slices create as many pyramids as there are vertices, implying each vertex owns 4 edge ends. There are twice as many edge-ends as there are edges, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/e/6ee322874cdb0a39ceabd81c780b88d69ff22588.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot 100 = 200$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="100" height="13" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/9/f993726c287e9c155dea872e45e4971780b7c7e9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{200}{4} = 50$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="76" height="38" />, so there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/6/906734e2f1010e9a171ffee8050cbc0649cea37c.png" class="latex" alt="$50$" width="17" height="12" /> vertices.
</p><p>The base of a pyramid has 4 edges, so each sliced vertex would add four edges to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/1/9c169eb0ab36ad113eb8d5b6b77c8c4fd14cef28.png" class="latex" alt="$100 + 4 \cdot 50$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="92" height="15" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/7/fa702fde2fa06249aed53d1578f7f7edced96f62.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) } 300}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="74" height="28" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation">Explanation</span></h2>
<p>I doubt anyone has the mental capacity to imagine <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> in its full complexity in their mind. So, we begin by examining a simpler structure: a cube.
</p><p><br />
Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/9/39926e95d3004b72afaae3b40c9266de11fbbfff.png" class="latex" alt="$E_C$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="23" height="15" /> denote the number of edges of a polyhedron <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/7/2e7f425547b24e641c8007bb92e86b02931736ec.png" class="latex" alt="$V_C$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="20" height="15" /> denote the number of vertices of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/9/1f95504c592eee3fd2716f03f5f842277b0d4c01.png" class="latex" alt="$B_C$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="24" height="15" /> denote the number of edges that meet at each vertex.
</p><p><br />
Suppose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> is a cube. Then
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/0/2d00c53b260fea61b6db385087b5e868457523bd.png" class="latex" alt="$E_C = 12$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="66" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/8/7080ce619cef413075bbcaa110e632b77fcf1984.png" class="latex" alt="$V_C = 8$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="54" height="15" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/c/9dcff27cef03d7ac59a8d58188ba0555d0abb084.png" class="latex" alt="$B_C = 3$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="57" height="15" />
</p><p><br />
(A cube has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> edges, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> vertices, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> edges meet per vertex)
</p><p><br />
Notice that
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/3/12361b628e40b5bc733f99ca2b91f5ab70e611ad.png" class="latex" alt="$V_C*B_C = 2E_C$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="120" height="15" /> (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/d/b4dea0226ae6e0035d01be55a6e779379a802c37.png" class="latex" alt="$8*3 = 2*12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="103" height="13" />)
</p><p><br />
Why? Well, imagine an ant walking along an edge of a cube. When it reaches the end of the edge, it will be standing on a vertex (by definition). But, what if it had walked the other way? Well, it would still be on the same edge, but it would reach the other endpoint of the edge, another vertex. Therefore, we can say that a polyhedron with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> edges has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/0/e509be2b0ceec3d3f54ae2e5e944024e6e943b7d.png" class="latex" alt="$2E$" width="23" height="12" /> total endpoints. In the case of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />, a cube, this means a cube has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/d/50db56972f37c74a6a9b05a759455af479054328.png" class="latex" alt="$2*12 = 24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="86" height="12" /> total endpoints. But a cube only has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> vertices, not <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/9/e290f2cba0ba0e11c0b91630da15a408da2fcf1c.png" class="latex" alt="$24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" />... This doesn't make any sense... Wait a minute, we're dealing with a polyhedron, which means multiple edges will be connected to the same vertex. In the case of a cube, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> edges meet at every vertex. So we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/d/47d4d7e78694280a39a998a9a0471a5df07de146.png" class="latex" alt="$24 \div 3$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="49" height="13" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> vertices. The math works out. Let's apply this to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />, our mystery polyhedron.
</p><p><br />
We are told that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/1/2b1ef254e71df75c96ba02a00ef90d9204445517.png" class="latex" alt="$E_Q = 100$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="75" height="17" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/9/ff935c32f04315cbc0eccb876e6d0c2da3a5969d.png" class="latex" alt="$V_Q = n$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="56" height="17" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/3/3b3ed0fd71011b51e86937935c36e7ce7bf8f29a.png" class="latex" alt="$B_Q$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="24" height="17" /> isn't given explicitly. So we'll let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/4/b446d148fb474b4dc164a1750467bc6467b9597e.png" class="latex" alt="$B_Q = b$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="57" height="17" />. Now our equation reads
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/9/7f9f9e845779b6705e3af87cba7410afc56377a7.png" class="latex" alt="$bn = 200$" width="69" height="12" />
</p><p><br />
What does this mean, exactly? Well <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> edges, so it must has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/3/8f37626d49b4082c9102bf930bca51277ff2eb43.png" class="latex" alt="$200$" width="26" height="12" /> endpoints (endpoints, not vertices). So if we can figure out either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, the number of vertices, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" />, the number of edges per vertex, we can figure out the other variable. Hmmmm... The problem doesn't give us any clues about either. How frustrating. Let's leave this alone and consider the other information given to us.
</p><p><br />
We are also told that "the polyhedron is cut by planes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/0/a300dc173a63ce2abb80d7504cf16d5336bb073f.png" class="latex" alt="$P_1, P_2,...,P_n$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="97" height="16" />" (at this point I imagine <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> is a giant block of cheese, potato, etc.), and also that "plane <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/e/76ebaba4ae3d5c911d6e31ad855d502ad88d48a6.png" class="latex" alt="$P_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> cuts only those edges that meet at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/b/40b514f3d50ab12888fa4886e266bb6a1e8a7e9f.png" class="latex" alt="$V_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="17" height="15" />". What does this mean?
</p><p><br />
Well, let's go back to our cube. Notice that there are exactly as many planes as vertices. Think of the planes as knives (or knife strokes) cutting into a big cube of cheese. We're cutting the cheese <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> times, since there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> vertices. Also, whenever we cut into the cheese, we have to direct the cuts near a vertex, cutting into only the edges connected to that vertex. How do we do that? Well, since we can't cut into any other edges, we're gonna have to settle for cutting corners (see what I did there? ...Ok, I admit it was cheesy... see what I did there, too?ðŸ˜‚). We have to shave off just the corners of the cheese block, because this way it won't interfere with any of the other edges.
</p><p><br />
The problem also tells us that none of the planes overlap. In our cheese analogy, this means that every time we cut, we have to make sure the cuts are always into new cheese, and not into previously cut areas (all of the cuts are distinct, and don't overlap). Sure enough, this is in agreement with the information that exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> pyramids are cut off. Let's go back to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />.
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> vertices, so we're gonna make <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> cuts. The question we want to answer is: "After all of the cuts are made, how many total edges will <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> (the figure that remains), have?". Well, we're gonna have to figure out either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" />... or do we? We're told that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> pyramids were made, but it doesn't specify exactly how many sides the pyramid has. So... that implies that it doesn't matter, or else the problem wouldn't be solvable. Let's suppose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/b/efb996ca207df5d210cd86914aed764adb55fcb3.png" class="latex" alt="$b = 4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="41" height="13" /> (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> edges meet at every vertex). Then we would have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/7/af769f16b2198ec902d798673a730cd9dcbe2f4e.png" class="latex" alt="$200 \div 4 = 50$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="100" height="13" /> vertices, and we would make <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/6/906734e2f1010e9a171ffee8050cbc0649cea37c.png" class="latex" alt="$50$" width="17" height="12" /> cuts. But how many more edges does it yield? (We are told that the planes don't overlap, so the original edges are all present.) Well, if we cut off the corner of a cube, it will create <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> more edges... which is coincident with the fact that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> edges meet at every vertex. So for every vertex, we gain exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> new edges. Rewriting the equation to find the number of vertices, we have
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/e/2be599df09f9db2a3d88f8e16bab992b265e7a36.png" class="latex" alt="$v = \frac{200}{b}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="63" height="37" />
</p><p><br />
But we just said that for every vertex/cut, we gain <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> more edges. So the total number of new edges is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/6/91685878474313b31073e469968910fa44d6c393.png" class="latex" alt="$v*b \Rightarrow \frac{200}{b}*b \Rightarrow 200$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="173" height="37" />. It doesn't matter how many vertices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> has, or how many edges meet at every vertex. Given the conditions in the problem, we will always gain <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/3/8f37626d49b4082c9102bf930bca51277ff2eb43.png" class="latex" alt="$200$" width="26" height="12" /> more edges, regardless of the values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" />. We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/3/8f37626d49b4082c9102bf930bca51277ff2eb43.png" class="latex" alt="$200$" width="26" height="12" /> new edges <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/c/77cf26be132ef93923e082ee4153b2cb0ef44a50.png" class="latex" alt="$+$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="13" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> old edges to make <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/9/b899aba26cc33f0026125daebb44a60349c999b6.png" class="latex" alt="$300$" width="26" height="12" /> edges <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/2/9a2fcf7ed738da955f8e2c141cb39c144cc8b1a1.png" class="latex" alt="$\Rightarrow \boxed{\text{C}}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="47" height="23" />.
</p>
