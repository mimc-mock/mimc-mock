<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/7/577991bfcbdc9404a5871bae06764c4785c7048a.png" class="latex" alt="$ACEG$" width="56" height="13" /> is a square. WLOG <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/6/f56cbc52e5c7c0fab4ac50957077b6d18fb2ca57.png" class="latex" alt="$AB = 1,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="65" height="16" /> then using Law of Cosines, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/5/115199b1bcbb30f74094ac15b18fe15c3e77a5d1.png" class="latex" alt="$AC^2 = [ACEG] = 1^2 + 1^2 - 2 \cos{135} = 2 + \sqrt{2}.$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="378" height="22" /> The area of the octagon is just <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/e/b5e361354ca3682b6ea91651307bfdd02b59acd1.png" class="latex" alt="$[ACEG]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="65" height="18" /> plus the area of the four congruent (by symmetry) isosceles triangles, all an angle of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/e/d8efc4595ab8d0d7d43d5ec1988b1b7dc8ecc7fc.png" class="latex" alt="$135$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> in between two sides of length 1. Now, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/5/a35b1f440d58e48966f283860ba651bd584c1a35.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\dfrac{m}{n} = \dfrac{2 + \sqrt{2}}{2 + \sqrt{2} + 4 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} \sin{135}} = \dfrac{2 + \sqrt{2}}{2 + 2 \sqrt{2}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}.\]" width="361" height="49" /> The answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/d/3bd9d1003fa0ecb744b1ebc09b28550c6bcf3372.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B)}}.$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="43" height="28" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Refer to the diagram. Call one of the side lengths of the square <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" />. Since quadrilateral ACEG is a square, the area of the square would just be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/2/752ccf51ef0be42644a0099346e876310df962f3.png" class="latex" alt="$s^2$" width="14" height="15" />, which we can find by applying Law of Cosines on one of the four triangles. Assume each of the sides of the octagon has length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. Since each angle measures <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/4/b54930ee3937d1087e4262a992f6770c261947e4.png" class="latex" alt="$135^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="13" /> in an octagon, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/c/36c27f90eb325794cd4828007d7b4692a3079860.png" class="latex" alt="$s^2 = 1^2+1^2-2*\cos(135^{\circ}) = 2+\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="297" height="21" /> 
</p><p>There are many ways to find the area of the octagon, but one way is to split the octagon into two trapezoids and one rectangle. We can easily compute AF to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/8/9c85140b66951d85558fc8f18c2bd1c8eacd221f.png" class="latex" alt="$1+\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="56" height="18" /> from splitting one of the sides into two <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/2/22231a5cdd4fe05a11f4b577e3b81221f3017a85.png" class="latex" alt="$45-45-90$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="98" height="13" /> triangles. So the area of the octagon is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/b/36bdef17c1527130ebef557b3c4402127ca306d4.png" class="latex" alt="$2*\frac{1+\sqrt{2}}{2}+1+\sqrt{2} \Rightarrow 2+2\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="257" height="41" />. 
</p><p>Hence, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/1/2f1330c1d39b51d859f54b4e08bcb24a19f88cf1.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{m}{n} = \frac{2+\sqrt{2}}{2+2\sqrt{2}}\]" width="109" height="45" /> 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/5/435f7caa420f82736a80ed942abb58a25165f0be.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\Rightarrow \frac{2+\sqrt{2}}{2+2\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{2-2\sqrt{2}}{2-2\sqrt{2}}\]" width="171" height="45" />
