<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" /> be the triangle in the coordinate plane with vertices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/7/2e7626fec3d1367ae2719f164fd4fe61fd7e76fc.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0), (4,0),$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="92" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/f/a6f098556cfb85d7349c23cc61021eeb28536f1c.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,3).$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="43" height="18" /> Consider the following five isometries (rigid transformations) of the plane: rotations of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/9/489d7b71b0aec448ba38c4236b4100537275ceac.png" class="latex" alt="$90^{\circ}, 180^{\circ},$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="73" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/5/bf5f6f71e34a8c314f0beff76bedf4e516eed1a0.png" class="latex" alt="$270^{\circ}$" width="33" height="13" /> counterclockwise around the origin, reflection across the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-axis, and reflection across the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />-axis. How many of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/2/b829a6f0810a25e9ca9f85d690f2bafd0f2c1773.png" class="latex" alt="$125$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> sequences of three of these transformations (not necessarily distinct) will return <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" /> to its original position? (For example, a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/0/3f0cd95f95238159d02dbcea0aca632c2928991c.png" class="latex" alt="$180^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="13" /> rotation, followed by a reflection across the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-axis, followed by a reflection across the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />-axis will return <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" /> to its original position, but a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/7/6a7dfa8a2a90d521b2b03f5b82a1fb9f85dce88c.png" class="latex" alt="$90^{\circ}$" width="24" height="13" /> rotation, followed by a reflection across the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-axis, followed by another reflection across the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-axis will not return <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" /> to its original position.)
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/e/0ceec7805d9b01f16fd3e23d00fb0bbd442ab7d6.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 12 \qquad \textbf{(B) } 15 \qquad \textbf{(C) } 17 \qquad \textbf{(D) } 20 \qquad \textbf{(E) } 25$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="398" height="17" />

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/5/fe5753684f6e33e53edeb74bb429a66ae1f2f6be.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(10cm); Label f;  f.p=fontsize(6);  xaxis(-6,6,Ticks(f, 2.0));  yaxis(-6,6,Ticks(f, 2.0));  filldraw(origin--(4,0)--(0,3)--cycle, gray, black+linewidth(1)); [/asy]" width="475" height="475" />
</p><p>First, any combination of motions we can make must reflect <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" /> an even number of times. This is because every time we reflect <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" />, it changes orientation. Once <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" /> has been flipped once, no combination of rotations will put it back in place because it is the mirror image; however, flipping it again changes it back to the original orientation. Since we are only allowed <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> transformations and an even number of them must be reflections, we either reflect <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> times or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> times.
</p><p><br />
</p><p>Case 1: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> reflections on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" />.
</p><p><br />
</p><p>In this case, we must use <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> rotations to return <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" /> to its original position. Notice that our set of rotations, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/d/a5d44b0e0f608b53f38b7474b06b2ce6a79da3ff.png" class="latex" alt="$\{90^\circ,180^\circ,270^\circ\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="129" height="18" />, contains every multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/e/22ec0d2e4202839322d26a23dcb13f79821df5d7.png" class="latex" alt="$90^\circ$" width="24" height="13" /> except for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/9/c79965629cf6c6f6e340be9c4b2384f5ef712316.png" class="latex" alt="$0^\circ$" width="15" height="13" />. We can start with any two rotations <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/b/e9bdf3e496517e8f8742f81dd774be1f93cf6e8c.png" class="latex" alt="$a,b$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="25" height="16" /> in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/d/a5d44b0e0f608b53f38b7474b06b2ce6a79da3ff.png" class="latex" alt="$\{90^\circ,180^\circ,270^\circ\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="129" height="18" /> and there must be exactly one <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/1/9a14418eeed5d1b2c8a578c291e04653471c95dd.png" class="latex" alt="$c \equiv -a - b \pmod{360^\circ}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="182" height="18" /> such that we can use the three rotations <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/d/f3d9e22097e20a1249b5332202b24941a4bf9e1f.png" class="latex" alt="$(a,b,c)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="54" height="18" /> which ensures that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/c/ebcb842f1d7d206835ea0cecb2c180a2a4c2b42c.png" class="latex" alt="$a + b + c \equiv 0^\circ \pmod{360^\circ}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="207" height="18" />. That way, the composition of rotations <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/b/a5b29b358e825defa3e11b7d903d43ec31e5909a.png" class="latex" alt="$a,b,c$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="41" height="16" /> yields a full rotation. For example, if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/0/df0416e39be0a72c8fe1c7cea0e975dd2371a474.png" class="latex" alt="$a = b = 90^\circ$" width="90" height="13" />, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/f/70f291da219020ff369bed53a909cfeaef5530ae.png" class="latex" alt="$c \equiv -90^\circ - 90^\circ = -180^\circ \pmod{360^\circ}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="290" height="18" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/e/86e404d84d2e16ae340cd297ef5a468a33bafb94.png" class="latex" alt="$c = 180^\circ$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="65" height="13" /> and the rotations <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/0/8b05fe6b67d6a9393a54f8dcc0c33886f32796ae.png" class="latex" alt="$(90^\circ,90^\circ,180^\circ)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="115" height="18" /> yields a full rotation.
</p><p>The only case in which this fails is when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> would have to equal <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/9/c79965629cf6c6f6e340be9c4b2384f5ef712316.png" class="latex" alt="$0^\circ$" width="15" height="13" />. This happens when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/8/8e8da0aef2d19017da7471378386d61620f288f5.png" class="latex" alt="$(a,b)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> is already a full rotation, namely, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/6/5c6c85bc59654d82ffd86557035523d35ab44d91.png" class="latex" alt="$(a,b) = (90^\circ,270^\circ),(180^\circ,180^\circ),$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="251" height="18" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/3/f13e66c5bc60995ce6fe90dfa4418fd218619279.png" class="latex" alt="$(270^\circ,90^\circ)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="81" height="18" />. However, we can simply subtract these three cases from the total. Selecting <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/8/8e8da0aef2d19017da7471378386d61620f288f5.png" class="latex" alt="$(a,b)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/d/a5d44b0e0f608b53f38b7474b06b2ce6a79da3ff.png" class="latex" alt="$\{90^\circ,180^\circ,270^\circ\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="129" height="18" /> yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/8/a08f8b84240fbf97ba60819f475b53b1a2295b9e.png" class="latex" alt="$3 \cdot 3 = 9$" width="64" height="12" /> choices, and with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> that fail, we are left with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> combinations for case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />.
</p><p><br />
</p><p>Case 2: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> reflections on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" />.
</p><p><br />
</p><p>In this case, we first eliminate the possibility of having two of the same reflection. Since two reflections across the x-axis maps <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" /> back to itself, inserting a rotation before, between, or after these two reflections would change <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" />'s final location, meaning that any combination involving two reflections across the x-axis would not map <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/2554b6496c3b678897e9b060ef00aa9f0a7d7ece.png" class="latex" alt="$T$" width="13" height="12" /> back to itself. The same applies to two reflections across the y-axis.
</p><p>Therefore, we must use one reflection about the x-axis, one reflection about the y-axis, and one rotation. Since a reflection about the x-axis changes the sign of the y component, a reflection about the y-axis changes the sign of the x component, and a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/4/c4460b5100c3ce982ab5ebb20a8c3871e9cfdc54.png" class="latex" alt="$180^\circ$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="13" /> rotation changes both signs, these three transformation composed (in any order) will suffice. It is therefore only a question of arranging the three, giving us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/b/4db3b59940e4f5cd42bbab2b7815c452a0197507.png" class="latex" alt="$3! = 6$" width="47" height="13" /> combinations for case 2.
</p><p>Combining both cases we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/6/41620e540372f0662a356dd67a2f992366477cbd.png" class="latex" alt="$6+6=\boxed{\textbf{(A)}  12}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="123" height="28" />.
</p>
