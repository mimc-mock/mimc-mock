<p>A frog sitting at the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/2/69273482bff8727194f6407e55ac1bfa94d9c555.png" class="latex" alt="$(1, 2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> begins a sequence of jumps, where each jump is parallel to one of the coordinate axes and has length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, and the direction of each jump (up, down, right, or left) is chosen independently at random. The sequence ends when the frog reaches a side of the square with vertices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/c/b8cafb7774031874f23ebb8f88f9d4bac2439d1b.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0), (0,4), (4,4),$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="141" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/6/b86f58cb609fa753024f1173510c877971f8e4e6.png" class="latex" alt="$(4,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />. What is the probability that the sequence of jumps ends on a vertical side of the square?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/3/8a3e088ff5b553720349acc5f41c3270d925fa32.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac12\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac 58\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac 23\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ \frac34\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \frac 78$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="367" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Drawing out the square, it's easy to see that if the frog goes to the left, it will immediately hit a vertical end of the square. Therefore, the probability of this happening is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/f/95f0230d253ca211940d330f84d80bc6e2bec342.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4} \cdot 1 = \frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="71" height="37" />. If the frog goes to the right, it will be in the center of the square at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/b/b2b596a120dcf60e33c7bfe19003bb1675bf37a7.png" class="latex" alt="$(2,2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />, and by symmetry (since the frog is equidistant from all sides of the square), the chance it will hit a vertical side of a square is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. The probability of this happening is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/9/b89dd914e7ca5fe75767314847a64d293245afcf.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{8}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="75" height="37" />.
</p><p><br />
If the frog goes either up or down, it will hit a line of symmetry along the corner it is closest to and furthest to, and again, is equidistant relating to the two closer sides and also equidistant relating the two further sides. The probability for it to hit a vertical wall is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. Because there's a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> chance of the frog going up or down, the total probability for this case is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e5992bf70a872b6e72313fc370541ec9733bb784.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="75" height="37" /> and summing up all the cases, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/e/97e47fc613df059c929ab7544d408ae094ba1cab.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{8} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{5}{8}       \implies \boxed{\textbf{(B) } \frac{5}{8}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="230" height="48" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Let's say we have our four by four grid and we work this out by casework. A is where the frog is, while B and C are possible locations for his second jump, while O is everything else. If we land on a C, we have reached the vertical side. However, if we land on a B, we can see that there is an equal chance of reaching the horizontal or vertical side, since we are symmetrically between them. So we have the probability of landing on a C is 1/4, while B is 3/4. Since C means that we have "succeeded", while B means that we have a half chance, we compute <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/b/feb4b4a16715c06661410e1b2f12ea474cd06736.png" class="latex" alt="$1 \cdot C + \frac{1}{2} \cdot B$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="99" height="37" />. 
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/b/cbb06523b7e97d5250055a346a0c8afef78e99bc.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[1 \cdot \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{3}{4}\]" width="94" height="38" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/1/611cedb65ea2d7177379132f3fde68966772da7e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{4} + \frac{3}{8}\]" width="45" height="38" />
We get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/3/f4344e4ce1c2a266b885d9b160544e3036de15f8.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5}{8}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/3/2031aeaac4cc06503f5c42a6d79fc080034d56db.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\text{O O O O O}\]" width="85" height="12" />                                                                                                                                                             
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/9/8a9df8d3488879aef4f7c60ba791a32180ae1052.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\text{O B O O O}\]" width="84" height="12" />

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>If the frog is on one of the 2 diagonals, the chance of landing on vertical or horizontal each becomes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. Since it starts on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/b/1bb913ad463dcc2916d4135977f0d06f20ad93cd.png" class="latex" alt="$(1,2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />, there is a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/1/e1150a17bf1b733cb666be84d920c71fc96bb3f7.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{3}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="11" height="38" /> chance (up, down, or right) it will reach a diagonal on the first jump and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c339afa03e060d959e4942be7522c0c992f4c41c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="11" height="37" /> chance (left) it will reach the vertical side. The probablity of landing on a vertical is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/2/602629e71786c8842d1dc8f4c59fcad1456479ec.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}+\frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{2}=\boxed{\textbf{(B)} \frac{5}{8}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="152" height="48" />.
