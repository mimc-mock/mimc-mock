(For example, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/0/c3047db038c0f014be85bb309d6d67e0d3a821da.png" class="latex" alt="$R, R, V, H$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="80" height="16" /> is one sequence of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> transformations that will send the vertices back to their original positions.)

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>For each transformation:
</p>
<ol style="margin-left: 1.5em;">
  <li>Each labeled vertex will move to an adjacent position.</li><p class="mw-empty-elt">
  </p><li>The labeled vertices will maintain the consecutive order <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> in either direction (clockwise or counterclockwise).</li><p class="mw-empty-elt">
  </p><li><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/9/859ccf4cd60c7bc6b8fa1afc9a42dc811a826d6f.png" class="latex" alt="$L$" width="12" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> will retain the direction of the labeled vertices, but <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/9/b1902d279ba37d60bdce4e0e987b7cd19d48974e.png" class="latex" alt="$H$" width="16" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/d/12d58aa29201da09d8e620f8698e3a37547f6b4a.png" class="latex" alt="$V$" width="14" height="12" /> will alter the direction of the labeled vertices.</li><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p></ol>
<p>After the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/b/efbfe273946ff9f971c5eab331ca39caa6fdf7f4.png" class="latex" alt="$19$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />th transformation, vertex <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> will be at either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/0/350caf4c4803973cd5992382cc5cac99f9bf45f0.png" class="latex" alt="$(1,-1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="52" height="18" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/2/db24ad52b6a99708f9d50823884bcd2032897bda.png" class="latex" alt="$(-1,1).$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="58" height="18" /> All possible configurations of the labeled vertices are shown below:

Each sequence of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/b/efbfe273946ff9f971c5eab331ca39caa6fdf7f4.png" class="latex" alt="$19$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> transformations generates one valid sequence of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/3/f8366cd6196dd8a9da6d38a3e9eafb109e99d53e.png" class="latex" alt="$20$" width="17" height="12" /> transformations. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/2/b525c364d11c8e5714136e9f1dfd4851ac129491.png" class="latex" alt="$(+)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" /> denote counterclockwise/starting orientation and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/5/585113764ddef84fa165af9f3f9a06397aa805bf.png" class="latex" alt="$(-)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" /> denote clockwise orientation.
Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/1/1614f2a4d8d2f0e92b1b76f39417aacfbaba9e2c.png" class="latex" alt="$1,2,3,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="47" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> denote which quadrant <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> is in.
</p><p>Realize that from any odd quadrant and any orientation, the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> transformations result in some permutation of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/4/4c416749eed7655df7b3e35febd3f9f1467f52ad.png" class="latex" alt="$(2+, 2-, 4+, 4-).$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="138" height="20" />
</p><p>The same goes that from any even quadrant and any orientation, the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> transformations result in some permutation of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/c/79cc1b4accff510403ca21790371b2353ad8fd57.png" class="latex" alt="$(1+, 1-, 3+, 3-).$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="138" height="20" />
</p><p>We start our first <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/b/efbfe273946ff9f971c5eab331ca39caa6fdf7f4.png" class="latex" alt="$19$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> moves by doing whatever we want, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> choices each time. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/b/efbfe273946ff9f971c5eab331ca39caa6fdf7f4.png" class="latex" alt="$19$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> is odd, we must end up on an even quadrant. 
</p><p>As said above, we know that exactly one of the four transformations will give us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/e/e5eb8287b201db7c80c5036d16d0207ba1e4fd47.png" class="latex" alt="$(1+),$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="44" height="20" /> and we must use that transformation.
</p><p>Thus, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/8/358173e95a748b13dfb199e3a91bc1058b686f55.png" class="latex" alt="$4^{19}=\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ 2^{38}}.$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="119" height="31" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Notice that any pair of two of these transformations either swaps the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />-coordinates, negates the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />-coordinates, swaps and negates the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />-coordinates, or leaves the original unchanged. Furthermore, notice that for each of these results, if we apply another pair of transformations, one of these results will happen again, and with equal probability. Therefore, no matter what state after we apply the first <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/b/efbfe273946ff9f971c5eab331ca39caa6fdf7f4.png" class="latex" alt="$19$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> pairs of transformations, there is a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/d/4cd0f9f9869b39e79527f9010293adf7bfe5210f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac14$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="11" height="37" /> chance the last pair of transformations will return the figure to its original position. Therefore, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/5/33585082fab74ef9251593fe6c5c1e14ba4a1796.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{4^{20}}4 = 4^{19} = \boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ 2^{38}}.$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="174" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>The total number of sequences is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/e/ade409b343a10bf196c9f5f1e5f6af9a29da25bb.png" class="latex" alt="$4^{20}=2^{40}.$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="77" height="17" />
</p><p>Note that there can only be an even number of reflections since they result in the same anti-clockwise orientation of the vertices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/5/3d5b473bd37f60d328c7aa4c9b4aee2ef5441711.png" class="latex" alt="$A,B,C,D.$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="85" height="16" /> Therefore, the probability of having the same anti-clockwise orientation with the original arrangement after the transformation is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/6/616d5a21c98919a0a286960273edefb463314726.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="16" height="37" />
</p><p>Next, the even number of reflections means that there must be an even number of rotations since their sum is even. Even rotations result in only the original position or a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/0/3f0cd95f95238159d02dbcea0aca632c2928991c.png" class="latex" alt="$180^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="13" /> rotation of it. 
</p><p>Since rotation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> and rotation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/9/859ccf4cd60c7bc6b8fa1afc9a42dc811a826d6f.png" class="latex" alt="$L$" width="12" height="12" /> cancel each other out, the difference between the numbers of them define the final position. The probability of the transformation returning the vertices to the original position, given that there are even number of rotations, is equivalent to the probability that
</p>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/7/037daf4bc2475b44ea893ba802172a764f9c4973.png" class="latex" alt="$|n(R)-n(L)|\equiv0\pmod{4}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="212" height="18" /> when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/4/e647378b853d03a8728f88d385827b467c540a1d.png" class="latex" alt="$|n(H)-n(V)|\equiv0\pmod{4}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="217" height="18" /></center>
<center>or</center>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/f/5efc205ae74ac19a12cb2b5f8643e09dfd6e371e.png" class="latex" alt="$|n(R)-n(L)|\equiv2\pmod{4}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="212" height="18" /> when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/2/3b23eda61df1960493754f47b25125417903fec9.png" class="latex" alt="$|n(H)-n(V)|\equiv2\pmod{4}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="217" height="18" /></center>
<p>which is again, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/6/616d5a21c98919a0a286960273edefb463314726.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="16" height="37" />
</p><p>Therefore, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/2/bb24f239b84320f1974f8194cbd3e28a18bb16c4.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{40}\cdot\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2}=\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ 2^{38}}.$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="175" height="38" />
</p><p>~joshuamh111
