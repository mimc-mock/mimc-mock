<p>For all integers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/4/5a4e67413d41d87783dc891188093a2ec53fbfdd.png" class="latex" alt="$n \geq 9,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="48" height="16" /> the value of
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/c/e4cf3e216ff29ec12bb85e389993280b1ca40180.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{(n+2)!-(n+1)!}{n!}\]" width="145" height="38" />is always which of the following?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/2/462e0fdce09069330a341099b7e91cb15a9ea144.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } \text{a multiple of 4} \qquad \textbf{(B) } \text{a multiple of 10} \qquad \textbf{(C) } \text{a prime number} \qquad \textbf{(D) } \text{a perfect square} \qquad \textbf{(E) } \text{a perfect cube}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="865" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We first expand the expression:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/2/862026a8218274b6eb950d238448ee739d863884.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{(n+2)!-(n+1)!}{n!} = \frac{(n+2)(n+1)n!-(n+1)n!}{n!}.\]" width="402" height="38" />
We can now divide out a common factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/f/63f344e7d8b14a7886fc7fb72da53cd0a3271dcc.png" class="latex" alt="$n!$" width="14" height="13" /> from each term of the numerator:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/5/a65f6dcdd7e879078eb8acdcb6c41ffcc21f8f0e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(n+2)(n+1)-(n+1).\]" width="193" height="18" />
Factoring out <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5060e6e853cd9417ac26f5ed2178d3c860519e.png" class="latex" alt="$(n+1),$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="63" height="20" /> we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/c/70c754fc426f757786accf6318b93d7bc4d1d175.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[[(n+2)-1](n+1) = (n+1)^2,\]" width="244" height="20" />
which proves that the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/a/3ba43987730039720ed6426b189cd4c6fe53c66c.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) } \text{a perfect square}}.$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="170" height="30" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>In the numerator, we factor out an <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/f/63f344e7d8b14a7886fc7fb72da53cd0a3271dcc.png" class="latex" alt="$n!$" width="14" height="13" /> to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/0/6605f384fc7eb446d310e1fc0b187538f98f32c1.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{(n+2)!-(n+1)!}{n!} = (n+2)(n+1)-(n+1).\]" width="366" height="38" /> Now, without loss of generality, test values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> until only one answer choice is left valid:
</p>
<ul><li><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/2/ee2d624a650299e2bfe09471d569ba74493879b7.png" class="latex" alt="$n = 1 \implies (3)(2) - (2) = 4,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="223" height="18" /> knocking out <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/7/7/677ada227f107b748cb1264377fb76f8c91c0843.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(B)},\textbf{(C)},$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="69" height="17" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/2/5729ea372cb53da5f2596d6f802dbd34914d4322.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(E)}.$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="31" height="17" /></li></ul>
<ul><li><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/1/3b1c2726be1d77928c6e5893c597e0385bd9a18b.png" class="latex" alt="$n = 2 \implies (4)(3) - (3) = 9,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="223" height="18" /> knocking out <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/5/425126545d8adb244da6faa9815f8e8d9989f9ca.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}.$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="33" height="17" /></li></ul>
<p>This leaves <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/d/7ed20ae3b4862cbe4d92496f3fd30fe6d4b1e7dc.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) } \text{a perfect square}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="163" height="28" /> as the only answer choice left.
</p><p>This solution does not consider the condition <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/f/6efbb7a032b125af43f65c169c44edeaf992a2a3.png" class="latex" alt="$n \geq 9.$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="47" height="15" /> The reason is that, with further testing it becomes clear that for all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/4/c54e7152acd659788861f0453ac195c635e0a005.png" class="latex" alt="$n,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="15" height="11" /> we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/7/227c9dcc1a25f21fe49c32d711cf9a9fb6086260.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(n+2)(n+1)-(n+1) = (n+1)^{2},\]" width="282" height="19" /> as proved in Solution 1. The condition <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/5/545cb201c2b28b17b453f6757b52fdb3b226b757.png" class="latex" alt="$n \geq 9$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="43" height="15" /> was added most likely to encourage picking <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/1/b7193f66028e13f331d53ff5e6942c4b05587465.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(B)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="27" height="17" /> and discourage substituting smaller values into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/9/bd923e41635af40a1abe58f230732f2280b0723a.png" class="latex" alt="$n.$" width="14" height="8" /> 
</p><p>~DBlack2021 (Solution)
</p><p>~MRENTHUSIASM (Edits in Logic)
</p><p>~Countmath1 (Minor Edits in Formatting)
</p>
