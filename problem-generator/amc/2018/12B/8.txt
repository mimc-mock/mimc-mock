<p>Line segment <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> is a diameter of a circle with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/8/0989b5e597cbb93095b7004ecf4f96b43c6fc40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AB = 24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="70" height="13" />. Point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />, not equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, lies on the circle. As point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> moves around the circle, the centroid (center of mass) of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> traces out a closed curve missing two points. To the nearest positive integer, what is the area of the region bounded by this curve?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/c/51cba69e406fcee981521efa5a98caf9b6ccb7fa.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 25 \qquad \textbf{(B) } 38  \qquad \textbf{(C) } 50  \qquad \textbf{(D) } 63 \qquad \textbf{(E) } 75$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="398" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>For each <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/f/8/6f8dcf98ed1b21dbc446aa28d097d4281599c25e.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="62" height="17" /> note that the length of one median is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/c/09c5d856a77ecc364482df35e3f1706366317369.png" class="latex" alt="$OC=12.$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="78" height="14" /> Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e28ce12d49d39f160d5a0ef54077fc98e4b9d2b.png" class="latex" alt="$G$" width="14" height="12" /> be the centroid of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/9/919b9f77a6985e760286799909be5bfe0152f40e.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC.$" width="61" height="13" /> It follows that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/a/c6a6859c2e198c08fa5904ddc3035afe6913b523.png" class="latex" alt="$OG=\frac13 OC=4.$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="135" height="38" />
</p><p>As shown below, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/0/fc005a4c724097fb8be534a8d669f107403e567d.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC_1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/8/f88b944dc82a95ef33acbb9c711682e396cfcc04.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="63" height="16" /> are two shapes of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> with centroids <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/b/8db8d2c3b6325306a87e8a10b0e5358afb2f23eb.png" class="latex" alt="$G_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="20" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/4/f64f63d2af7c0ad90ae9c7ea95b4b725c031083c.png" class="latex" alt="$G_2,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="29" height="18" /> respectively:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/d/f7d390ddcbaa38a41dd608a1c53e7396c707a2a3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] /* Made by MRENTHUSIASM */ size(200); pair O, A, B, C1, C2, G1, G2, M1, M2; O = (0,0); A = (-12,0); B = (12,0); C1 = (36/5,48/5); C2 = (-96/17,-180/17); G1 = O + 1/3 * C1; G2 = O + 1/3 * C2; M1 = (4,0); M2 = (-4,0);  draw(Circle(O,12)); draw(Circle(O,4),red);  dot(&quot;$O$&quot;, O, (3/5,-4/5), linewidth(4.5)); dot(&quot;$A$&quot;, A, W, linewidth(4.5)); dot(&quot;$B$&quot;, B, E, linewidth(4.5)); dot(&quot;$C_1$&quot;, C1, dir(C1), linewidth(4.5)); dot(&quot;$C_2$&quot;, C2, dir(C2), linewidth(4.5)); dot(&quot;$G_1$&quot;, G1, 1.5*E, linewidth(4.5)); dot(&quot;$G_2$&quot;, G2, 1.5*W, linewidth(4.5)); draw(A--B^^A--C1--B^^A--C2--B); draw(O--C1^^O--C2); dot(M1,red+linewidth(0.8),UnFill); dot(M2,red+linewidth(0.8),UnFill); [/asy]" width="335" height="295" />
Therefore, point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e28ce12d49d39f160d5a0ef54077fc98e4b9d2b.png" class="latex" alt="$G$" width="14" height="12" /> traces out a circle (missing two points) with the center <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" /> and the radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/1/dd1b666c7c427332e7a785c9a7e0b7ac7b718efe.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{OG},$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="32" height="19" /> as indicated in red. To the nearest positive integer, the area of the region bounded by the red curve is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/3/713bc22da26a549d6ff1efb860f08db2cb908b27.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi\cdot OG^2=16\pi\approx\boxed{\textbf{(C) } 50}.$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="209" height="30" />
</p><p>~MRENTHUSIASM
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We assign coordinates. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/6/dc6e58dfe37aecac319f75d810e1b3a00d923ed5.png" class="latex" alt="$A = (-12,0)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="103" height="20" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/6/7c6e85f012ec80c2afaa06f5b3acfa78b0fd357a.png" class="latex" alt="$B = (12,0)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="90" height="20" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/5/a75223f77adc95dd3a89adb27f1a6262cebb1ee5.png" class="latex" alt="$C = (x,y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="79" height="18" /> lie on the circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/b/74b8eba96ae72e9a06970b68d52fe091c070c81a.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2 +y^2 = 12^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="109" height="20" />. Then, the centroid of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/1/d91943026440807e9f4957218d8de1cf562cd969.png" class="latex" alt="$G = \left(\frac{-12 + 12 + x}{3}, \frac{0 + 0 + y}{3}\right) = \left(\frac x3,\frac y3\right)$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="342" height="45" />. Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e28ce12d49d39f160d5a0ef54077fc98e4b9d2b.png" class="latex" alt="$G$" width="14" height="12" /> traces out a circle with a radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/a/ebafcc60a28d8759185d7808c60ea53efc73938e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac13$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> of the radius of the circle that point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> travels on. Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e28ce12d49d39f160d5a0ef54077fc98e4b9d2b.png" class="latex" alt="$G$" width="14" height="12" /> traces out a circle of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/5/235820ed89f9ed54954565f6f7e529e091874bde.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{12}{3} = 4$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="55" height="37" />, which has area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/7/8371d4021e8fbbf678f202ab059a88fb2c5f3fa9.png" class="latex" alt="$16\pi\approx \boxed{\textbf{(C) } 50}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="120" height="30" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>First we can draw a few conclusions from the given information. Firstly we can see clearly that the distance from the centroid to the center of the circle will remain the same no matter <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> is on the circle. Also we can see that because the two legs of every triangles will always originate on the diameter, using inscribed angle rules, we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/2/c32872cc0b45af345655ede8cb60e93f428e53b3.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle C = \frac{180^\circ}{2} = 90^\circ$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="142" height="39" />. Now we know that all triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> will be a right triangle. We also know that the closed curve will simply be a circle with radius equal to the centroid of each triangle. We can now pick any arbitrary triangle, calculate its centroid, and the plug it in to the area formula. Using a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/d/8adb649f063149ebc97d3ee58c4fd38d4c440846.png" class="latex" alt="$45^\circ$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="24" height="13" />-<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/d/8adb649f063149ebc97d3ee58c4fd38d4c440846.png" class="latex" alt="$45^\circ$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="24" height="13" />-<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/e/22ec0d2e4202839322d26a23dcb13f79821df5d7.png" class="latex" alt="$90^\circ$" width="24" height="13" /> triangle in conjunction with the properties of a centroid, we can quickly see that the length of the centroid is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> now we can plug it in to the area formula where we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/4/a94932c889b1d896f255c19941c4d5b7a7f2d077.png" class="latex" alt="$16\pi\approx\boxed{\textbf{(C) } 50}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="120" height="30" />.
</p>
