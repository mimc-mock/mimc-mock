<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> be the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/4/70422100db8de33f8e5e0ab5062c5bb6a5ac969d.png" class="latex" alt="$ADE$" width="43" height="13" />. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>

Then, there is a triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/4/70422100db8de33f8e5e0ab5062c5bb6a5ac969d.png" class="latex" alt="$ADE$" width="43" height="13" /> encompassing the 7 small triangles and sharing the top angle with a base length of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/4/ed4977828049d99f20ccd3b4acb6bbd43c2eb70f.png" class="latex" alt="$4x$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="19" height="12" />. Because the area is proportional to the square of the side, let the base <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/0/87011f179ed9d5c9f06855d94dc1db619cfdcc61.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{40}x$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="43" height="18" />. The ratio of the area of triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/4/70422100db8de33f8e5e0ab5062c5bb6a5ac969d.png" class="latex" alt="$ADE$" width="43" height="13" /> to triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/4/f246f8a67996397ea55b8e6d355ae3c5565d302f.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{4x}{\sqrt{40}x}\right)^2 = \frac{16}{40}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="130" height="48" />. The problem says the area of triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/7/78792fe4680a164f537ea72a4f900e4471433c3b.png" class="latex" alt="$40$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />, so the area of triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/4/70422100db8de33f8e5e0ab5062c5bb6a5ac969d.png" class="latex" alt="$ADE$" width="43" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/5/9a5b4928c8fe50ce3c2428da3bee3505e891b788.png" class="latex" alt="$16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. So the area of trapezoid <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/1/761d66a035210a0d4f5858dc15c9427e7056782e.png" class="latex" alt="$DBCE$" width="58" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/b/c9babe88f36707314a3f7aab9a8f165093d2ef12.png" class="latex" alt="$40 - 16 = \boxed{24}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="112" height="23" />.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5">Solution 5</span></h2>
<p>You can see that we can create a "stack" of 5 triangles congruent to the 7 small triangles shown here, arranged in a row above those 7, whose total area would be 5. Similarly, we can create another row of 3, and finally 1 more at the top, as follows. We know this cumulative area will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/0/ab0396c994674f986d53bdc6055a1f827961e127.png" class="latex" alt="$7+5+3+1=16$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="145" height="14" />, so to find the area of such trapezoid <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/c/91c493dd2882ca1eb621281106a4d91bd73e092e.png" class="latex" alt="$BCED$" width="58" height="12" />, we just take <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/0/42020e3e114cde94c2e9eabd8981bb9c605cd623.png" class="latex" alt="$40-16=\boxed{24}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="112" height="23" />, like so.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_6">Solution 6</span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_8">Solution 8</span></h2>
<p>You can construct another trapezoid directly above the one shown, with it's bottom length as the top length of the original. Its area would then be 9/16 of the original. Repeating this process infinitely gives us the sequence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/4/564fefe2df66794b778077a3bc8066203bbb9ff3.png" class="latex" alt="$7\cdot\left(1+\left(\frac{9}{16}\right)+\left(\frac{9}{16}\right)^2+\left(\frac{9}{16}\right)^3\dots\right)$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="309" height="53" /> . Using the infinite geometric series sum formula gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/1/881a0523d81083c60bd7df5570017ea46950a7a5.png" class="latex" alt="$7\cdot\left(\frac{1}{1-\frac{9}{16}}\right)=7\cdot\frac{16}{7}=16$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="211" height="45" />. The triangle's area would thus be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/0/42020e3e114cde94c2e9eabd8981bb9c605cd623.png" class="latex" alt="$40-16=\boxed{24}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="112" height="23" />.
