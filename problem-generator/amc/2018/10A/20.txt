<p>A scanning code consists of a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/4/5d4f93df0d5696a7a30826c6a7c604d1352c6198.png" class="latex" alt="$7 \times 7$" width="40" height="12" /> grid of squares, with some of its squares colored black and the rest colored white. There must be at least one square of each color in this grid of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/c/34c8e6bf4d57cd6f2bb0d7d43e00f0f442f53b84.png" class="latex" alt="$49$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> squares. A scanning code is called <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/3/fa3021e911d666c749c43cb1b5de961e0e303116.png" class="latex" alt="$\textit{symmetric}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="75" height="15" /> if its look does not change when the entire square is rotated by a multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/d/f9d38eab3efc0dbd74471a71de651ff67c88fb63.png" class="latex" alt="$90 ^{\circ}$" width="24" height="13" /> counterclockwise around its center, nor when it is reflected across a line joining opposite corners or a line joining midpoints of opposite sides. What is the total number of possible symmetric scanning codes?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/3/0d319fa2e95be938d301fb7e08e715cf2bfe4b4b.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)} \text{ 510} \qquad \textbf{(B)} \text{ 1022} \qquad \textbf{(C)} \text{ 8190} \qquad \textbf{(D)} \text{ 8192} \qquad \textbf{(E)} \text{ 65,534}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="474" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Draw a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/4/5d4f93df0d5696a7a30826c6a7c604d1352c6198.png" class="latex" alt="$7 \times 7$" width="40" height="12" /> square.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/e/78ea46dd064f1505682597da32c8dcb3a351a9e6.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline K &amp; J &amp; H &amp; G &amp; H &amp; J &amp; K \\ \hline J &amp; F &amp; E &amp; D &amp; E &amp; F &amp; J \\ \hline H &amp; E &amp; C &amp; B &amp; C &amp; E &amp; H \\ \hline G &amp; D &amp; B &amp; A &amp; B &amp; D &amp; G \\ \hline H &amp; E &amp; C &amp; B &amp; C &amp; E &amp; H \\ \hline J &amp; F &amp; E &amp; D &amp; E &amp; F &amp; J \\ \hline K &amp; J &amp; H &amp; G &amp; H &amp; J &amp; K \\ \hline \end{tabular}\]" width="230" height="145" />
</p><p>Start from the center and label all protruding cells symmetrically. (Note that "I" is left out of this labelling, so there are only 10 labels, not 11, as ending in K would suggest!)
</p><p>More specifically, since there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> given lines of symmetry (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> diagonals, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> vertical, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> horizontal) and they split the plot into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> equivalent sections, we can take just one-eighth and study it in particular. Each of these sections has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> distinct sub-squares, whether partially or in full. So since each can be colored either white or black, we choose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/8/50811f350197d47b2ddb684f3b7d07ce96a0bdca.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{10}=1024$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="83" height="15" /> but then subtract the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> cases where all are white or all are black. That leaves us with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/4/55496b6c63d07400111f4577b899d4feeb05243f.png" class="latex" alt="$\fbox{\textbf{(B)} \text{ 1022}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="84" height="28" />.
</p><p>There are only ten squares we get to actually choose, and two independent choices for each, for a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/8/cf856775e3de1168a9a4f29bc68d3651d0bf6051.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{10} = 1024$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="83" height="15" /> codes. Two codes must be subtracted (due to the rule that there must be at least one square of each color) for an answer of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/1/0713782428e94227d70dc64d113770680287f33b.png" class="latex" alt="$\fbox{\textbf{(B) }1022}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="79" height="28" />.
</p><p><br />
Note that this problem is very similar to the 1996 AIME Problem 7 <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="https://artofproblemsolving.com/wiki/index.php/1996_AIME_Problems/Problem_7">https://artofproblemsolving.com/wiki/index.php/1996_AIME_Problems/Problem_7</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/3/993726c46cf1e3aae9c28fd10602f60f078311ad.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(100pt); draw((1,0)--(8,0),linewidth(0.5)); draw((1,2)--(6,2),linewidth(0.5)); draw((1,4)--(4,4),linewidth(0.5)); draw((1,6)--(2,6),linewidth(0.5)); draw((2,6)--(2,0),linewidth(0.5)); draw((4,4)--(4,0),linewidth(0.5)); draw((6,2)--(6,0),linewidth(0.5)); draw((1,0)--(1,7),dashed+linewidth(0.5)); draw((1,7)--(8,0),dashed+linewidth(0.5)); [/asy]" width="168" height="168" />
</p><p>Imagine folding the scanning code along its lines of symmetry. There will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> regions which you have control over coloring. Since we must subtract off <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> cases for the all-black and all-white cases, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/5/725f99eaea80983b28986b53a1eb793c4b763698.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{10}-2=\boxed{\textbf{(B) } 1022.}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="166" height="28" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>This <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/4/5d4f93df0d5696a7a30826c6a7c604d1352c6198.png" class="latex" alt="$7 \times 7$" width="40" height="12" /> square drawn in Solution 1 satisfies the conditions given in the problem. Calculating the number of ways of coloring it will solve the problem.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/e/78ea46dd064f1505682597da32c8dcb3a351a9e6.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline K &amp; J &amp; H &amp; G &amp; H &amp; J &amp; K \\ \hline J &amp; F &amp; E &amp; D &amp; E &amp; F &amp; J \\ \hline H &amp; E &amp; C &amp; B &amp; C &amp; E &amp; H \\ \hline G &amp; D &amp; B &amp; A &amp; B &amp; D &amp; G \\ \hline H &amp; E &amp; C &amp; B &amp; C &amp; E &amp; H \\ \hline J &amp; F &amp; E &amp; D &amp; E &amp; F &amp; J \\ \hline K &amp; J &amp; H &amp; G &amp; H &amp; J &amp; K \\ \hline \end{tabular}\]" width="230" height="145" />
</p><p>In the grid, 10 letters are used: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e28ce12d49d39f160d5a0ef54077fc98e4b9d2b.png" class="latex" alt="$G$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/9/b1902d279ba37d60bdce4e0e987b7cd19d48974e.png" class="latex" alt="$H$" width="16" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/b/abb5588023cfa1ac14643e9778699f03eecc57a3.png" class="latex" alt="$J$" width="12" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/b/dfb064112b6c94470339f6571f69d07afc1c024c.png" class="latex" alt="$K$" width="16" height="12" />. Each of the letters must have its own color, either white or black. This means, for example, all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/b/dfb064112b6c94470339f6571f69d07afc1c024c.png" class="latex" alt="$K$" width="16" height="12" />'s must have the same color for the grid to be symmetrical.
</p><p>So there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/b/b3b2422eb1e019642032c00b0c5d5982903fe45d.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{10}$" width="21" height="15" /> ways to color the grid, including a completely black grid and a completely white grid. Since the grid must contain at least one square with each color, the number of ways is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/a/55a5963089ed1328eceee00d51cde9b06777407b.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{10}-2=1024-2=$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="165" height="15" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/4/2f4459a6769f6ce685b50b74e741f8665ab7bc46.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B) } 1022}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="83" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/8/30813847c1a6a391c8c6368465c8a8337f0cb9e0.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline T &amp; T &amp; T &amp; X &amp; T &amp; T &amp; T \\ \hline T &amp; T &amp; T &amp; Y&amp; T &amp; T &amp; T \\ \hline T &amp; T &amp; T &amp; Z &amp; T &amp; T &amp; T \\ \hline X &amp; Y &amp; Z &amp; W &amp; Z &amp; Y &amp; X \\ \hline T &amp; T &amp; T &amp; Z &amp; T &amp; T &amp; T \\ \hline T &amp; T &amp; T &amp; Y &amp; T &amp; T &amp; T \\ \hline T &amp; T &amp; T &amp; X &amp; T &amp; T &amp; T \\ \hline \end{tabular}\]" width="231" height="145" />
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/7/d278803d72fcc493afb1559174a483aa7f41d143.png" class="latex" alt="$3 \times 3$" width="40" height="12" /> squares in the corners of this <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/4/5d4f93df0d5696a7a30826c6a7c604d1352c6198.png" class="latex" alt="$7 \times 7$" width="40" height="12" /> square, and there is a horizontal and vertical stripe through the middle. Because we need to have symmetry when the diagonals and midpoints of the large square is connected, we can create a table like this: (Different letters represent different color choices between black and white)
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/8/4485cb12038ad89a92620c16ef5cb752449440d0.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|} \hline A &amp; B &amp; C \\ \hline B &amp; D &amp; E \\ \hline C &amp; E &amp; F \\ \hline \end{tabular}\]" width="98" height="63" />
(Note that coloring one <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/7/d278803d72fcc493afb1559174a483aa7f41d143.png" class="latex" alt="$3 \times 3$" width="40" height="12" /> square will also determine the colorings of the other <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> because the large <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/4/5d4f93df0d5696a7a30826c6a7c604d1352c6198.png" class="latex" alt="$7 \times 7$" width="40" height="12" /> square must look the same when it is rotated by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/e/22ec0d2e4202839322d26a23dcb13f79821df5d7.png" class="latex" alt="$90^\circ$" width="24" height="13" />)
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> different letters and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> choices of color (black and white) for each letter, so there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/3/c23f7dacd533c95a26bd926214b42d6cc00fb29f.png" class="latex" alt="$2^6=64$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="59" height="15" /> colorings of a proper <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/7/d278803d72fcc493afb1559174a483aa7f41d143.png" class="latex" alt="$3 \times 3$" width="40" height="12" /> square. Now, all that's left are the horizontal and vertical stripes. Using similar logic, we can see that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> different letters, so there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/8/9584bdb500d49089d884caad5e9f4484b6267bbf.png" class="latex" alt="$2^4=16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="58" height="15" /> different colorings. Multiplying them together gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/6/0f656921ab7904a3829d77eccc955f816ddd2da4.png" class="latex" alt="$16 \times 64 = 1024$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="119" height="13" />. Going back to the question, we see that "there must be at least one square of each color in this grid of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/c/34c8e6bf4d57cd6f2bb0d7d43e00f0f442f53b84.png" class="latex" alt="$49$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> squares." We must then eliminate <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> options: an all-black grid and an all-white grid. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/9/e79f33cbbc9e1282512dabbddfb603cf540e1f20.png" class="latex" alt="$1024-2= \boxed{\textbf{(B)} 1022} \\ \phantom{}$" style="vertical-align: 10px" width="168" height="28" /> 
-hansenhe
</p>
