<p>Right triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> has leg lengths <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/5/775555f62256a77b1244bfe91ea5b61c033bb476.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=20$" width="70" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/1/ed1c81d6c115b80b9bacee43e8607767c92de967.png" class="latex" alt="$BC=21$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="70" height="13" />. Including <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/3/e33fe7d65facd8868f58b6e94ddc7f153a5a3f9f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BC}$" width="29" height="15" />, how many line segments with integer length can be drawn from vertex <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> to a point on hypotenuse <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/5/12517bbc31cb9026c71299b7a0d232d07e60f284.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AC}$" width="28" height="15" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/f/3ffff03db6c00014af49657a228c3778d38c6b00.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }5 \qquad \textbf{(B) }8 \qquad \textbf{(C) }12 \qquad \textbf{(D) }13 \qquad \textbf{(E) }15 \qquad$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="380" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/f/0ef5ecee03d86a1f795fcb2c11283dd1a2d4017d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(4); pair A, B, C, E, P; A=(-20, 0); B=origin; C=(0,21); E=(-21, 20); P=extension(B,E, A, C); draw(A--B--C--cycle); draw(B--P); dot(&quot;$A$&quot;, A, SW); dot(&quot;$B$&quot;, B, SE); dot(&quot;$C$&quot;, C, NE); dot(&quot;$P$&quot;, P, NW); [/asy]" width="178" height="182" />
</p><p>As the problem has no diagram, we draw a diagram. The hypotenuse has length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/5/0657f5cd42256c0a39d45b265235e2a939625515.png" class="latex" alt="$29$" width="17" height="12" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> be the foot of the altitude from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/7/a179ff2638e4799cadd820db205c2beff6299ce9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC$" width="27" height="13" />. Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/7/f874b39870586f0504f50a667f2bafedddf47904.png" class="latex" alt="$BP$" width="28" height="12" /> is the shortest possible length of any segment. Writing the area of the triangle in two ways, we can solve for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/1/1f1fbb076ece8ffd4d3c5069b8066410e5a363dc.png" class="latex" alt="$BP=\dfrac{20\cdot  21}{29}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="104" height="37" />, which is between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/0/820a20de2847dfdc1bc5cffc8d904933150032bd.png" class="latex" alt="$14$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/9/f1965fae079a9ba2c0726c307070c2355dfcb213.png" class="latex" alt="$15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. 
</p><p>Let the line segment be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/a/b7a4b9c32938e96f30b59dcb29f410cd4c2e3982.png" class="latex" alt="$BX$" width="30" height="12" />, with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/7/a179ff2638e4799cadd820db205c2beff6299ce9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC$" width="27" height="13" />. As you move <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> along the hypotenuse from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />, the length of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/a/b7a4b9c32938e96f30b59dcb29f410cd4c2e3982.png" class="latex" alt="$BX$" width="30" height="12" /> strictly decreases, hitting all the integer values from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/f/1bf26c35467b62acb3dc526076ce076dc4aacfc5.png" class="latex" alt="$20, 19, \dots 15$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="94" height="16" /> (IVT). Similarly, moving <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> hits all the integer values from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/7/cb75558a6889a7e67925bd90665876f37bbb60bd.png" class="latex" alt="$15, 16, \dots, 21$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="102" height="16" />. This is a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/0/2e03ea24ebe7ef7a0073ab912955227fef362069.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{(D) 13}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="59" height="29" /> distinct line segments.
(asymptote diagram added by elements2015)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2_-_Circles">Solution 2 - Circles</span></h2>
<p>Note that if a circle with an integer radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> centered at vertex <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> intersects hypotenuse <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" />, the lines drawn from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> to the points of intersection are integer lengths. As in the previous solution, the shortest distance <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/3/5f36de671edb841ba2cf4743ca25c836c60f9cfc.png" class="latex" alt="$14&lt;\overline{BP}&lt;15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="113" height="16" />. As a result, a circle of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/0/820a20de2847dfdc1bc5cffc8d904933150032bd.png" class="latex" alt="$14$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> will <b>not</b> reach the hypotenuse and thus does not intersect it. We also know that a circle of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/5/f25d31faf472ddb4c698a14522fe8125c06f8dd8.png" class="latex" alt="$21$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> intersects the hypotenuse once and a circle of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/c/f8c08a1a6cf34af327ee462d225aad68db45a197.png" class="latex" alt="$\{15, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20 \}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="166" height="18" /> intersects the hypotenuse twice. Quick graphical thinking or Euclidean construction will prove this.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/8/0b81d2900bccc7870e731abd49af60e93adcb37d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(4); pair A, B, C, E, P; A=(-20, 0); B=origin; C=(0,21); E=(-21, 20); P=extension(B,E, A, C); draw(A--B--C--cycle); draw(B--P); dot(&quot;$A$&quot;, A, SW); dot(&quot;$B$&quot;, B, SE); dot(&quot;$C$&quot;, C, NE); dot(&quot;$P$&quot;, P, S); draw(arc((0,0),21, 90, 180)); draw(arc((0,0),20, 90, 180)); draw(arc((0,0),19, 90, 180)); draw(arc((0,0),18, 90, 180)); draw(arc((0,0),17, 90, 180)); draw(arc((0,0),16, 90, 180)); draw(arc((0,0),15, 90, 180)); [/asy]" width="178" height="182" />
</p><p>It follows that we can draw circles of radii <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/a/55a50e44fa5db981c3c4be44e890b4c9f4f8125c.png" class="latex" alt="$15, 16, 17, 18, 19,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="127" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/5/d859b55509f630845eb1bec092041feb5b3fe460.png" class="latex" alt="$20,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="22" height="16" /> that each contribute <b>two</b> integer lengths (since these circles intersect the hypotenuse twice) from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/5/12517bbc31cb9026c71299b7a0d232d07e60f284.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AC}$" width="28" height="15" /> and one circle of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/5/f25d31faf472ddb4c698a14522fe8125c06f8dd8.png" class="latex" alt="$21$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> that contributes only one such segment. Our answer is then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/e/51ecfade984a88a7670f07dec953eed79302951f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[6 \cdot 2 + 1 = 13 \implies \boxed{D}\]" width="181" height="24" /> ~samrocksnature
</p>
