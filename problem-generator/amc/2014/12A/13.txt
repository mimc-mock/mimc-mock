<p>A fancy bed and breakfast inn has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> rooms, each with a distinctive color-coded decor.  One day <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> friends arrive to spend the night.  There are no other guests that night.  The friends can room in any combination they wish, but with no more than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> friends per room.  In how many ways can the innkeeper assign the guests to the rooms?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/b/bfbeeb2868a821ba951bc6ba4fd8e6dba549546a.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }2100\qquad \textbf{(B) }2220\qquad \textbf{(C) }3000\qquad \textbf{(D) }3120\qquad \textbf{(E) }3125\qquad$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="489" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We can discern three cases.
</p><p><b>Case 1:</b> Each room houses one guest. In this case, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> guests to choose for the first room, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> for the second, ..., for a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/e/f0e2850ccfde503d9f36015142df292230ca1b9c.png" class="latex" alt="$5!=120$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="65" height="13" /> assignments.
</p><p><b>Case 2:</b> Three rooms house one guest; one houses two. We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/f/b5fefbb8ab341ebf885d0070f5c69a6e8c292b63.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{5}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" /> ways to choose the three rooms with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> guest, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/7/72758d868be50905b36bea0f3e4ed4a0bb17d663.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{2}{1}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" /> to choose the remaining one with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/a/fcacab3d266669d655edd5b809811a5f4c480abc.png" class="latex" alt="$5\cdot4\cdot3$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="53" height="13" /> ways to place guests in the first three rooms, with the last two residing in the two-person room, for a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/a/03ab70991f39cd19c6167fc5054bccbc1ae5641e.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{5}{3}\binom{2}{1}\cdot5\cdot4\cdot3=1200$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="201" height="53" /> ways.
</p><p><b>Case 3:</b> Two rooms house two guests; one houses one. We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/1/53130d1c08583295cd18d9042920935e82bae549.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{5}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" /> to choose the two rooms with two people, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/d/65dee855c110867e51584e5829fca4f285c61d8f.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{3}{1}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" /> to choose one remaining room for one person. Then there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> choices for the lonely person, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/2/54216aeffa0fa278199cd3ceeb64cc8400c4a8cd.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{4}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" /> for the two in the first two-person room. The last two will stay in the other two-room, so there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/2/0420160eceeb55bc84ac11fccfc2ce75d2da7091.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{5}{2}\binom{3}{1}\cdot5\cdot\binom{4}{2}=900$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="198" height="53" /> ways.
</p><p>In total, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/3/ca32fc989dda8ffd02ef2a352cdc6010880b09e1.png" class="latex" alt="$120+1200+900=2220$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="195" height="14" /> assignments, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/1/a2194b81820e8380be586c1564c3f5d03d5c100d.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B)}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="40" height="28" />.
</p><p>(Solution by AwesomeToad16)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We can work in reverse by first determining the number of combinations in which there are more than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> friends in at least one room. There are three cases:
</p><p><b>Case 1:</b> Three friends are in one room. Since there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> possible rooms in which this can occur, we are choosing three friends from the five, and the other two friends can each be in any of the four remaining rooms,
there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/1/d81b4d2ffb227397b42dc9c7bea5269cf0b8d177.png" class="latex" alt="$5\cdot\binom{5}{3}\cdot4\cdot4 = 800$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="154" height="53" /> possibilities.
</p><p><b>Case 2:</b> Four friends are in one room. Again, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> possible rooms, we are choosing four of the five friends, and the other one can be in any of the other four rooms, so there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/2/4e283ea26703d6e8f75edab9be3158cda9b5c629.png" class="latex" alt="$5\cdot\binom{5}{4}\cdot4= 100$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="132" height="53" /> possibilities.
</p><p><b>Case 3:</b> Five friends are in one room. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> possible rooms in which this can occur, so there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> possibilities.
</p><p>Since there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/3/7c368490079fe7e1b3c350e6436de634b432d12f.png" class="latex" alt="$5^5 = 3125$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="76" height="15" /> possible combinations of the friends, the number fitting the given criteria is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/a/f7a0ee289127b14b3fa837313f27410bed1ab103.png" class="latex" alt="$3125 - (800+100+5) = \boxed{2220}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="253" height="23" />.
</p>

</p><p>~ pi_is_3.14
