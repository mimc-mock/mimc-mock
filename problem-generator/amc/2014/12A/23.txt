<p>The fraction 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/1/2e1249826c419acf9b5a00eb3f3f9acb191cc1d1.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\dfrac1{99^2}=0.\overline{b_{n-1}b_{n-2}\ldots b_2b_1b_0},\]" width="211" height="37" />
</p><p>where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> is the length of the period of the repeating decimal expansion.  What is the sum <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/5/f65fb8d86531d4ed6d4a522c85c51e62274f2972.png" class="latex" alt="$b_0+b_1+\cdots+b_{n-1}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="150" height="15" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/d/89da78ebdeb8bc758e2bcd7137abe6ace4206e29.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }874\qquad \textbf{(B) }883\qquad \textbf{(C) }887\qquad \textbf{(D) }891\qquad \textbf{(E) }892\qquad$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="443" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The fraction <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/7/1a741b33c2652e9b903f40a1420679d0dcdee06a.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}{99}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" /> can be written as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/9/4390881e118c5b4c8240b842b466517591116226.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum^{\infty}_{n=1}\dfrac{1}{10^{2n}}\]" width="64" height="48" />.
Similarly, thy fraction <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/2/3b263e48248c123429879fb4d1a449ea8860f026.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}{99^2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="28" height="37" /> can be written as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/e/e5e43af96acecd4b77ff2136c074a1b50781a701.png" class="latex" alt="$\sum^{\infty}_{m=1}\dfrac{1}{10^{2m}}\sum^{\infty}_{n=1}\dfrac{1}{10^{2n}}$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="140" height="48" /> which, thy equivalent to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/a/5fa6acb4ddde579cb4a0ed9a8338a9aad195b09d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum^{\infty}_{m=1}\sum^{\infty}_{n=1} \dfrac{1}{10^{2(m+n)}}\]" width="128" height="48" />
and we can see that for each <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/4/1e4323b5dd841ea9ef2dca82e10cbc9deef9b326.png" class="latex" alt="$n+m=k$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="83" height="14" /> there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/c/7dc6fab4c2fdb4e9046030fbfdcf6ea721b05602.png" class="latex" alt="$k-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="13" />   <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/b/79b1612f577754eb38a3a61b200883fce9f03a1d.png" class="latex" alt="$(n,m)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="47" height="18" /> combinations so the above sum is equivalent to:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/d/06db84aab337411b42a9811c3454520c711583f7.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum^{\infty}_{k=2}\dfrac{k-1}{10^{2k}}\]" width="71" height="48" />
we theeby note that the sequence starts repeating at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/2/e2268394d59ffa8259b6e03efa10ad21133a6355.png" class="latex" alt="$k = 102$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="61" height="13" />
yet consider <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/0/90065a67f95644855ef2b955fc89a6e91107e38e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum^{101}_{k=99}\dfrac{k-1}{10^{2k}}=\dfrac{98}{{10^{198}}}+\dfrac{99}{{10^{200}}}+\dfrac{100}{10^{{202}}}=\dfrac{1}{10^{198}}(98+\dfrac{99}{100}+\dfrac{100}{10000})=\dfrac{1}{10^{198}}(98+\dfrac{99}{100}+\dfrac{1}{100})=\dfrac{1}{10^{198}}(98+\dfrac{100}{100})=\dfrac{1}{10^{198}}(99)\]" width="900" height="50" />
so the decimal will go from 1 to 99 skipping the number 98
and thee can easily compute the sum of the digits from 0 to 99 to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/8/ab8befb03ae1a73d638ebde336a7967cd10c8f82.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[45\cdot10\cdot2=900\]" width="122" height="13" /> subtracting the sum of the digits of 98 which is 17 we get
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/8/1f865fdf8ef47666208820fda82567e614f0df96.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[900-17=883\textbf{(B) }\qquad\]" width="145" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/f/b9f4ef18b27722e1e14a56f715893d8d00beaf25.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{99^2}\\\\ =\frac{1}{99} \cdot \frac{1}{99}\\\\ =\frac{0.\overline{01}}{99}\\\\ =0.\overline{00010203...9799}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="157" height="159" />
</p><p>So, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/9/9c935af325194e48f0bf7760edd02b01409ea10c.png" class="latex" alt="$0+0+0+1+0+2+0+3+...+9+7+9+9=2\cdot10\cdot\frac{9\cdot10}{2}-(9+8)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="588" height="37" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/8/748f63c1d6644030089d4c983f994e5c00a002aa.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B)}\ 883}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="73" height="28" />.
</p><p>There are two things to notice here. First, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/4/70444365e2b2f1ff8ab870e7c64ea5311d86f5f2.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{99}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" /> has a very simple and unique decimal expansion, as shown. Second, for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/8/0e81a2270c43eb1c6a437e2bb00bb813e106854b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{0.\overline{01}}{99}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="34" height="39" /> to itself produce a repeating decimal, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/1/a313bf06349648df7be807bee95b651604ce7a7e.png" class="latex" alt="$99$" width="17" height="12" /> has to evenly divide a sufficiently extended number of the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/c/fdcf7c34633dc11d3bef08bbedb12352d21319bd.png" class="latex" alt="$101010101..$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="90" height="13" />. This number will have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/1/a313bf06349648df7be807bee95b651604ce7a7e.png" class="latex" alt="$99$" width="17" height="12" /> ones (197 digits in total), as to be divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/8/c6878713578626763c38433b3f4c8c2205ad0c15.png" class="latex" alt="$11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" />. The enormity of this number forces us to look for a pattern, and so we divide out as shown. Indeed, upon division (seeing how the remainders always end in "501" or "601" or, at last, "9801"), we find the repeating part <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/6/e16108d2dbaf3391934a94d31b4bbd889f4ed09b.png" class="latex" alt="$.00010203040506070809101112131415....9799$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="351" height="13" />. If we wanted to further check our pattern, we could count the total number of digits in our quotient (not counting the first three): 195. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/c/5ac002e358da97f56413b26f1eee415c827fa6b1.png" class="latex" alt="$99&lt;100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="69" height="13" />, multiplying by it will produce either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> extra digits, so our quotient passes the test.
</p><p>Or note that the 1 has to be carried when you get to 100 so the 99 becomes 00 and the 1 gets carried again to 98 which becomes 99. 
</p><p><br />
</p>
