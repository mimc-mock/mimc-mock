<p>Three congruent isosceles triangles are constructed with their bases on the sides of an equilateral triangle of side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />.  The sum of the areas of the three isosceles triangles is the same as the area of the equilateral triangle.  What is the length of one of the two congruent sides of one of the isosceles triangles?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/0/4c0c50bd24a380ae01c4141c2e203ee7d5bde350.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }\dfrac{\sqrt3}4\qquad \textbf{(B) }\dfrac{\sqrt3}3\qquad \textbf{(C) }\dfrac23\qquad \textbf{(D) }\dfrac{\sqrt2}2\qquad \textbf{(E) }\dfrac{\sqrt3}2$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="432" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Reflect each of the triangles over its respective side.  Then since the areas of the triangles total to the area of the equilateral triangle, it can be seen that the triangles fill up the equilateral one and the vertices of these triangles concur at the circumcenter of the equilateral triangle.  Hence the desired answer is just its circumradius, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/e/10ea1170c77a4f71712472a0e0bc30dd7129c661.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\dfrac{\sqrt3}3\textbf{ (B)}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="74" height="53" />.
</p><p>(Solution by djmathman)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Since the total area of each <a href="/wiki/index.php/Congruent_(geometry)" title="Congruent (geometry)">congruent</a> isosceles triangle is the same, the area of each is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/9/d69e75241b5dc755a5b395b748fc53018d642bff.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}3$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> the total <a href="/wiki/index.php/Area_of_an_equilateral_triangle" title="Area of an equilateral triangle">area of the equilateral triangle</a> of side length 1, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/9/d69e75241b5dc755a5b395b748fc53018d642bff.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}3$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> x <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/b/07b30d1822446a0286407f9070fe08a4eaeeb1ba.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{\sqrt3}4$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="26" height="41" />. Likewise, the area of each can be defined as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/5/c95a1e861eb151fdc2fa3cf0ee4cff81928b47e1.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{bh}2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" /> with base <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> equaling 1, meaning that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/9/4a9023308e90d7a0d8333ba1f335d1bf32bc5941.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{h}2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="12" height="37" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/9/d69e75241b5dc755a5b395b748fc53018d642bff.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}3$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> x <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/b/07b30d1822446a0286407f9070fe08a4eaeeb1ba.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{\sqrt3}4$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="26" height="41" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/8/8189a5b5a0917b8c93350827be4038af1839139d.png" class="latex" alt="$h$" width="10" height="12" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/6/47666f5e057ca830d70a60689ed42744e1cd7880.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{\sqrt3}6$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" />. A side length of the isosceles triangle is the hypotenuse with legs <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/9/61973d2d272346b3842846d75d26d27dddc40c0c.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{b}2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/8/8189a5b5a0917b8c93350827be4038af1839139d.png" class="latex" alt="$h$" width="10" height="12" />. Using the Pythagorean Theorem, the side length is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/f/c4fcb6d69e20ef6630d888094d9d0989fde27a28.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{{(\dfrac{1}2)}^2 + {(\dfrac{\sqrt3}6)}^2}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="125" height="53" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/e/10ea1170c77a4f71712472a0e0bc30dd7129c661.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\dfrac{\sqrt3}3\textbf{ (B)}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="74" height="53" />.
</p><p>(Solution by johnstucky)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Note that each of the congruent sides meets at the circumcenter of the equilateral triangle. Therefore, they are equal to the circumradius. The area is then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/b/07b4eb0cb5269e1069fdc002af68949a73de272f.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{abc}{4R}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="27" height="38" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/0/ec0c7d6e8449f1fba08fd4784ac989dfe40f3679.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}{4R}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="29" height="38" />. The area can also be found using <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/e/6de697c8744e7a95426a25f87a9beecda5c47d4a.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{\sqrt3}{4}s^2$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="42" height="41" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/8/53837f2ded54e102c94834d7ea63a3ea8a619798.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{\sqrt3}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="26" height="41" />. Setting these equal and solving entails <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/5/0a5fce5cba2815083ea93a0985e0d8e255bfc268.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}{\sqrt3}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="30" height="43" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/e/10ea1170c77a4f71712472a0e0bc30dd7129c661.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\dfrac{\sqrt3}3\textbf{ (B)}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="74" height="53" />.
</p><p>solution by Proper
</p><p><br />
</p><p><br />
Additional Insight:
After noting that the congruent sides meet at the circumcenter, you can just use the Extended Law of Sines to see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/7/e8718f0ab5ea314cd7933a766d7157c80d48501b.png" class="latex" alt="$R = \dfrac{1}{2\sin 60^{\circ}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="160" height="43" />, which is choice B
~Extremelysupercooldude
</p>
