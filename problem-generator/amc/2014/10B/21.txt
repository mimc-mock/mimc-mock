<p>Trapezoid <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> has parallel sides <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/f/b6f3bccae16d5e31266370fced24edc41538d5da.png" class="latex" alt="$33$" width="17" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/3/7c337e833a82ec0650a1a82a4443a76307ebe7c9.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline {CD}$" width="30" height="15" /> of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/5/f25d31faf472ddb4c698a14522fe8125c06f8dd8.png" class="latex" alt="$21$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" />. The other two sides are of lengths <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/0/820a20de2847dfdc1bc5cffc8d904933150032bd.png" class="latex" alt="$14$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" />. The angles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> are acute. What is the length of the shorter diagonal of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0aed7d897ce38d40ac5bf7c7d26a214b63ba3ea.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }10\sqrt{6}\qquad\textbf{(B) }25\qquad\textbf{(C) }8\sqrt{10}\qquad\textbf{(D) }18\sqrt{2}\qquad\textbf{(E) }26$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="471" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/c/f9ca0851053447734faf67e2fea554d233570a13.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(7cm); pair A,B,C,D,CC,DD; A = (-2,7); B = (14,7); C = (10,0); D = (0,0); CC = (10,7); DD = (0,7); draw(A--B--C--D--cycle); //label(&quot;33&quot;,(A+B)/2,N); label(&quot;21&quot;,(C+D)/2,S); label(&quot;10&quot;,(A+D)/2,W); label(&quot;14&quot;,(B+C)/2,E); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,NW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,NE); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,SE); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,SW); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,DD,N); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,CC,N); draw(C--CC); draw(D--DD); [/asy]" width="332" height="172" />
</p><p>In the diagram, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/3/82304399561c9bbbd28355f0001538d85e5fcdd1.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{DE} \perp \overline{AB}, \overline{FC} \perp \overline{AB}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="172" height="19" />. 
Denote <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/d/edd8e7c503014899bfa7079243ea69ab00a33998.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AE} = x$" width="62" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/b/bcb0ff07868f6a99309fa2a1aee14913ad6f9da0.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{DE} = h$" width="64" height="15" />. In right triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/f/1/6f1bf55dee68ba4123ca736e3b4b2121ffebc98a.png" class="latex" alt="$AED$" width="43" height="13" />, we have from the Pythagorean theorem: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/b/3bbe5bda57187178092f83408440794bfcb46f2b.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2+h^2=100$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="108" height="16" />. Note that since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/e/85e66384f6436471e6db2fc5b83b070d37139f35.png" class="latex" alt="$EF = DC$" width="82" height="12" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/e/32e4bed409872576b489f0989ba3125adb639214.png" class="latex" alt="$BF = 33-DC-x = 12-x$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="230" height="13" />. Using the Pythagorean theorem in right triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/d/efdd26b7cbfad505c1adbdfccc28d3ab1eb91872.png" class="latex" alt="$BFC$" width="43" height="12" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/c/2cc51f6f2244f6fdd290377e06e3d357edc6de0a.png" class="latex" alt="$(12-x)^2 + h^2 = 196$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="163" height="19" />. 
</p><p><br />
We isolate the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/9/319c4d6557b81962a2076f2017878709cf698551.png" class="latex" alt="$h^2$" width="16" height="15" /> term in both equations, getting <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/0/a1065d321fa2989d3caf0d15c0c9574087c56df7.png" class="latex" alt="$h^2= 100-x^2$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="108" height="15" /> and
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/1/ff1ddacef6d6a27413c8ba66dc48283e9bc9028a.png" class="latex" alt="$h^2 = 196-(12-x)^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="162" height="19" />.
</p><p>Setting these equal, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/2/942906c980a60bf45f3ee51ec5db24fc5ef996f3.png" class="latex" alt="$100-x^2 = 196 - 144 + 24x -x^2 \implies 24x = 48 \implies x = 2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="472" height="16" />. Now, we can determine that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/9/8696119ba2d8f3c2e29747818af22f124d45d0db.png" class="latex" alt="$h^2 = 100-4 \implies h = 4\sqrt{6}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="218" height="18" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/5/e6521da560e1ec7a858424e7feccb41d363af4d9.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(7cm); pair A,B,C,D,CC,DD; A = (-2,7); B = (14,7); C = (10,0); D = (0,0); CC = (10,7); DD = (0,7); draw(A--B--C--D--cycle); //label(&quot;33&quot;,(A+B)/2,N); label(&quot;21&quot;,(C+D)/2,S); label(&quot;10&quot;,(A+D)/2,W); label(&quot;14&quot;,(B+C)/2,E); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,NW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,NE); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,SE); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,SW); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,SW); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,DD,SE); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,CC,SW); draw(C--CC); draw(D--DD); label(&quot;21&quot;,(CC+DD)/2,N); label(&quot;$2$&quot;,(A+DD)/2,N); label(&quot;$10$&quot;,(CC+B)/2,N); label(&quot;$4\sqrt{6}$&quot;,(C+CC)/2,W); label(&quot;$4\sqrt{6}$&quot;,(D+DD)/2,E); pair X = (-2,0); //draw(X--C--A--cycle,black+2bp); [/asy]" width="332" height="168" />
</p><p>The two diagonals are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/5/12517bbc31cb9026c71299b7a0d232d07e60f284.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AC}$" width="28" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/7/4373e7b4be983c0315e82e1fda1df66ed34dd7fb.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BD}$" width="30" height="15" />. Using the Pythagorean theorem again on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/f/8bf1a9b5d47656f521d715d9b85bd2c2d9490473.png" class="latex" alt="$\bigtriangleup AFC$" width="58" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/d/a2da1bcd0c5138dfe5f66d1ebe0b46542ed6bbc7.png" class="latex" alt="$\bigtriangleup BED$" width="60" height="13" />, we can find these lengths to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/3/ca34bc631b783aecc29ef49389a73f0977498c5d.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{96+529} = 25$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="124" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/d/76d1b476bae5560ab678ab869fae8ff794f94e77.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{96+961} = \sqrt{1057}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="158" height="19" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/0/cc04297061c012b247afae1b71fd2b70971bdd21.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{96+529}&lt;\sqrt{96+961}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="190" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/6/66644fd9f411299c3c1eabf151b5f601c9ef6c21.png" class="latex" alt="$25$" width="17" height="12" /> is the shorter length*, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/3/dc3d885331dfd1c95d782aac3adccec7dd046fb7.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B) }25}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="64" height="28" />.
</p>
<ul><li>Or, alternatively, one can notice that the two triangles have the same height but <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/f/8bf1a9b5d47656f521d715d9b85bd2c2d9490473.png" class="latex" alt="$\bigtriangleup AFC$" width="58" height="13" /> has a shorter base than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/d/a2da1bcd0c5138dfe5f66d1ebe0b46542ed6bbc7.png" class="latex" alt="$\bigtriangleup BED$" width="60" height="13" />.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/a/3ba9cc897cd3d3cc662ace19dc67b9f53085f698.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(7cm); pair A,B,C,D,E; A = (-2,7); B = (14,7); C = (10,0); D = (0,0); E = (4,7); draw(A--B--C--D--cycle); draw(D--E); label(&quot;21&quot;,(C+D)/2,S); label(&quot;10&quot;,(A+D)/2,W); label(&quot;14&quot;,(12,1),E); label(&quot;14&quot;,(2,1),E); label(&quot;12&quot;,(A+E)/2,N); label(&quot;21&quot;,(E+B)/2,N); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,NW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,NE); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,SE); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,SW); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,SW); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,E,N); [/asy]" width="332" height="172" />
</p><p>The area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/4/b64782f3ed925c790fed487d26744eb0ed100f70.png" class="latex" alt="$\Delta AED$" width="58" height="13" /> is by Heron's, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/a/5ca8160d993e3ec4abc00255c9ebd54e4a9fb3cb.png" class="latex" alt="$4\sqrt{9(4)(3)(2)}=24\sqrt{6}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="172" height="32" />. This makes the length of the altitude from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> onto <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/8/5e8bc36793a7c64d05fff20d519f0f72d9256fab.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AE}$" width="28" height="15" /> equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/2/772b05fb973d72e457456b2a4cf59dce04562003.png" class="latex" alt="$4\sqrt{6}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />. One may now proceed as in Solution <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> to obtain an answer of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/3/dc3d885331dfd1c95d782aac3adccec7dd046fb7.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B) }25}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="64" height="28" />.
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Using the same way as Solution 1, obtain that AE=2. Let us assume that point G is point A moving in a straight line down until it touches DC if DC was extended both ways by infinity. We know that GC=23. Thus the answer would be the square root of (23 square and (4*square root of 6) square). Thus we get 625. Square root of 625 would be 25.
-Reality Writes
</p>
