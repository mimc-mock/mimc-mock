<p>Four fair six-sided dice are rolled. What is the probability that at least three of the four dice show the same value?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/4/874cd8cb82e0654f319290a2438ea15362511beb.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf {(A) } \frac{1}{36} \qquad \textbf {(B) } \frac{7}{72} \qquad \textbf {(C) } \frac{1}{9} \qquad \textbf {(D) } \frac{5}{36} \qquad \textbf {(E) } \frac{1}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="399" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>We split this problem into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> cases.
</p><p>First, we calculate the probability that all four are the same. After the first dice, all the numbers must be equal to that roll, giving a probability of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/0/af004b62c752ea71dbdf7b8de35a33b64f8838b5.png" class="latex" alt="$1 \cdot \dfrac{1}{6} \cdot \dfrac{1}{6} \cdot \dfrac{1}{6} = \dfrac{1}{216}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="141" height="37" />.
</p><p>Second, we calculate the probability that three are the same and one is different. After the first dice, the next two must be equal and the third different. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> orders to roll the different dice, giving <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/5/795b12de9ca375d869ce0388899cce913c037cd8.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \cdot 1 \cdot \dfrac{1}{6} \cdot \dfrac{1}{6} \cdot \dfrac{5}{6} = \dfrac{5}{54}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="154" height="38" />.
</p><p>Adding these up, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/2/d52d445b0110603f72bae2c45afdae075e7e28c7.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{7}{72}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/1/a2194b81820e8380be586c1564c3f5d03d5c100d.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B)}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="40" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Note that there are two cases for this problem
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/5/9154c3cf3af5df5469bbb7b40718758979f2bb95.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{Case 1}$" width="54" height="11" />: Exactly three of the dices show the same value.
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> values that the remaining die can take on, and there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/5/6e5cd3ae46a937d51bc177caf31a4147709ce168.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{4}{3}=4$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="70" height="53" /> ways to choose the die. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> ways that this can happen. Hence, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/4/bd41ea2eb0e2912f2f65b42b3dc2030a6223921c.png" class="latex" alt="$6\cdot 4\cdot5=120$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="104" height="13" /> ways.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/1/901dd3d64f25ed84522adca7c7693a46693cbcf5.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{Case 2}$" width="54" height="11" />: Exactly four of the dices show the same value.
</p><p>This can happen in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> ways.
</p><p>Hence, the probability is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/9/069e95624ca3b8f2cbc88ded1736438dc0715591.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{120+6}{6^{4}}=\frac{21}{216}\implies \frac{7}{72}\implies \boxed{\textbf{(B)}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="279" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>We solve using PIE.
</p><p>We first calculate the number of ways that we can have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> dice be the same and the other dice be anything. We therefore have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/4/0146848d7c58cc28c2dee10f3affeb24f742f876.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{4}{3} \cdot 6 \cdot 6 = 144$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="133" height="53" /> ways to have at least <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> dice be the same.
</p><p>But wait! We have overcounted the case where all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> dice are the same! Since the previous case occurs in each of these cases <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> times, we must subtract the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />-dice total three times in order to have them counted once. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> ways to have four dice be the same, so we our total count is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/9/739b13da9f90e37e5d016d03408de4d04eb56475.png" class="latex" alt="$144 - 3(6) = 126$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="132" height="18" />.
</p><p>Therefore, our probability is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/7/99768ed17cebcd728b3550f17a75699514ed067c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{126}{6^4} = \boxed{\frac{7}{72}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="89" height="48" />, which is answer choice <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/1/a2194b81820e8380be586c1564c3f5d03d5c100d.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B)}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="40" height="28" />.
</p><p>-FIREDRAGONMATH16
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>There are two cases to consider: Three of the dice roll the same number, and all four of the dice roll the same number. 
</p><p><br />
For the first case, there is a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/b/79b434cc056bf004a266516a536c6f8f6c417ec1.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{6^4}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="19" height="37" /> chance that one number will be rolled four times in a row. Since there are six numbers on a die, we multiply by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> to see that the probability for the first case is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/7/f272f2d107e44d88efe072188f972fb6bdb08d71.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{6^3}.$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="27" height="38" /> 
</p><p><br />
For the second case, consider the roll <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/1/5613f5027d7aa2ddb768800bea7fea760ea62cb1.png" class="latex" alt="$AAAB$" width="54" height="13" />, where three of the dice are identical and the fourth differs. The probability of the first three rolling the same number is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/d/81d42316da6f51313f1ae29c0f5262644674784a.png" class="latex" alt="$1\cdot{\frac{1}{6}}\cdot{\frac{1}{6}},$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="68" height="38" /> because the first number can be anything, and the second must be identical. The probability of the last roll being different is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/3/5533bc48602603f16d18c04858cad119c04833c0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />, as it can be anything except for what has been previously rolled.
</p><p><br />
Multiplying these together, the probability for the second case is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/8/f88c40b55ec8d2e9fbbcc6d6836060c013d81429.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5}{6^3}.$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="27" height="38" /> However, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/0/cb0c01876b3199f023ba3f336164642b146425e4.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{4!}{3!\cdot{1!}} = 4$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="81" height="39" /> ways to arrange <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/1/5613f5027d7aa2ddb768800bea7fea760ea62cb1.png" class="latex" alt="$AAAB$" width="54" height="13" />, so we must multiply by a factor of 4 to get the true probability for this case, which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/4/9d407095c0c78ddeeb7c9f5a607f512e272ba559.png" class="latex" alt="$4(\frac{5}{6^4}) = \frac{20}{6^4}.$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="97" height="38" />
</p><p><br />
Adding these two cases, we get the requested probability: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/9/509d949bc50bed316136c11ade2bd4d8273ea1ab.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1+20}{6^3} = \frac{21}{216} = \frac{7}{72},$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="162" height="38" /> or answer choice <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/1/a2194b81820e8380be586c1564c3f5d03d5c100d.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B)}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="40" height="28" />
</p><p><br />
-Benedict T (countmath1)
</p>
