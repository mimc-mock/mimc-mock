<p>The two legs of a right triangle, which are altitudes, have lengths <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/5/5d590f3358bac936c098c5c9b60ecdf068b17371.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt3$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />. How long is the third altitude of the triangle?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/5/9959d47fffb760b6cf89894df252b99436e4d8f5.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 1\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 2\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 3\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 4\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 5$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="348" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>We find that the area of the triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/3/ca33857ca26506f41045eb76c6a8c74214c6276c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{6\times 2\sqrt{3}}{2} =6\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="128" height="43" />. By the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Pythagorean_Theorem" title="Pythagorean Theorem">Pythagorean Theorem</a>, we have that the length of the hypotenuse is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/3/733044a4c211f8b9337ae8215cb32e1e28b1285b.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{(2\sqrt{3})^2+6^2}=4\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -8px" width="169" height="32" />. Dropping an altitude from the right angle to the hypotenuse, we can calculate the area in another way.
</p><p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/8/8189a5b5a0917b8c93350827be4038af1839139d.png" class="latex" alt="$h$" width="10" height="12" /> be the third height of the triangle. We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/e/9ee9aea7ebfd23abe0bde4c482dc1bd25c0f2dd8.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{4\sqrt{3}h}{2}=6\sqrt{3}\implies h = \frac{6 \cdot 2}{4} \implies h=\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ 3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="365" height="43" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>By the Pythagorean Theorem, we have that the length of the hypotenuse is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/3/733044a4c211f8b9337ae8215cb32e1e28b1285b.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{(2\sqrt{3})^2+6^2}=4\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -8px" width="169" height="32" />. Notice that we now have a 30-60-90 triangle, with the angle between sides <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/5/e455d80f4657b1112ba302a6e2c42e9c81f780ee.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/b/a9bddfa0dd561449e70720e765113a3c75dc6382.png" class="latex" alt="$4\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" /> equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/d/08d5c45388de5c75af641c046d466de3592ffa98.png" class="latex" alt="$60^{\circ}$" width="24" height="13" />. Dropping an altitude from the right angle to the hypotenuse, we see that our desired height is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/8/c88676177603d4e7134e7819bb31db2c8ca62f28.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ 3}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="55" height="28" /> (We can also check from the other side).
</p>
