<p>A pair of standard <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />-sided dice is rolled once. The sum of the numbers rolled determines the diameter of a circle. What is the probability that the numerical value of the area of the circle is less than the numerical value of the circle's circumference?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/2/be287cfc64dd90985523555323fec49cccf79d25.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{1}{36} \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ \frac{1}{12} \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ \frac{1}{6} \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ \frac{1}{4} \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ \frac{5}{18}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="394" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>For the circumference to be greater than the area, we must have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/b/a4b762907122c836f221242a6fc07e23e678cd9e.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi d &gt; \pi \left( \frac{d}{2} \right) ^2$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="104" height="46" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/0/ec0062e1b0b4aeaab69aaf5044069ffadbec8280.png" class="latex" alt="$d&lt;4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="13" />. Now since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> is determined by a sum of two dice, the only possibilities for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> are thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />. In order for two dice to sum to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, they most both show a value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. The probability of this happening is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/1/731f6869a5c488a1a8bba39817cc1591f7815e09.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{6} \times \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{36}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="93" height="37" />. In order for two dice to sum to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />, one must show a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> and the other must show a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />. Since this can happen in two ways, the probability of this event occurring is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/d/48d91f444e570db06921dc5566771c12aaf88037.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \times \frac{1}{6} \times \frac{1}{6} = \frac{2}{36}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="124" height="37" />. The sum of these two probabilities now gives the final answer: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/b/c5bc0d13eb53f9ab392a16fef023ee6592758f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{36} + \frac{2}{36} = \frac{3}{36} = \frac{1}{12} \rightarrow \boxed{\textbf{B}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="213" height="38" />
</p>
