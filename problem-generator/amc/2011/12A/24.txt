<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Note as above that ABCD must be tangential to obtain the circle with maximal radius. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e28ce12d49d39f160d5a0ef54077fc98e4b9d2b.png" class="latex" alt="$G$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/9/b1902d279ba37d60bdce4e0e987b7cd19d48974e.png" class="latex" alt="$H$" width="16" height="12" /> be the points on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/4/0948661e822f2a953c43a57ac9e40b2734476de4.png" class="latex" alt="$CD$" width="29" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/3/0b31cd391bebc36897a8c10ea78e5d584980b049.png" class="latex" alt="$DA$" width="29" height="13" /> respectively where the circle is tangent. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/0/870bd4e7e7d40b2bfa324853d7ec4ef1014c2336.png" class="latex" alt="$\theta=\angle BAD$" width="89" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/8/728963c4921c823fe45e2e97f463b3fba636a9d9.png" class="latex" alt="$\alpha=\angle ADC$" width="92" height="13" />. Since the quadrilateral is cyclic(because we want to maximize the circle, so we set the quadrilateral to be cyclic), <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/1/a1184d41f201179eb55444d3a845b0cfe11cba91.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle ABC=180^{\circ}-\alpha$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="148" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/0/5403d3e5c22b3becc3fb50d091c4f9f0e2488428.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BCD=180^{\circ}-\theta$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="147" height="13" />. Let the circle have center <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" /> and radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />. Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/c/c5c5aae3a2e4f7c28e415432df0119b556f040f8.png" class="latex" alt="$OHD$" width="46" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/d/e5d3dd849052f9a889796920dd3d7dcb6e816d3b.png" class="latex" alt="$OGC$" width="43" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/c/44cc455fb75ab658fa40b887505970e7a4122684.png" class="latex" alt="$OFB$" width="42" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/9/b993f0fe6fd7f85c4fd135b6fee75f4200431ab6.png" class="latex" alt="$OEA$" width="42" height="13" /> are right angles. 
</p><p>Hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/0/5f0847e000d7be2e83c751c21ac50d1cec854dc7.png" class="latex" alt="$FOG=\theta$" width="75" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/6/f56b843355e2cb58b9b6f2b41dcda465ac949446.png" class="latex" alt="$GOH=180^{\circ}-\alpha$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="137" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/d/97da0bb17b15090fce31d2870391269f0edd8bc0.png" class="latex" alt="$EOH=180^{\circ}-\theta$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="135" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/0/bc054e962cfd97d212ea84df0c340bc97491326c.png" class="latex" alt="$FOE=\alpha$" width="78" height="12" />. 
</p><p>Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/5/7758341c74d404fb0083c0de0628d0f7255a69d7.png" class="latex" alt="$AEOH\sim OFCG$" width="140" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/f/f4fcfea12c0b33ad3430e393bce8d214d57460b2.png" class="latex" alt="$EBFO\sim HOGD$" width="142" height="12" />. 
</p><p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/7/f075d8e77b16e2e748b49a47fdce475219b6ecdd.png" class="latex" alt="$x=CG$" width="63" height="12" />. Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/3/0d3e56f92344bf5332032c7d9818ed749bc50c68.png" class="latex" alt="$CF=x$" width="63" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/c/16c894a061c85dbdf6f6acf035f57e3718079c91.png" class="latex" alt="$BF=BE=9-x$" width="148" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/e/3ae79ff3fa6d4dc3862617c6bb4b975046c46b42.png" class="latex" alt="$GD=DH=7-x$" width="152" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/3/2936d5abdee5e37087979dbc83fff67a09f17aca.png" class="latex" alt="$AH=AE=x+5$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="148" height="14" />. Using <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/5/7758341c74d404fb0083c0de0628d0f7255a69d7.png" class="latex" alt="$AEOH\sim OFCG$" width="140" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/f/f4fcfea12c0b33ad3430e393bce8d214d57460b2.png" class="latex" alt="$EBFO\sim HOGD$" width="142" height="12" /> we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/c/f1c3ef8a215e1a5409942f88dadfb187971b734e.png" class="latex" alt="$r/(x+5)=x/r$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="125" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/9/8695e0a6ff7f6d2df2d9d2a8a13d7b25238740fb.png" class="latex" alt="$(9-x)/r=r/(7-x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="170" height="18" />. By equating the value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/2/e2221c84f16a3f75ae07e5023384cc40d9c05357.png" class="latex" alt="$r^2$" width="14" height="15" /> from each, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/0/f90504bb587c2746c8a272f7651a258d7c05b679.png" class="latex" alt="$x(x+5)=(7-x)(9-x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="200" height="18" />. Solving we obtain <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/2/b022dc8ef6d33931fc1ae3d27df3ea0a81b52936.png" class="latex" alt="$x=3$" width="43" height="12" /> so that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/e/cfe79efccfc86efb2d1976a24423fe54cf3148f8.png" class="latex" alt="$r=\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ 2\sqrt{6}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="112" height="33" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>To maximize the radius of the circle, we also need to maximize its area. To maximize the area of the circle, the quadrilateral must be tangential (have an incircle). In a tangential quadrilateral, the sum of opposite sides is equal to the semiperimeter of the quadrilateral. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/d/91d93f76ed341e0487f837eacc7f68bfe6cf468b.png" class="latex" alt="$14+7=12+9$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="122" height="14" />, so this particular quadrilateral has an incircle. By definition, given <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> side lengths, a cyclic quadrilateral has the maximum area of any quadrilateral with those side lengths. Therefore, to maximize the area of the quadrilateral and thus the incircle, we assume that this quadrilateral is cyclic. 
</p><p>For cyclic quadrilaterals, <a href="/wiki/index.php/Brahmagupta%27s_formula" class="mw-redirect" title="Brahmagupta&#39;s formula">Brahmagupta's formula</a> gives the area as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/1/ec165011b7b836e991da4bf263b05d932f056b94.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)(s-d)}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="232" height="32" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" /> is the semiperimeter and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/0/2d02bb2275100261109b043261e702124460a83e.png" class="latex" alt="$a, b, c,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="45" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> are the side lengths. Breaking it up into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> triangles, we see the area of a tangential quadrilateral is also equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/8/3d84c3c39b42fb2b850558fa117ecb2df8c1233d.png" class="latex" alt="$r*s$" width="34" height="8" />. Equate these two equations.
Substituting <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" />, the semiperimeter, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, the area and solving for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />,we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/1/831c7cde6aa3012d53ba135c93cdb7643a8f40f4.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ 2\sqrt{6}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="79" height="33" />.
</p>
