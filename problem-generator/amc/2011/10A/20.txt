<p>Two points on the circumference of a circle of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> are selected independently and at random. From each point a chord of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> is drawn in a clockwise direction. What is the probability that the two chords intersect?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/1/4e1aadb05de22a36916774168f491dc3267e596c.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{1}{6}\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{1}{5}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{1}{4}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{1}{3}\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="367" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Fix a point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> from which we draw a clockwise chord. In order for the clockwise chord from another point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> to intersect that of point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> must be no more than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> units apart. By drawing the circle, we quickly see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> can be on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/e/4cedbb01bbcaee13c150a8d20ddabb8d35e22df0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{120}{360}=\boxed{\frac{1}{3}  \ \textbf{(D)}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="115" height="48" /> of the perimeter of the circle. (Imagine a regular hexagon inscribed in the circle)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Do what Solution 1 did until the guessing part. We then realize that the chords and radii make an equilateral triangle of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />. Therefore the arc degree is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/c/d9cc0e60360d27bb0d68fba6cd6fc1f5163aedd2.png" class="latex" alt="$60.$" width="21" height="12" /> The other arc degree is also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/c/d9cc0e60360d27bb0d68fba6cd6fc1f5163aedd2.png" class="latex" alt="$60.$" width="21" height="12" /> Therefore the sum is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/7/557ee2448ea207545d7db5c8bc28897950b52175.png" class="latex" alt="$120.$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="30" height="13" /> Continue as follows.
</p>
