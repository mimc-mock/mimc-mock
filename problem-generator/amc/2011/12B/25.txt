</p><p><br />
For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/2/bc250069c3955c465dc8e9df78fc00d80e9c1c9c.png" class="latex" alt="$k &gt; \frac{200}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="64" height="37" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/7/86703d93f687e1ec52fa49632bd087c3e52b18c7.png" class="latex" alt="$\left[\frac{100}{k}\right] = 1$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="82" height="43" />. 3 of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> that we should consider land in here.

</p><p><br />
If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/6011055dfbf1965f27662e00d836c10c5f1719a4.png" class="latex" alt="$\left[\frac{100}{k}\right]$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="45" height="43" /> got round down, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/2/6824caf8e57ec63ed69638b47e531fb46d0199ee.png" class="latex" alt="$1 \le n \le \frac{k}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="81" height="37" /> all satisfy the condition along with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/f/14f9a7f0a078b361958b8019646cc77aaac7ef78.png" class="latex" alt="$n = k$" width="44" height="12" />
</p><p>because if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/4/f24821d405d24fc7e049b0b16e8c30c72eb69d68.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{fpart}\left(\frac{100}{k}\right) &lt; \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="131" height="43" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/f/c2fbc6b63431e5eba99db7ef0ecf5d659a8b0b25.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{fpart} \left(\frac{n}{k}\right) &lt; \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="110" height="37" />, so must <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/4/854dc0a90cc5d8c8c0a1a33a846fd0ec57490bc6.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{fpart} \left(\frac{100 - n}{k}\right) &lt; \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="165" height="43" />
</p><p>and for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/f/14f9a7f0a078b361958b8019646cc77aaac7ef78.png" class="latex" alt="$n = k$" width="44" height="12" />, it is the same as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/6/2d6fc266463b33c8b1c079a50909f3fd03ed5ffd.png" class="latex" alt="$n = 0$" width="43" height="12" />.
</p><p>, which makes 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/c/81cc36671489896e18246bda66f4e25699ea396f.png" class="latex" alt="$P(k) \ge \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2k}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="119" height="37" />.
</p><p><br />
If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/6011055dfbf1965f27662e00d836c10c5f1719a4.png" class="latex" alt="$\left[\frac{100}{k}\right]$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="45" height="43" /> got round up, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/0/270636a0fd0c4ba37caf8162b1833bce1c7f2f4a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{k}{2} \le n \le k$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="83" height="37" /> all satisfy the condition along with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/6/c766f9fce6070981e62f805a7cf7294baed4498e.png" class="latex" alt="$n = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="12" />
</p><p><br />
because if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/f/5ff7787b58a26fe7e790cb5e406b61e794a6e173.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{fpart}\left(\frac{100}{k}\right) &gt; \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="131" height="43" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/4/e643c1bc552b1522acbce851ebe8936bc21b7976.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{fpart} \left(\frac{n}{k}\right) &gt; \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="110" height="37" />
</p><p>Case 1)
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/4/854dc0a90cc5d8c8c0a1a33a846fd0ec57490bc6.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{fpart} \left(\frac{100 - n}{k}\right) &lt; \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="165" height="43" /> 
</p><p>-&gt; <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/b/96bdf9e72da90887e7978840f90bb650eeafe83d.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{fpart}\left(\frac{100}{k}\right) = \text{fpart} \left(\frac{n}{k}\right) +\text{fpart} \left(\frac{100 - n}{k}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="342" height="43" /> 
</p><p>Case 2)
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/a/41a35858cb97806cbc525126304cf97208c34464.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{fpart} \left(\frac{100 - n}{k}\right) &gt; \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="165" height="43" /> 
</p><p>-&gt; <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/e/d3e3d00ceb17c0075b6370c2c836856e8373b9c2.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{fpart}\left(\frac{100}{k}\right) + 1 = \text{fpart} \left(\frac{n}{k}\right) +\text{fpart} \left(\frac{100 - n}{k}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="374" height="43" /> 
</p><p><br />
and for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/6/c766f9fce6070981e62f805a7cf7294baed4498e.png" class="latex" alt="$n = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="12" />, since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> is odd, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/0/6d07a284176626b45584b1e9154aed0e6212e811.png" class="latex" alt="$\left[\frac{99}{k}\right] \neq \left[\frac{100}{k}\right]$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="111" height="43" /> 
</p><p>-&gt; <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/a/8da906c78f8647ca6ff2ccb3f73eb5966a2d4a9e.png" class="latex" alt="$99.5 = k (p + .5)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="124" height="18" />  -&gt; <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/e/f1ef8249f9b744122e85bf2245072d733e46b9ce.png" class="latex" alt="$199 = k (2p + 1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="123" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/3/0f31ff033089cb9cfa5a6ce6cd9a0ce6b5c57422.png" class="latex" alt="$199$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> is prime so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/f/00fab47c0b9d0ce398d03e57d6720129d53434f2.png" class="latex" alt="$k = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="13" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/e/b8ecac2603a9f1fb9342567ccecf56aab677f0b5.png" class="latex" alt="$k =199$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="61" height="13" />, which is not in this set
</p><p>, which makes 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/c/81cc36671489896e18246bda66f4e25699ea396f.png" class="latex" alt="$P(k) \ge \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2k}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="119" height="37" />.
</p><p><br />
Now the only case without rounding, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/f/00fab47c0b9d0ce398d03e57d6720129d53434f2.png" class="latex" alt="$k = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="13" />. It must be true.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>It suffices to consider <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/2/ee229b474e444ed7ea397f7f9711e0b55cbe6be9.png" class="latex" alt="$0\le n &lt; k-1.$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="113" height="15" /> Now for each of these <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/4/c54e7152acd659788861f0453ac195c635e0a005.png" class="latex" alt="$n,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="15" height="11" /> let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/e/69ea981866f74dc92c8f4d17c68c62979e2ff452.png" class="latex" alt="$f(n)=\left[\frac{n}{k}\right], g(n)=\left[\frac{100}{k}\right]-\left[\frac{100-n}{k}\right].$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="327" height="45" /> If we let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/8/5e85c24799cb6eeeb42bc36df1fa85fadee2f1c9.png" class="latex" alt="$k=67,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="56" height="16" /> then the following graphs result for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/2/bb2c93730dbb48558bb3c4738c956c4e8f816437.png" class="latex" alt="$f$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/6/ff6dc3c1362b8ec95b7e5fd733ee15d949286c15.png" class="latex" alt="$g.$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="11" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/1/b4181a762d816ecb36d8d732e1d956c26da1c681.png" class="latex" alt="$f:$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="19" height="16" /> 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/c/14c273b0b4cb3f9d24c44255247bb85d0dbf422a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(10cm); for (int i = 0; i &lt; 67; ++i) { 	if (i&lt;=33) dot((i,0));     else dot((i,1)); } label(&quot;(0,0)&quot;,(0,0),SW); label(&quot;(66,1)&quot;,(66,1),NE); [/asy]" width="475" height="62" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/6/ad6b2df4617e3583ab1d34583bdeb7efa4eab6f7.png" class="latex" alt="$g:$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="18" height="11" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/1/171af043dce2de6988de612217afb07df93e5f21.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(10cm); for (int i = 0; i &lt; 67; ++i) { 	dot((i,0)); } label(&quot;(0,0)&quot;,(0,0),NW); label(&quot;(66,0)&quot;,(66,0),SE); [/asy]" width="475" height="55" />
</p><p>Our probability is the number of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/8/3188c60bcc5b67e8ec3fb7ed21a54bb00ba3f786.png" class="latex" alt="$0\le i&lt;k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="73" height="15" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/3/3934fd8f52694b8fdd4dfb6bb836ca69e7f352d2.png" class="latex" alt="$f(i)+g(i)=0$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="116" height="18" /> over <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/4/5d48e0968a64d782fe75bca620ce749938b7d0bb.png" class="latex" alt="$k.$" width="13" height="12" /> Of course, this always holds for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/0/0c083ad92a7539dd40c35425bc483ababe56ecb7.png" class="latex" alt="$i=0.$" width="43" height="12" /> If we let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> vary, then  the graph of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/2/bb2c93730dbb48558bb3c4738c956c4e8f816437.png" class="latex" alt="$f$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="16" /> is always very similar to what it looks like above (groups of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/9/ad92c8dacdeaf2ef65f9d82064d008eaa0e57c21.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{k+1}{2},\frac{k-1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="97" height="37" /> dots). However, the graph of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/1/311cabda3a9b09f0dde217303ca9d1cd9201dcf6.png" class="latex" alt="$g$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> can vary greatly. In the above diagram, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/1/b813b02386eae410e77aa4752337c52e45a01f4e.png" class="latex" alt="$g(i)=0$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="62" height="18" /> for all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/7/8c7b75235740f61a772820eb1b981f55c080ebaa.png" class="latex" alt="$i,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="16" /> while it is possible for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/d/34d3d2890afb62494aff2f1cefe72cb7c4ac19fc.png" class="latex" alt="$g(i)=-1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="76" height="18" /> for all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/f/fcfea14ba85ddf01e852f8cf169578f0c0b9c790.png" class="latex" alt="$i\neq 0.$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="43" height="17" /> In order to minimize the number of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/8/34857b3ba74ce5cd8607f3ebd23e9015908ada71.png" class="latex" alt="$i$" width="5" height="12" /> which satisfy <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/b/ccb06e92b542ce10cb15f1aef8fc2cec5e707de8.png" class="latex" alt="$f(i)+g(i)=0,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="120" height="18" /> we either want <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/1/b813b02386eae410e77aa4752337c52e45a01f4e.png" class="latex" alt="$g(i)=0$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="62" height="18" /> for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/a/2fae2ef552ecba296032606be4a7894784ee5131.png" class="latex" alt="$0\le i&lt;k,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="78" height="16" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/d/34d3d2890afb62494aff2f1cefe72cb7c4ac19fc.png" class="latex" alt="$g(i)=-1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="76" height="18" /> for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/0/c5039a47bb4737cbabf394e87f72a1a453b491e1.png" class="latex" alt="$1\le i&lt;k.$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="77" height="15" /> This way, we see that at least half of the numbers from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/c/7dc6fab4c2fdb4e9046030fbfdcf6ea721b05602.png" class="latex" alt="$k-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="13" /> satisfy the given equation. So, our desired probability is at least <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/e/b6ebde9b554d0891679c8f5a427fbf91c4ed5308.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{k+1}{2k}.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="49" height="37" /> As shown by the diagram above, the probability is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/f/8ffd86b0a6fdeff0827c1cf3be3302b6b58575cd.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{34}{67}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" /> for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/3/463b86ba197b326846e87c02f8c0a147d3c7721d.png" class="latex" alt="$k=67.$" width="56" height="12" /> Clearly no better solutions can exist when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/3/9f3fde2a1191ee8396f90edc231127a54736e4e5.png" class="latex" alt="$k&lt;67.$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="56" height="13" /> On the other hand, for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/c/33c261e38b09f13b5599f4b56d9e1fb0b17de444.png" class="latex" alt="$k&gt;67$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="52" height="13" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/1/711f5fc6f8d53d2565cff62b15f932f02e0dbc58.png" class="latex" alt="$87$" width="18" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/1/a313bf06349648df7be807bee95b651604ce7a7e.png" class="latex" alt="$99$" width="17" height="12" /> do not yield better probabilities. Therefore, our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/6/3d657da2d62b1d61f88ea1dc87b12e45b4fdaa6d.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\frac{34}{67}}.$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="37" height="48" />
</p>
