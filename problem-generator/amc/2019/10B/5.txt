<p>Triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> lies in the first quadrant. Points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> are reflected across the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/1/741a74d86df899c10724dc2f80638f5e7f599194.png" class="latex" alt="$y=x$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="11" /> to points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/9/1090530ae883b627e090bfb5e450ef796febca40.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;$" width="17" height="14" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/7/4c71f19552a05ee5e2b31e94aff3fb986884bf31.png" class="latex" alt="$B&#39;$" width="18" height="14" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/7/9b798193cb94562989018728e1151587c618655b.png" class="latex" alt="$C&#39;$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="21" height="16" />, respectively. Assume that none of the vertices of the triangle lie on the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/1/741a74d86df899c10724dc2f80638f5e7f599194.png" class="latex" alt="$y=x$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="11" />. Which of the following statements is <i><u>not</u></i> always true?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/7/e8738bbc91069d2db6a97b6ab9b1e13052ac4606.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="28" height="17" /> Triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/2/022948689ac03ea1705a6af78b86d32ae2a369eb.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;$" width="55" height="14" /> lies in the first quadrant.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/6/506e909041d36bd739c8c959d341fcfbba3e9ad7.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(B) }$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="27" height="17" /> Triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/2/022948689ac03ea1705a6af78b86d32ae2a369eb.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;$" width="55" height="14" /> have the same area.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/b/0ab3a9c13a5c376b1660f46d00b9007dbae240cb.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(C) }$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="27" height="17" /> The slope of line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/9/d59f5d75cc27d504ede022aab803742e799709f4.png" class="latex" alt="$AA&#39;$" width="30" height="14" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/e/bae5aba07d37ff6ff813107e76260fb31ad5794e.png" class="latex" alt="$-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="22" height="12" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/8/3787f1b7ce783a57c3a0d02ca4d485ccc950ff0a.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(D) }$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="28" height="17" /> The slopes of lines <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/9/d59f5d75cc27d504ede022aab803742e799709f4.png" class="latex" alt="$AA&#39;$" width="30" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/b/44b7058f8a28ca225136b284b337138a54e39e8c.png" class="latex" alt="$CC&#39;$" width="32" height="14" /> are the same.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/d/81d05263c2e18459105296c8e3e06c9fddddb735.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(E) }$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="17" /> Lines <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/3/4e39919f572d9421d9fe01d80321554dfafdeced.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;B&#39;$" width="36" height="14" /> are perpendicular to each other.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Let's analyze all of the options separately. 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/6/a46f6af01fcf3bb5f6cac2316bc4de9bb3906bec.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="28" height="17" />: Clearly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/6/a46f6af01fcf3bb5f6cac2316bc4de9bb3906bec.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="28" height="17" /> is true, because a point in the first quadrant will have non-negative <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />- and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />-coordinates, and so its reflection, with the coordinates swapped, will also have non-negative <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />- and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />-coordinates.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/1/b7193f66028e13f331d53ff5e6942c4b05587465.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(B)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="27" height="17" />: The triangles have the same area, since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/a/73a764854d1073fac0a788e874db580d732c7c89.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;$" width="71" height="14" /> are the same triangle (congruent). More formally, we can say that area is <i>invariant</i> under reflection.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/9/169b8d69bd74a91e72ec43123070d770633a340a.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="27" height="17" />: If point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> has coordinates <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/b/b6b7a40ab0d8178e83fe1b91c511d04b1246059b.png" class="latex" alt="$(p,q)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/9/1090530ae883b627e090bfb5e450ef796febca40.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;$" width="17" height="14" /> will have coordinates <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/d/12d17bc46f825fc94cca0349fe3bdeaa75c54a1e.png" class="latex" alt="$(q,p)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />. The gradient is thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/4/0647f84325716bba8753c434d05e05324338d980.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{p-q}{q-p} = -1$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="90" height="36" />, so this is true. (We know <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/e/75e83f9ab9dfbf8453c11434327dd45cd61cb5f0.png" class="latex" alt="$p \neq q$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="43" height="17" /> since the question states that none of the points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> lies on the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/1/741a74d86df899c10724dc2f80638f5e7f599194.png" class="latex" alt="$y=x$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="11" />, so there is no risk of division by zero).
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/0/b305929a95b53f14a0ad53eba1d8560626e1f28b.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(D)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="28" height="17" />: Repeating the argument for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/9/169b8d69bd74a91e72ec43123070d770633a340a.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="27" height="17" />, we see that both lines have slope <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/e/bae5aba07d37ff6ff813107e76260fb31ad5794e.png" class="latex" alt="$-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="22" height="12" />, so this is also true.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/e/75e41e3cd48cee2c6866cfbb4386a8e99d3482f5.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(E)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="17" />: By process of elimination, this must now be the answer. Indeed, if point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> has coordinates <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/b/b6b7a40ab0d8178e83fe1b91c511d04b1246059b.png" class="latex" alt="$(p,q)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> and point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> has coordinates <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/f/cdfbe2b8882f9559dc575237eb1cd460933d987c.png" class="latex" alt="$(r,s)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="37" height="18" />, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/9/1090530ae883b627e090bfb5e450ef796febca40.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;$" width="17" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/7/4c71f19552a05ee5e2b31e94aff3fb986884bf31.png" class="latex" alt="$B&#39;$" width="18" height="14" /> will, respectively, have coordinates <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/d/12d17bc46f825fc94cca0349fe3bdeaa75c54a1e.png" class="latex" alt="$(q,p)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/a/bfa2d71780c97102b6290b8af4cf49446abd24c5.png" class="latex" alt="$(s,r)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />. The product of the gradients of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/3/4e39919f572d9421d9fe01d80321554dfafdeced.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;B&#39;$" width="36" height="14" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/7/07741713dfbeb5734185b645c5a46eb7fabefdab.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{s-q}{r-p} \cdot \frac{r-p}{s-q} = 1 \neq -1$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="181" height="36" />, so in fact these lines are <b>never</b> perpendicular to each other (using the "negative reciprocal" condition for perpendicularity).
</p><p>Thus the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/f/6bfc0829f3f29bbf35d0a861644e00cfef0f50ca.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(E)}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="39" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Counterexamples">Counterexamples</span></h2>
<p>If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/f/fff670f716b67617215dcd601aa3f8c850eb25bc.png" class="latex" alt="$(x_1,y_1) = (2,3)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="123" height="20" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/a/88aac3895e185efb942efed241f2b375cb213059.png" class="latex" alt="$(x_2,y_2) = (7,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="119" height="18" />, then the slope of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/1/6815ca413f9a168a773155e1e20814360e270475.png" class="latex" alt="$m_{AB}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="36" height="10" />, is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/8/df86e496511e1668e1b8cb0a116c6f015a63e65a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1 - 3}{7 - 2} = -\frac{2}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="94" height="37" />, while the slope of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/3/4e39919f572d9421d9fe01d80321554dfafdeced.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;B&#39;$" width="36" height="14" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/0/ad0245fdda39511516bdd1b15e6284741d334508.png" class="latex" alt="$m_{A&#39;B&#39;}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="44" height="10" />, is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/2/732fa3486d4cd6402005524cf0c12237fabfe944.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{7 - 2}{1 - 3} = -\frac{5}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="94" height="38" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/0/ad0245fdda39511516bdd1b15e6284741d334508.png" class="latex" alt="$m_{A&#39;B&#39;}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="44" height="10" /> is the <b>reciprocal</b> of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/1/6815ca413f9a168a773155e1e20814360e270475.png" class="latex" alt="$m_{AB}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="36" height="10" />, but it is not the negative reciprocal of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/1/6815ca413f9a168a773155e1e20814360e270475.png" class="latex" alt="$m_{AB}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="36" height="10" />. To generalize, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/5/b354638f824de285dce6d6d3f72163cb287aeff7.png" class="latex" alt="$(x_1,y_1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="55" height="18" /> denote the coordinates of point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/4/574cd26ba0fbfb46272d82eb95420c5fdbc53546.png" class="latex" alt="$(x_2, y_2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="55" height="18" /> denote the coordinates of point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/1/6815ca413f9a168a773155e1e20814360e270475.png" class="latex" alt="$m_{AB}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="36" height="10" /> denote the slope of segment <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" />, and let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/0/ad0245fdda39511516bdd1b15e6284741d334508.png" class="latex" alt="$m_{A&#39;B&#39;}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="44" height="10" /> denote the slope of segment <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/6/a66bcf1b702aa4823d591a3a43757c06b3e45ca2.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{A&#39;B&#39;}$" width="38" height="15" />. Then, the coordinates of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/9/1090530ae883b627e090bfb5e450ef796febca40.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;$" width="17" height="14" /> are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/4/5b437f30359b4f7479b96fdec0d788dc22b7a6c3.png" class="latex" alt="$(y_1, x_1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="55" height="18" />, and of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/7/4c71f19552a05ee5e2b31e94aff3fb986884bf31.png" class="latex" alt="$B&#39;$" width="18" height="14" /> are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/6/3c6ed2ec7bafa43309f1eb3898f19c947e8a019b.png" class="latex" alt="$(y_2, x_2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="55" height="18" />. Then, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/c/08c5f51ac93ec4b7e27b669b725fb57ba0889bce.png" class="latex" alt="$m_{AB} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="121" height="35" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/b/93ba757b7b661c2baf3df025e5b40f03fa24c2b4.png" class="latex" alt="$m_{A&#39;B&#39;} = \frac{x_2 - x_1}{y_2 - y_1} = \frac{1}{m_{AB}}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="196" height="40" />, so slopes arent negative reciprocals of each other.
</p>
