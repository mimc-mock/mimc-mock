<p>The figure below shows a square and four equilateral triangles, with each triangle having a side lying on a side of the square, such that each triangle has side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> and the third vertices of the triangles meet at the center of the square. The region inside the square but outside the triangles is shaded. What is the area of the shaded region?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/d/cdd3a7f0a4a7e0d25eb30b89d96ca4cd2e86dcb9.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pen white = gray(1); pen gray = gray(0.5); draw((0,0)--(2sqrt(3),0)--(2sqrt(3),2sqrt(3))--(0,2sqrt(3))--cycle); fill((0,0)--(2sqrt(3),0)--(2sqrt(3),2sqrt(3))--(0,2sqrt(3))--cycle, gray); draw((sqrt(3)-1,0)--(sqrt(3),sqrt(3))--(sqrt(3)+1,0)--cycle); fill((sqrt(3)-1,0)--(sqrt(3),sqrt(3))--(sqrt(3)+1,0)--cycle, white); draw((sqrt(3)-1,2sqrt(3))--(sqrt(3),sqrt(3))--(sqrt(3)+1,2sqrt(3))--cycle); fill((sqrt(3)-1,2sqrt(3))--(sqrt(3),sqrt(3))--(sqrt(3)+1,2sqrt(3))--cycle, white); draw((0,sqrt(3)-1)--(sqrt(3),sqrt(3))--(0,sqrt(3)+1)--cycle); fill((0,sqrt(3)-1)--(sqrt(3),sqrt(3))--(0,sqrt(3)+1)--cycle, white); draw((2sqrt(3),sqrt(3)-1)--(sqrt(3),sqrt(3))--(2sqrt(3),sqrt(3)+1)--cycle); fill((2sqrt(3),sqrt(3)-1)--(sqrt(3),sqrt(3))--(2sqrt(3),sqrt(3)+1)--cycle, white); [/asy]" width="252" height="252" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/3/723468a068b8528afd8174a8dce054423909053e.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 4 \qquad \textbf{(B) } 12 - 4\sqrt{3} \qquad \textbf{(C) } 3\sqrt{3}\qquad \textbf{(D) } 4\sqrt{3} \qquad \textbf{(E) } 16 - 4\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="531" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We notice that the square can be split into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> congruent smaller squares, with the altitude of the equilateral triangle being the side of this smaller square. Note that area of shaded part in a quarter square is the total area of quarter square minus a white triangle (which has already been split in half).
</p><p>When we split an equilateral triangle in half, we get two <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/b/abb4e8f1484876dd33e74dde765bfff59c9e6939.png" class="latex" alt="$30^{\circ}-60^{\circ}-90^{\circ}$" width="120" height="13" /> triangles. Therefore, the altitude, which is also the side length of one of the smaller squares, is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/7/6c77cf4b63af80bfbc6a9ad9b4b9e8e549e77d78.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" />. We can then compute the area of the two triangles as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/f/98f819577df3009ed9adb737e634e319f9def8c6.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot \frac{1 \cdot \sqrt{3}}{2} = \sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="121" height="41" />.
</p><p>The area of the each small squares is the square of the side length, i.e. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/1/5c18bb8e10bc404f2a42c55f7a613f08d96c3a37.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\sqrt{3}\right)^2 = 3$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="85" height="35" />. Therefore, the area of the shaded region in each of the four squares is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/6/9264dcef0db1064eed480c83bceb12392cebbc00.png" class="latex" alt="$3 - \sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="56" height="18" />.
</p><p>Since there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> of these squares, we multiply this by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/e/7aef1fb3e954900786ef7be33303149f32ceb52e.png" class="latex" alt="$4\left(3 - \sqrt{3}\right) = \boxed{\textbf{(B) } 12 - 4\sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="233" height="35" /> as our answer.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We can see that the side length of the square is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/5/e455d80f4657b1112ba302a6e2c42e9c81f780ee.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" /> by considering the altitude of the equilateral triangle as in Solution 1. Using the Pythagorean Theorem, the diagonal of the square is thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/0199afd555a2b409b4fac2664f0781345a853531.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{12+12}=\sqrt{24}=2\sqrt{6}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="189" height="19" />. Because of this, the height of one of the four shaded kites is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/f/0af834b481b16223a0f04fa9a05f4f8a581e0182.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{6}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" />. Now, we just need to find the length of that kite. By the Pythagorean Theorem again, this length is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/d/57d86551021ed4c8af73a8228cca7e835367a040.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2\sqrt{3} - 2}{2} \times \sqrt{2} = \sqrt{3} - 1 = \sqrt{6} - \sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="290" height="41" />. Now using <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/2/582496e2e16f0bccb980d78f608b89ca4b5d0ed9.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{area} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \text{length} \cdot \text{width}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="180" height="37" />, the area of one of the four kites is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/6/476e69ae23c7e07babcb8845f318c1d65e1f39d7.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \sqrt{6} \times \left(\sqrt{6}-\sqrt{2}\right) = 12 - 2\sqrt{12} = \boxed{\textbf{(B) } 12 - 4\sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="399" height="35" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Based on the previous solutions, we know that the side length of the square is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/5/e455d80f4657b1112ba302a6e2c42e9c81f780ee.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />. That means that the area of the square is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/d/90d1e3351e939fd32f2d4832dd92fe429c54d3a8.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{3} \times 2\sqrt{3} = 12$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="130" height="18" />
</p><p>We also know that the area of one of the triangles is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/c/8fc76afc7ff8e80c88602e612856a03bd05376d1.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{3} \times 2 \div 2 = \sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="136" height="18" />. That means that all four triangles have a total area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/4/8847db4e2d17c89c20872e74ed02b623594bd9bf.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{3} \times 4 = 4\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="113" height="18" />
</p><p>So the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/f/a6fa5948bfa4e45ab8979e92e883f8e62b494e3b.png" class="latex" alt="$12 - 4\sqrt{3} = \boxed{\textbf{(B) } 12 - 4\sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="218" height="33" />.
</p>
