<p>A red ball and a green ball are randomly and independently tossed into bins numbered with the positive integers so that for each ball, the probability that it is tossed into bin <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/a/cba5226e90f4d5104850cd306ee1ab94098fb100.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{-k}$" width="26" height="15" /> for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/3/a630e681aae088a245f92a56ff60f591c6afea32.png" class="latex" alt="$k = 1,2,3....$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="96" height="16" />  What is the probability that the red ball is tossed into a higher-numbered bin than the green ball?<br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/a/d3ab2efacf3ed5d63a088526c990ee828031ae8e.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } \frac{1}{4} \qquad\textbf{(B) } \frac{2}{7} \qquad\textbf{(C) } \frac{1}{3} \qquad\textbf{(D) } \frac{3}{8} \qquad\textbf{(E) } \frac{3}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="371" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions">Solutions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>By symmetry, the probability of the red ball landing in a higher-numbered bin is the same as the probability of the green ball landing in a higher-numbered bin. Clearly, the probability of both landing in the same bin is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/0/a10767bc9538afe72ef4cc2af9ced4d8dc778563.png" class="latex" alt="$\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}{2^{-k} \cdot 2^{-k}} = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}2^{-2k} = \frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="220" height="48" /> (by the geometric series sum formula). Therefore, since the other two probabilities have to both the same, they have to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/d/bedd97dddbf64581d520169fd8859c50110c47c7.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1-\frac{1}{3}}{2} = \boxed{\textbf{(C) } \frac{1}{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="130" height="48" />.
</p><p><br />
Note: the formula is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/f/58f42d371261e9c99868fc466cc3073c548f1948.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a_{1}}{1-r}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="46" height="36" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/a/1da7198d9a79e0a6dc32e60162044ee0f726e4e7.png" class="latex" alt="$a_{1}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="10" /> is the first term and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> is the common ratio.
Derivation of the geometric series sum formula:
Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/d/c1dcefed6d4ef2693243c07d2f1be30e0d0ca1a7.png" class="latex" alt="$S = S=a_{1}+a_{1}r+a_{1}r^{2}+a_{1}r^{3}+...$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="288" height="20" /> and so on to infinity.
Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/f/48f0d2d20e65da9a45e13c53d8963c8c29122d07.png" class="latex" alt="$rS=a_{1}r+a_{1}r^{2}+a_{1}r^{3}+...$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="221" height="20" /> and so on to infinity. Notice that the terms in the second expression are the same as all the terms in the first EXCEPT for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/a/1da7198d9a79e0a6dc32e60162044ee0f726e4e7.png" class="latex" alt="$a_{1}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="10" />.
Subtract <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/b/0ebb3218b3071adf2f741052d1c4d309248bd613.png" class="latex" alt="$S-rS=a_{1}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="98" height="17" />, factor <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/2/272203b4ab4c36f8d9e6732b3fbc5d016b36c05d.png" class="latex" alt="$S\left(1-r\right)=a_{1}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="113" height="20" />, and finally <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/3/c934b7e03da6620969995dabb5a94ea615618179.png" class="latex" alt="$S=\frac{a_{1}}{1-r}$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="82" height="36" />.
</p><p>Post scriptum. The formula only works if r is less than 1; otherwise, the series will diverge to infinity or negative infinity.
~JH. L
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>Suppose the green ball goes in bin <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/8/34857b3ba74ce5cd8607f3ebd23e9015908ada71.png" class="latex" alt="$i$" width="5" height="12" />, for some <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/f/77f4c05d0eb759cf6cb1c3ef6e665b85e0d56713.png" class="latex" alt="$i \ge 1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="38" height="15" />. The probability of this occurring is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/c/2ecfc7d19cbf628a71fe5da71c78b658afb8b28d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2^i}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="17" height="37" />. Given that this occurs, the probability that the red ball goes in a higher-numbered bin is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/a/1badd93bc078e9a135394199fd3a43d2f131ef57.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2^{i+1}} + \frac{1}{2^{i+2}} + \ldots = \frac{1}{2^i}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="177" height="37" /> (by the geometric series sum formula). Thus the probability that the green ball goes in bin <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/8/34857b3ba74ce5cd8607f3ebd23e9015908ada71.png" class="latex" alt="$i$" width="5" height="12" />, and the red ball goes in a bin greater than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/8/34857b3ba74ce5cd8607f3ebd23e9015908ada71.png" class="latex" alt="$i$" width="5" height="12" />, is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f50edb605ecdbddee5b6518075578ae28e18ccbb.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{1}{2^i}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{4^i}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="93" height="46" />. Summing from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/e/50ed4b89a6aeb4814026a2be01bde8bd7d264b71.png" class="latex" alt="$i=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="38" height="13" /> to infinity, we get
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/c/f5ceb3e9f73709133ebf37ac3df13b5163669f05.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{4^i} = \boxed{\textbf{(C) } \frac{1}{3}}\]" width="130" height="50" />
where we again used the geometric series sum formula. (Alternatively, if this sum equals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, then by writing out the terms and multiplying both sides by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, we see <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/a/a3af5aaa420ba7be529e3f31ff753e9d5c419c38.png" class="latex" alt="$4n = n+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="85" height="13" />, which gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/1/801f18334ec3d48d9bdb33fa190c197cc81db908.png" class="latex" alt="$n = \frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="46" height="37" />.)
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h3>
<p>For red ball in bin <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/1/0a11a61ed6546fa2b711508f8b8a6548708e8ede.png" class="latex" alt="$\Pr(\text{Green Below Red})=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{k-1}2^{-i}$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="237" height="51" /> (GBR) and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/9/b39ef086d59bbe9226365b4a2651eead21ec2c10.png" class="latex" alt="$\Pr(\text{Red in Bin k}=2^{-k}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="171" height="20" /> (RB). 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/5/d1513fd81e56ef636618bfa9662023e4f6adc747.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\Pr(\text{GBR}|\text{RB})=\sum\limits_{k=1}^{\infty}2^{-k}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{k-1}2^{-i}=\sum\limits_{k=1}^{\infty}2^{-k}\cdot\frac{1}{2}(\frac{1-(1/2)^{k-1}}{1-1/2})\]" width="458" height="51" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/e/30e05eaa6173b3889c0694ca1d5b5f56b72b3d99.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum\limits_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{2^{-k}}-2\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty\frac{1}{(2^2)^{-k}}\implies 1-2/3=\boxed{(\textbf{C}) \frac{1}{3}}\]" width="361" height="51" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h3>
<p>The probability that the two balls will go into adjacent bins is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/0/dc06d7d6eab2e9e374efb433cc18dfc4a4eaadbc.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2\times4} + \frac{1}{4\times8} + \frac{1}{8 \times 16} + ... = \frac{1}{8} + \frac{1}{32} + \frac{1}{128} + \cdots = \frac{1}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="438" height="37" /> by the geometric series sum formula. Similarly, the probability that the two balls will go into bins that have a distance of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> from each other is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/f/33f4b21709c45d67aacebaaa33d4dfef96405f9b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2 \times 8} + \frac{1}{4 \times 16} + \frac{1}{8 \times 32} + \cdots = \frac{1}{16} + \frac{1}{64} + \frac{1}{256} + \cdots = \frac{1}{12}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="471" height="37" /> (again recognizing a geometric series). We can see that each time we add a bin between the two balls, the probability halves. Thus, our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/0/1501305b2c7a2365b6545d0a752288ec1841d66c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{12} + \frac{1}{24} + \cdots$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="143" height="37" />, which, by the geometric series sum formula, is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/3/553d5c27d65f56c6d4f6e548a4d2343d8d0d89df.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) } \frac{1}{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="59" height="48" />.
-fidgetboss_4000
</p>
<h3><span id="Solution_5_(quick,_conceptual)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5_.28quick.2C_conceptual.29">Solution 5 (quick, conceptual)</span></h3>
<p>Define a win as a ball appearing in higher numbered box.
</p><p>Start from the first box. 
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> possible results in the box: Red, Green, Red and Green, or none, with an equal probability of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c339afa03e060d959e4942be7522c0c992f4c41c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="11" height="37" /> for each. If none of the balls is in the first box, the game restarts at the second box with the same kind of probability distribution, so if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/f/36f73fc1312ee0349b3f3a0f3bd9eb5504339011.png" class="latex" alt="$p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" /> is the probability that Red wins, we can write <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/9/859db4442cdae566128b14903134b707005e7631.png" class="latex" alt="$p = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{4}p$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="92" height="37" />: there is a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c339afa03e060d959e4942be7522c0c992f4c41c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="11" height="37" /> probability that "Red" wins immediately, a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> probability in the cases "Green" or "Red and Green", and in the "None" case (which occurs with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c339afa03e060d959e4942be7522c0c992f4c41c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="11" height="37" /> probability), we then start again, giving the same probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/f/36f73fc1312ee0349b3f3a0f3bd9eb5504339011.png" class="latex" alt="$p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" />. Hence, solving the equation, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/d/34d844a58af54753224cc27c2930170e7002a680.png" class="latex" alt="$p = \boxed{\textbf{(C) } \frac{1}{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="94" height="48" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_6">Solution 6</span></h3>
<p>Write out the infinite geometric series as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/8/5d8630a92fe741a0c28c7ac49fedc4dc347eed3f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}, \frac{1}{8}, \frac{1}{16}, \cdots$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="92" height="37" />. To find the probablilty that red goes in a higher-numbered bin than green, we can simply remove all odd-index terms (i.e term <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, term <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />, etc.), and then sum the remaining terms - this is in fact precisely equivalent to the method of Solution 2. Writing this out as another infinite geometric sequence, we are left with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/3/973948535378d33b2854b05dedd4de7e99d79299.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}, \frac{1}{16}, \frac{1}{64}, \cdots$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="101" height="37" />. Summing, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/c/f5ceb3e9f73709133ebf37ac3df13b5163669f05.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{4^i} = \boxed{\textbf{(C) } \frac{1}{3}}\]" width="130" height="50" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_7">Solution 7</span></h3>
<p>Fixing the green ball to fall into bin <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> gives a probability of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/3/893094dff46fa444bf0264da73d01e213532118a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{2^2}+\frac{1}{2^3} +...\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="139" height="43" /> for the red ball to fall into a higher bin. Fixing the green ball to fall into bin <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> gives a probability of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/b/5cb59b1532a0338ffb7c26f45030c6892f2ecb16.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2^2}\left(\frac{1}{2^3}+\frac{1}{2^4} +...\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="146" height="43" />. Factoring out the denominator of the first fraction in each probability gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/6/3b665f9be5acf956905cbd327664275b5635d0cb.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2^3}\left(1+\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2^2}+...\right)+\frac{1}{2^5}\left(1+\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2^2}+...\right)+...$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="406" height="43" /> so factoring out <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/1/99168b4561205095b28e56ee64bafdffdffc1162.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(1+\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2^2}+\frac{1}{2^3}+...\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="189" height="43" /> results in the probability simplifying to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/8/f/18fbd0cead659b183a6f915421e737bb5c99cde0.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{1}{2^3}+\frac{1}{2^5}+\frac{1}{2^7}+...\right)\left(1+\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2^2}+\frac{1}{2^3}+...\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="362" height="43" /> and using the formula <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/2/d92b8c5d55f66f20ed3f225d99a712796b2efced.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a}{1-r}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="42" height="33" /> to find both series, we obtain <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/8/158847cd512318683f7b13ca6da8925039e58745.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{\frac{1}{2^3}}{1-\frac{1}{4}}\right)\left(\frac{1}{1-\frac{1}{2}}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="143" height="47" /> which simplifies to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/3/553d5c27d65f56c6d4f6e548a4d2343d8d0d89df.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) } \frac{1}{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="59" height="48" /> -- OGBooger
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_8">Solution 8</span></h3>
<p>We can think of this problem as "what is the probability that the green ball's bin is less than the red ball's bin". We do not consider the case where the red ball goes into bin <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> because the green ball has no where to go then. The chance that the green one is below the red one if the red one goes to bin <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c339afa03e060d959e4942be7522c0c992f4c41c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="11" height="37" /> chance that the red ball even goes in bin <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> chance that the green ball goes into any bin less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />. Similarly, if the red goes into bin <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />, there is a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/8/c28c04fbf9be34cf9d89d3faa161a9874fcd8d97.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{8} \cdot \left(\frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{2}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="97" height="43" /> chance, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/e/3ee66c9325b562140f40a1fe274aae1900873d8a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{3}{32}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" />, continuing like this, we get this sequence:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/e/3eeb7523dd4a832176ec8dc0a810f9da0548d41b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{8}, \frac{3}{32}, \frac{7}{128}, ...$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="104" height="38" />
</p><p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> equal the sum of our series:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/3/3537cd7593e9645c25cd5913c8957199262f9cd1.png" class="latex" alt="$S = \frac{1}{8} + \frac{3}{32} + \frac{7}{128} + ...$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="183" height="38" />. That means we can write another equation:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/5/6b59e29d03f83eb1a61eb70e1d0eb1f884c3b906.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{S}{4} = \frac{1}{32} + \frac{3}{128} + ...$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="152" height="38" />
</p><p>Subtracting <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/9/9c91a93c788c1d211aac3ed9f831ae1e90def36d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{S}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="14" height="38" /> from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" />, yields:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/8/4f8713f8dbffc6177ed87d8877eaaea53085a762.png" class="latex" alt="$S - \frac{S}{4} = \frac{1}{8} + \frac{2}{32} + \frac{4}{128} + ...$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="221" height="38" />
</p><p>We see that the above series is a infinite geometric sequence with common ratio <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. Therefore, the sum of that infinite series is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/a/0ba8ff21e92e1988e4ca3462d3bc3a21311ed6d8.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\frac{1}{8}}{\frac{1}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="13" height="47" />, which equals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c339afa03e060d959e4942be7522c0c992f4c41c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="11" height="37" />. Our equation is now <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/0/c10dbe8fe291197e3b904a5540d6be17a454483f.png" class="latex" alt="$S - \frac{S}{4} = \frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="86" height="38" />. Solving for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> shows that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/e/3ae317a494cb552080fc01cc6197cf49f7a3552e.png" class="latex" alt="$S = \frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="48" height="37" />.
</p><p>Our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/5/6456cefcb4a5262eee52eda1b544496a5efc20d8.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) }\frac{1}{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="59" height="48" />
</p><p>~ericshi1685
</p>
<h3><span id="Solution_9_(quick,_symmetry)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_9_.28quick.2C_symmetry.29">Solution 9 (quick, symmetry)</span></h3>
<p>Denote <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/4/804193ebd710d0de2bf490450b84a07320933eba.png" class="latex" alt="$G,R$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> the bin numbers of the green and red balls, respectively. The common probability distribution of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/a/8caf104369f71e46f4ed602584a629ad843ff818.png" class="latex" alt="$G,B$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="36" height="16" /> can be constructed by keep splitting the remaining unassigned probability into two halves: one goes to the smallest number that has not been assigned, and the other goes to the rest. In other words, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/2/692e404cd8774fdc0c2d00d27d92ca14f8500ee3.png" class="latex" alt="$\Pr(G=k) = \Pr (G&gt;k), \forall k \in \mathbb{N}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="254" height="20" />. Then,
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/5/2651e88f52c773e4d1aeed180d1ea90fd37b5ff6.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\Pr(G&gt;R)=\sum_{k=1}^\infty \Pr(G&gt;k) \Pr(R=k) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \Pr(G=k) \Pr(R=k) = \Pr (G=R)\]" width="634" height="48" />
</p><p>Similarly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/9/a49a4a62c02beaf292af6b6e934fe541209d70e4.png" class="latex" alt="$\Pr(G&lt;R)=\Pr(G=R)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="198" height="20" />. Therefore all three probabilities equal <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/5/6456cefcb4a5262eee52eda1b544496a5efc20d8.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) }\frac{1}{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="59" height="48" />.
</p><p>~asops
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_10">Solution 10</span></h3>
<p>The probability of the red ball falling ahead of the green ball is the same as the probability of the green ball falling ahead of the red ball. Therefore, if we calculate the probability of the red ball and the green ball falling inside the same box, we get the answer by subtracting that probability from 1. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/1/4315f82b83d0695f768d06c191ef4b7b43f65a78.png" class="latex" alt="$P(same) = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{1}{8}\right)^2 + \cdots = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{16} + \frac{1}{64} = \frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="488" height="48" />. Therefore, our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/f/45fd3fd3c905de57e73998b42adeb2e1a65400ae.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1-\frac{1}{3}}{2} = \boxed{\frac{1}{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="96" height="49" />
</p><p>-NL008
</p>
<h3><span id="Solution_11_(This_method_might_be_a_coincidence)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_11_.28This_method_might_be_a_coincidence.29">Solution 11 (This method might be a coincidence)</span></h3>
<p>My method could be completely coincidental, but, there are 3 different cases we have to consider: Red in a higher numbered bin than Green, Red in the same numbered bin as Green, or Green in a higher bin than Red. Since only the first case satisfies these conditions, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/6/566b6fe3538f1ba1fd77816fc24ee9c620cfb6ac.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) }\frac{1}{3}}.$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="66" height="49" />
</p>
<pre>- ethancui0529
</pre>
<p>This is a coincidence, as the infinite geometric sum of the series that starts with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/4/4a4a7df1100efe353cc612d5d1c289c22d5bf8c1.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> and has a common ratio of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/4/4a4a7df1100efe353cc612d5d1c289c22d5bf8c1.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/4/c443e0e31bcbc53302d23755444ed9e46190f1f1.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. With any other starting term or ratio, this answer would not be valid.
</p><p>~russellk
</p>
<h3><span id="Solution_12_(a_little_bit_like_Archimedes's_Cheese_Argument)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_12_.28a_little_bit_like_Archimedes.27s_Cheese_Argument.29">Solution 12 (a little bit like Archimedes's Cheese Argument)</span></h3>
<p>Let's draw a square (if you want to, draw it larger) and divide it into four parts: Red on the top left corner, Green on the bottom right corner, Red and Green on the bottom left corner, and none on the top right. We then divide the "none" part also the same as we divided the original large square. Thus, the "none" probability decreases, and gradually it is divided into three parts: Red, Green, or Red and Green. We can visually see that the probability will be one third. The answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/6/566b6fe3538f1ba1fd77816fc24ee9c620cfb6ac.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) }\frac{1}{3}}.$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="66" height="49" />
~EDDY12597
</p>
