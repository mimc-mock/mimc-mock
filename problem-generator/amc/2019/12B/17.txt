<p>How many nonzero complex numbers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> have the property that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/c/35c9a4f95bd6fbb33216946d3d18f4fa7d3adf71.png" class="latex" alt="$0, z,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="30" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/a/b3af613ff090ca0cfcbbf042818d59b89b9dd3dc.png" class="latex" alt="$z^3,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="20" height="18" /> when represented by points in the complex plane, are the three distinct vertices of an equilateral triangle?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/4/2647f5cfaaab208d8f0206b83e4f49b19c351e26.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }0\qquad\textbf{(B) }1\qquad\textbf{(C) }2\qquad\textbf{(D) }4\qquad\textbf{(E) }\text{infinitely many}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="453" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Convert <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/4/704e9969bd090004be46cb1cbb380cd1bc2525c0.png" class="latex" alt="$z^3$" width="15" height="15" /> into modulus-argument (polar) form, giving <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/d/41d518bdae6178bf7e1a81f56609c779fd374b92.png" class="latex" alt="$z=r\text{cis}(\theta)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="82" height="18" /> for some <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/e/52e8ed7a3ba22130ad3984eb2cd413406475a689.png" class="latex" alt="$\theta$" width="8" height="13" />. Thus, by De Moivre's Theorem, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/3/383d2e5998225ab1b52f5e0771e3429009d0fd01.png" class="latex" alt="$z^3=r^3\text{cis}(3\theta)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="106" height="19" />. Since the distance from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />, and the triangle is equilateral, the distance from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/4/704e9969bd090004be46cb1cbb380cd1bc2525c0.png" class="latex" alt="$z^3$" width="15" height="15" /> must also be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/d/ffd7edae9f69195d412c250ecd3cef71ef495717.png" class="latex" alt="$r^3=r$" width="48" height="15" />, giving <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/3/1930619a38fceee8e02990ea79026a348193307e.png" class="latex" alt="$r=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="41" height="12" />. (We know <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/d/ddde7dd752b934381ecdc777b24c5156bdfd3a8e.png" class="latex" alt="$r \neq 0$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="41" height="17" /> since the problem statement specifies that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> must be nonzero.)
</p><p>Now, to get from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/4/704e9969bd090004be46cb1cbb380cd1bc2525c0.png" class="latex" alt="$z^3$" width="15" height="15" />, which should be a rotation of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/d/08d5c45388de5c75af641c046d466de3592ffa98.png" class="latex" alt="$60^{\circ}$" width="24" height="13" /> if the triangle is equilateral, we multiply by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/e/70ed4cd1df102b0fb4f43a44a4830e813785890a.png" class="latex" alt="$z^2 = r^2\text{cis}(2\theta)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="106" height="19" />, again using De Moivre's Theorem. Thus we require <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/a/f1abfb58f4459704f272f41ba4bac8d14cebfd33.png" class="latex" alt="$2\theta=\pm\frac{\pi}{3} + 2\pi k$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="123" height="33" /> (where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> can be any integer). If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/c/2ecac93b909219bcf23fa6048e956f82b26c9b19.png" class="latex" alt="$0 &lt; \theta &lt; \frac{\pi}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="80" height="33" />, we must have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/4/40448f6970d277745d3544863f7007df728816b1.png" class="latex" alt="$\theta=\frac{\pi}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="47" height="33" />, while if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/9/4e9a791080c71c7aa65a833ea1ab1d0d4092dc04.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\pi}{2} \leq \theta &lt; \pi$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="83" height="33" />, we must have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/b/a6b6db8519f76a0ddbbca599dce9510009413f05.png" class="latex" alt="$\theta = \frac{5\pi}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="56" height="37" />. Hence there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> values that work for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/8/958fcbb0cff7fde42d3f720e6c7a236e6a5fa515.png" class="latex" alt="$0 &lt; \theta &lt; \pi$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="77" height="13" />. By symmetry, the interval <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/3/823c80ee3e7514cad73aafd2734ae9e5de86b3ac.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi \leq \theta &lt; 2\pi$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="88" height="15" /> will also give <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> solutions. The answer is thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/a/21ad40ad3e244763448ea312395d26457c4aba4f.png" class="latex" alt="$2 + 2 = \boxed{\textbf{(D) }4}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="121" height="28" />.
