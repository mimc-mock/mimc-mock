<p>For how many of the following types of quadrilaterals does there exist a point in the plane of the quadrilateral that is equidistant from all four vertices of the quadrilateral?
</p>
<ul><li>a square</li>
<li>a rectangle that is not a square</li>
<li>a rhombus that is not a square</li>
<li>a parallelogram that is not a rectangle or a rhombus</li>
<li>an isosceles trapezoid that is not a parallelogram</li></ul>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/c/e5cd2f52f773419967fcc6aa79da9d35c6183905.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 0 \qquad\textbf{(B) } 2 \qquad\textbf{(C) } 3 \qquad\textbf{(D) } 4 \qquad\textbf{(E) } 5$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="353" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>This question is simply asking how many of the listed quadrilaterals are cyclic (since the point equidistant from all four vertices would be the center of the circumscribed circle). A square, a rectangle, and an isosceles trapezoid (that isn't a parallelogram) are all cyclic, and the other two are not. Thus, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/e/44eb511c4f725b6b8f6c6a941d77324031885652.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) } 3}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="55" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We can use a process of elimination. Going down the list, we can see a square obviously works. A rectangle that is not a square works as well. Both rhombi and parallelograms don't have a point that is equidistant, but isosceles trapezoids do have such a point, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/e/44eb511c4f725b6b8f6c6a941d77324031885652.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) } 3}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="55" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>The perpendicular bisector of a line segment is the locus of all points that are equidistant from the endpoints. The question then boils down to finding the shapes where the perpendicular bisectors of the sides all intersect at a point. This is true for a square, rectangle, and isosceles trapezoid, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/e/44eb511c4f725b6b8f6c6a941d77324031885652.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) } 3}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="55" height="28" />.
</p>
