<p>For a certain complex number <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" />, the polynomial
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/8/07858e6ad5c6c6f79e5c145fe6e55e5cee64606e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[P(x) = (x^2 - 2x + 2)(x^2 - cx + 4)(x^2 - 4x + 8)\]" width="374" height="19" />has exactly 4 distinct roots. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/7/ee71b080efbd8d38394b4e41eeb0b02b750ecfed.png" class="latex" alt="$|c|$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="16" height="18" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/2/2124ceece736cfba437582756f22b84eca68d496.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 2 \qquad \textbf{(B) } \sqrt{6} \qquad \textbf{(C) } 2\sqrt{2} \qquad \textbf{(D) } 3 \qquad \textbf{(E) } \sqrt{10}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="417" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>The polynomial can be factored further broken down into
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/6/5c699aaebd44347c7bbcd4487c7a83adac5cfb21.png" class="latex" alt="$P(x) = (x - [1 - i])(x - [1 + i])(x - [2 - 2i])(x - [2 + 2i])(x^2 - cx + 4)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="561" height="20" />
</p><p>by using the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Quadratic_formula" title="Quadratic formula">quadratic formula</a> on each of the quadratic factors. Since the first four roots are all distinct, the term <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/5/f85cd9272ddc9936c2ad6e154ba173918b5bd0b8.png" class="latex" alt="$(x^2 - cx + 4)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="102" height="19" /> must be a product of any combination of two (not necessarily distinct) factors from the set: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/2/f22162fa0a2e41e73b5ea80719408d8f5ecd9535.png" class="latex" alt="$(x - [1 - i]), (x - [1 + i]), (x - [2 - 2i]),$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="312" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/1/e51477ed120dfc37e1a7449a26b34b662503c537.png" class="latex" alt="$(x - [2 + 2i])$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="102" height="18" />. We need the two factors to yield a constant term of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> when multiplied together. The only combinations that work are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/5/0a56acd71bbc5cc4f1df5e2c28d794b5de4bca82.png" class="latex" alt="$(x - [1 - i])$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="93" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/1/e51477ed120dfc37e1a7449a26b34b662503c537.png" class="latex" alt="$(x - [2 + 2i])$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="102" height="18" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/e/ace596aab495a876b2c5bfc8e9673d094e3292f8.png" class="latex" alt="$(x - [1+i])$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="93" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/d/8ddb804ea20abbba4aa7a2b276616fafeaf31d2c.png" class="latex" alt="$(x - [2-2i])$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="102" height="18" />. When multiplied together, the polynomial is either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/0/7f07038ca07aad913679c4d0920aa39f31ee6247.png" class="latex" alt="$(x^2 + [-3 + i]x + 4)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="156" height="20" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/d/6ad26b8c16cb83978041d57f12ef16b7d8a611dd.png" class="latex" alt="$(x^2+[-3-i]x+4)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="156" height="20" />. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/d/95d25896da3417f497283bee36b121d1128a3a8b.png" class="latex" alt="$c = 3 \pm i$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="69" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/c/d7cacb8f47339aadbfa496291b74ac9c4747c0ec.png" class="latex" alt="$|c| = \boxed{\textbf{(E) } \sqrt{10}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="121" height="33" />.
</p>
