<p>For a set of four distinct lines in a plane, there are exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" /> distinct points that lie on two or more of the lines. What is the sum of all possible values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/9/e4992213d9dab4831495b64f45f5a48ae7e23e62.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 14 \qquad \textbf{(B) } 16 \qquad \textbf{(C) } 18 \qquad \textbf{(D) } 19 \qquad \textbf{(E) } 21$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="397" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>It is possible to obtain <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> points of intersection, as demonstrated in the following figures:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/7/787467e802a4d645adb31721e25647810a24a75f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(2cm); real d = 2.5; draw((-1,.6)--(1,.6),Arrows); draw((-1,.2)--(1,.2),Arrows); draw((-1,-.2)--(1,-.2),Arrows); draw((-1,-.6)--(1,-.6),Arrows);  draw((-1+d,0)--(1+d,0),Arrows); draw((0+d,1)--(0+d,-1),Arrows); draw(dir(45)+(d,0)--dir(45+180)+(d,0),Arrows); draw(dir(135)+(d,0)--dir(135+180)+(d,0),Arrows); dot((0+d,0));  draw((-1+2*d,sqrt(3)/3)--(1+2*d,sqrt(3)/3),Arrows); draw((-1/4-1/2+2*d, sqrt(3)/12-sqrt(3)/2)--(-1/4+1/2+2*d,sqrt(3)/12+sqrt(3)/2),Arrows); draw((1/4+1/2+2*d, sqrt(3)/12-sqrt(3)/2)--(1/4-1/2+2*d,sqrt(3)/12+sqrt(3)/2),Arrows); draw((-1+2*d,-sqrt(3)/6)--(1+2*d,-sqrt(3)/6),Arrows); dot((0+2*d,sqrt(3)/3)); dot((-1/2+2*d,-sqrt(3)/6)); dot((1/2+2*d,-sqrt(3)/6));  draw((-1/3,1-d)--(-1/3,-1-d),Arrows);  draw((1/3,1-d)--(1/3,-1-d),Arrows); draw((-1,-1/3-d)--(1,-1/3-d),Arrows); draw((-1,1/3-d)--(1,1/3-d),Arrows); dot((1/3,1/3-d)); dot((-1/3,1/3-d)); dot((1/3,-1/3-d)); dot((-1/3,-1/3-d));  draw((-1+d,sqrt(3)/12-d)--(1+d,sqrt(3)/12-d),Arrows); draw((-1/4-1/2+d, sqrt(3)/12-sqrt(3)/2-d)--(-1/4+1/2+d,sqrt(3)/12+sqrt(3)/2-d),Arrows); draw((1/4+1/2+d, sqrt(3)/12-sqrt(3)/2-d)--(1/4-1/2+d,sqrt(3)/12+sqrt(3)/2-d),Arrows); draw((-1+d,-sqrt(3)/6-d)--(1+d,-sqrt(3)/6-d),Arrows); dot((0+d,sqrt(3)/3-d)); dot((-1/2+d,-sqrt(3)/6-d)); dot((1/2+d,-sqrt(3)/6-d)); dot((-1/4+d,sqrt(3)/12-d)); dot((1/4+d,sqrt(3)/12-d));  draw((-1/4-1/2+2*d, sqrt(3)/12-sqrt(3)/2-d)--(-1/4+1/2+2*d,sqrt(3)/12+sqrt(3)/2-d),Arrows); draw((1/4+1/2+2*d, sqrt(3)/12-sqrt(3)/2-d)--(1/4-1/2+2*d,sqrt(3)/12+sqrt(3)/2-d),Arrows); draw(dir(30)+(2*d,-d)--dir(30+180)+(2*d,-d),Arrows); draw(dir(150)+(2*d,-d)--dir(-30)+(2*d,-d),Arrows); dot((0+2*d,0-d)); dot((0+2*d,sqrt(3)/3-d)); dot((-1/2+2*d,-sqrt(3)/6-d)); dot((1/2+2*d,-sqrt(3)/6-d)); dot((-1/4+2*d,sqrt(3)/12-d)); dot((1/4+2*d,sqrt(3)/12-d)); [/asy]" width="665" height="428" />
</p><p>It is clear that the maximum number of possible intersections is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/f/b5f3803bb802b59969d7e135f0235385427bfc79.png" class="latex" alt="${4 \choose 2} = 6$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="70" height="53" />, since each pair of lines can intersect at most once.  We now prove that it is impossible to obtain two intersections.
</p><p>We proceed by contradiction.  Assume a configuration of four lines exists such that there exist only two intersection points.  Let these intersection points be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />.  Consider two cases:
</p><p><b>Case 1</b>:  No line passes through both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />
</p><p>Then, since an intersection point is obtained by an intersection between at least two lines, two lines pass through each of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />.  Then, since there can be no additional intersections, the 2 lines that pass through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> cant intersect the 2 lines that pass through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, and so 2 lines passing through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> must be parallel to 2 lines passing through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />. Then the two lines passing through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> are parallel to each other by transitivity of parallelism, so they coincide, contradiction.
</p><p><b>Case 2</b>:  There is a line passing through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />
</p><p>Then there must be a line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/f/0ff0f596610f8933f48ade44d1684f69d83f80c1.png" class="latex" alt="$l_a$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="15" /> passing through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, and a line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/5/bd5977454df593c9bc9767f923da2ab880dd70f1.png" class="latex" alt="$l_b$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="11" height="15" /> passing through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />.  These lines must be parallel.  The fourth line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/2/9b25f8e64b484493fda944d25cad453423041fe6.png" class="latex" alt="$l$" width="5" height="12" /> must pass through either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />.  Without loss of generality, suppose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/2/9b25f8e64b484493fda944d25cad453423041fe6.png" class="latex" alt="$l$" width="5" height="12" /> passes through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />.  Then since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/2/9b25f8e64b484493fda944d25cad453423041fe6.png" class="latex" alt="$l$" width="5" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/f/0ff0f596610f8933f48ade44d1684f69d83f80c1.png" class="latex" alt="$l_a$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="15" /> cannot coincide, they cannot be parallel.  Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/2/9b25f8e64b484493fda944d25cad453423041fe6.png" class="latex" alt="$l$" width="5" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/5/bd5977454df593c9bc9767f923da2ab880dd70f1.png" class="latex" alt="$l_b$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="11" height="15" /> cannot be parallel either, so they intersect, contradiction.
</p><p>All possibilities have been exhausted, and thus we can conclude that two intersections is impossible.  Our answer is given by the sum <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/2/6520389075427f755bc76e9d9851206a953c75c8.png" class="latex" alt="$0+1+3+4+5+6=\boxed{\textbf{(D) } 19}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="255" height="28" />.
</p>
