<p>The figure below shows <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/0/030e19e4e2f1c074127cb50581e8496470c5419f.png" class="latex" alt="$13$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> circles of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> within a larger circle. All the intersections occur at points of tangency. What is the area of the region, shaded in the figure, inside the larger circle but outside all the circles of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840ada191969e6fa3a7d80cd83ed73e46ea56082.png" class="latex" alt="$1&#160;?$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/4/fc485e1337895925d85375cce1a9d3908ca8b7e3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy]unitsize(20);filldraw(circle((0,0),2*sqrt(3)+1),rgb(0.5,0.5,0.5));filldraw(circle((-2,0),1),white);filldraw(circle((0,0),1),white);filldraw(circle((2,0),1),white);filldraw(circle((1,sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((3,sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((-1,sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((-3,sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((1,-1*sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((3,-1*sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((-1,-1*sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((-3,-1*sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((0,2*sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((0,-2*sqrt(3)),1),white);[/asy]" width="302" height="302" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/a/c8af64604ae67be27e0fa6e8131b5e1c1508d4d3.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 4 \pi \sqrt{3} \qquad\textbf{(B) } 7 \pi \qquad\textbf{(C) } \pi\left(3\sqrt{3} +2\right) \qquad\textbf{(D) } 10 \pi \left(\sqrt{3} - 1\right) \qquad\textbf{(E) } \pi\left(\sqrt{3} + 6\right)$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="675" height="32" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/6/536ec6d4db8bfd8e8b6847b9cd5afcea22a3dd27.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(20);filldraw(circle((0,0),2*sqrt(3)+1),rgb(0.5,0.5,0.5));filldraw(circle((-2,0),1),white);filldraw(circle((0,0),1),white);filldraw(circle((2,0),1),white);filldraw(circle((1,sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((3,sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((-1,sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((-3,sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((1,-1*sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((3,-1*sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((-1,-1*sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((-3,-1*sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((0,2*sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((0,-2*sqrt(3)),1),white);  pair O,A,B,C; O=(0,0); A=(-1,sqrt(3)); B=(1,sqrt(3)); C=(0,sqrt(3)*2); draw(O--A); draw(A--C); draw(B--C); draw(O--B); draw(A--B); draw(O--C); dot(A); dot(B); dot(C); dot(O); label(&quot;A&quot;,A, W); label(&quot;O&quot;,O,S); label(&quot;B&quot;,B,E); label(&quot;C&quot;,C, N); [/asy]" width="302" height="302" />
</p><p>In the diagram above, notice that triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/e/a1e220396bf8f6704ea4a5f1745cc64ce339090b.png" class="latex" alt="$OAB$" width="42" height="13" /> and triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> are congruent and equilateral with side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />. We can see the radius of the larger circle is two times the altitude of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/e/a1e220396bf8f6704ea4a5f1745cc64ce339090b.png" class="latex" alt="$OAB$" width="42" height="13" /> plus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> (the distance from point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> to the edge of the circle). Using <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/b/abb4e8f1484876dd33e74dde765bfff59c9e6939.png" class="latex" alt="$30^{\circ}-60^{\circ}-90^{\circ}$" width="120" height="13" /> triangles, we know the altitude is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/7/6c77cf4b63af80bfbc6a9ad9b4b9e8e549e77d78.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" />. Therefore, the radius of the larger circle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/4/344a0493df512f93761670fc283d90a4bd06e2f4.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{3}+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="63" height="18" />.
</p><p>The area of the larger circle is thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/1/131ad752e290c00cc1b11a65b0f632d69df83f4e.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(2\sqrt{3}+1\right)^2 \pi = \left(13+4\sqrt{3}\right)\pi$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="240" height="35" />, and the sum of the areas of the smaller circles is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/9/399705eca82e3904ea74de0cbbc82b209ee05616.png" class="latex" alt="$13\pi$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="28" height="13" />, so the area of the dark region is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/3/d639ead8a9c624d55695a38446a0fd102d10e29b.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(13+4\sqrt{3}\right)\pi-13\pi = \boxed{\textbf{(A) } 4 \pi \sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="276" height="35" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We can form an equilateral triangle with side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> from the centers of three of the unit circles tangent to the outer circle. The radius of the outer circle is the circumradius of the triangle plus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. By using <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/3/3930c90edbe133a3a6bf1e68c87654f8b99ad291.png" class="latex" alt="$R = \frac{abc}{4K}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="66" height="38" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/e/96e2bd2f0024a9704783019b423972db4ccfe825.png" class="latex" alt="$R=\frac{a}{2\sin{A}}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="91" height="33" />, we get the radius as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/1/e718c46d212545d72e79a1c0855c0a107cfb54a9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{6}{\sqrt{3}}+1$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="58" height="42" />.
</p><p>The shaded area is thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/3/2136976508f39981d798285f35b5f46378dcc4e4.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi((\frac{6}{\sqrt{3}}+1)^2-13) = \boxed{\textbf{(A) } 4 \pi \sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="262" height="42" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Like in Solution 2, we can form an equilateral triangle with side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> from the centers of three of the unit circles tangent to the outer circle. We can find the height of this triangle to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/7/4f7e99b6a177dd82046ee2ae91b50c143a72b417.png" class="latex" alt="$3\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />. Then, we can form another equilateral triangle from the centers of the second and third circles in the third row and the center of the bottom circle with side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />. The height of this triangle is clearly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/7/6c77cf4b63af80bfbc6a9ad9b4b9e8e549e77d78.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" />. Therefore the diameter of the large circle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/7/b87271abd21fa4606c6bf342184401c64f5fdca1.png" class="latex" alt="$4\sqrt{3} + 2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="64" height="18" /> and the radius is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/f/9ef0852cc7fee01781d56fb2eeb54479b7f3d718.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{4\sqrt{3}+2}{2} = 2\sqrt{3} + 1$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="156" height="41" />. The area of the large circle is thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/5/8a57fa7005ab23551ade62e69e9e25bacc320d92.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi\left(2\sqrt{3} + 1\right)^{2} = \pi \cdot \left(13 + 4\sqrt{3}\right) = 13\pi + 4\pi\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="371" height="35" />. The total area of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/0/030e19e4e2f1c074127cb50581e8496470c5419f.png" class="latex" alt="$13$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> smaller circles is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/9/399705eca82e3904ea74de0cbbc82b209ee05616.png" class="latex" alt="$13\pi$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="28" height="13" />, so the shaded area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/e/96e5296960c237edbf0ae63073a37964647d4fc2.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(13\pi + 4\pi\sqrt{3}\right) - 13\pi = \boxed{\textbf{(A) } 4 \pi \sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="284" height="35" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/c/8eca5fbf9037f73d0dd12797cfcedaf5cd548981.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(20);filldraw(circle((0,0),2*sqrt(3)+1),rgb(0.5,0.5,0.5));filldraw(circle((-2,0),1),white);filldraw(circle((0,0),1),white);filldraw(circle((2,0),1),white);filldraw(circle((1,sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((3,sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((-1,sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((-3,sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((1,-1*sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((3,-1*sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((-1,-1*sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((-3,-1*sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((0,2*sqrt(3)),1),white);filldraw(circle((0,-2*sqrt(3)),1),white);  pair A,B,C; A=(0,sqrt(3)*2); B=(-2,0); C=(0,0); draw(A--B); draw(A--C); draw(B--C); dot(A); dot(B); dot(C); label(&quot;A&quot;,A, N); label(&quot;B&quot;,B, W); label(&quot;C&quot;,C, S); [/asy]" width="302" height="302" />
</p><p>In the diagram above, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b44beb78e8be18179bfb7fe6b3424e7fb1aef32.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="61" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/1/8b1929493a2042af1208c7b6bb285c11b19a6108.png" class="latex" alt="$BC=2$" width="61" height="12" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/d/c3de5ae8f7bba53c8ee9999656f30b72126742c9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC=\sqrt{4^2-2^2}=2\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="180" height="19" />. The larger circle's radius is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/1/db1973e7ca2641b477e44b191fcd5037e96a3fd5.png" class="latex" alt="$AC+1=2\sqrt{3}+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="147" height="18" />, so the larger circle's area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/2/c42a5ccd46e7a26f7e46a3d3d6035d114f16580c.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi\left(2\sqrt{3}+1\right)^2=\pi\left(13+4\sqrt{3}\right)=13\pi+4\pi\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="361" height="35" />. Now, subtracting the combined area of the smaller circles gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/8/068441f7c9bd528e205925382dc1d13dcb9a45f9.png" class="latex" alt="$13\pi+4\pi\sqrt{3}-13\pi=\boxed{\textbf{(A) } 4 \pi \sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="263" height="33" />.
</p>
