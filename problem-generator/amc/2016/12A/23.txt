<p>Three numbers in the interval <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/4/a14eeeecde5d3951c357b4a7e280daacdef23ae7.png" class="latex" alt="$\left[0,1\right]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="34" height="18" /> are chosen independently and at random. What is the probability that the chosen numbers are the side lengths of a triangle with positive area?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/4/7642d19927814dd8de3eca57eb03b470f2a8d406.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }\frac16\qquad\textbf{(B) }\frac13\qquad\textbf{(C) }\frac12\qquad\textbf{(D) }\frac23\qquad\textbf{(E) }\frac56$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="371" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1:_Super_WLOG">Solution 1: Super WLOG</span></h3>
<p>WLOG assume <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is the largest. Then, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/5/92517f1f9ab681652c5c0ab5831b8429b8d6a263.png" class="latex" alt="$b+c&gt;a$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="71" height="14" />, meaning the solution is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/0/6a06610ecc504c327fe8b4fe0cee368426d4f556.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\;1/2}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="72" height="29" />, as shown in the graph below.
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/8/728a81800c2d06bbb7470db1e901910404bb8ff8.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pair A = (0,0); pair B = (1,0); pair C = (1,1); pair D = (0,1); pair E = (0,0);  draw(A--B--C--D--cycle); draw(B--D,dashed); fill(B--D--C--cycle,gray);  label(&quot;$0$&quot;,A,SW); label(&quot;$a$&quot;,B,S); label(&quot;$a$&quot;,D,W); label(&quot;$y$&quot;,(0,.5),W); label(&quot;$x$&quot;,(.5,0),S); label(&quot;$x+y&gt;a$&quot;,(5/7,5/7)); [/asy]" width="252" height="252" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2:_Conditional_Probability">Solution 2: Conditional Probability</span></h3>
<p>WLOG, let the largest of the three numbers drawn be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/2/4c28242522f23e896074dc89612847f8317911d7.png" class="latex" alt="$a&gt;0$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="13" />.  Then the other two numbers are drawn uniformly and independently from the interval <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/b/68bacdc4b96052586744d16169ccd8c3233ed09f.png" class="latex" alt="$[0,a]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="34" height="18" />.  The probability that their sum is greater than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/e/d9e79a047e35e7d5971ec3f0f30f49df52cfc4aa.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\;1/2.}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="77" height="29" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3:_Calculus">Solution 3: Calculus</span></h3>
<p>When <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/2/082852571ecc48ed136ba13dcffa8f6e40064bbd.png" class="latex" alt="$a&gt;b$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="13" />, consider two cases:
</p><p>1) <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/d/42d0153e2e0f64b23aa32adbc293122df5526299.png" class="latex" alt="$0&lt;a&lt;\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="78" height="37" />, then 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/9/6d987a28b05eb0878152b2b68ea0eaaaa4bbc6dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\int_{0}^{\frac{1}{2}} \int_{0}^{a}2b \,\text{d}b\,\text{d}a=\frac{1}{24}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="163" height="46" />
</p><p>2)<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/7/cc74e630a31bbbd3d21216dd2e5a9ca8a2eb3eb2.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}&lt;a&lt;1$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="79" height="37" />, then 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/6/8a6df757a1ae68af4730f34731c2c6e796dbdd5a.png" class="latex" alt="$\int_{\frac{1}{2}}^{1} \left(\int_{0}^{1-a}2b \,\text{d}b + \int_{1-a}^{a}1+b-a \,db\right)\text{d}a=\frac{5}{24}$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="355" height="47" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/0/710e3e4f18d7c0270ab719a00adc61fa12363b1e.png" class="latex" alt="$a&lt;b$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="13" /> is the same. Thus the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4:_Geometry">Solution 4: Geometry</span></h3>
<p>The probability of this occurring is the volume of the corresponding region within a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/8/c58deba0f41c4da22eb2c1016638fd874231c042.png" class="latex" alt="$1 \times 1 \times 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="70" height="12" /> cube, where each point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/3/c73ed093cc73a8d9a14185fa525e7067e5c3725d.png" class="latex" alt="$(x,y,z)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="58" height="18" /> corresponds to a choice of values for each of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/5/0b56d32444d8efd65eb0cb6c01f8ac72c32587e2.png" class="latex" alt="$x, y,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="32" height="11" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" />. The region where, WLOG, side <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> is too long, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/2/d72b305f5efbeaaf693f87af9ded5a0bd45aefcc.png" class="latex" alt="$z\geq x+y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="75" height="15" />, is a pyramid with a base of area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> and height <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, so its volume is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/0/910b8d7425df17b8fadbd0318b52d3806c3b9ecc.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\frac{1}{2}\cdot 1}{3}=\frac{1}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="72" height="40" />. Accounting for the corresponding cases in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> multiplies our answer by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />, so we have excluded a total volume of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> from the space of possible probabilities. Subtracting this from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> leaves us with a final answer of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5:_More_Calculus">Solution 5: More Calculus</span></h3>
<p>The probability of this occurring is the volume of the corresponding region within a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/8/c58deba0f41c4da22eb2c1016638fd874231c042.png" class="latex" alt="$1 \times 1 \times 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="70" height="12" /> cube, where each point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/3/c73ed093cc73a8d9a14185fa525e7067e5c3725d.png" class="latex" alt="$(x,y,z)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="58" height="18" /> corresponds to a choice of values for each of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/5/0b56d32444d8efd65eb0cb6c01f8ac72c32587e2.png" class="latex" alt="$x, y,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="32" height="11" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" />.  We take a horizontal cross section of the cube, essentially picking a value for z.  The area where the triangle inequality will not hold is when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/e/75e0e24e41ec135f6792371e26848a8b3f5d2ac3.png" class="latex" alt="$x + y &lt; z$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="75" height="14" />, which has area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/3/6c38220d8bae37fefd41c64fad605f4f1d56e467.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{z^2}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="19" height="39" /> or when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/d/17d80bbd71ff902c12773bccfd4e14b05d00871a.png" class="latex" alt="$x+z&lt;y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="75" height="14" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/3/8f368db2996d76ee0ee445bce95d0a8ae5bc7049.png" class="latex" alt="$y+z&lt;x$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="75" height="14" />, which have an area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/0/960ff99e1a7427d8a087d099c135f84dccc3f2f0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{(1-z)^2}{2}+\frac{(1-z)^2}{2} = (1-z)^2.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="244" height="39" /> Integrating this expression from 0 to 1 in the form
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/a/9aa60728b3b0f9daeb903b2bcbe8f72bf533b0d9.png" class="latex" alt="$\int_0^1 \left(\frac{z^2}{2} + (1-z)^2\right) dz = \bigg[\frac{z^3}{2} - z^2 + z \biggr |_0^1 = \frac{1}{2} -1 + 1 = \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="455" height="48" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_6:_Geometry_in_2-D">Solution 6: Geometry in 2-D</span></h3>
<p>WLOG assume that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> is the largest number and hence the largest side. Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/e/48e39400a3321eb32dd96cdc7ae5b39ccf4c7145.png" class="latex" alt="$x,y \leq z$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="61" height="15" />. We can set up a square that is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> in the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/7/3f79c3676b62fb47243e0d357c87115b6f3395e4.png" class="latex" alt="$xy$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="19" height="11" /> plane. We are wanting all the points within this square that satisfy <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/9/3f9fa8428a775868bea315cd370809c97e328a1a.png" class="latex" alt="$x+y &gt; z$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="75" height="14" />. This happens to be a line dividing the square into 2 equal regions. Thus the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />.
</p><p><br />
[][] diagram for this problem goes here (z by z square)
</p>
<h3><span id="Solution_7:_More_WLOG,_Complementary_Probability"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_7:_More_WLOG.2C_Complementary_Probability">Solution 7: More WLOG, Complementary Probability</span></h3>
<p>The triangle inequality simplifies to considering only one case: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/8/198b22fe799225d451192790a96cfbda3e2ab150.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{the smallest side+ the second smallest side} &gt; \text{the largest side}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="446" height="15" />. Consider the complement (the same statement, except with a less than or equal to).  Assume (WLOG) <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is the largest, so on average <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/f/aaf1f5f10f3c4e234a8e3407f3be0fa04ffa0bc2.png" class="latex" alt="$a=1/2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="60" height="18" /> (now equal to becomes a degenerate case with probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />, so we no longer need to consider it). We now want <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/b/ccbf66b6f5f9a8d29c16c85792263f8d2411b804.png" class="latex" alt="$b+c&lt;1/2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="88" height="18" />, so imagine choosing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/b/c9bbeb7caa1770d1d3cb842655f27e245b08188f.png" class="latex" alt="$b+c$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="38" height="14" /> at once rather than independently. But we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/b/c9bbeb7caa1770d1d3cb842655f27e245b08188f.png" class="latex" alt="$b+c$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="38" height="14" /> is between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />. The complement is thus: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/1/bf1e99e7f8a903e5164889a972d067f0f906c7a1.png" class="latex" alt="$(1/2-0)/2=1/4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="142" height="18" />. But keep in mind that we choose each <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> randomly and independently, so if there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> ways to choose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/b/c9bbeb7caa1770d1d3cb842655f27e245b08188f.png" class="latex" alt="$b+c$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="38" height="14" /> together, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/e/b8ea7ee8a282949fd853a01537ec7a2b55e56f75.png" class="latex" alt="$2k$" width="18" height="12" /> ways to choose them separately, and therefore the complement actually doubles to match each case (a good example of this is to restrict b and c to integers such that if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/e/a4ec832b2dc1dcb6a1cfb2da85ed4183256d77cc.png" class="latex" alt="$b+c=3$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="70" height="14" />, then we only count this once, but in reality: we have two cases <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/3/3537ecc982707e8637f03c8470642bbcd57a2ab5.png" class="latex" alt="$1+2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="40" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/2/a021347acd933e8bb33455fa2875bdc548bf3ad4.png" class="latex" alt="$2+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="39" height="13" />; similar reasoning also generalizes to non-integral values). The complement is then actually <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/0/800ea59f6426d78dc6bdea43a3bae6a6436d9a56.png" class="latex" alt="$2(1/4)=1/2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="101" height="18" />. Therefore, our desired probability is given by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/c/8ccd449463c978808074492e092c4669790891cf.png" class="latex" alt="$1-\text{complement}=1/2, C$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="192" height="18" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_8:_3D_geometry">Solution 8: 3D geometry</span></h3>
<p>We can draw a 3D space where each coordinate is in the range [0,1]. Drawing the lines <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/d/4ed2dff59d93aedb4f4edac4c07d3fab61198966.png" class="latex" alt="$x+y&gt;z, x+z&gt;y,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="163" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/5/e45546d333038cecd5f61d2197a6041d24951657.png" class="latex" alt="$y+z&gt;x,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="80" height="14" /> We have a 3D space that consists of two tetrahedrons. One is a regular tetrahedron with side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/4/114e91200cd4c857d233c7fed6cbe7451f3a82fc.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" /> and the other has 3 sides of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/4/114e91200cd4c857d233c7fed6cbe7451f3a82fc.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" /> and 3 sides of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/d/6cdd1b53e5faf7a118222e4b7571d1e9afa0cf56.png" class="latex" alt="$1.$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="12" height="12" /> The volume of this region is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/3/3e3a915d521036135df8f84dce16eac5ef6d2c98.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac 1 2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. Hence our solution is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/2/792daa030c96693198f143f9176bf888b7ca25cd.png" class="latex" alt="$C.$" width="17" height="12" />
</p>
