<p>In <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/9/9596b5a2993d0f1ce48e542106bde5b216018861.png" class="latex" alt="$AB = 6$" width="61" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/9/f390fa8bd457fce21ba5afd3176954fe6bdf222a.png" class="latex" alt="$BC = 7$" width="62" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/f/38f0c77899e00092beb318a9f166b328e8b359df.png" class="latex" alt="$CA = 8$" width="60" height="13" />. Point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> lies on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/3/e33fe7d65facd8868f58b6e94ddc7f153a5a3f9f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BC}$" width="29" height="15" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/b/cfbb4c52e011ceed6e813118a7d59e931af444a2.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AD}$" width="29" height="15" /> bisects <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/6/5862947b38a495ec9be80e5b9c7582de6cf0d269.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BAC$" width="55" height="13" />. Point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> lies on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/5/12517bbc31cb9026c71299b7a0d232d07e60f284.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AC}$" width="28" height="15" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/3/0f32db217d7630882c3441fa8821cd70a6958c3a.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BE}$" width="29" height="15" /> bisects <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/a/a3ad57710b6810476e6017ea2c2cc6d41219c218.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle ABC$" width="55" height="13" />. The bisectors intersect at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" />. What is the ratio <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/d/c9daba06f5ec9c5069f24bbeab9c7151ab4f68c2.png" class="latex" alt="$AF$" width="27" height="13" />&#160;: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/8/7a8b5db9ff00c554cedc81b37d6c23b268ce29f1.png" class="latex" alt="$FD$" width="29" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/b/50b0f8f69ee6677a5d5e89b5d864839232ff3faa.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pair A = (0,0), B=(6,0), C=intersectionpoints(Circle(A,8),Circle(B,7))[0], F=incenter(A,B,C), D=extension(A,F,B,C),E=extension(B,F,A,C); draw(A--B--C--A--D^^B--E); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,SW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,SE); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,N); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,NE); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,E,NW); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,F,1.5*N); [/asy]" width="228" height="252" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/f/4bf18ffe5a430881f4810212739008f5ff4312b9.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 3:2\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 5:3\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 2:1\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 7:3\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 5:2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="470" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>By the angle bisector theorem, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/b/a3b04690579f6827d60f0007c00e195430fbfb78.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AB}{AE} = \frac{CB}{CE}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="87" height="38" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/9/6a951731ae12f400e9b3cb720eef9b1e184254f9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{6}{AE} = \frac{7}{8 - AE}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="118" height="37" /> so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/3/e632b6e1665a5fb1dcdb50e3fceb79988bc09736.png" class="latex" alt="$AE = \frac{48}{13}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="73" height="38" />
</p><p>Similarly, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/b/c5b3c9b777daa994886ac518a6988e60c53c22c1.png" class="latex" alt="$CD = 4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="63" height="13" />.
</p><p>There are two ways to solve from here.
First way: 
</p><p>Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/a/f0a278a9972238169ab5ac190058118d48dfcf3e.png" class="latex" alt="$DB = 7 - 4 = 3.$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="135" height="16" /> By the angle bisector theorem on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/5/2651e7507255fa684a661ac3c18181a75f270e39.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ADB,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="64" height="17" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/7/f576ba4798778a84b880e9c4913af8e62ca10c42.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AF}{FD} = \frac{AB}{DB} = \frac{6}{3}.$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="136" height="39" /> Thus the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/b/32b9d367bfb450c4504b47286e89c630e167b3f3.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\; 2&#160;: 1}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="78" height="28" />
</p><p>Second way:
</p><p>Now, we use <a href="/wiki/index.php/Mass_points" title="Mass points">mass points</a>. Assign point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> a mass of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/0/4a055072df5f66385050812271716e05ea308b5f.png" class="latex" alt="$mC \cdot CD = mB \cdot DB$" width="171" height="12" /> , so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/a/9dab16acd80b94d7728d4559129370f314fe4747.png" class="latex" alt="$mB = \frac{4}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="66" height="37" />
</p><p>Similarly, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> will have a mass of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/4/e14066033865a9474b53c073a08c70fca00df757.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{7}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/7/ca789fc58643ac3937bcf269b3f23ad3cb3f21f6.png" class="latex" alt="$mD = mC + mB = 1 + \frac{4}{3} = \frac{7}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="243" height="37" />
</p><p>So <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/0/4c07c8936220774c72b4b7ff68d2ca4c0826581a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AF}{FD} = \frac{mD}{mA} = \boxed{\textbf{(C)}\; 2&#160;: 1}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="198" height="39" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Denote <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/a/1/ea10fad601af0fb0660213a0e841d49ba3dfcad0.png" class="latex" alt="$[\triangle{ABC}]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="67" height="18" /> as the area of triangle ABC and let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> be the inradius. Also, as above, use the angle bisector theorem to find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/5/fd5828647f7d2a8d60ff73bb0e6686110093d922.png" class="latex" alt="$BD = 3$" width="63" height="12" />. There are two ways to continue from here:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/d/6cdd1b53e5faf7a118222e4b7571d1e9afa0cf56.png" class="latex" alt="$1.$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="12" height="12" /> Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> is the incenter. Then, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/4/b044d5ddc28420135d6b984fec10d0fc2f79f655.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AF}{FD} = \frac{[\triangle{AFB}]}{[\triangle{BFD}]} = \frac{AB * \frac{r}{2}}{BD * \frac{r}{2}} = \frac{AB}{BD} =  \boxed{\textbf{(C)}\; 2&#160;: 1}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="380" height="45" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/7/e4797950b5e812b65381cbc5fc33ad2db5f57aff.png" class="latex" alt="$2.$" width="12" height="12" /> Apply the angle bisector theorem on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/1/c51019a63cc8943b31b8c1c550273bdf188b5af8.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{ABD}$" width="59" height="13" /> to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/d/9bd853329671982c03426859e50700fffbe1ca7e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AF}{FD} = \frac{AB}{BD} = \frac{6}{3} = \boxed{\textbf{(C)}\; 2&#160;: 1}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="232" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Draw the third angle bisector, and denote the point where this bisector intersects <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />. Using angle bisector theorem, we see <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/0/3a01db2d9b9887efc8c4bea96c9c1fcbd8c46f91.png" class="latex" alt="$AE=48/13 , EC=56/13, AP=16/5, PB=14/5$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="397" height="18" />. Applying <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://artofproblemsolving.com/wiki/index.php/Van_Aubel%27s_Theorem">Van Aubel's Theorem</a>, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/3/ca3a1b6377f18e86e037d852e09a1484f02a8e22.png" class="latex" alt="$AF/FD=(48/13)/(56/13) + (16/5)/(14/5)=(6/7)+(8/7)=14/7=2/1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="590" height="18" />, and so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/b/32b9d367bfb450c4504b47286e89c630e167b3f3.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\; 2&#160;: 1}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="78" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>One only needs the angle bisector theorem to solve this question.
</p><p>The question asks for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/c/95ce7b59b95744b82973a0900963bb17e2995bef.png" class="latex" alt="$AF:FD = \frac{AF}{FD}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="133" height="39" />. Apply the angle bisector theorem to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/5/f75d6d58b03031bd7cb52a7ca886a8ddf369d21c.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD$" width="59" height="13" /> to get
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/4/b643c4ea6d84ceb4a8a47b51d83f7ec338d60420.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{AF}{FD} = \frac{AB}{BD}.\]" width="93" height="37" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/9/9596b5a2993d0f1ce48e542106bde5b216018861.png" class="latex" alt="$AB = 6$" width="61" height="13" /> is given. To find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/f/89fff82bb65d0215e49c8c91cb7c553da52205e2.png" class="latex" alt="$BD$" width="29" height="12" />, apply the angle bisector theorem to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/8/5f8106d761155c78be9c31cf6666818e88a1013f.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BAC$" width="58" height="13" /> to get
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/2/df2e272f04212713790cd9437f2ba8738f632cf4.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{BD}{DC} = \frac{BA}{AC} = \frac{6}{8} = \frac{3}{4}.\]" width="167" height="37" />
</p><p>Since
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/0/d809dc8f5763e876876eefc09b441c334ccfca5e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[BD + DC = BC = 7,\]" width="172" height="16" />
it is immediately obvious that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/5/fd5828647f7d2a8d60ff73bb0e6686110093d922.png" class="latex" alt="$BD = 3$" width="63" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/4/b241b95d045968ea069313295c398ac3fdd082a7.png" class="latex" alt="$DC = 4$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="65" height="14" /> satisfies both equations.
</p><p>Thus,
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/d/e1d3af7243c1d07e5cd9b22805271e313578ce0d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[AF:FD = AB:BD = 6:3 = \boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ 2:1}.\]" width="334" height="28" />
~revision by <a href="/wiki/index.php/User:Emerald_block" title="User:Emerald block">emerald_block</a>
</p>
