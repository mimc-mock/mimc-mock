<p>Jerry starts at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> on the real number line. He tosses a fair coin <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> times. When he gets heads, he moves <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> unit in the positive direction; when he gets tails, he moves <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> unit in the negative direction. The probability that he reaches <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> at some time during this process <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/7/607943b57145a0237752faabe3610e15589a953c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a}{b},$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="17" height="33" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> are relatively prime positive integers. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/5/bb5a4fd31b9e73386f35f8c3a4ef700932d34d2e.png" class="latex" alt="$a + b?$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="48" height="14" /> (For example, he succeeds if his sequence of tosses is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/6/1e631810071927cd19b831342dce604e99d3b34a.png" class="latex" alt="$HTHHHHHH.$" width="132" height="12" />)
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/7/5c7b5383e99b4ecc42471e563271f74f64d6c37c.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 69\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 151\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 257\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 293\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 313$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="430" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> heads, we are guaranteed to hit <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> heads, so the sum here is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/a/13a3ee36aa863b010862fa7507867c5a3b8bab6a.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{8}{2}+\binom{8}{1}+\binom{8}{0}=28+8+1=37$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="303" height="53" />.
</p><p>For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> heads, you have to hit the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> heads at the start so there's only one way, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />.
</p><p>For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> heads, we either start off with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> heads, which gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/7/6/676d8b877d02c2e3b2a640933ff54f2752393db2.png" class="latex" alt="$_4\text{C}_1=4$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="60" height="14" /> ways to arrange the other flips, or we start off with five heads and one tail, which has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> ways minus the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> overlapping cases, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/a/eeab9595b69398ced5f1515245a9f00f32f0863c.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{HHHHHTTT}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="98" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/9/009ef9ecf0a011d3294b7fb938b50d90eee40f9e.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{HHHHTHTT}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="98" height="12" />. Total ways: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" />.
</p><p>Then we sum to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/b/5abef9fdb0235b2ede2639538b5bd76711e058fa.png" class="latex" alt="$46$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. There are a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/e/a5efb3bc415def343ddc4f461d5d608c5dd6c548.png" class="latex" alt="$2^8=256$" width="67" height="15" /> possible sequences of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> coin flips, so the probability is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/a/5aafecf4113f6f79bab595c8607f069556aa499e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{46}{256}=\frac{23}{128}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="85" height="38" />. Summing, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/e/4eeecb82b0fcd390eeedc66c0bd6a4e216876f22.png" class="latex" alt="$23+128=\boxed{\textbf{(B) }151}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="165" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Reaching 4 will require either 4, 6, or 8 flips. Therefore we can split into 3 cases: 
</p><p>(Case 1): The first four flips are heads. Then, the last four flips can be anything so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/8/9584bdb500d49089d884caad5e9f4484b6267bbf.png" class="latex" alt="$2^4=16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="58" height="15" /> possibilities work. 
</p><p>(Case 2): It takes 6 flips to reach 4. There must be one tail in the first four flips so we don't repeat case 1. The tail can be in one of 4 positions. The next two flips must be heads. The last two flips can be anything so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/c/3fcc33a7c840ca476cc3df92991ac6edc1acb78d.png" class="latex" alt="$2^2=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="50" height="15" /> flips work. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/9/099f1cc4df10b723603df01f246b706f0c9a9cad.png" class="latex" alt="$4*4=16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="77" height="13" />. 
</p><p>(Case 3): It takes 8 flips to reach 4. We can split this case into 2 sub-cases. There can either be 1 or 2 tails in the first 4 flips. 
</p><p>(1 tail in first four flips). In this case, the first tail can be in 4 positions. The second tail can be in either the 5th or 6th position so we don't repeat case 2. Thus, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/5/d8532fea6e90aca51de5a596c8914b62863d4407.png" class="latex" alt="$4*2=8$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="68" height="13" /> possibilities.  
</p><p>(2 tails in first four flips). In this case, the tails can be in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/9/5c9b68a04fadb4cbe90954c537de424c1519ef51.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{4}{2}=6$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="70" height="53" /> positions. 
</p><p>Adding these cases up and taking the total out of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/e/a5efb3bc415def343ddc4f461d5d608c5dd6c548.png" class="latex" alt="$2^8=256$" width="67" height="15" /> yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/b/2/7b2eda73181054c5e3a32b03bbf2da70de34be96.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{16+16+8+6}{256}=\frac{46}{256}=\frac{23}{128}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="234" height="38" />. This means the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/e/4eeecb82b0fcd390eeedc66c0bd6a4e216876f22.png" class="latex" alt="$23+128=\boxed{\textbf{(B) }151}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="165" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>(Inspired by <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EZYzjmd_f2g">https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EZYzjmd_f2g</a> by Walt S)
</p><p>Draw a triangle of dots as seen in the video. Now, we'll do something different than he did. Draw a dotted line between dots that correspond to 3 on the number line and dots that correspond to 4 on the number line. This is the threshold that must be passed to have a successful sequence. We consider the three entry points to the immediate right of this line. Doing a tiny bit of fun bashing as we slide down this triangle, we find there is 1 way to cross the line for the first time through the uppermost entry point, 4 ways to cross the line for the first time through the middle entry point, and 14 ways to cross the line for the first time through the lowermost entry point.
</p><p>Superimpose Pascal's triangle over the dots. Notice that the number of ways to get to a dot is equal to its corresponding number on Pascal's triangle. Rows on Pascal's triangle sum to powers of 2. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/8/9584bdb500d49089d884caad5e9f4484b6267bbf.png" class="latex" alt="$2^4=16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="58" height="15" /> ways to get to the 4th row (remember that when working with Pascal's triangle, we start counting with a 0th row), and we found that 1 of them makes us cross the threshold for the first time. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/3/c23f7dacd533c95a26bd926214b42d6cc00fb29f.png" class="latex" alt="$2^6=64$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="59" height="15" /> ways to get to the 6th row, and we found that 4 of them make us cross the threshold for the first time. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/e/a5efb3bc415def343ddc4f461d5d608c5dd6c548.png" class="latex" alt="$2^8=256$" width="67" height="15" /> ways to get to the 8th row, and we found that 14 of them make us cross the threshold for the first time.
</p><p>Adding up these probabilities <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/a/74a6815c185d2aee49123528ebcc98fca543df28.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{16}+\frac{4}{64}+\frac{14}{256}=\frac{23}{128}\]" width="172" height="38" /> and summing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/4/c343d5ebb859f03a9b926dc9ce1d5eb5b28e42da.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[a+b=23+128=\boxed{\textbf{(B)}\ 151}\]" width="228" height="28" /> we get our answer.
</p>
