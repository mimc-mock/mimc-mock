<p>Tom, Dick, and Harry are playing a game. Starting at the same time, each of them flips a fair coin repeatedly until he gets his first head, at which point he stops. What is the probability that all three flip their coins the same number of times?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/e/6de29a0e36ed3c428e6b87c2c0fed21cf406aa12.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{1}{8} \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ \frac{1}{7} \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ \frac{1}{6} \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ \frac{1}{4} \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ \frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="367" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>By: dragonfly
</p><p>We can solve this problem by listing it as an infinite geometric equation. We get that to have the same amount of tosses, they have a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/b/5db9e227e8177d26de452c2a2faeb6d45cc53d7b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{8}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> chance of getting all heads. Then the next probability is all of them getting tails and then on the second try, they all get heads. The probability of that happening is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/f/e4fd5186d1addce37ab968f92438d9337dd1fea4.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{1}{8}\right)^2$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="45" height="46" />.We then get the geometric equation
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/c/c9c2811227d7e4e6d450dfc4eeb027a7fbae6e53.png" class="latex" alt="$x=\frac{1}{8}+\left(\frac{1}{8}\right)^2+\left(\frac{1}{8}\right)^3...$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="202" height="46" />
</p><p>And then we find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> equals to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/d/5edf51fe473abbd5f4206cfef9858a86ee222aab.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{1}{7}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="58" height="48" /> because of the formula of the sum for an infinite series, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/f/4afcfc1803d5483a5a035c630dbd5dab4c483918.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\frac{1}{8}}{1-\frac{1}{8}} = \frac{1}{8}*\frac{8}{7} = \frac{1}{7}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="149" height="47" />.
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Call it a "win" if the boys all flip their coins the same number of times, and the probability that they win is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />. The probability that they win on their first flip is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/b/5db9e227e8177d26de452c2a2faeb6d45cc53d7b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{8}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. If they don't win on their first flip, that means they all flipped tails (which also happens with probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/b/5db9e227e8177d26de452c2a2faeb6d45cc53d7b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{8}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> ) and that their chances of winning have returned to what they were at the beginning. This covers all possible sequences of winning flips. So we have
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/4/a348dac001fa202e876891f8c4ba05cb010f98ae.png" class="latex" alt="$P = \frac{1}{8} + \frac{1}{8}P$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="101" height="37" />
</p><p>Solving for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/d/5edf51fe473abbd5f4206cfef9858a86ee222aab.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{1}{7}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="58" height="48" />.
</p>
