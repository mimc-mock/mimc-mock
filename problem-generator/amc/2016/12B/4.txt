<p>The ratio of the measures of two acute angles is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/0/ad0a4bc0ad5d6415575678e779682fb207069c5b.png" class="latex" alt="$5:4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="13" />, and the complement of one of these two angles is twice as large as the complement of the other. What is the sum of the degree measures of the two angles?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/1/1413d92141324ac3232d118be4e1533d6d5befc2.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 75\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 90\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 135\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 150\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 270$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="421" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>By: dragonfly
</p><p>We set up equations to find each angle. The larger angle will be represented as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> and the smaller angle will be represented as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />, in degrees. This implies that 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/2/0a2673439280a3687a0f7b484d07dc9e24fd02b3.png" class="latex" alt="$4x=5y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="61" height="16" />
</p><p>and
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/4/b84b242cf338eec53fe4c263964ae1bfc4c8ad6b.png" class="latex" alt="$2\times(90-x)=90-y$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="170" height="18" />
</p><p>since the larger the original angle, the smaller the complement.
</p><p>We then find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/8/348a512e9432ac7de8fd056959cb18e94ffa6224.png" class="latex" alt="$x=75$" width="52" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/a/2/ea29f2f445c19bbcd8a952252d92229b783d9374.png" class="latex" alt="$y=60$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="51" height="16" />, and their sum is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/4/414da89c5cbe83120d734b32f0077b98684aa15f.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ 135}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="73" height="28" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We can visualize the problem like so:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/3/3c363938cdab3ccee9d3ec538a6815acf71fd045.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] path b = brace((0,10),(90,10),5); draw(b); label(&quot;$90^\circ$&quot;,b,N);  draw(&quot;$5x$&quot;,(0,0)--(75,0),N); draw((75,2.5)--(75,-2.5)); draw(&quot;$1y$&quot;,(75,0)--(90,0),N);  draw(&quot;$4x$&quot;,(0,-10)--(60,-10),S); draw((60,-7.5)--(60,-12.5)); draw(&quot;$2y$&quot;,(60,-10)--(90,-10),S);  draw((0,5)--(0,-15)); draw((90,5)--(90,-15)); [/asy]" width="252" height="118" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e74a0011e4998cf849f4168982b02d5f39a7f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[5x+1y = 90^\circ = 4x+2y\]" width="194" height="16" />
Moving like terms to the same side gets <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/3/2e33fa7c7b2ce1a18e6e0a6d3ef6c08bd1958722.png" class="latex" alt="$x = y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="43" height="11" />, and substituting this back gets <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/8/e78f1d161ee6b2afdd74ebcf5fe94ff8799ab32c.png" class="latex" alt="$6x = 90^\circ \implies x = \frac{90^\circ}{6} = 15^\circ$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="235" height="39" />, so the sum of the degree measures is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/8/5b8f91f1c9895012f8a381a9a90e3a30306f7d04.png" class="latex" alt="$5x + 4x = 9x = 9(15) = \boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ 135}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="269" height="30" />. ~<a href="/wiki/index.php/User:Emerald_block" title="User:Emerald block">emerald_block</a>
</p>
