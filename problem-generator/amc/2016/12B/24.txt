<p>There are exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/d/c9d05fdf80c742cec3f5b6e6ac0008fa297b2714.png" class="latex" alt="$77,000$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="53" height="16" /> ordered quadruplets <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/8/eb8c31b328eb3b0252134a4720e161c0fdf641e2.png" class="latex" alt="$(a, b, c, d)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="71" height="18" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/5/e1526cb9932bec037165ffee1da1e72935cf3ee6.png" class="latex" alt="$\gcd(a, b, c, d) = 77$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="142" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/1/ce164c229181099582e3d92da175d8a662ad9b30.png" class="latex" alt="$\operatorname{lcm}(a, b, c, d) = n$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="136" height="18" />. What is the smallest possible value for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/2/d928edc4d617c71d486dac956c842bddb612ad82.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 13,860\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 20,790\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 21,560 \qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 27,720 \qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 41,580$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="570" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/8/0a8d72c47b9bfc67ae154ebb59baffdde1df9450.png" class="latex" alt="$A=\frac{a}{77},\ B=\frac{b}{77}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="133" height="37" />, etc., so that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/f/44fb1d4b71d92557038d0dd2dcb47e89d20ad669.png" class="latex" alt="$\gcd(A,B,C,D)=1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="155" height="18" />. Then for each prime power <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/8/6585024dc6db82bcfd717e03212be80536d94a83.png" class="latex" alt="$p^k$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="17" height="19" /> in the prime factorization of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/6/2f6e7126c9bd9467cc9273e3849170cacf7d8800.png" class="latex" alt="$N=\frac{n}{77}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="61" height="33" />, at least one of the prime factorizations of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/9/37994d1ae8ac92909339d865d551ad53d798fae6.png" class="latex" alt="$(A,B,C,D)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="94" height="18" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/8/6585024dc6db82bcfd717e03212be80536d94a83.png" class="latex" alt="$p^k$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="17" height="19" />, at least one has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/a/6ca495cdbf2b1c55754412b61246a678e8e1726a.png" class="latex" alt="$p^0$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="16" height="18" />, and all must have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/7/6d736e1e57cbc014d1c0b60eade924e42f92d9da.png" class="latex" alt="$p^m$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="21" height="16" /> with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/0/3402c8c30e2c94482c4c650520f63115469dfc54.png" class="latex" alt="$0\le m\le k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="83" height="15" />.
</p><p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/2/81232ecd8d9e4b5c0f24a00dabec238068f26de9.png" class="latex" alt="$f(k)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="33" height="18" /> be the number of ordered quadruplets of integers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/4/87417e9cbbf687a67ad915c542c33623c3d149c8.png" class="latex" alt="$(m_1,m_2,m_3,m_4)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="130" height="18" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/9/13992427c38817d86a2fdee32c4bff1c0aeda9e6.png" class="latex" alt="$0\le m_i\le k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="88" height="15" /> for all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/8/34857b3ba74ce5cd8607f3ebd23e9015908ada71.png" class="latex" alt="$i$" width="5" height="12" />, the largest is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />, and the smallest is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />. Then for the prime factorization <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/1/3c1593bf5cd6d9d4bb149ba59abaeb0fba3200c3.png" class="latex" alt="$N=2^{k_2}3^{k_3}5^{k_5}\ldots$" width="134" height="15" /> we must have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/9/b69b7b0690bfdbab1dc3fc70e7c3bf357a9a563c.png" class="latex" alt="$77000=f(k_2)f(k_3)f(k_5)\ldots$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="218" height="18" /> So let's take a look at the function <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/2/81232ecd8d9e4b5c0f24a00dabec238068f26de9.png" class="latex" alt="$f(k)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="33" height="18" /> by counting the quadruplets we just mentioned.
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/0/820a20de2847dfdc1bc5cffc8d904933150032bd.png" class="latex" alt="$14$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> quadruplets which consist only of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />. Then there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/b/95bf835c4c3d917c3c2948c4171afb5eb0dc0a96.png" class="latex" alt="$36(k-1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="72" height="18" /> quadruplets which include three different values, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/8/71899ccb7658c1e0a7654370ccc783b7d83c7ecc.png" class="latex" alt="$12(k-1)(k-2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="127" height="18" /> with four. Thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a1f078b19b7a8f21374e72f1d5995cd9e4eaa.png" class="latex" alt="$f(k)=14+12(k-1)(3+k-2)=14+12(k^2-1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="404" height="19" /> and the first few values from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/d/5ed8470bc717a49aafe4556c7e2c2e11ddc7b687.png" class="latex" alt="$k=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="13" /> onwards are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/3/0630f92a145feebcfabafd814895991521afbf9f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[14,50,110,194,302,434,590,770,\ldots\]" width="283" height="16" /> Straight away we notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/0/88080cb75ba99ab939ec75a5e563c60517447250.png" class="latex" alt="$14\cdot 50\cdot 110=77000$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="158" height="13" />, so the prime factorization of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" /> can use the exponents <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/c/f3c13f88915d101b401e1fdeac1b195262655caa.png" class="latex" alt="$1,2,3$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="43" height="16" />. To make it as small as possible, assign the larger exponents to smaller primes. The result is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/3/6838143c2eba84cea46179f16ece385c761c4dfc.png" class="latex" alt="$N=2^33^25^1=360$" width="141" height="15" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/3/363acf742ae0fecaeef2bae9f016ebac4d788cba.png" class="latex" alt="$n=360\cdot 77=27720$" width="162" height="12" /> which is answer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/3/693c4044f8526a8179956e8ac7a3f2f9d61e2c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D)}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="41" height="28" />.
</p><p>Also, to get the above formula of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/9/e09a50e41262b1c6e6c490d3edb356a62fa981b9.png" class="latex" alt="$f(k)=12 k^2+2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="125" height="19" />, we can also use the complementary counting by doing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/d/56d1f9b94e8d2dc24d1b6b7ed3e47a1f603133ad.png" class="latex" alt="$(k+1)^4-k^4-k^4+(k-1)^4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="226" height="19" />, while the first term <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/b/d/7bd4ca370d0cb43da0750c6559170a569e20bbbb.png" class="latex" alt="$(k+1)^4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="62" height="19" /> is for the four integers to independently have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/5/f05f085f774fc8ab50676b778b86a1f1d1114fc8.png" class="latex" alt="$k+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="40" height="14" /> choices each, with the second term indicating to subtract all the possibilities for the four integers to have values between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/c/7dc6fab4c2fdb4e9046030fbfdcf6ea721b05602.png" class="latex" alt="$k-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="13" />, and similarly the third term indicating to subtract all the possibilities for the four integers to have values between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />, in the end the fourth term meaning the make up for the values between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/c/7dc6fab4c2fdb4e9046030fbfdcf6ea721b05602.png" class="latex" alt="$k-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="13" />.
</p>
