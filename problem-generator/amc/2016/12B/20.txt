<p>A set of teams held a round-robin tournament in which every team played every other team exactly once. Every team won <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> games and lost <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> games; there were no ties. How many sets of three teams <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/9/a893950303884881aa1782b5a020fef26c0be5d2.png" class="latex" alt="$\{A, B, C\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="76" height="18" /> were there in which <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> beat <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> beat <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> beat <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/8/238ec1279fb21bfa298a7681dc37d6e15c48f48b.png" class="latex" alt="$A?$" width="21" height="13" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/6/de6c59c1821795c2893442ab9de02e6ef964617e.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 385 \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 665 \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 945 \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 1140 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 1330$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="457" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We use complementary counting. First, because each team played <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/3/f8366cd6196dd8a9da6d38a3e9eafb109e99d53e.png" class="latex" alt="$20$" width="17" height="12" /> other teams, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/5/f25d31faf472ddb4c698a14522fe8125c06f8dd8.png" class="latex" alt="$21$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> teams total. All sets that do not have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> beat <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> beat <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> beat <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> have one team that beats both the other teams. Thus we must count the number of sets of three teams such that one team beats the two other teams and subtract that number from the total number of ways to choose three teams.
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/5/f25d31faf472ddb4c698a14522fe8125c06f8dd8.png" class="latex" alt="$21$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> ways to choose the team that beat the two other teams, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/a/b4a36d045406715079df9fc3e9053bd327aa03cc.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{10}{2} = 45$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="88" height="53" /> to choose two teams that the first team both beat. This is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/4/1f44062f7bf5b756af021fb09b0646bc968bcedb.png" class="latex" alt="$21 * 45 = 945$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="104" height="13" /> sets. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/d/7ed25fdd3bddf3c2aa2b184e3fb0135adb048487.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{21}{3} = 1330$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="106" height="53" /> sets of three teams total. Subtracting, we obtain <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/f/8dfee75ad8217910e7cc9cd34a2dc1a208ea56e2.png" class="latex" alt="$1330 - 945 = \boxed{385}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="149" height="23" />, thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/0/d201cab20df0295da5551a91cb815ed07e59cf27.png" class="latex" alt="$(\text{A})$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="25" height="18" />  is our answer.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>As above, note that there are 21 teams, and call them A, B, C, ... T, U. WLOG, assume that A beat teams B-L and lost to teams M-U. We will count the number of sets satisfying the conditions of the problem that include A, then multiply by 21 (for each other team) and divide by 3 (since every set will be counted by each of the 3 teams that are apart of that set). To do this, let X<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/8/6983e6f5701670416e0d8e6a238219083ce80f4a.png" class="latex" alt="$=\{B, ..., L\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="95" height="18" /> and Y<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/8/ac872a7d6e673d16bfeda29fba0609a718844e8a.png" class="latex" alt="$=\{M, ..., U\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="100" height="18" />. Since a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/e/c5e075a5b0c90fcc04dde64cf70c1000d3baa50e.png" class="latex" alt="$10*10=100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="104" height="13" /> losses total were suffered by teams in Y and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/6/cc61bcd70dd08ca88b8f214b03d595ad1613d97a.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{10}{2}=45$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="88" height="53" /> losses were suffered by teams in Y from teams in Y, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/9/8f9a23429d7a6828676553901bf62326e32fb83f.png" class="latex" alt="$100-45=55$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="109" height="13" /> losses suffered by teams in Y from teams in X. Hence, for each of these <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/9/b79030abeb688469b5ffc2e1c4e37fee225a529e.png" class="latex" alt="$55$" width="17" height="12" /> losses, there is exactly one set of three teams that includes A that satisfies the problem conditions. Thus, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/2/bc2741f41271a87a72bf899fa543adb0576896a4.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{55\cdot 21}{3}=\boxed{385}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="116" height="37" />.
</p>
