<p>A set of teams held a round-robin tournament in which every team played every other team exactly once. Every team won <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> games and lost <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> games; there were no ties. How many sets of three teams <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/9/a893950303884881aa1782b5a020fef26c0be5d2.png" class="latex" alt="$\{A, B, C\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="76" height="18" /> were there in which <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> beat <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> beat <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> beat <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/8/238ec1279fb21bfa298a7681dc37d6e15c48f48b.png" class="latex" alt="$A?$" width="21" height="13" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/6/de6c59c1821795c2893442ab9de02e6ef964617e.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 385 \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 665 \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 945 \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 1140 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 1330$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="457" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/7/3075512568118e43ae92c5dc2c229e21e263b353.png" class="latex" alt="$10 \cdot 2+1=21$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="116" height="15" /> teams. Any of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/f/36fa7690fc1c2c810adc987e825a3a12c16a34bd.png" class="latex" alt="$\tbinom{21}3=1330$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="89" height="22" /> sets of three teams must either be a fork (in which one team beat both the others) or a cycle:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/2/d2263ff1d6c823d89ca67e97da2b41f44cb74a4b.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy]size(7cm);label(&quot;A&quot;,(5,5));label(&quot;C&quot;,(10,0));label(&quot;B&quot;,(0,0));draw((4,4)--(1,1),EndArrow);draw((6,4)--(9,1),EndArrow); label(&quot;A&quot;,(20,5));label(&quot;C&quot;,(25,0));label(&quot;B&quot;,(15,0));draw((19,4)--(16,1),EndArrow);draw((16,0)--(24,0),EndArrow);draw((24,1)--(21,4),EndArrow); [/asy]" width="332" height="82" />
But we know that every team beat exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> other teams, so for each possible <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> at the head of a fork, there are always exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/c/9dc09700c2a696838f4e588ac6727f74c15e08ea.png" class="latex" alt="$\tbinom{10}2$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="27" height="22" /> choices for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> beat exactly 10 teams and we are choosing 2 of them. Therefore there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/f/70f5733c34288312f9148f6725871d5f053d0068.png" class="latex" alt="$21\cdot\tbinom{10}2=945$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="111" height="22" /> forks, and all the rest must be cycles.
</p><p>Thus the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/0/090102a454910a2d1069ee044d6960d6e0265d01.png" class="latex" alt="$1330-945=385$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="136" height="13" /> which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/e/35e8ea2d16518197b5113002b8c0c7da6c311a3e.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(A)}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="41" height="28" />.
</p>
