<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We draw a diagram to make our work easier:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/f/78f3f0db2e8e680eaa9f6314cdfae136adf80fd5.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pair A,B,C,D,E,F,W,X,Y,Z; A=(0,0); B=(1,0); C=(3/2,sqrt(3)/2); D=(1,sqrt(3)); E=(0,sqrt(3)); F=(-1/2,sqrt(3)/2); W=(4/3,2sqrt(3)/3); X=(4/3,sqrt(3)/3); Y=(-1/3,sqrt(3)/3); Z=(-1/3,2sqrt(3)/3); draw(A--B--C--D--E--F--cycle); draw(W--Z); draw(X--Y);  label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,SW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,SE); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,ESE); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,NE); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,E,NW); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,F,WSW); label(&quot;$W$&quot;,W,ENE); label(&quot;$X$&quot;,X,ESE); label(&quot;$Y$&quot;,Y,WSW); label(&quot;$Z$&quot;,Z,WNW); [/asy]" width="252" height="218" />
</p><p>Assume that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> is of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />.  Therefore, the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/2/8029902caf855d940e465d04ff40101d8850c3e0.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCDEF$" width="86" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/c/d3c359ac4aabc75ca8a05c537db8bbf204bd6bc0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{3\sqrt 3}2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="35" height="41" />.  To find the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/2/e22b6c158909fddd16ece048eab3d7f4a31d06c2.png" class="latex" alt="$WCXYFZ$" width="93" height="12" />, we draw <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/7/bc7f4fd0e5897e8ac812a9a562556b8cdd87341f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CF}$" width="29" height="15" />, and find the area of the trapezoids <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/f/bff758cd30d3dcc2d3a5c7c86a260d04706b119d.png" class="latex" alt="$WCFZ$" width="61" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/d/7dd4014bcd26587f515354e663970814c0362a68.png" class="latex" alt="$CXYF$" width="59" height="12" />.  
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/7/ca78893545f400ec7f9ce2f99b8fb0d2e2312f7b.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pair A,B,C,D,E,F,W,X,Y,Z; A=(0,0); B=(1,0); C=(3/2,sqrt(3)/2); D=(1,sqrt(3)); E=(0,sqrt(3)); F=(-1/2,sqrt(3)/2); W=(4/3,2sqrt(3)/3); X=(4/3,sqrt(3)/3); Y=(-1/3,sqrt(3)/3); Z=(-1/3,2sqrt(3)/3); draw(A--B--C--D--E--F--cycle); draw(W--Z); draw(X--Y); draw(F--C--B--E--D--A);  label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,SW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,SE); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,ESE); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,NE); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,E,NW); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,F,WSW); label(&quot;$W$&quot;,W,ENE); label(&quot;$X$&quot;,X,ESE); label(&quot;$Y$&quot;,Y,WSW); label(&quot;$Z$&quot;,Z,WNW); [/asy]" width="252" height="218" />
</p><p>From this, we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/a/03a1d58c360bbc87c34aa7849f2e19c3447de15a.png" class="latex" alt="$CF=2$" width="61" height="12" />.  We also know that the combined heights of the trapezoids is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/7/ff7d9ba8f041af8b7eab5bf19e172d1f6567f187.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt 3}3$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" />, since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/a/53a5e1ede49ac7beb997b5adad187c79984dbe57.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{ZW}$" width="33" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/b/e0b7689880884578382ed0c7cb496c2edffb979c.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{YX}$" width="31" height="15" /> are equally spaced, and the height of each of the trapezoids is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/c/6ecc32936c8d8bf93cdfb9457606f4e7ee70d586.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt 3}6$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" />.  From this, we know <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/a/53a5e1ede49ac7beb997b5adad187c79984dbe57.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{ZW}$" width="33" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/b/e0b7689880884578382ed0c7cb496c2edffb979c.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{YX}$" width="31" height="15" /> are each <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/5/5758a2adf3204f50fd381ff16022b80ba995dacd.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac 13$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> of the way from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/7/bc7f4fd0e5897e8ac812a9a562556b8cdd87341f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CF}$" width="29" height="15" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/9/699784c989c3db382520e6f92819e062d239f504.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{DE}$" width="30" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" />, respectively.  We know that these are both equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/1/a01c26f7e52c563d363ae28cadbc1e4010c7ea04.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac 53$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />.
</p><p>We find the area of each of the trapezoids, which both happen to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/f/45f9a44ce7da80782317218706a9229bf78d9cdc.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{11}6 \cdot \frac{\sqrt 3}6=\frac{11\sqrt 3}{36}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="132" height="41" />, and the combined area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/4/7541ad5d204824e0218b51d2475bb4276a21113b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{11\sqrt 3}{18}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="45" height="41" />.
</p><p>We find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/f/eef712bc45f07dd2815db6ac00552e49ac31f777.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{\frac{11\sqrt 3}{18}}{\frac{3\sqrt 3}2}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="37" height="53" /> is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/9/c2937c21927513c3b9747a15b7e215b0f27ef0fc.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{22}{54}=\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{11}{27}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="114" height="48" />.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/7/ca78893545f400ec7f9ce2f99b8fb0d2e2312f7b.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pair A,B,C,D,E,F,W,X,Y,Z; A=(0,0); B=(1,0); C=(3/2,sqrt(3)/2); D=(1,sqrt(3)); E=(0,sqrt(3)); F=(-1/2,sqrt(3)/2); W=(4/3,2sqrt(3)/3); X=(4/3,sqrt(3)/3); Y=(-1/3,sqrt(3)/3); Z=(-1/3,2sqrt(3)/3); draw(A--B--C--D--E--F--cycle); draw(W--Z); draw(X--Y); draw(F--C--B--E--D--A);  label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,SW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,SE); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,ESE); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,NE); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,E,NW); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,F,WSW); label(&quot;$W$&quot;,W,ENE); label(&quot;$X$&quot;,X,ESE); label(&quot;$Y$&quot;,Y,WSW); label(&quot;$Z$&quot;,Z,WNW); [/asy]" width="252" height="218" />
</p><p>First, like in the first solution, split the large hexagon into 6 equilateral triangles. Each equilateral triangle can be split into three rows of smaller equilateral triangles. The first row will have one triangle, the second three, the third five. Once you have drawn these lines, it's just a matter of counting triangles. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/1/1d111a5e00ee5ea6700bb629994dc629874c505b.png" class="latex" alt="$22$" width="17" height="12" /> small triangles in hexagon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/d/50dc85e677bfc696f808a0c19c36dbe83ff9c154.png" class="latex" alt="$ZWCXYF$" width="93" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/c/d1c8bcb1f1fc3fb57d91a2157cea099474fed37c.png" class="latex" alt="$9 \cdot 6 = 54$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="73" height="13" /> small triangles in the whole hexagon. 
</p><p>Thus, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/9/c2937c21927513c3b9747a15b7e215b0f27ef0fc.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{22}{54}=\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{11}{27}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="114" height="48" />.
</p>
