<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/3/6a337dc07dc2f387002568ef9bde5f7582e26b6a.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)=\sum_{k=2}^{10}(\lfloor kx \rfloor -k \lfloor x \rfloor)$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="193" height="50" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/1/af185d2067f3958364022a1d2cc3b49403514b39.png" class="latex" alt="$\lfloor r \rfloor$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="22" height="19" /> denotes the greatest integer less than or equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />. How many distinct values does <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c96dd6ec1dc4ad7520fbdc78fcdbec9edd068d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" /> assume for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/f/dff5a847457c4beaa2c6809fc9d7b26ebcaa398e.png" class="latex" alt="$x \ge 0$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="43" height="15" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/9/d198ac6d88a07f07fbf635e4f116bf103a9eab13.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 32\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 36\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 45\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 46\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \text{infinitely many}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="485" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/3/bc310567b2886d93d974b5baa837b04460876ca7.png" class="latex" alt="$x = \lfloor x \rfloor + \{ x \}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="111" height="19" />, we have 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/f/6df7133ee3a36b071e58a336d9d97fd86948893c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[f(x) = \sum_{k=2}^{10} (\lfloor k \lfloor x \rfloor +k \{ x \} \rfloor - k \lfloor x \rfloor)\]" width="269" height="50" />
</p><p>The function can then be simplified into 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/e/fcef0323311a4ec76e2064194d95814155ed2e69.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[f(x) = \sum_{k=2}^{10} ( k \lfloor x \rfloor + \lfloor k \{ x \} \rfloor - k \lfloor x \rfloor)\]" width="269" height="50" />
</p><p>which becomes
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/c/08c34dd2e7a954f419f6c3192d31eb0e8ab01238.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[f(x) = \sum_{k=2}^{10} \lfloor k \{ x \} \rfloor\]" width="136" height="50" />
</p><p>We can see that for each value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/d/c1dc7431aeec69cd3762d10cc66e397d557ad717.png" class="latex" alt="$\lfloor k \{ x \} \rfloor$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="51" height="19" /> can equal integers from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/c/7dc6fab4c2fdb4e9046030fbfdcf6ea721b05602.png" class="latex" alt="$k-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="13" />. 
</p><p>Clearly, the value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/d/c1dc7431aeec69cd3762d10cc66e397d557ad717.png" class="latex" alt="$\lfloor k \{ x \} \rfloor$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="51" height="19" /> changes only when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/2/b32c61a02899ed4a12995b9c6a635d30920325e5.png" class="latex" alt="$\{ x \}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="27" height="18" /> is equal to any of the fractions <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/f/84fd43ca6ec1ac1e34ae8f467900e0130202582e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{k}, \frac{2}{k} \dots \frac{k-1}{k}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="107" height="37" />.
</p><p>So we want to count how many distinct fractions less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> have the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/f/27f583697295fc6f723862c13228ee55107c33ca.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{m}{n}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="18" height="33" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/a/86a435cb6450f07753ec6e06e24ff6f9730930f6.png" class="latex" alt="$n \le 10$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="52" height="15" />. <b>Explanation for this is provided below.</b> We can find this easily by computing
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/7/57764d9e498281fbf75592968356664165e205ff.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum_{k=2}^{10} \phi(k)\]" width="61" height="50" />
</p><p>where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/7/2b7ab540afc32a08901f42c02dd6092729c2bd6d.png" class="latex" alt="$\phi(k)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="33" height="18" /> is the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Euler_Totient_Function" class="mw-redirect" title="Euler Totient Function">Euler Totient Function</a>. Basically <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/7/2b7ab540afc32a08901f42c02dd6092729c2bd6d.png" class="latex" alt="$\phi(k)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="33" height="18" /> counts the number of fractions with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> as its denominator (after simplification). This comes out to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/6/296fac336caaa4265aa24b6ecbc408a348ad7e0b.png" class="latex" alt="$31$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />.
</p><p>Because the value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c96dd6ec1dc4ad7520fbdc78fcdbec9edd068d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" /> is at least <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> and can increase <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/6/296fac336caaa4265aa24b6ecbc408a348ad7e0b.png" class="latex" alt="$31$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> times, there are a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/0/7e0a280c8de583c9796ae5914356d4cbc62e3cb3.png" class="latex" alt="$\fbox{\textbf{(A)}\ 32}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="63" height="28" /> different possible values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c96dd6ec1dc4ad7520fbdc78fcdbec9edd068d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation:">Explanation:</span></h3>
<p>Arrange all such fractions in increasing order and take a current <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/f/27f583697295fc6f723862c13228ee55107c33ca.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{m}{n}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="18" height="33" /> to study. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/f/36f73fc1312ee0349b3f3a0f3bd9eb5504339011.png" class="latex" alt="$p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" /> denote the previous fraction in the list and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/7/2/67282d8b6f63091401f6028f5461526110fb8210.png" class="latex" alt="$x_\text{old}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="28" height="11" /> (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/9/259f720fbaf9f4899a61abf95711ffda8a9ae285.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \le x_\text{old} &lt; k$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="96" height="15" /> for each <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />) be the largest so that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/f/56f2993d9827d52c2cbf4bd30dd91f5efb283788.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{x_\text{old}}{k} \le p$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="66" height="33" />. Since  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/8/ca8e0514830fd6e12710d6bb96455ee675244856.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{ }\text{ }\frac{m}{n} &gt; p$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="64" height="33" />, we clearly have all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/2/d720098e7610f7913af741a95ea122549172f206.png" class="latex" alt="$x_\text{new} \ge x_\text{old}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="86" height="15" />. Therefore, the change must be nonnegative.
</p><p>But among all numerators coprime to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> so far, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> is the largest. Therefore, choosing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/f/27f583697295fc6f723862c13228ee55107c33ca.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{m}{n}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="18" height="33" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/4/f64c96395619031dbe5b637faeb4c1fe609bd56c.png" class="latex" alt="${x}$" width="10" height="8" /> increases the value <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/a/fea184e75b1f5883e63849abbe63783a17817560.png" class="latex" alt="$\lfloor n \{ x \} \rfloor$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="52" height="19" />. Since the overall change in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/6/c96dd6ec1dc4ad7520fbdc78fcdbec9edd068d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$f(x)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" /> is positive as fractions <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/c/83ce0c4966858ed8ef848e596e2bd7e8f694dc81.png" class="latex" alt="$m/n$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="35" height="18" /> increase, we deduce that all such fractions correspond to different values of the function. 
</p><p>Minor Latex Edits made by MathWizard10.
