<p>The sum of an infinite geometric series is a positive number <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" />, and the second term in the series is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. What is the smallest possible value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/7/4d753026173a63cfed0ea918be191cc3b451677e.png" class="latex" alt="$S?$" width="20" height="12" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/5/005618c9563fac3bc6e4c33239cb85fe48ba31aa.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2} \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 2 \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ \sqrt{5} \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 3 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 4$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="414" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The sum of an infinite geometric series is of the form:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/9/a697cd5bfeed3bf0b41ac850488992117247afd4.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\begin{split} S &amp; = \frac{a_1}{1-r}  \end{split}\]" width="78" height="33" />
where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/b/1ab39d761413804680d26d972381f028001562f5.png" class="latex" alt="$a_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="10" /> is the first term and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> is the ratio whose absolute value is less than 1.
</p><p>We know that the second term is the first term multiplied by the ratio. 
In other words:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/1/dd15facf892da1c8aaca8cfe51e4945d9e0e7c49.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\begin{split} a_1 \cdot r &amp; = 1 \\ a_1 &amp; = \frac{1}{r} \end{split}\]" width="74" height="61" />
</p><p>Thus, the sum is the following:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/2/632da0913e6a0176c85f01f4b34a0f6e860638c4.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\begin{split} S &amp; = \frac{\frac{1}{r}}{1-r} \\\\ S &amp; =\frac{1}{r-r^2} \end{split}\]" width="85" height="111" />
</p><p>Since we want the minimum value of this expression, we want the maximum value for the denominator, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/e/b1e6a75067c021e01759924ed91f4cc59b46d8e1.png" class="latex" alt="$-r^2+r$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="60" height="16" />.
The maximum x-value of a quadratic with leading coefficient <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/b/5ab0d6b0f629cdfba5b6ce68695ff0c5a2d6fd8d.png" class="latex" alt="$-a$" width="23" height="8" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/5/b250a7d94be19932d73b6f79e32ec07dbb15e390.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{-b}{2a}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="24" height="37" />.
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/a/52a3bcebe1757c74c9a058f36cab56296cc76818.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\begin{split} r &amp; = \frac{-(1)}{2(-1)} \\\\ r &amp; = \frac{1}{2}  \end{split}\]" width="81" height="112" />
</p><p>Plugging <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/e/d5eb8726a34932d28103ce7ce17456421222603f.png" class="latex" alt="$=$" style="vertical-align: 2px" width="13" height="5" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> into the quadratic yields:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/4/b04356d84bcc75bd9078cebba044ef9c8a5dcd24.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\begin{split} S &amp; = \frac{1}{\frac{1}{2} -\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2} \\\\ S &amp; = \frac{1}{\frac{1}{4}}  \end{split}\]" width="105" height="123" />
</p><p>Therefore, the minimum sum of our infinite geometric sequence is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/0/420eb4362326f8089ce352b60312f07889d512b2.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(E)}\ 4}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="53" height="28" />.
(Solution by akaashp11)
</p><p>As an extension to find the maximum value for the denominator we can find the derivative of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/e/b1e6a75067c021e01759924ed91f4cc59b46d8e1.png" class="latex" alt="$-r^2+r$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="60" height="16" /> to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/0/ca05a70e360960f4b80279282ec77ab4d8edf57f.png" class="latex" alt="$1-2r$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="51" height="15" />. we know that this changes sign when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/5/875b1512877521e24e5424a39aba90a573f7dc7c.png" class="latex" alt="$r = \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="44" height="37" /> so plugging it in into the original equation we find the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/0/420eb4362326f8089ce352b60312f07889d512b2.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(E)}\ 4}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="53" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>After observation we realize that in order to minimize our sum <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/2/d92b8c5d55f66f20ed3f225d99a712796b2efced.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a}{1-r}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="42" height="33" /> with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> being the reciprocal of r. The common ratio <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> has to be in the form of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/a/79a0901419c277658469226431138dfb5ede474b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{x}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="12" height="37" /> with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> being an integer as anything more than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> divided by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> would give a larger sum than a ratio in the form of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/a/79a0901419c277658469226431138dfb5ede474b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{x}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="12" height="37" />. 
</p><p>The first term has to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />, so then in order to minimize the sum, we have minimize <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />. 
</p><p>The smallest possible value for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> such that it is an integer that's greater than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />. So our first term is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> and our common ratio is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. Thus the sum is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/d/bfd8ab77948e6e31c1cc8d6c2c9324b524f80906.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{\frac {1}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="16" height="45" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/0/420eb4362326f8089ce352b60312f07889d512b2.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(E)}\ 4}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="53" height="28" />.
Solution 2 by No_One
</p><p>(edited)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>We can see that if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is the first term, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> is the common ratio between each of the terms, then we can get
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/b/adbaef963036b32da5c449fc3cb7297470b4779f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[S=\frac{a}{1-r} \implies S-Sr=a\]" width="218" height="33" />
Also, we know that the second term can be expressed as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/e/40ee52af95da9d98c6262bdd5264df65569bffb4.png" class="latex" alt="$a\cdot r$" width="30" height="8" />
notice if we multiply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/b/2dbdd141cf17e7619068b2eadea5e7c55fe90af2.png" class="latex" alt="$S-Sr=a$" width="89" height="12" /> by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />, we would get
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/7/10721f8f40778c79d481ab4ad623c7bcaad89ee3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[r(S-Sr)=ar \implies Sr-Sr^2=1 \implies Sr^2-Sr+1=0\]" width="459" height="19" />
This quadratic has real solutions if the discriminant is greater than or equal to zero, or
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/3/8335bbaac48d9e07285e50a19806030cfbfacd89.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[S^2-4\cdot S \cdot 1 \ge 0\]" width="131" height="17" />
This yields that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/0/db08a80c2965d7e0ef356596bb69d01732505af0.png" class="latex" alt="$S\le 0$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="45" height="15" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/7/347c4fb3a88d6bdb3551a6a9a3597be1fd905d3b.png" class="latex" alt="$S\ge 4$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="45" height="15" />. 
However, since we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> has to be positive, we can safely conclude that the minimum possible value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/0/420eb4362326f8089ce352b60312f07889d512b2.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(E)}\ 4}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="53" height="28" />.
</p>
