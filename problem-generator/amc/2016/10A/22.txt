<p>For some positive integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, the number <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/f/c8fdbc5bb4b55101f2901d609fcd2c7c2adb8af7.png" class="latex" alt="$110n^3$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="44" height="15" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/9/d79dc7d13216568e73c831d7b8c14c1a30f7831a.png" class="latex" alt="$110$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> positive integer divisors, including <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> and the number <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/f/c8fdbc5bb4b55101f2901d609fcd2c7c2adb8af7.png" class="latex" alt="$110n^3$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="44" height="15" />. How many positive integer divisors does the number <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/a/2ca43ef5875132ecc9859a2baa9fea24fae7198f.png" class="latex" alt="$81n^4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="15" /> have?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/4/924d18c22c4c4b57af174a726bf49adc7f25e5fd.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }110\qquad\textbf{(B) }191\qquad\textbf{(C) }261\qquad\textbf{(D) }325\qquad\textbf{(E) }425$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="443" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Since the prime factorization of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/9/d79dc7d13216568e73c831d7b8c14c1a30f7831a.png" class="latex" alt="$110$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/f/ccfb827b85397ce4a9927cae6ce7b371b34668bd.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot 5 \cdot 11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="61" height="13" />, we have that the number is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/8/f8872fb6fb0b9e3d06bddbebd373fd5be3e681db.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot 5 \cdot 11 \cdot n^3$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="93" height="15" />.  This has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/9/ba98e2769ef16cbf2ce58a9feb9b70f796c64fe7.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2=8$" width="86" height="12" /> factors when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/9/1d96ecc15129c5631ba4a2c6578e91e3d13e3baf.png" class="latex" alt="$n=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="12" />.  This needs a multiple of 11 factors, which we can achieve by setting <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/b/71bb034bcd1758202c8134299144a7bb7543e262.png" class="latex" alt="$n=2^3$" width="50" height="15" />, so we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/7/6b72cefefed9405e00bfdf21753207c812d4e07d.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{10} \cdot 5 \cdot 11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="75" height="15" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/4/5b45828f292e161290be6ee62bc4c9df081ee1e1.png" class="latex" alt="$44$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> factors.  To achieve the desired <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/9/d79dc7d13216568e73c831d7b8c14c1a30f7831a.png" class="latex" alt="$110$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> factors, we need the number of factors to also be divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, so we can set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/a/53af0c8130eb33b087616d6031ec944480d93f95.png" class="latex" alt="$n=2^3 \cdot 5$" width="73" height="15" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/e/3ee854d4ed59ad4c61ed7c156a643029868cebf2.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{10} \cdot 5^4 \cdot 11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="83" height="15" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/9/d79dc7d13216568e73c831d7b8c14c1a30f7831a.png" class="latex" alt="$110$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> factors.  Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/a/53af0c8130eb33b087616d6031ec944480d93f95.png" class="latex" alt="$n=2^3 \cdot 5$" width="73" height="15" />.  In order to find the number of factors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/a/2ca43ef5875132ecc9859a2baa9fea24fae7198f.png" class="latex" alt="$81n^4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="15" />, we raise this to the fourth power and multiply it by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/e/87edbdda753b32453027dc7cab023c0743b6b243.png" class="latex" alt="$81$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />, and find the factors of that number.  We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/4/6345d3e37614bba3417c1ae733cfda3505f0b6e2.png" class="latex" alt="$3^4 \cdot 2^{12} \cdot 5^4$" width="81" height="15" />, and this has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/9/6490c343ffecca2de42b96d282c14ffa26f07bff.png" class="latex" alt="$5 \cdot 13 \cdot 5=\boxed{\textbf{(D) }325}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="161" height="28" /> factors.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/f/c8fdbc5bb4b55101f2901d609fcd2c7c2adb8af7.png" class="latex" alt="$110n^3$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="44" height="15" /> clearly has at least three distinct prime factors, namely 2, 5, and 11.
</p><p>The number of factors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/8/fa845e3f90329a3279333984ae5dffde15f2064b.png" class="latex" alt="$p_1^{n_1}\cdots p_k^{n_k}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="76" height="17" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/2/1c250873bf27057429c10224b651fd2058dd391c.png" class="latex" alt="$(n_1+1)\cdots(n_k+1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="154" height="18" /> when the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/f/36f73fc1312ee0349b3f3a0f3bd9eb5504339011.png" class="latex" alt="$p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" />'s are distinct primes. This tells us that none of these factors can be 1. The number of factors is given as 110. The only way to write 110 as a product of at least three factors without <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />s is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/d/acd78e6b70f080120fd3080563c827095e590cbb.png" class="latex" alt="$2\cdot 5\cdot 11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="61" height="13" />.
</p><p>We conclude that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/f/c8fdbc5bb4b55101f2901d609fcd2c7c2adb8af7.png" class="latex" alt="$110n^3$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="44" height="15" /> has only the three prime factors 2, 5, and 11 and that the multiplicities are 1, 4, and 10 in some order.  I.e., there are six different possible values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> all of the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/9/b59f6e614dee5986f823558b8c75f022d863fd56.png" class="latex" alt="$n=p_1\cdot p_2^3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="80" height="19" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/a/2ca43ef5875132ecc9859a2baa9fea24fae7198f.png" class="latex" alt="$81n^4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="15" /> thus has prime factorization <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/3/9e3b63ff578476d7bb2291c03a61c46e2db0c927.png" class="latex" alt="$81n^4=3^4\cdot p_1^4\cdot p_2^{12}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="141" height="19" /> and a factor count of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/6/1163f7d7dd04a8169d7f9c663c87768c2771e719.png" class="latex" alt="$5\cdot5\cdot13=\boxed{\textbf{(D) }325}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="161" height="28" />
</p>
