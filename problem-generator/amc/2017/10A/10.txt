<p>Joy has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/1/651ba418a810d971dbc8a326d49a71fe1541fae0.png" class="latex" alt="$30$" width="17" height="12" /> thin rods, one each of every integer length from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> cm through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/1/651ba418a810d971dbc8a326d49a71fe1541fae0.png" class="latex" alt="$30$" width="17" height="12" /> cm. She places the rods with lengths <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> cm, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" /> cm, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/9/f1965fae079a9ba2c0726c307070c2355dfcb213.png" class="latex" alt="$15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> cm on a table. She then wants to choose a fourth rod that she can put with these three to form a quadrilateral with positive area. How many of the remaining rods can she choose as the fourth rod?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/7/697d6dc64346b98f9830d6d95a8ca3617351211d.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 16\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 17\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 18\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 19\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 20$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="394" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>The triangle inequality generalizes to all polygons, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/b/5abb2109b354c763cf1bacb02c2bd07c7d7d0aac.png" class="latex" alt="$x &lt; 3+7+15$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="115" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/d/5ad034548856bfe4f2af12e1e1e429e5576981bf.png" class="latex" alt="$15&lt;x+3+7$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="115" height="14" /> yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/8/a38176c8505a827c3dab192a81c22899ee9f7425.png" class="latex" alt="$5&lt;x&lt;25$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="85" height="13" />. Now, we know that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/b/efbfe273946ff9f971c5eab331ca39caa6fdf7f4.png" class="latex" alt="$19$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> numbers between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/6/66644fd9f411299c3c1eabf151b5f601c9ef6c21.png" class="latex" alt="$25$" width="17" height="12" /> exclusive, but we must subtract <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> to account for the 2 lengths already used that are between those numbers, which gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/3/c631df4761c074815acc390250af98f167b4c05f.png" class="latex" alt="$19-2=\boxed{\textbf{(B)}\ 17}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="137" height="28" />
</p>
