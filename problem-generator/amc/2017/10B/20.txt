<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We note that the only thing that affects the parity of the factor are the powers of 2. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/7/b571c9943e2adb35a1a586e45eb73c8ba38dea4e.png" class="latex" alt="$10+5+2+1 = 18$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="154" height="14" /> factors of 2 in the number. Thus, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/7/5e7a1b6098a8c98b8e4adae526aeef4b91712620.png" class="latex" alt="$18$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> cases in which a factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/4/c1408c98668f8913671ca718ac80fab5ae886a70.png" class="latex" alt="$21!$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="21" height="13" /> would be even (have a factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> in its prime factorization), and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> case in which a factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/4/c1408c98668f8913671ca718ac80fab5ae886a70.png" class="latex" alt="$21!$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="21" height="13" /> would be odd. Therefore, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/4/f64bf24fa9f33d9550c24a37a2d9672d894f00d9.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B)} \frac 1{19}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="62" height="48" />.
</p><p>Note from Williamgolly: To see why symmetry occurs here, we group the factors of 21! into 2 groups, one with powers of 2 and the others odd factors. For each power of 2, the factors combine a certain number of 2's from the first group and numbers from the odd group. That is why symmetry occurs here.
</p>
