Point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> is the foot of the perpendicular from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> to diagonal <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/5/12517bbc31cb9026c71299b7a0d232d07e60f284.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AC}$" width="28" height="15" />. What is the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/8/1/18167d788fd698322ba516cf8bfdba2c931fa17d.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AED$" width="59" height="13" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/1/2012330cfee5c0001f6b85cba02da4e5ef800b5b.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 1\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{42}{25}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{28}{15}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 2\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \frac{54}{25}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="387" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/e/81ebb7263d49644ae842d3b0e2a3cfce7c5885ce.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pair A,B,C,D,E; A=(0,4); B=(3,4); C=(3,0); D=(0,0); draw(A--B--C--D--cycle); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,N); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,N); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,S); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,S); E=foot(B,A,C); draw(E--B); draw(A--C); draw(rightanglemark(B,E,C)); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,E,N); draw(D--E); label(&quot;$3$&quot;,A--B,N); label(&quot;$4$&quot;,B--C,E); [/asy]" width="182" height="252" />
</p><p>First, note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/b/edb20df9f4016a15bd68c2c0286c8b5e540ced58.png" class="latex" alt="$AC=5$" width="60" height="13" /> because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> is a right triangle. In addition, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/7/98758b43351b7737fc5a633ef12749dedd2574ca.png" class="latex" alt="$AB\cdot BC=2[ABC]=AC\cdot BE$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="250" height="18" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/f/42fbba24bfa34b22a53dfb4aaffe720961407a75.png" class="latex" alt="$BE=\frac{12}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="74" height="37" />. Using similar triangles within <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" />, we get that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/d/bed83b63f5e218122dd8425817ffa7ff26833e56.png" class="latex" alt="$AE=\frac{9}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="64" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/6/be6e3ae7c31ddee0686c91d2468ed9b64d62e5fe.png" class="latex" alt="$CE=\frac{16}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="73" height="37" />.
</p><p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> be the foot of the perpendicular from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/2/c128243ea8125d0bb007b8132dcfa44461df73a7.png" class="latex" alt="$EF$" width="28" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" /> are parallel, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/e/c4e52346474d4fa79bc0b5c9de3b4a49319b19e9.png" class="latex" alt="$\Delta AFE$" width="57" height="13" /> is similar to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/6/3b6d5d23a7e91fb9f6c248fb4cd43111165540f9.png" class="latex" alt="$\Delta ABC$" width="57" height="13" />. Therefore, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/c/92c8b0e69144e18e1dc2ba9ca15d9b64d32047aa.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AF}{AB}=\frac{AE}{AC}=\frac{9}{25}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="133" height="38" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/6/c56ff6dcfd62e4e93c0469964d28b362ccff9c2b.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=3$" width="61" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/f/79f4f7f9e75eec6ec31cff6f7a28087ab4e239ca.png" class="latex" alt="$AF=\frac{27}{25}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="72" height="37" />. Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/d/c9daba06f5ec9c5069f24bbeab9c7151ab4f68c2.png" class="latex" alt="$AF$" width="27" height="13" /> is an altitude of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/4/b64782f3ed925c790fed487d26744eb0ed100f70.png" class="latex" alt="$\Delta AED$" width="58" height="13" /> from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc56862b1fdb71204894da278e42ef75de73a40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AD$" width="28" height="13" />, which has length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />. Therefore, the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/4/b64782f3ed925c790fed487d26744eb0ed100f70.png" class="latex" alt="$\Delta AED$" width="58" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/f/def2fa86406297f072325a336f284d6c361f94bc.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{27}{25}\cdot4=\boxed{\textbf{(E)}\frac{54}{25}}.$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="159" height="48" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>From similar triangles, we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/d/bed83b63f5e218122dd8425817ffa7ff26833e56.png" class="latex" alt="$AE=\frac{9}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="64" height="37" /> (see Solution 1). Furthermore, we also know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/0/820bf77420841403304ea0b1c6eadf5e50bd1b36.png" class="latex" alt="$AD=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="62" height="13" /> from the rectangle. Using the sine formula for area, we have
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/9/069e6b7cde718a709e1143585a98a93262414657.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{2}(AE)(AD)(\sin(m\angle EAD)).\]" width="223" height="37" />
But, note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/d/b4ddbbb07508650e45e613e540a63f922246cbd3.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin(m\angle EAD)=\sin(m\angle CAD)=\frac{O}{H}=\frac{3}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="322" height="37" />. Thus, we see that
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/2/eb2bce2aaf565d036d31d674e6ff4e38b8f18a16.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[[AED]=\frac{1}{2}\cdot \frac{9}{5}\cdot 4\cdot\frac{3}{5}=\boxed{\textbf{(E)}\frac{54}{25}}.\]" width="252" height="48" />
~coolwiz
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Alternatively, we can use coordinates. Denote <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> as the origin. We find the equation for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/7/a179ff2638e4799cadd820db205c2beff6299ce9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC$" width="27" height="13" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/d/b0ddc335b49553a4d64347a9daac81c9d5ee3b7f.png" class="latex" alt="$y=-\frac{4}{3}x+4$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="102" height="37" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/b/a7baf95ecafc2d7791788ec79aeeae390d60b119.png" class="latex" alt="$BE$" width="28" height="12" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/5/2951b460d39d5fee756f8d6b8b795e62525a426c.png" class="latex" alt="$y=\frac{3}{4}x+\frac{7}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="91" height="38" />. Solving for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/4/544f4ef1bb08d6b3d779137071044082b1e581a5.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{27}{25}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" />. Our final answer then becomes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/f/def2fa86406297f072325a336f284d6c361f94bc.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{27}{25}\cdot4=\boxed{\textbf{(E)}\frac{54}{25}}.$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="159" height="48" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>We note that the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/8/fd85454c0ce4000c0e63b4a1c4a12ef6f8b56e9d.png" class="latex" alt="$ABE$" width="42" height="13" /> must equal the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/f/1/6f1bf55dee68ba4123ca736e3b4b2121ffebc98a.png" class="latex" alt="$AED$" width="43" height="13" /> because they share the base  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/6/9a65c05bd85d2609bea5db2b109ed5f556463511.png" class="latex" alt="$AE$" width="27" height="13" /> and the height of both is the altitude of congruent triangles. Therefore, we find the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/8/fd85454c0ce4000c0e63b4a1c4a12ef6f8b56e9d.png" class="latex" alt="$ABE$" width="42" height="13" /> to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/c/5ccd4d33750ed9da25b16bcfb034ebdcc3310022.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}*\frac{9}{5}*\frac{12}{5}=\boxed{\textbf{(E)}\frac{54}{25}}.$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="171" height="48" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5">Solution 5</span></h2>
<p>We know all right triangles are 5-4-3, so the areas are proportional to the square of corresponding sides. Area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/8/fd85454c0ce4000c0e63b4a1c4a12ef6f8b56e9d.png" class="latex" alt="$ABE$" width="42" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/3/1b38877d3c047021d2bc0c24b1e7327e19317791.png" class="latex" alt="$(\dfrac{3}{5})^2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="33" height="37" /> of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/5/cc52ec32ebb7a57e2d2b65905506684505a93633.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC = \frac{54}{25}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="87" height="37" />. Using similar logic in Solution 4, Area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/f/1/6f1bf55dee68ba4123ca736e3b4b2121ffebc98a.png" class="latex" alt="$AED$" width="43" height="13" /> is the same as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/8/fd85454c0ce4000c0e63b4a1c4a12ef6f8b56e9d.png" class="latex" alt="$ABE$" width="42" height="13" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_6">Solution 6</span></h2>
<p>Drop an altitude from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" /> and call its foot <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" />. We have that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/4/ec434de73cdfe63f9bea4fb7215cafa7a0a0405b.png" class="latex" alt="$EX \cdot BC=BE \cdot EC$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="170" height="14" /> since both are equal to two times the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/1/ef14ba8fc495f2a5750a5ed394ebf75a28641658.png" class="latex" alt="$BEC$" width="43" height="12" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/3/a5322c7f896502c208bbb4a354efc8d212620667.png" class="latex" alt="$BC=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="62" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/f/42fbba24bfa34b22a53dfb4aaffe720961407a75.png" class="latex" alt="$BE=\frac{12}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="74" height="37" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c33c655be1acd4891851edadf51ca7570409effd.png" class="latex" alt="$EC=\frac{16}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="77" height="38" />, we can calculate that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/2/e122066cd21166d005575e7ee528929a116f36e3.png" class="latex" alt="$EX=\frac{48}{25}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="79" height="38" />. If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/0/ec020ff3117019c0a1a0c7cd9c7004c88bfe5388.png" class="latex" alt="$EX$" width="30" height="12" /> is extended to meet <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc56862b1fdb71204894da278e42ef75de73a40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AD$" width="28" height="13" /> at point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/5/ce58e4af225c93d08606c26554caaa5ae32edeba.png" class="latex" alt="$Y$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/c/b5c0e8089b67b442f4f4975a7cd773ffc0cb136c.png" class="latex" alt="$EY=3-\frac{48}{25}=\frac{27}{25}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="155" height="38" />. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/3/7335bf424a67818293ffc82c14ac50e3bb91989d.png" class="latex" alt="$[AED]=\frac{EY \cdot AD}{2}=\frac{\frac{27}{25} \cdot 4}{2}=\boxed{\textbf{(E)}\frac{54}{25}}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="308" height="49" />.
</p><p>- Fasolinka
</p>
