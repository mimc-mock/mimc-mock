<p>Points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/3/5637fc75c9c9da168b51f81b23865cc654f02831.png" class="latex" alt="$A(11, 9)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="61" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/5/f45605e2d1092abdc7f8ff726507a664ca582a97.png" class="latex" alt="$B(2, -3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" /> are vertices of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/9/3c99bbe74cccdc92b249d376f13d9429d41a8b7a.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=AC$" width="80" height="13" />. The altitude from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> meets the opposite side at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/b/21b93a6c5c49cbd77f587aa830e632f576585b36.png" class="latex" alt="$D(-1, 3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="68" height="18" />. What are the coordinates of point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/4/2b48be0a6bc04615eda7bb3c23b49b8f6fb5a987.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ (-8, 9)\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ (-4, 8)\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ (-4, 9)\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ (-2, 3)\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ (-1, 0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="575" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/a/f3ac37c9fa391eebb9601bfaca8bfbfaabdf5ca1.png" class="latex" alt="$AB = AC$" width="80" height="13" />, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> is isosceles, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/3/383377396cd2b6bd95f64d0e79ecd0f0eac25dad.png" class="latex" alt="$BD = CD$" width="84" height="12" />. Therefore, the coordinates of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/1/a91e096a8783312bc8c982fbc2c68cc751e653ad.png" class="latex" alt="$(-1 - 3, 3 + 6) = \boxed{\textbf{(C) } (-4,9)}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="242" height="29" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/c/d6c903419860d4d2d6fb832a27d73e8e7acd40ae.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pair A,B,C,D; A=(11,9); B=(2,-3); C=(-4,9); D=(-1,3); draw(A--B--C--cycle); draw(A--D); draw(rightanglemark(A,D,B)); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,E); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,S); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,W); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,N); [/asy]" width="252" height="218" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Calculating the equation of the line running between points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/b/4/7b4dd8e3fd5bd866cf3f796d90e93e68f26e91fa.png" class="latex" alt="$y = -2x + 1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="97" height="15" />. The only coordinate of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> that is also on this line is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/c/19cf935578c84bbbc3ee1b03236afae1d921ced5.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) } (-4,9)}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="101" height="29" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Similar to the first solution, because the triangle is isosceles, then the line drawn in the middle separates the triangle into two smaller congruent triangles. To get from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />, we go to the right <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> and up <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />. Then to get to point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> from point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />, we go to the right <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> and up <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />, getting us the coordinates <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/c/19cf935578c84bbbc3ee1b03236afae1d921ced5.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) } (-4,9)}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="101" height="29" />. ~<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/a/b/eab10c25a519751033aabcf20d0e967f40c170e0.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{KLBBC}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="59" height="12" />
</p>
