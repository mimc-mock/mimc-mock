<p>The vertices of an equilateral triangle lie on the hyperbola <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa22a3b6fdd8321ce93427ac874f16557593d663.png" class="latex" alt="$xy=1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="52" height="15" />, and a vertex of this hyperbola is the centroid of the triangle. What is the square of the area of the triangle?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/7/5/6752c5fae2fdc4eda1f4db6f45e50e5bb40c9bd3.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 48\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 60\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 108\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 120\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 169$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="421" height="17" />
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>WLOG, let the centroid of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/8/16898c93ca99e972aba8081eabf034d780e0e88a.png" class="latex" alt="$I = (-1,-1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="100" height="18" />. The centroid of an equilateral triangle is the same as the circumcenter. It follows that the circumcircle must intersect the graph exactly three times. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/8/cf85dcc67cf0ef126cd0512c43f43ae9bfd7db24.png" class="latex" alt="$A = (1,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="76" height="18" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/a/f7a37a45cff81da9aba4eb4cbf683c636778f34f.png" class="latex" alt="$AI = BI = CI = 2\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="177" height="18" />, so since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/d/78da149a793157060c45ee9704b865f9d54e7b7c.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AIB$" width="53" height="13" /> is isosceles and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/9/a694ac77946619bb145e1369d36a06106943094b.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle AIB = 120^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="108" height="13" />, then by the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Law_of_Cosines" title="Law of Cosines">Law of Cosines</a>, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/2/d0263d16f1251c61576d785332109eb5740e787d.png" class="latex" alt="$AB = 2\sqrt{6}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="86" height="18" />. Alternatively, we can use the fact that the circumradius of an equilateral triangle is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/3/213c1b759acf6b85b5f511deb5bb31025c98d591.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {s}{\sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="26" height="37" />. Therefore, the area of the triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/e/37eb6edafc274ae12676f12c5752c1c76214beae.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{(2\sqrt{6})^2\sqrt{3}}4 = 6\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="141" height="41" />, so the square of the area of the triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/3/e933aa6bba7566d5f501c446724b423041c6fc2a.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) } 108}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="74" height="28" />.
</p><p><br />
Note: We could’ve also noticed that the centroid divides the median into segments of ratio <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/f/b2f31a0a79d505a8d4f4ff508d10f8c2f7fed729.png" class="latex" alt="$2:1.$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="36" height="12" /> ~peelybonehead
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Without loss of generality, let the centroid of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/e/1ee63938d8ef80d717a3827df5564ac035d7d5b8.png" class="latex" alt="$G = (-1,-1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="105" height="18" />. Then, one of the vertices must be the other curve of the hyperbola. Without loss of generality, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/8/cf85dcc67cf0ef126cd0512c43f43ae9bfd7db24.png" class="latex" alt="$A = (1,1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="76" height="18" />. Then, point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> must be the reflection of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> across the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/1/741a74d86df899c10724dc2f80638f5e7f599194.png" class="latex" alt="$y=x$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="11" />, so let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/f/44f898eb4223c8eb73cf528d263a7f7d518e48e6.png" class="latex" alt="$B = \left(a,\frac{1}{a}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="93" height="43" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13a8e9af4febe855527deb438e1b943e4bd0d9e.png" class="latex" alt="$C=\left(\frac{1}{a},a\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="92" height="43" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/2/eb2f33e3db3947650ec017f8836475391f35f1e2.png" class="latex" alt="$a &lt;-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="56" height="12" />. Because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e28ce12d49d39f160d5a0ef54077fc98e4b9d2b.png" class="latex" alt="$G$" width="14" height="12" /> is the centroid, the average of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-coordinates of the vertices of the triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/e/bae5aba07d37ff6ff813107e76260fb31ad5794e.png" class="latex" alt="$-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="22" height="12" />. So we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/9/6493b1b19e136c858bfaba8dd4bab633411a4143.png" class="latex" alt="$a + 1/a+ 1 = -3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="137" height="18" />. Multiplying by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and solving gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/6/7e6c50a1a2ff8085fd999ac678959049f516367e.png" class="latex" alt="$a=-2-\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="104" height="18" />. So <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/a/76a895022d85b63c25a886d21fe405b31b2efbfa.png" class="latex" alt="$B=(-2-\sqrt{3},-2+\sqrt{3})$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="199" height="21" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/e/34e5ab4227eee92b4363465a3501a31af17be499.png" class="latex" alt="$C=(-2+\sqrt{3},-2-\sqrt{3})$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="198" height="21" />. So <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/1/29112155be403ad7624df5645ccfad2b2368a37f.png" class="latex" alt="$BC=2\sqrt{6}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="87" height="18" />, and finding the square of the area gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/3/e933aa6bba7566d5f501c446724b423041c6fc2a.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) } 108}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="74" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Without loss of generality, let the centroid of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/3/d439dad48713ae50859b5bbac0bdcf77499e0e21.png" class="latex" alt="$G = (1, 1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="77" height="18" /> and let point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/5/6155e28d15217ab6e8ea537771fdce74d647ed6e.png" class="latex" alt="$(-1, -1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" />. It is known that the centroid is equidistant from the three vertices of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" />. Because we have the coordinates of both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e28ce12d49d39f160d5a0ef54077fc98e4b9d2b.png" class="latex" alt="$G$" width="14" height="12" />, we know that the distance from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e28ce12d49d39f160d5a0ef54077fc98e4b9d2b.png" class="latex" alt="$G$" width="14" height="12" /> to any vertice of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/0/1f00821a356192ebf7f7a266c5560324e45e4e56.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{(1-(-1))^2+(1-(-1))^2} = 2\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="278" height="32" />. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/1/3f1186c8c3dd7d11441f72924d62a352c56344ba.png" class="latex" alt="$AG=BG=CG=2\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="192" height="18" />. It follows that from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/d/94dfa01e383fbd30d7b1970446754029302b6d7f.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABG$" width="58" height="13" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/e/e5ec3fee1157c1eff42a9a04e8f5437b2cf7c485.png" class="latex" alt="$AG=BG=2\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="139" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/4/7849ec00c5a35b5a0ad2ae28da21f59bdcedfe7e.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle AGB = \dfrac{360^{\circ}}{3} = 120^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="176" height="37" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/9/b797916969ec922b52dcddaf59d2dc673d9dd8f4.png" class="latex" alt="$[\triangle ABG]= \dfrac{(2\sqrt{2})^2 \cdot \sin(120)}{2} = 4 \cdot \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = 2\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="361" height="41" /> using the formula for the area of a triangle with sine <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/0/210e8feaa83afafb48ff3115889923c9c6536ef5.png" class="latex" alt="$\left([\triangle ABC]= \dfrac{1}{2} AB \cdot BC \sin(\angle ABC)\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="294" height="43" />. Because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/b/06bb54e126b1f04f05f2a2943488bc5c7a13255a.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ACG$" width="58" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/3/4c3a6769640eb1f33831325c0589b50f7a83d7fc.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BCG$" width="59" height="13" /> are congruent to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/d/94dfa01e383fbd30d7b1970446754029302b6d7f.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABG$" width="58" height="13" />, they also have an area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/5/e455d80f4657b1112ba302a6e2c42e9c81f780ee.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/c/fbce22c863efbff58786e61b2c4572f1bcb2c2a8.png" class="latex" alt="$[\triangle ABC] = 3(2\sqrt{3}) = 6\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="207" height="22" />. Squaring that gives us the answer of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/3/1739da8eb05ec2eedd781d1cd667661b961e787e.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) }108}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="74" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>Without loss of generality, let the centroid of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/3/d439dad48713ae50859b5bbac0bdcf77499e0e21.png" class="latex" alt="$G = (1, 1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="77" height="18" />. Assuming we don't know one vertex is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/5/6155e28d15217ab6e8ea537771fdce74d647ed6e.png" class="latex" alt="$(-1, -1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" /> we let the vertices be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/f/75f324c52322f195a7d590a59cbf12642d370730.png" class="latex" alt="$A\left(x_1, \frac{1}{x_1}\right), B\left(x_2, \frac{1}{x_2}\right), C\left(x_3, \frac{1}{x_3}\right).$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="302" height="43" />
</p><p>Since the centroid coordinates are the average of the vertex coordinates, we have that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/f/3bf569aad38f078f2ad3f313d38d6863cc5ae0da.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{x_1+x_2+x_3}{3}=1$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="134" height="35" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/2/272d030febc2c120f2c1330bfd8557ae864a07b8.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\frac{1}{x_1}+\frac{1}{x_2}+\frac{1}{x_3}}{3}=1.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="139" height="42" />
</p><p>We also know that the centroid is the orthocenter in an equilateral triangle, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/e/64ed328abcfcc0b4c4838e4e3f943f74a4a9e3b3.png" class="latex" alt="$CG \perp AB.$" width="84" height="13" /> Examining slopes, we simplify the equation to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/f/38f5db6827d4cc05447770cc10737c97344b73d0.png" class="latex" alt="$x_1x_2x_3 = -1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="100" height="14" />. From the equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/c/28c31fd39141745737c8f33992fad43d9c37b85e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\frac{1}{x_1}+\frac{1}{x_2}+\frac{1}{x_3}}{3}=1,$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="139" height="42" /> we get that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/4/2f4f5bf11b20d35ee20e87907b42a06f4c3858c9.png" class="latex" alt="$x_1x_2+x_1x_3+x_2x_3 = -3$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="198" height="15" />. These equations are starting to resemble Vieta's:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/d/3ad776d24c7700fedd619dc68d51e7875f17927d.png" class="latex" alt="$x_1+x_2+x_3=3$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="130" height="15" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/4/2f4f5bf11b20d35ee20e87907b42a06f4c3858c9.png" class="latex" alt="$x_1x_2+x_1x_3+x_2x_3 = -3$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="198" height="15" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/7/a/67a83e92e3eeefc0f94fa2f4c3f9972731488d35.png" class="latex" alt="$x_1x_2x_3=-1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="100" height="14" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/0/d500fb815f92dec3dba9e71115d728583cb171d6.png" class="latex" alt="$x_1,x_2,x_3$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="68" height="11" /> are the roots of the equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/b/e1babe322eb195daad6644c0b495bdfc00762c6d.png" class="latex" alt="$x^3 - 3x^2 - 3x + 1 = 0$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="172" height="16" />. This factors as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/7/37744129a8a6786225d0f040b605d817860c1270.png" class="latex" alt="$(x+1)(x^2-4x+1)=0 \implies x = -1, 2 \pm \sqrt3,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="369" height="21" /> for the points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/7/1a707f9399f72efa9ad704c9409596233775ebbe.png" class="latex" alt="$(-1, -1), (2+\sqrt3, 2-\sqrt3), (2-\sqrt3, 2+\sqrt3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="350" height="21" />. The side length is clearly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/8/ed8dcdaee5c02f5afb2e8774644fc8fbc0b614f3.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{24}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />, so the square of the area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/c/14c90110320934839f4312bb7c8f7a351675b5d6.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{108}.$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="42" height="23" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/e/5ced40cce6ef244fae2a1fca18eb07682709b1dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\sim\textbf{Leonard\_my\_dude}\sim$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="185" height="15" />
</p>
