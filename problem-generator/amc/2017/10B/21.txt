<p>In <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/e/c8e7d324ea465aef56f3f3e708d966da9db8e90e.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=6$" width="61" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/b/8bb2b95ffbb95380ba201d65798db632de5df06c.png" class="latex" alt="$AC=8$" width="60" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/8/c68227ec2efd9339ffd16251b000f7b70017e121.png" class="latex" alt="$BC=10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="70" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> is the midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/3/e33fe7d65facd8868f58b6e94ddc7f153a5a3f9f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BC}$" width="29" height="15" />. What is the sum of the radii of the circles inscribed in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/8/e08af8f3acc34996e3cba566dc970b7ed496ba4b.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ADB$" width="59" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/e/b8eabc87afe4c11a47e4aa27628d5517af4866b9.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ADC$" width="59" height="13" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/7/2b7cf280c2a8195722f87c66425ba5c20a0a2898.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \sqrt{5}\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{11}{4}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 2\sqrt{2}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{17}{6}\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 3$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="414" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We note that by the converse of the Pythagorean Theorem, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> is a right triangle with a right angle at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/d/5fd398d04326b0aac763aad4b212c265d692ced0.png" class="latex" alt="$AD = BD = CD = 5$" width="170" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/c/72c87824e54a05f84b4aebb510f8b049a35cac2f.png" class="latex" alt="$[ADB] = [ADC] = 12$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="173" height="18" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/3/d63483ea6a1ce6f3e0eed9672216454744670a60.png" class="latex" alt="$A = rs,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="59" height="16" /> we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/8/2/182207d28676fb38d26499da8850a8715e60635b.png" class="latex" alt="$r = \frac As$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="52" height="39" />, so the inradius of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/8/e08af8f3acc34996e3cba566dc970b7ed496ba4b.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ADB$" width="59" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/5/f55371e1def12f1db8dec8323966b86c6b3368ff.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{12}{(5+5+6)/2} = \frac 32$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="144" height="42" />, and the inradius of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/e/b8eabc87afe4c11a47e4aa27628d5517af4866b9.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ADC$" width="59" height="13" />  is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/e/39e15571b6741ca18b329698a6b9415d4321ac81.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{12}{(5+5+8)/2} = \frac 43$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="144" height="41" />. Adding the two together, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/b/1cbf4694c9c896b70a5a862b0d889208cccc5509.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) } \frac{17}6}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="69" height="48" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We have
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f372b4037a532f261b763a969515ff2d75cf60d9.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] draw((0,0)--(8,0)); draw((0,0)--(0,6)); draw((8,0)--(0,6)); draw((0,0)--(4,3)); label(&quot;A&quot;,(0,0),W); label(&quot;B&quot;,(0,6),N); label(&quot;C&quot;,(8,0),E); label(&quot;D&quot;,(4,3),NE); label(&quot;H&quot;,(2.3,4.2),NE); label(&quot;K&quot;,(2.3,1.8),S); draw(circle((1.54,3),1.49)); draw(circle((4,1.35),1.33)); dot((4,1.35)); dot((1.54,3)); label(&quot;F&quot;,(1.54,3),S); label(&quot;J&quot;,(4,1.35),SW); label(&quot;G&quot;,(0,3),W); label(&quot;$x$&quot;,(1,3),S); label(&quot;$y$&quot;,(4,1),E); draw((1.54,3)--(0,3)); draw((1.54,3)--(2.3,1.8)); draw((1.54,3)--(2.3,4.2)); draw((4,1.35)--(4,0)); draw((4,1.35)--(3.12,2.4)); draw((4,1.35)--(4.8,2.3)); label(&quot;L&quot;,(4.9,2.4),NE); label(&quot;E&quot;,(3.11,2.3),S); label(&quot;I&quot;,(4,0),S); [/asy]" width="252" height="198" />
Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> be the radius of circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" />, and let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> be the radius of circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/b/abb5588023cfa1ac14643e9778699f03eecc57a3.png" class="latex" alt="$J$" width="12" height="12" />. We want to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/d/d3da9f8c7fe0473fdd6f8597eff61d71fedda430.png" class="latex" alt="$x+y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="41" height="14" />.
</p><p>We form 6 kites: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/d/c6dbd7114b8d56880a1fafbba8332c1c05671990.png" class="latex" alt="$GAKF$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="61" height="14" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/5/d35d766f43c491016f1d8768584825eb2a96e093.png" class="latex" alt="$HFKD$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="65" height="14" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/1/a013e6914dfafda08ea21182a43d4b860e5b5619.png" class="latex" alt="$GFHB$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="62" height="14" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/4/9e464a1b562bf52615aad9f259fca6f389a93653.png" class="latex" alt="$EJIA$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="51" height="14" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/d/5ed2e6f7d90df718c412248dd970de005e2fb50a.png" class="latex" alt="$LJIC$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="50" height="14" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/0/a80753d106f46472ab8cfc27a6389a3f6c8f85e4.png" class="latex" alt="$JEDL$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="56" height="14" />.
Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e28ce12d49d39f160d5a0ef54077fc98e4b9d2b.png" class="latex" alt="$G$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/7/027f4a11d6090f9eac0ce2488df6384dad1263ea.png" class="latex" alt="$I$" width="9" height="12" /> are the midpoints of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/5/12517bbc31cb9026c71299b7a0d232d07e60f284.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AC}$" width="28" height="15" />, respectively, this means that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/9/70974f91a2c21b8b26145dc79fd7cc77f12a9538.png" class="latex" alt="$BG = AG = \frac{6}{2} = 3$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="154" height="38" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/0/5a0e00522a47ba1ae78dcd2a4476791458dadd9b.png" class="latex" alt="$AI = IC = \frac{8}{2} = 4$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="143" height="38" />. 
</p><p>Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/2/852a70ac23e0c2fbdcf3c167cf2b2222ccca599a.png" class="latex" alt="$AGFK$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="61" height="14" /> is a kite, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/6/61686631acca165d10f19bae532993fd61bc1e67.png" class="latex" alt="$GF = FK = x$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="120" height="14" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/c/91c561caad1f1a8a82e41ca4db1a84d5ca8545b8.png" class="latex" alt="$AG = AK = 3$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="118" height="14" />. The same applies to all kites in the diagram.
</p><p>Now, we see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/9/cf943b0aac1ae4408899fc5680f4f58f773b4835.png" class="latex" alt="$AK = 3$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="66" height="14" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/6/e465d1e9884c49266c441d5265bdb53eaa6c3844.png" class="latex" alt="$KD = 2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="67" height="14" />, thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc56862b1fdb71204894da278e42ef75de73a40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AD$" width="28" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, making <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/e/b8eabc87afe4c11a47e4aa27628d5517af4866b9.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ADC$" width="59" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/5/f75d6d58b03031bd7cb52a7ca886a8ddf369d21c.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD$" width="59" height="13" /> isosceles. So, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/8/27810beb45880f65f0d6dcf970cd8320c39ef1d9.png" class="latex" alt="$DI=3$" width="57" height="12" /> using the Pythagorean Theorem, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/f/66f3202c0e2aa0a213909636d4476477ad0e717d.png" class="latex" alt="$GD=4$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="65" height="14" /> also using the Theorem. Hence, we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/a/9ba977697c4c91ca36fec99f198384b9261d8591.png" class="latex" alt="$[ADC] = [ABD] = 12$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="176" height="20" />. 
</p><p>Notice that the area of the kite (if the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> opposite angles are right) is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/8/cc83fcab971bda01c9f6f603be3088f4c45f9dad.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{s_1 \cdot s_2}{2} \cdot 2$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="73" height="35" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/e/7ae90d71491f591ab5a16d19ee7629e8226ec9ce.png" class="latex" alt="$s_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="14" height="10" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/6/bb6c5ddfd368673c178f3323ce2994cdbc8fbdab.png" class="latex" alt="$s_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="14" height="10" /> denoting each of the 2 congruent sides. This just simplifies to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/b/e5be3d50536a2714517afcc5ac05ec5054e74121.png" class="latex" alt="$s_1 \cdot s_2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="47" height="13" />.
Hence, we have 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/7/4e74f3cd35dcba5b6bc8f017c43a649417e481a8.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[4b+4b+b = 12\]" width="127" height="14" />
</p><p>and
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/a/93af8c7b29cf1fb7e414d808d26cde8040a3217b.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[3a+3a+2a = 12\]" width="141" height="13" />
</p><p>Solving for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" />, we find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/1/4d1b9a588658924fc4f3492498819598a697cf21.png" class="latex" alt="$a = \frac{3}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="49" height="38" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/e/07e723692dbf599eb0ec460ee77ce93bb5e75894.png" class="latex" alt="$b = \frac{4}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="47" height="38" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/a/48aab4e6dbeabcab69edd274d5c358c913905a90.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b = \frac{3}{2} + \frac {4}{3} = \boxed{\textbf{(D)} ~\frac{17}6}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="207" height="49" />.
</p><p>~MrThinker
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Applying Stewart’s theorem gives us the length of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/c/4dc6e3a1c2ff7e943dc0bf9412e21ad7183b193f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AD}.$" width="32" height="15" /> Using that length, we can find the areas of triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/5/f75d6d58b03031bd7cb52a7ca886a8ddf369d21c.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD$" width="59" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/7/0f7a55113a84ee4c04eb66f66d08310c32533ca2.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ACD$" width="59" height="13" /> by using Heron’s formula. We can use that area to find the inradius of the circles by the inradius formula <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/0/830f38d2c5cfdda54363ab9047b136fbf5fce1c2.png" class="latex" alt="$A=sr.$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="61" height="14" /> Therefore, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/e/b2ea5df0ffb68912b35376300a91815c090cc778.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) }\frac{17}{6}}.$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="76" height="49" /> Although this solution works perfectly fine, it takes lots of time and steps so apply Stewart’s and Heron’s with caution. ~peelybonehead
</p>
