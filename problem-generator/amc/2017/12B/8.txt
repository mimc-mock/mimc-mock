<p>The ratio of the short side of a certain rectangle to the long side is equal to the ratio of the long side to the diagonal. What is the square of the ratio of the short side to the long side of this rectangle?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/5/5e5577c6b7ec881aa4eea977c9473b9a1f976646.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{\sqrt{3}-1}{2}\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{1}{2}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{2} \qquad\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \frac{\sqrt{6}-1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="522" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1:_Cross-Multiplication">Solution 1: Cross-Multiplication</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> be the short side of the rectangle, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> be the long side of the rectangle. The diagonal, therefore, is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/2/bc241e5b2c6a386e1177b9d8349a2dead9cb37ec.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="70" height="18" />. We can get the equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/4/a44e39c26292dc0088b234cde0b2c7f58f67fad3.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a}{b} = \frac{b}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="110" height="42" />. Cross-multiplying, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/7/657e032753794bc3ac192c61e3ed715b2c9990cf.png" class="latex" alt="$a\sqrt{a^2 + b^2} = b^2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="117" height="18" />. Squaring both sides of the equation, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/a/b/eab383d02db49031dd83f9a96fc2bfcd87b40b23.png" class="latex" alt="$a^2 (a^2 + b^2) = b^4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="124" height="19" />, which simplifies to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/2/a42c79376efdfd55a8c5696588c9c30899c9fec2.png" class="latex" alt="$a^4 + a^2b^2 - b^4 = 0$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="142" height="16" />. Solving for a quadratic in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/9/9698c992334be77221be8c8c819909eca34fde9b.png" class="latex" alt="$a^2$" width="15" height="15" />, using the quadratic formula we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/7/f173e29bbea0634ea4540cb122a30429c0844d87.png" class="latex" alt="$a^2 = \frac{-b^2 \pm \sqrt{5b^4}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="135" height="41" /> which gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/b/42b3cc8c79c313da2ba0dff905fc7675fc5604eb.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a^2}{b^2} = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{5}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="117" height="41" />. We know that the square of the ratio must be positive (the square of any real number is positive), so the solution is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/2/ce20387121915c952daaed2821492a654a1eb5d9.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C)} \frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="100" height="53" />.
</p><p>Solution by: vedadehhc
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2:_Substitution">Solution 2: Substitution</span></h2>
<p>Solution by HydroQuantum
</p><p><br />
Let the short side of the rectangle be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and let the long side of the rectangle be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" />. Then, the diagonal, according to the Pythagorean Theorem, is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/9/729acb7391552a1f8d893217944a9d7004a5b32b.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="70" height="18" />. Therefore, we can write the equation:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/4/a44e39c26292dc0088b234cde0b2c7f58f67fad3.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a}{b} = \frac{b}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="110" height="42" />.
</p><p>We are trying to find the square of the ratio of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" />. Let's let our answer, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/6/2d61dba2e4b918cb0df217e5ebfcb8c043daf8a0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a^2}{b^2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="19" height="39" />, be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />. Then, squaring the above equation,
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/6/246f8302c3e766153d0852cbd61e9e3de5983608.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a^2}{b^2}=k=\frac{b^2}{a^2 + b^2}=\frac{b^2}{a^2}-\frac{b^2}{b^2}=\frac{1}{k}-1$" style="vertical-align: -14px" width="293" height="40" />.
</p><p>Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/3/e93f52eac4ae97c810eddc522d48fac92fc40226.png" class="latex" alt="$k=\frac{1}{k}-1$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="78" height="37" />.
</p><p>Multiplying each side of the equation by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />,
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/4/d84d70a9c3b15c0b87cfa676b1767c13550f2cd8.png" class="latex" alt="$k^2=1-k$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="82" height="15" />.
</p><p>Adding each side by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/c/7dc6fab4c2fdb4e9046030fbfdcf6ea721b05602.png" class="latex" alt="$k-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="13" />,
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/9/7291cc1cff49a16bb0da8071007349f58c239e66.png" class="latex" alt="$k^2+k-1=0$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="114" height="16" />.
</p><p>Solving for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> using the Quadratic Formula,
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/8/b78fe2370ff59664d743b019a5e7588cbf00e3f0.png" class="latex" alt="$k=\frac{-1\pm\sqrt{1^2-4(1)(-1)}}{2}=\frac{-1\pm\sqrt{5}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="306" height="41" />.
</p><p>Since the ratio of lengths and diagonals of a rectangle cannot be negative, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/3/473a691010d3d45421ba61d020ae913829407fe4.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{5}&gt;1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="56" height="18" />, the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/2/91205e81c98c486418902c88d5c396f5355491f1.png" class="latex" alt="$\pm$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="13" height="12" /> symbol can only take on the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/c/77cf26be132ef93923e082ee4153b2cb0ef44a50.png" class="latex" alt="$+$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="13" height="12" />. Therefore,
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/c/5fc70fedf2a38eb371409a813bc1f015ca55748a.png" class="latex" alt="$k=\frac{-1+\sqrt{5}}{2}=\boxed{\textbf{(C)} \frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="232" height="53" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>WLOG, assume the short side of the rectangle is 1 and the long side is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />.  Then, we wish to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/4/a84a13e3fc6963417df14ae3af524f14e4b53fd3.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{x^2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" /> when we are given <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/9/cf9dc125c9e25e492ecca80b8fca92253ede523f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{x}=\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+1}}\implies x^4-x^2-1=0.\]" width="280" height="41" />  Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/f/8ef862bdd16c2d5356e11735b8c766c402f5e10c.png" class="latex" alt="$y=x^2$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="50" height="18" /> and note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/f/19f5fee18454f3957300e34de79868df6b6532c5.png" class="latex" alt="$y^2-y-1=0$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="113" height="18" /> has solutions <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/e/faed16545d28d960e430487ff2af91fab08a8208.png" class="latex" alt="$y=\frac{1\pm\sqrt{5}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="92" height="41" />.  Since we want the positive answer, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/e/29e2650ebaaed12308f7e3c21a0a17c3c5b0a424.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2=y=\frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="134" height="41" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/d/6bd500515e22998a995abcc7d2f839a72999300f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{x^2}=\boxed{\textbf{(C)} \frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="146" height="53" />. 
</p><p>Solution by Jzhang21
</p>
