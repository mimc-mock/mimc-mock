<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> be an equilateral triangle. <h3><span id="Solution_1_(Uses_Trig)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1_.28Uses_Trig.29">Solution 1 (Uses Trig)</span></h3>
<p>Note that by symmetry, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/a/73a764854d1073fac0a788e874db580d732c7c89.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;$" width="71" height="14" /> is also equilateral. Therefore, we only need to find one of the sides of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/2/022948689ac03ea1705a6af78b86d32ae2a369eb.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;$" width="55" height="14" /> to determine the area ratio. WLOG, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/2/992d84200f48288979eb109b78b045f1645addad.png" class="latex" alt="$AB = BC = CA = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="166" height="13" />. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/9/5691d153a8a5d263583fb7c744b8ffa39f516974.png" class="latex" alt="$BB&#39; = 3$" width="67" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/6/7665cb803b39d9140e5d8bc51614f13fcaa2508d.png" class="latex" alt="$BC&#39; = 4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="67" height="14" />. Also, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/7/517fca5db8a2ace746b6069f0164ea86d2d432c5.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle B&#39;BC&#39; = 120^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="123" height="14" />, so by the Law of Cosines, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/e/79ee5a69b0338e3b340da0f4bca033f346775bca.png" class="latex" alt="$B&#39;C&#39; = \sqrt{37}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="96" height="18" />. Therefore, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/9/aa97d0bf4ec28efbc06471fa215f6f7dbc269892.png" class="latex" alt="$(\sqrt{37})^2&#160;: 1^2 = \boxed{\textbf{(E) } 37}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="174" height="28" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>As mentioned in the first solution, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/a/73a764854d1073fac0a788e874db580d732c7c89.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;$" width="71" height="14" /> is equilateral. WLOG, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/2/d5204a6910d20a4ff2968ad55671e3cfa350e9da.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=2$" width="61" height="13" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> be on the line passing through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/1/561ef57922570a73076ecae87b5e6f1f464c656b.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;D$" width="33" height="14" /> is perpendicular to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" />. Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/f/3ff95da7031c0bb6709711079f6002d07acec007.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle A&#39;DA$" width="63" height="14" /> is a 30-60-90 with right angle at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/4/494ffe68767cbafe60f91be48c865b5d370d90d2.png" class="latex" alt="$AA&#39;=6$" width="64" height="14" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/8/6e867467073fae9550c977a21ee2cb19426c4f92.png" class="latex" alt="$AD=3$" width="62" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cf90a18215ed88aee755e9d572983d0a9dd23.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;D=3\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="92" height="18" />. So we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/8/7a83e78a95eb73a2ea527d2855cb29e758f7cad4.png" class="latex" alt="$DB&#39;=11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="76" height="14" />. Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/d/1fd94ad8efd1037f076ce43ad7e8c8f0b1214f7a.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle A&#39;DB&#39;$" width="68" height="14" /> is a right triangle with right angle at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />. So by the Pythagorean theorem, we find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/1/1019741e70f16b1dc815c82cc9d031799c63c1d5.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;B&#39;= \sqrt{(3\sqrt{3})^2 + 11^2} = 2\sqrt{37}.$" style="vertical-align: -9px" width="256" height="35" /> Therefore, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/3/593808d13fe9011e82d836f056dcf89be7e78a7f.png" class="latex" alt="$(2\sqrt{37})^2&#160;: 2^2 = \boxed{\textbf{(E) } 37}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="183" height="28" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h3>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/5/715114c5f4c2bd8fd51b2578d6c45c095ce1e71f.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=BC=CA=x$" width="168" height="13" />. We start by noting that we can just write <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/b/2eb2c7600634636b0a25b56d087d112d6fbae523.png" class="latex" alt="$AB&#39;$" width="32" height="14" /> as just <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/6/d962025d8008c94272a07eafd35cab6bbae992ae.png" class="latex" alt="$AB+BB&#39;=4AB$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="145" height="15" />.
Similarly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/e/01ed5e76b77db33870fbda50a65aef6a2b535deb.png" class="latex" alt="$BC&#39;=4BC$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="95" height="14" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/0/99008f997baef172bb1e754011c0d8b907315ca6.png" class="latex" alt="$CA&#39;=4CA$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="93" height="14" />. We can evaluate the area of triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> by simply using Heron's formula, 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/f/60f2b804519b772cda25aa3d4c85fd502af042b5.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABC]=\sqrt{\frac{3x}{2}\cdot {\left(\frac{3x}{2}-x\right)}^3}=\frac{x^2\sqrt{3}}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="293" height="56" />.
Next in order to evaluate <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/2/022948689ac03ea1705a6af78b86d32ae2a369eb.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;$" width="55" height="14" /> we need to evaluate the area of the larger triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/0/7109d88a8c72abb47e4ddaa1389dd7b736925388.png" class="latex" alt="$AA&#39;B&#39;,BB&#39;C&#39;, \text{ and } CC&#39;A&#39;$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="208" height="17" />.
In this solution we shall just compute <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> of these as the others are trivially equivalent.
In order to compute the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/5/45535496cd4b6811a0e9ea0924c49ee8f4d33fab.png" class="latex" alt="$\Delta{AA&#39;B&#39;}$" width="65" height="14" /> we can use the formula <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/7/5b7f5fe808888c333eecf3d6d0541f2f5c790bb9.png" class="latex" alt="$[XYZ]=\frac{1}{2}xy\cdot\sin{z}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="159" height="37" />.
Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> is equilateral and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/7/4c71f19552a05ee5e2b31e94aff3fb986884bf31.png" class="latex" alt="$B&#39;$" width="18" height="14" /> are collinear, we already know <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/2/38200f7cb70d9afcf4c3bbd709367988fe27660f.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle{A&#39;AB&#39;}=180-60=120$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="207" height="14" />
Similarly from above we know <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/b/2eb2c7600634636b0a25b56d087d112d6fbae523.png" class="latex" alt="$AB&#39;$" width="32" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/3/0532b5b66c8152522b7b61ec5b738fc785ccc1a2.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;A$" width="32" height="14" /> to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/4/ed4977828049d99f20ccd3b4acb6bbd43c2eb70f.png" class="latex" alt="$4x$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="19" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/e/70e634348f8df9eaeac4ced35db64d08a906532e.png" class="latex" alt="$3x$" width="19" height="12" /> respectively. Thus the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/5/45535496cd4b6811a0e9ea0924c49ee8f4d33fab.png" class="latex" alt="$\Delta{AA&#39;B&#39;}$" width="65" height="14" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/1/0a1ce4651abf67fab4679880bdbb367c5ac9fb0d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}\cdot 4x\cdot 3x \cdot \sin{120}=3x^2\cdot\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="233" height="37" />. Likewise we can find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/0/8607a960022545e5df86e3b8c78f1f4f58bdeac5.png" class="latex" alt="$BB&#39;C&#39;, \text{ and } CC&#39;A&#39;$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="149" height="17" /> to also be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/8/f183f243c6fbaa928cee919ce864c6c38172d1a8.png" class="latex" alt="$3x^2\cdot\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="64" height="18" />.
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/5/4457e3784c4aacc134c78abf017c2ad465ecaed1.png" class="latex" alt="$[A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;]=[AA&#39;B&#39;]+[BB&#39;C&#39;]+[CC&#39;A&#39;]+[ABC]=3\cdot3x^2\cdot\sqrt{3}+\frac{x^2\sqrt{3}}{4}=\sqrt{3}\cdot\left(9x^2+\frac{x^2}{4}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="736" height="48" />.
Therefore the ratio of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/b/a3b07015f72b103b98ad239191fe287cdb444857.png" class="latex" alt="$[A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="64" height="19" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/3/d33cc80fa8f093e155c5be46d2e5d9da3d7e1ef5.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABC]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="50" height="18" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/5/525ee3dba522944ec7786c7b94b6e3297a70cd7d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{3}\cdot\left(9x^2+\frac{x^2}{4}\right)}{\frac{x^2\sqrt{3}}{4}}=\boxed{\textbf{(E) } 37}$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="219" height="63" />

<h3><span id="Solution_4_(Elimination)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4_.28Elimination.29">Solution 4 (Elimination)</span></h3>

<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/4/f34aace1435f9df7ca89b2e172d44309fb529921.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[[A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;]=[ABC]\cdot\left| \begin{array}{ccc} 4 &amp; 0 &amp; -3 \\ -3 &amp; 4 &amp; 0 \\ 0 &amp; -3 &amp; 4 \end{array} \right| = (64-27)[ABC]=37[ABC],\]" width="519" height="65" />

<h3><span id="Solution_8_(Sin_area_formula)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_8_.28Sin_area_formula.29">Solution 8 (Sin area formula)</span></h3>
<p>Drawing the diagram, we see that the large triangle, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/2/022948689ac03ea1705a6af78b86d32ae2a369eb.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;$" width="55" height="14" />, is composed of three congruent triangles with the triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> at the center. Let each of the sides of triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />. Therefore, using the equilateral triangle area formula, the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/2/8f260937ea29573c67cb244977784a9f720d56d9.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABC] = \frac{x^2\sqrt{3}}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="121" height="41" />. We also know now that the sides of the triangles are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/e/70e634348f8df9eaeac4ced35db64d08a906532e.png" class="latex" alt="$3x$" width="19" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/6/1b69a6bbbacda980f28012271c02f59000e23c5e.png" class="latex" alt="$3x + x$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="51" height="14" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/4/ed4977828049d99f20ccd3b4acb6bbd43c2eb70f.png" class="latex" alt="$4x$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="19" height="12" />. We also know that since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/a/c2ae0e30605dc87c83aecb3f588afa7129bee6aa.png" class="latex" alt="$BB&#39;$" width="33" height="14" /> are collinear, as are the others, angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/1/4110e9042bd316b4f37a8a79a82e12d6d9b18c5c.png" class="latex" alt="$C&#39;BB&#39;$" width="52" height="14" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/d/84d8c83fd2eea6b534b12d0fb2a5dc4239069f0b.png" class="latex" alt="$180 - 60$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="67" height="13" />, which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/a/aaa4de95cc6371d125d5cf773cdd9f9913dd1d8e.png" class="latex" alt="$120$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> degrees. Because that angle is an included angle, we get the area of all three congruent triangle's are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/7/63733bf7a626a18543ba22c0a6545437b9ef0202.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{12x^2\sin120}{2} \cdot 3$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="117" height="39" />. Simplifying that yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/7/837212be0a3269ea85bb221ac78183969d73ebdf.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{36x^2\sqrt{3}}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="62" height="41" />. Adding that to the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/3/d33cc80fa8f093e155c5be46d2e5d9da3d7e1ef5.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABC]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="50" height="18" /> yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/5/e7558ccb11a2e5617a427fbaa2e0fe6f9965d6df.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{37x^2\sqrt{3}}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="62" height="41" />. From this, we can compare the ratios by canceling everything out except for the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/9/78916550838bd31d66e4cdefbc366edb11fd00bd.png" class="latex" alt="$37$" width="18" height="12" />, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/d/38dd7f5437bf18c9f1ea64722cb1329891d85efe.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(E) }37}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="63" height="28" />
</p><p>~Solution by EricShi1685

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_9:_Law_of_Cosines">Solution 9: Law of Cosines</span></h3>
<p>Solution by HydroQuantum
</p><p><br />
Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/5/715114c5f4c2bd8fd51b2578d6c45c095ce1e71f.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=BC=CA=x$" width="168" height="13" />.
</p><p><br />
Recall The Law of Cosines. Letting <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/d/f8d2b293c791bbbdfc4dd7e85e34efe28c37fb71.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;B&#39;=B&#39;C&#39;=C&#39;A&#39;=y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="195" height="17" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/9/3f90198005de2c1ebce6fb836083bb5032914fe7.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[y^2=(3x)^2+(x+3x)^2-2(3x)(x+3x)(\cos 120) =\]" width="396" height="20" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/d/9eddc6080904fe8ed3701795375b23b3c2b9e33a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(3x)^2+(4x)^2-2(3x)(4x)(\cos 120)=9x^2+16x^2-24x(\cos 120)=25x^2+12x^2=37x^2.\]" width="641" height="20" /> Since both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/a/73a764854d1073fac0a788e874db580d732c7c89.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;$" width="71" height="14" /> are both equilateral triangles, they must be similar due to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/b/b0b3dbb5e0c16cabfa02f10ace626714ac385dbd.png" class="latex" alt="$AA$" width="27" height="13" /> similarity. This means that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/2/0425b99c0526d78886c618766802049e9295aa63.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{A&#39;B&#39;}{AB}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="40" height="38" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/e/d5eb8726a34932d28103ce7ce17456421222603f.png" class="latex" alt="$=$" style="vertical-align: 2px" width="13" height="5" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/5/90593fd0be4468067d6086a311f09fb78c9e2384.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{B&#39;C&#39;}{BC}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="40" height="38" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/e/d5eb8726a34932d28103ce7ce17456421222603f.png" class="latex" alt="$=$" style="vertical-align: 2px" width="13" height="5" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/f/75f6f3657c6d739a69950653b528ccb5624ceeea.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{C&#39;A&#39;}{CA}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="39" height="38" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/e/d5eb8726a34932d28103ce7ce17456421222603f.png" class="latex" alt="$=$" style="vertical-align: 2px" width="13" height="5" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/1/4e1098c3ec81ea78c5dc0d5b20c57d427cc05cf0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{[\triangle A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;]}{[\triangle ABC]}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="85" height="43" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/e/d5eb8726a34932d28103ce7ce17456421222603f.png" class="latex" alt="$=$" style="vertical-align: 2px" width="13" height="5" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/1/59157c47c6a1a9a1b34e80db2be5e9e029bd7b5b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{37}{1}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="38" />.
</p><p><br />
Therefore, our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/d/38dd7f5437bf18c9f1ea64722cb1329891d85efe.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(E) }37}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="63" height="28" />.
</p>
<h3><span id="Solution_10:_Inspection(easiest_solution)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_10:_Inspection.28easiest_solution.29">Solution 10: Inspection(easiest solution)</span></h3>
<p>Note that the height and base of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/8/fe898d3d0ecd61c503bbfc69d338ca2235e5bb58.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle A&#39;CC&#39;$" width="66" height="14" /> are respectively 4 times and 3 times that of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" />. Therefore the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/8/fe898d3d0ecd61c503bbfc69d338ca2235e5bb58.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle A&#39;CC&#39;$" width="66" height="14" /> is 12 times that of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" />.
</p><p>By symmetry, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/c/72c55cc515aad04251040c436152786ea94f0c21.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle A&#39;CC&#39; \cong \triangle B&#39;AA&#39; \cong \triangle C&#39;BB&#39;$" width="251" height="14" />. Adding the areas of these three triangles and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> for the total area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/a/73a764854d1073fac0a788e874db580d732c7c89.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;$" width="71" height="14" /> gives a ratio of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/a/87a92f86dd33badec7cf17e6e81f39ed75ce0ffd.png" class="latex" alt="$(12 + 12 + 12 + 1)&#160;: 1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="167" height="18" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/4/254526b2cc13f3b884323ac12334150934ddbb38.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(E) } 37}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="63" height="28" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_11:_Coordinates">Solution 11: Coordinates</span></h3>
<p>First we note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/e/20e50cdc41c9b1ca328755fb2d522a6243c516b9.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;B&#39;C&#39;\sim ABC$" width="123" height="14" /> due to symmetry. WLOG, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/3/943df464f6bacb9e19cddd41029163b289104bb9.png" class="latex" alt="$B = (0, 0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="77" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/a/e5a649aee592a9c37d2f9b5d5239f5e06e533130.png" class="latex" alt="$AB = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="60" height="13" /> Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/b/3abacaaffed9120bade32a76321c30813158629a.png" class="latex" alt="$C = (1, 0), A = \frac{1}{2}, \left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="198" height="53" />. Using the condition that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/8/868e64d4e442149faf8cf61ba7d7220ed6ce2175.png" class="latex" alt="$CC&#39; = 3$" width="66" height="14" />, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/5/435117e4ce73cd27982b3338bb6767f8d32c04af.png" class="latex" alt="$C&#39; = (4, 0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="82" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/d/57d80cdee293b343f7b281e3679be8a1f51e1f1d.png" class="latex" alt="$B&#39; = \left(\frac{-3}{2}, \frac{-3\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="154" height="53" />. It is easy to check that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/e/79ee5a69b0338e3b340da0f4bca033f346775bca.png" class="latex" alt="$B&#39;C&#39; = \sqrt{37}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="96" height="18" />. Since the area ratios of two similar figures is the square of the ratio of their lengths, the ratio is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/4/254526b2cc13f3b884323ac12334150934ddbb38.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(E) } 37}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="63" height="28" />
</p><p>Solution by <i>mathwiz0803</i>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_12:_Computing_the_Areas">Solution 12: Computing the Areas</span></h3>
<p>Note that angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/1/4110e9042bd316b4f37a8a79a82e12d6d9b18c5c.png" class="latex" alt="$C&#39;BB&#39;$" width="52" height="14" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/a/aaa4de95cc6371d125d5cf773cdd9f9913dd1d8e.png" class="latex" alt="$120$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />°, as it is supplementary to the equilateral triangle. Then, using area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/2/ee2aceed1a2e93936cd28ffffd51db6a952f9965.png" class="latex" alt="$= \frac{1}{2}ab\sin\theta$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="86" height="37" /> and letting side <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/a/e5a649aee592a9c37d2f9b5d5239f5e06e533130.png" class="latex" alt="$AB = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="60" height="13" /> for ease, we get: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/5/da596ec0ee0426c8f50342a0e20145ac85cf0466.png" class="latex" alt="$4\cdot3\cdot\frac{\sin120}{2} = 3\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="159" height="37" /> as the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/1/4110e9042bd316b4f37a8a79a82e12d6d9b18c5c.png" class="latex" alt="$C&#39;BB&#39;$" width="52" height="14" />. Then, the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/b/b9b30d7166f38d8f18e66dfd6e9473880efaca2e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="26" height="41" />, so the ratio is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/e/12e00a40f64f453220a3f1192e0aa488d58977df.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{3(3\sqrt{3})+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}}{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}} = \boxed{\textbf{(E) } 37}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="192" height="53" />
