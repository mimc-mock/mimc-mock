<p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/9/e290f2cba0ba0e11c0b91630da15a408da2fcf1c.png" class="latex" alt="$24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> different complex numbers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/5/9d557abc96ef5645714518cb85cdfe172fc7273e.png" class="latex" alt="$z^{24}=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="55" height="15" />. For how many of these is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/7/8f7106dbd058b0b498357412899776516a95b9a2.png" class="latex" alt="$z^6$" width="15" height="15" /> a real number?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/9/289cb30a8689be6853d6c4373080b584498105a7.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 0 \qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 4 \qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 6 \qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 12 \qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 24$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="367" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Note that these <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/5/9d557abc96ef5645714518cb85cdfe172fc7273e.png" class="latex" alt="$z^{24}=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="55" height="15" /> are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/a/f0ab7246f1fbac4aaece41f78c056008feba13f9.png" class="latex" alt="$e^{\frac{ni\pi}{12}}$" width="29" height="17" /> for integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/8/ee805b70af45e6662c40274ea0a0cc712fd32044.png" class="latex" alt="$0\leq n&lt;24$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="86" height="15" />. So
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/a/8ca9e170bfba50d0e8b7f3a68f53669c18e27598.png" class="latex" alt="$z^6=e^{\frac{ni\pi}{2}}$" width="70" height="17" />
</p><p>This is real if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/6/886398fe5e786f59e0f74dc564cf5e7cd98038af.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{n}{2}\in \mathbb{Z} \Leftrightarrow (n$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="94" height="33" /> is even<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/8/85852dfb3301b06f35017d1598f80915cce9a9ce.png" class="latex" alt="$)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="5" height="18" />. Thus, the answer is the number of even <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/8/ee805b70af45e6662c40274ea0a0cc712fd32044.png" class="latex" alt="$0\leq n&lt;24$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="86" height="15" /> which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/d/bcd831667ce2ed63775c6ccf9073c587604aec11.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{(D)=\ 12}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="89" height="29" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/6/4060c4913d436489aa0d9801d50b7de6a677eee4.png" class="latex" alt="$z = \sqrt[24]{1} = 1^{\frac{1}{24}}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="111" height="19" />
</p><p>By <a href="/wiki/index.php/Euler%27s_identity" title="Euler&#39;s identity">Euler's identity</a>, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/9/fa9df0f9646313d5a26679a4167a49aee96a87f6.png" class="latex" alt="$1 = e^{0 \times i} = \cos (0+2k\pi) + i \sin(0+2k\pi)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="316" height="19" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> is an integer.
</p><p>Using <a href="/wiki/index.php/De_Moivre%27s_Theorem" title="De Moivre&#39;s Theorem">De Moivre's Theorem</a>, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/b/49bc4d3ca55bbb431fdc9ec494e596215990b2f6.png" class="latex" alt="$z = 1^{\frac{1}{24}} = {\cos (\frac{k\pi}{12}) + i \sin (\frac{k\pi}{12})}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="238" height="38" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/e/70e12ecb2330aa467078d8d06dc0d435c3882a36.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \leq k&lt;24$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="85" height="15" /> that produce <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/9/e290f2cba0ba0e11c0b91630da15a408da2fcf1c.png" class="latex" alt="$24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> unique results.
</p><p>Using De Moivre's Theorem again, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/0/3400a7919a098078a28785d6ebf06d3d9b5481b5.png" class="latex" alt="$z^6 = {\cos (\frac{k\pi}{2}) + i \sin (\frac{k\pi}{2})}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="196" height="37" />
</p><p>For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/7/8f7106dbd058b0b498357412899776516a95b9a2.png" class="latex" alt="$z^6$" width="15" height="15" /> to be real, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/0/be0fbb0de7701a08c8639924a96d5a22c17ecc9a.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin(\frac{k\pi}{2})$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="60" height="37" /> has to equal <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> to negate the imaginary component. This occurs whenever <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/8/f18205c5a2f5ef1d7294be718b6aee8eeaba9567.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{k\pi}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="23" height="37" /> is an integer multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/c/f2ca003a7da0de4994b4733e203b74ff52d42553.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi$" width="10" height="8" />, requiring that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> is even. There are exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/6/38693a75a9e00c962455566cda9d06d94da1738b.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{12}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="30" height="23" /> even values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" /> on the interval <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/e/70e12ecb2330aa467078d8d06dc0d435c3882a36.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \leq k&lt;24$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="85" height="15" />, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/e/8de641a309fc5878c26baf699f2382b7d4b02b5e.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{(D)}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="41" height="29" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>From the start, recall from the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/e/f2ec0dd00b0822202787cd8c58b56972e607af79.png" class="latex" alt="$z^{24} = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="55" height="15" /> must have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/9/e290f2cba0ba0e11c0b91630da15a408da2fcf1c.png" class="latex" alt="$24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> solutions (and these must be distinct since the equation factors into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/d/57d39fe27d2cf2d2ccffd0fd59e42980554de271.png" class="latex" alt="$0 = (z-1)(z^{23} + z^{22} + z^{21}... + z + 1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="293" height="19" />), or notice that the question is simply referring to the 24th roots of unity, of which we know there must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/9/e290f2cba0ba0e11c0b91630da15a408da2fcf1c.png" class="latex" alt="$24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" />. Notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/f/f/6ff26f03bdf71a6bc20a9a5b166cb1f3a3aaec00.png" class="latex" alt="$1 = z^{24} = (z^6)^4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="118" height="19" />, so for any solution <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/7/8f7106dbd058b0b498357412899776516a95b9a2.png" class="latex" alt="$z^6$" width="15" height="15" /> will be one of the 4th roots of unity (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/8/34857b3ba74ce5cd8607f3ebd23e9015908ada71.png" class="latex" alt="$i$" width="5" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/e/bae5aba07d37ff6ff813107e76260fb31ad5794e.png" class="latex" alt="$-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="22" height="12" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/d/0fda51aabf3fa68a72ba3b46518abc175c76dab2.png" class="latex" alt="$-i$" width="19" height="12" />). Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> solutions <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> will satisfy <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/8/728eaf9a93a6a111a69ede7008d2959ecb91ded8.png" class="latex" alt="$z^6 = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="49" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> will satisfy <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/9/929d561188c1d4a191c240422acf81986c6144cb.png" class="latex" alt="$z^6 = -1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="63" height="15" /> (and this is further justified by knowledge of the 6th roots of unity), so there must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/9/0d985dba1770e9e2e805dc4acf8b8ed54902acca.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{(D) \: 12}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="63" height="29" /> such <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" />.
</p>
