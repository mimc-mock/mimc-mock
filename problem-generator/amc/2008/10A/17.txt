<p>An <a href="/wiki/index.php/Equilateral_triangle" title="Equilateral triangle">equilateral triangle</a> has side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />. What is the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Area" title="Area">area</a> of the region containing all points that are outside the triangle but not more than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> units from a point of the triangle?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/a/89a7885cad0cd66ca41ed468313a5ba38dc74459.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ 36+24\sqrt{3}\qquad\mathrm{(B)}\ 54+9\pi\qquad\mathrm{(C)}\ 54+18\sqrt{3}+6\pi\qquad\mathrm{(D)}\ \left(2\sqrt{3}+3\right)^2\pi\\\mathrm{(E)}\ 9\left(\sqrt{3}+1\right)^2\pi$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="610" height="72" />
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/a/e9aa15f65931b4eee7d795103170e3680b8a36f3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pointpen = black; pathpen = black+linewidth(0.7); pen d = linetype(&quot;6 6&quot;)+linewidth(0.7); pair A=(0,0),B=(6,0),C=6*expi(-pi/3); D(arc(A,3,90,210)); D(arc(B,3,-30,90)); D(arc(C,3,210,330)); D(arc(A,-3,90,210),d); D(arc(B,-3,-30,90),d); D(arc(C,-3,210,330),d); D(D(A)--D(B)--D(C)--cycle,linewidth(1)); D(A--(0,3)--(6,3)--B); D(A--3*expi(7/6*pi)--C+3*expi(7/6*pi)--C); D(B--B+3*expi(11/6*pi)--C+3*expi(11/6*pi)--C); MP(&quot;3&quot;,(0,1.5),W); MP(&quot;6&quot;,(3,0),NW); [/asy]" width="252" height="235" /></center> 
<p>The region described contains three rectangles of dimensions <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/0/1c012dd11e5f2b2b1353be14f10a523ba7de5910.png" class="latex" alt="$3 \times 6$" width="40" height="12" />, and three <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/4/9b49fcb2f74ccae5ec0e722a8c65b5b9bbd93720.png" class="latex" alt="$120^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="13" /> degree arcs of circles of <a href="/wiki/index.php/Radius" title="Radius">radius</a> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />. Thus the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/6/7364eac7efec70a0ea974a671cb20e987912389c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[3(3 \times 6) + 3 \left( \frac{120^{\circ}}{360^{\circ}} \times 3^2 \pi\right) = 54 + 9\pi \Longrightarrow \mathrm{(B)}.\]" width="367" height="43" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>After a quick sketch of the problem, one can deduce that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> rectangles in the figure, each with side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> and width <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />. Therefore, the combined areas of the rectangles is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/c/b1cb5bdce2d06d2419066a2b55ab01d824217bd2.png" class="latex" alt="$54$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="13" />. The other three regions are circle-shaped areas, probably expressed in some form of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/c/f2ca003a7da0de4994b4733e203b74ff52d42553.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi$" width="10" height="8" />. Answer choices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/2/ca2f4283cfd441270cb90bfd2cf89a533f3dde81.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/b/bfbf4df7c25681bb178ddc50ee0c0d656aa25b5e.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(D)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/3/3a3faf1572d785ef1d5d72f120fb3a16190b4b61.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(E)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="25" height="18" /> are impossible because they either lack an integer or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/c/f2ca003a7da0de4994b4733e203b74ff52d42553.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi$" width="10" height="8" /> in the answer, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/8/2484f88154fadcf9c781bba19becdae88696b77f.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="25" height="18" /> is impossible since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/b/29b61ebd60e6692266056ca59dae692faec01e4c.png" class="latex" alt="$18\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="43" height="18" /> clearly does not belong to the rectangle or the circular areas. We can conclude that the only choice left is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/2/c72a08c9de59fb963e17912f4b2127a20340346a.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\mathrm{(B)}}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="38" height="29" />. 
</p><p>Solution by Airplane50
</p>
