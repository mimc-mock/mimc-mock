<p>On each side of a unit square, an equilateral triangle of side length 1 is constructed. On each new side of each equilateral triangle, another equilateral triangle of side length 1 is constructed. The interiors of the square and the 12 triangles have no points in common. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> be the region formed by the union of the square and all the triangles, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> be the smallest convex polygon that contains <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" />. What is the area of the region that is inside <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> but outside <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/c/03cda09c6f96b1ee9a102e90ec609d2c22ea1d26.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)} \; \frac{1}{4} \qquad \textbf{(B)} \; \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4} \qquad \textbf{(C)} \; 1 \qquad \textbf{(D)} \; \sqrt{3} \qquad \textbf{(E)} \; 2 \sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="409" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/7/8c7af6121e2831aa5bf8db7a271830fc340d0c5d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] real a = 1/2, b = sqrt(3)/2; draw((0,0)--(1,0)--(1,1)--(0,1)--cycle); draw((0,0)--(a,-b)--(1,0)--(1+b,a)--(1,1)--(a,1+b)--(0,1)--(-b,a)--(0,0)); draw((0,0)--(-1+a,-b)--(1+a,-b)--(1,0)--(1+b,-1+a)--(1+b,1+a)--(1,1)--(1+a,1+b)--(-1+a,1+b)--(0,1)--(-b,1+a)--(-b,-1+a)--(0,0)); filldraw((1+a,-b)--(1,0)--(1+b,-1+a)--cycle,gray(0.9)); filldraw((1+b,1+a)--(1,1)--(1+a,1+b)--cycle,gray(0.9)); filldraw((-1+a,1+b)--(0,1)--(-b,1+a)--cycle,gray(0.9)); filldraw((-b,-1+a)--(0,0)--(-1+a,-b)--cycle,gray(0.9)); [/asy]" width="252" height="252" />
</p><p>The equilateral triangles form trapezoids with side lengths <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/4/bd4b4b96cedeaa282f935930ccb5928c5221496e.png" class="latex" alt="$1, 1, 1, 2$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="60" height="15" /> (half a unit hexagon) on each face of the unit square.  The four triangles "in between" these trapezoids are isosceles triangles with two side lengths <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> and an angle of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/3/e73393067e6d6e401a5354accc7a730c7a2977aa.png" class="latex" alt="$30^{\circ}$" width="24" height="13" /> in between them, so the total area of these triangles (which is the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/e/21eefc2b9db61a1c8cacef6f069a0dadbfa1945f.png" class="latex" alt="$S - R$" width="48" height="12" />) is, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/6/7368e0f6b590107c020e72c36894411a49164d7d.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \left( \frac {1}{2} \sin 30^{\circ} \right) = 1$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="138" height="43" /> which makes the answer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/5/3f58d2efaaa17d28cec9dd927d78708a63b43e24.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{C}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="26" height="24" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/f/6ef509ed715cf1801d20e8dcbbaf70f0c4d7fe3c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] real a = 1/2, b = sqrt(3)/2; draw((0,0)--(1,0)--(1,1)--(0,1)--cycle); draw((a+b+a,a+b+a)--(a+b+a,-b)--(-b,-b)--(-b,a+a+b)--cycle,dashed); draw((0,0)--(a,-b)--(1,0)--(1+b,a)--(1,1)--(a,1+b)--(0,1)--(-b,a)--(0,0)); draw((0,0)--(-1+a,-b)--(1+a,-b)--(1,0)--(1+b,-1+a)--(1+b,1+a)--(1,1)--(1+a,1+b)--(-1+a,1+b)--(0,1)--(-b,1+a)--(-b,-1+a)--(0,0)); filldraw((1+a,-b)--(1,0)--(1+b,-1+a)--cycle,gray(0.9)); filldraw((1+b,1+a)--(1,1)--(1+a,1+b)--cycle,gray(0.9)); filldraw((-1+a,1+b)--(0,1)--(-b,1+a)--cycle,gray(0.9)); filldraw((-b,-1+a)--(0,0)--(-1+a,-b)--cycle,gray(0.9)); [/asy]" width="252" height="252" />
</p><p>The area of the largest square is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/0/eb05c099d6ddf2df70b23c1bd5db26a381304a8d.png" class="latex" alt="$(1+\sqrt{3})^2=4+2\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="168" height="24" />. The area of region <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/2/a62d325e29dcf5ebed3c517ccc2fa100e146bc2d.png" class="latex" alt="$1+12\frac{1}{2}\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}=1+3\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="182" height="43" />. The total area of four small 45-45-90 triangles at corner is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/3/df3f97e1209ab0bbd1da4b3f67e76fe47b4aed21.png" class="latex" alt="$4*\frac{1}{2}(\frac{\sqrt{3}+1-2}{2})^2=2-\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="233" height="43" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/2/e025613825fa50ddfb3f3fe6ef16650ec378d880.png" class="latex" alt="$S=4+2\sqrt{3}-(2-\sqrt{3})=2+3\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="284" height="24" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/7/c478db9c690602b7e89dfa2971b7d8cf11e93a3d.png" class="latex" alt="$S-R=1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="84" height="16" />.
</p><p><br />
~Bran Qin
</p>
