<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/f/53fd509e8cd0c9d7f8287f39d93b39d1630aa606.png" class="latex" alt="$A_0=(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="84" height="18" />. Distinct points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/d/a0d7c3807b00a066e6380ab832ca42f1712b11df.png" class="latex" alt="$A_1,A_2,\dots$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="78" height="16" /> lie on the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-axis, and distinct points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/6/a466dde1d2d484c5f4a084e1582dacbd471a87af.png" class="latex" alt="$B_1,B_2,\dots$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="78" height="16" /> lie on the graph of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/3/09321f3751bf85c830a8aabd84cd4e07eed40f99.png" class="latex" alt="$y=\sqrt{x}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="59" height="18" />. For every positive integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/8/788cefe8c6c3920b48c5a89339da32cbef1ff080.png" class="latex" alt="$n,\ A_{n-1}B_nA_n$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="108" height="16" /> is an equilateral triangle. What is the least <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> for which the length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/a/08a331e5e9ec809aa764ca4ea2fb5388be17bc0b.png" class="latex" alt="$A_0A_n\geq100$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="94" height="15" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/9/519a2cd89edee4ccfddfd84c949d216f516c5486.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 13\qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 15\qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 17\qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 19\qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 21$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="393" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/a/5/2a5079965c8801142383863bcc66a4d5a7cdf185.png" class="latex" alt="$a_n=|A_{n-1}A_n|$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="113" height="18" />. We need to rewrite the recursion into something manageable. The two strange conditions, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />'s lie on the graph of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/3/09321f3751bf85c830a8aabd84cd4e07eed40f99.png" class="latex" alt="$y=\sqrt{x}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="59" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/0/7500db8fed36f5c1b0bea0c42269a86b8a9f7b99.png" class="latex" alt="$A_{n-1}B_nA_n$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="84" height="15" /> is an equilateral triangle, can be compacted as follows: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/0/d60edc8787d7eaca25eb23c4e0208d57a934a0e0.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\left(a_n\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)^2=\frac{a_n}{2}+a_{n-1}+a_{n-2}+\cdots+a_1\]" width="322" height="56" />
which uses <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/5/5858a1d23a3435a0bf363db4dd9be6376601de25.png" class="latex" alt="$y^2=x$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="51" height="18" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> is the height of the equilateral triangle and therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/9/4e9191a5007067cff8f6d73f4d3419e08238f00b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" /> times its base.
</p><p>The relation above holds for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/f/d6fbf6b1b2bc81af471428fe2dc93cf475d6cd05.png" class="latex" alt="$n=k$" width="44" height="12" /> and for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/0/bf08f4d4d7f8dc8631a30576240cdda9db56f889.png" class="latex" alt="$n=k-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="75" height="13" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/c/5ec6b2397abb175794be4cabc058b184eee65bc7.png" class="latex" alt="$(k&gt;1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="56" height="18" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/e/aee559eb17dd729174f088536fdf6f4423e141d1.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\left(a_k\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)^2-\left(a_{k-1}\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)^2=\]" width="217" height="56" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/6/776c0072515385ad79f2f7555242059ea29c4042.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[=\left(\frac{a_k}{2}+a_{k-1}+a_{k-2}+\cdots+a_1\right)-\left(\frac{a_{k-1}}{2}+a_{k-2}+a_{k-3}+\cdots+a_1\right)\]" width="525" height="33" />
Or, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/6/89680f85c9c0c03aa4edc6d2754755eaa8ed5247.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[a_k-a_{k-1}=\frac23\]" width="110" height="37" /> This implies that each segment of a successive triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/1/611d08a8c57a214b2b964bba16da8157a5b81c9a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac23$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> more than the last triangle. To find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/a/1da7198d9a79e0a6dc32e60162044ee0f726e4e7.png" class="latex" alt="$a_{1}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="10" />, we merely have to plug in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/d/5ed8470bc717a49aafe4556c7e2c2e11ddc7b687.png" class="latex" alt="$k=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="13" /> into the aforementioned recursion and we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/8/078cb74a72a0817bf5a5a93df263a853313e8a14.png" class="latex" alt="$a_{1} - a_{0} = \frac23$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="92" height="37" />. Knowing that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/a/b0aeaf146217e6bebf22b3bc00c18edc0ef35bf4.png" class="latex" alt="$a_{0}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="10" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />, we can deduce that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/f/ecf576e4fab9f5e9567c657041a0b1161d9be149.png" class="latex" alt="$a_{1} = 2/3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="68" height="18" />.Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/e/24e85c4e40a8966bba2afefbca21b0f0be43536d.png" class="latex" alt="$a_n=\frac{2n}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="65" height="37" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/e/f8ed303576c431697737ed758536313424aa35b1.png" class="latex" alt="$A_0A_n=a_n+a_{n-1}+\cdots+a_1=\frac{2}{3} \cdot \frac{n(n+1)}{2} = \frac{n(n+1)}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="442" height="38" />. We want to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> so that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/c/59ca72c13bb3d8e886f83b65b8e4b177da741fe5.png" class="latex" alt="$n^2&lt;300&lt;(n+1)^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="156" height="19" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/d/59dad6b14c8943e8bf726f37f78bc1912a6f26df.png" class="latex" alt="$n=\boxed{17}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="65" height="23" /> is our answer.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Consider two adjacent equilateral triangles obeying the problem statement. For each, drop an altitude to the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> axis and denote the resulting heights <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/5/7959d34399ed5624538871f01af487ebd831572c.png" class="latex" alt="$h_n$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/1/ee1b605502ebb6294740b8b7e634e9a280e185bf.png" class="latex" alt="$h_{n+1}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="16" />. From 30-60-90 rules, the distance between the points where these altitudes meet the x-axis is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/f/64f93cd900dd51dd35c2f3e5b911b287ecd86fb6.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{h_{n+1}}{\sqrt{3}}+\frac{h_n}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{h_{n+1}+h_n}{\sqrt{3}}\]" width="193" height="42" />
</p><p>But the square root curve means that this distance is also expressible as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/0/7f044ccb9a8337955b3ecc67849fd75ce03d1eb4.png" class="latex" alt="$h_{n+1}^2-h_n^2$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="77" height="21" /> (the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> coordinates are the squares of the heights). Setting these expressions equal and dividing throughout by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/2/172d43c38015a8f794cefc4bf8a48fdbefb1452a.png" class="latex" alt="$h_{n+1}+h_n$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="77" height="16" /> leaves <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/1/fb16a45768455991eddd73c58e0536bfc3b4457e.png" class="latex" alt="$h_{n+1}-h_n=\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="129" height="41" />. So the difference in height of successive triangles is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/1/821ee242c2d18e8db70f539d073b0ace2ddc1301.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="26" height="41" />, meaning their bases are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/b/38bd36e353df831303c0de895a9ec614cf3e7202.png" class="latex" alt="$2/3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" /> wider and wider each time. From here, one can proceed as in Solution 1 to arrive at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/d/59dad6b14c8943e8bf726f37f78bc1912a6f26df.png" class="latex" alt="$n=\boxed{17}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="65" height="23" />.
</p>
