<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> be a trapezoid with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/9/2197078c09b27720f42bf0f176a6a2659cabdfec.png" class="latex" alt="$AB||CD, AB=11, BC=5, CD=19,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="301" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/1/691560de1e47d08d827fe5c6afcf3c3eb20991a9.png" class="latex" alt="$DA=7$" width="62" height="13" />. Bisectors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/b/1/7b1ac662f7ddbbce489953c8b7693848239564aa.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle A$" width="26" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/1/ae15cd6fc137587ad1676133c8342dc210dc6364.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle D$" width="28" height="12" /> meet at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />, and bisectors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/1/2918f7d23f9ee8c04baabad86100d68023ec03f9.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle B$" width="27" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/5/4056d41f42aa8a00adca0ff5d1209b6bb0e92d6b.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle C$" width="27" height="12" /> meet at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />. What is the area of hexagon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/9/219964a919925d926160d1e59b02ef06b8f439ef.png" class="latex" alt="$ABQCDP$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="86" height="16" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/a/1/ea181068c50424f83d1cd07e5da973f897db6500.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 28\sqrt{3}\qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 30\sqrt{3}\qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 32\sqrt{3}\qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 35\sqrt{3}\qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 36\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="516" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<center><a href="/wiki/index.php/File:2008_AMC_12B_25.jpg" class="image"><img alt="2008 AMC 12B 25.jpg" src="https://wiki-images.artofproblemsolving.com//2/28/2008_AMC_12B_25.jpg" width="507" height="188" /></a></center>
<p>Note: In the image AB and CD have been swapped from their given lengths in the problem. However, this doesn't affect any of the solving.
</p><p>Drop perpendiculars to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/4/0948661e822f2a953c43a57ac9e40b2734476de4.png" class="latex" alt="$CD$" width="29" height="12" /> from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, and call the intersections <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/c/81cee936d2982eea5fb6e68b6167da70a1c690fb.png" class="latex" alt="$X,Y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="37" height="16" /> respectively. Now, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/1/311d3eb091e850c096570428a84cfb13097aebec.png" class="latex" alt="$DA^2-BC^2=(7-5)(7+5)=DX^2-CY^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="349" height="19" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/0/b70f295d0785677868bbd2a30fb740d0a4467f49.png" class="latex" alt="$DX+CY=19-11=8$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="198" height="14" />. Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/8/a283bad2928946ba876e1984d6e4cc6bf286815c.png" class="latex" alt="$DX-CY=3$" width="116" height="12" />.
We conclude <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/a/91abff5ed5a48a6ef454b083745edf005a07bbf2.png" class="latex" alt="$DX=\frac{11}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="77" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/7/657f5dafa407345c092bef739f372107f809660d.png" class="latex" alt="$CY=\frac{5}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="64" height="37" />.
To simplify things even more, notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/4/5746e8c049b5945b9eff15c91acffb8ee8b8554a.png" class="latex" alt="$90^{\circ}=\frac{\angle D+\angle A}{2}=180^{\circ}-\angle APD$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="268" height="38" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/5/5e56fc194b49b4242079a87e536daea8d1506d1f.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle P=\angle Q=90^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="126" height="16" />.
</p><p>Also, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/e/aee035a5db85bf6bcea46f999b213a8bd3e7fb7d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sin(\angle PAD)=\sin(\frac12\angle XDA)=\sqrt{\frac{1-\cos(\angle XDA)}{2}}=\sqrt{\frac{3}{28}}\]" width="463" height="44" />
So the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/7/e575bffe19e53a0038b1d33fe76d61bdfadfad02.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle APD$" width="59" height="13" /> is: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/c/01c94bf0a03a6982136159ed50e4d4d95004e7e2.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[R\cdot c\sin a\sin b =\frac{7\cdot7}{2}\sqrt{\frac{3}{28}}\sqrt{1-\frac{3}{28}}=\frac{35}{8}\sqrt{3}\]" width="350" height="43" />
</p><p>Over to the other side: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/a/2ea0a36992a9d85c8d94e07c6613739ee2fc35e6.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BCY$" width="59" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/c/47c78b85058ee5e17f88e2cde302e48aad826b35.png" class="latex" alt="$30-60-90$" width="98" height="12" />, and is therefore congruent to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/4/0d4056be5ba6e4107a1691bc996ad3722689fe52.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BCQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="58" height="17" />. So <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/d/e4da615a8dd1e1f3027a0717ba732da6c7e8702e.png" class="latex" alt="$[BCQ]=\frac{5\cdot5\sqrt{3}}{8}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="135" height="41" />.
</p><p>The area of the hexagon is clearly 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/b/a7b4eb144baec3cf70039f824d59d1460106e0d5.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} [ABCD]-([BCQ]+[APD]) &amp;=\frac{15\cdot5\sqrt{3}}{2}-\frac{60\sqrt{3}}{8}\\ &amp;=30\sqrt{3}\implies\boxed{\mathrm{B}} \end{align*}" width="391" height="71" />
</p><p>Note: Once <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/1/521eb037ef25ecef93c9644d789eed0163a05c52.png" class="latex" alt="$DY$" width="29" height="12" /> is found, there is no need to do the trig. Notice that the hexagon consists of two trapezoids, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/5/f95b70bf537e4b66f49e5b146cd9e92e0c48b74d.png" class="latex" alt="$ABPQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="56" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/c/2bcac3f9eea6964a89e8132654c894b260dd3d97.png" class="latex" alt="$CDPQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="57" height="16" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/6/e5627732900b20257596dd2f53c1b8978715bc39.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ = \frac{19-7-5 +11}{2} = 9$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="210" height="37" />. The height is one half of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/2/ed2a785c017799008f04738684e86dba12bfbb28.png" class="latex" alt="$BY$" width="28" height="12" /> which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/6/7966f5ae22674e710e4b38c31cb2d51ace648f0b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5\sqrt{3}}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="35" height="41" />. So the area is 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/a/a2a452f0214a55c68f1f370414680789d81d4817.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{5\sqrt{3}}{4}(19+9+11+9)=30\sqrt{3}\]" width="264" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<center><a href="/wiki/index.php/File:2008_AMC_12B_25_II.JPG" class="image"><img alt="2008 AMC 12B 25 II.JPG" src="https://wiki-images.artofproblemsolving.com//a/a8/2008_AMC_12B_25_II.JPG" width="514" height="192" /></a></center>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/b/e3bb4eeb347e74e025fd175c22f1f7275af0555e.png" class="latex" alt="$AP$" width="27" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/f/6bf1515283dbd146b76682d75b2ebb84d8a0214f.png" class="latex" alt="$BQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="28" height="16" /> meet <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/4/0948661e822f2a953c43a57ac9e40b2734476de4.png" class="latex" alt="$CD$" width="29" height="12" /> at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/5/ce58e4af225c93d08606c26554caaa5ae32edeba.png" class="latex" alt="$Y$" width="14" height="12" />, respectively.
</p><p>Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/7/0378a0d72e0b0cf7c122fc0e184960d3a919287e.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle APD=90^{\circ}$" width="104" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/0/720d95c64d057179d64d0c3185035efabcf8dc4d.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle ADP=\angle XDP$" width="139" height="13" />, and they share <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/3/783d500281f8f46167dff3b071c543d42150e67f.png" class="latex" alt="$DP$" width="29" height="12" />, triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/5/be5705efbd8cadc9f9e150349d31b4f9cf1a24b0.png" class="latex" alt="$APD$" width="43" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/d/c1d9351232f6cee22c5bea57eabd6c9ff9f37268.png" class="latex" alt="$XPD$" width="45" height="12" /> are congruent.
</p><p>By the same reasoning, we also have that triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/2/5c27703e0d7603c1407eee5fc8e573b60cd95f14.png" class="latex" alt="$BQC$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="43" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/2/1424c7bf38e25abfd1c1f2dc12cfb741973c331a.png" class="latex" alt="$YQC$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="43" height="16" /> are congruent.
</p><p>Hence, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/b/bfb2914813fba3795b47a0eb75a784b81c6c8122.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABQCDP]=[ABYX]+\frac{[ABCD]-[ABYX]}{2}=\frac{[ABCD]+[ABYX]}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="563" height="38" />.
</p><p>If we let the height of the trapezoid be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/2/7c29389ba69f210c3c25d7c75f204cb7b99ad66e.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABQCDP]=\frac{\frac{11+19}{2}\cdot x+\frac{11+7}{2}\cdot x}{2}=12x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="321" height="40" />.
</p><p>Thusly, if we find the height of the trapezoid and multiply it by 12, we will be done.
</p><p>Let the projections of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/4/0948661e822f2a953c43a57ac9e40b2734476de4.png" class="latex" alt="$CD$" width="29" height="12" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/9/1090530ae883b627e090bfb5e450ef796febca40.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;$" width="17" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/7/4c71f19552a05ee5e2b31e94aff3fb986884bf31.png" class="latex" alt="$B&#39;$" width="18" height="14" />, respectively.
</p><p>We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/1/6519adb12520fbabe400bf153960fa061ff00a34.png" class="latex" alt="$DA&#39;+CB&#39;=19-11=8$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="205" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/0/da02222eb52a35a5734350f2ab42bd9a833f9c8b.png" class="latex" alt="$DA&#39;=\sqrt{DA^2-AA&#39;^2}=\sqrt{49-x^2}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="268" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/0/70008cc7e2cd765b44840400beba6b6218819621.png" class="latex" alt="$CB&#39;=\sqrt{CB^2-BB&#39;^2}=\sqrt{25-x^2}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="270" height="18" />.
</p><p>Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/d/85dc3aba04574bdede014c76fac812c241d03385.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{49-x^2}+\sqrt{25-x^2}=8$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="202" height="18" />. Solving this, we easily get that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/3/073b8293b19266685337f184aef27b8aa707a4bd.png" class="latex" alt="$x=\frac{5\sqrt{3}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="70" height="41" />.
</p><p>Multiplying this by 12, we find that the area of hexagon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/9/219964a919925d926160d1e59b02ef06b8f439ef.png" class="latex" alt="$ABQCDP$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="86" height="16" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/b/b4b90c3ef7a6a6b0568d7152df1859cc4c55799e.png" class="latex" alt="$30\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="43" height="18" />, which corresponds to answer choice <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/e/f2e33ab9a00e021af49e888212645d4667903c49.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{B}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="26" height="24" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/9/439a142ee816512f6ac356f1871ce0028a1f7458.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(0.6cm); import olympiad; pair A,B,C,D,P,Q,M,N,W,X,Y,Z; A=(11/2,5sqrt(3)/2); B=(33/2,5sqrt(3)/2); C=(19,0); D=(0,0); P=incenter(A,D,(99999,5sqrt(3)/4)); Q=incenter(B,C,(-99999,5sqrt(3)/4)); W=P+(0,5sqrt(3)/4); X=P-(0,5sqrt(3)/4); Y=Q+(0,5sqrt(3)/4); Z=Q-(0,5sqrt(3)/4); M=reflect(A,P)*W; N=reflect(B,Q)*Y; draw(A--B--C--D--cycle); draw(A--P--D); draw(B--Q--C); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,dir(135)); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,dir(45)); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,dir(315)); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,dir(225)); dot(&quot;$P$&quot;,P,dir(0)); dot(&quot;$Q$&quot;,Q,dir(180)); draw(W--X); draw(Y--Z); draw(M--P); draw(N--Q); label(&quot;$11$&quot;,midpoint(A--B),dir(90)); label(&quot;$5$&quot;,midpoint(B--C),dir(45)); label(&quot;$19$&quot;,midpoint(C--D),dir(270)); label(&quot;$7$&quot;,midpoint(D--A),dir(135)); label(&quot;$x$&quot;,midpoint(P--W),dir(0)); label(&quot;$x$&quot;,midpoint(P--X),dir(0)); label(&quot;$x$&quot;,midpoint(P--M),dir(225)); label(&quot;$x$&quot;,midpoint(Q--Y),dir(180)); label(&quot;$x$&quot;,midpoint(Q--Z),dir(180)); label(&quot;$x$&quot;,midpoint(Q--N),dir(315)); draw(rightanglemark(P,W,B,12.5)); draw(rightanglemark(P,X,C,12.5)); draw(rightanglemark(P,M,D,12.5)); draw(rightanglemark(Q,Y,A,12.5)); draw(rightanglemark(Q,Z,D,12.5)); draw(rightanglemark(Q,N,C,12.5)); [/asy]" width="585" height="168" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> is the intersection of the angle bisectors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/b/1/7b1ac662f7ddbbce489953c8b7693848239564aa.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle A$" width="26" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/1/ae15cd6fc137587ad1676133c8342dc210dc6364.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle D$" width="28" height="12" />. By definition, angle bisectors are always equidistant from the sides of the angle, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> is equidistant from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/b/cfbb4c52e011ceed6e813118a7d59e931af444a2.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AD}$" width="29" height="15" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/0/ce0bfc603f9a044e308b1a1fdadca917e81ad2dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CD}$" width="30" height="15" />.  Likewise, point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> is equidistant from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/3/e33fe7d65facd8868f58b6e94ddc7f153a5a3f9f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BC}$" width="29" height="15" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/0/ce0bfc603f9a044e308b1a1fdadca917e81ad2dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CD}$" width="30" height="15" />.  Because both points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> are equidistant from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/0/ce0bfc603f9a044e308b1a1fdadca917e81ad2dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CD}$" width="30" height="15" /> and the distance between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/0/ce0bfc603f9a044e308b1a1fdadca917e81ad2dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CD}$" width="30" height="15" /> is constant, the common distances from each of the points to the mentioned segments is equal for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" />.  Call this distance <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />.
</p><p>The distance between a point and a line is the length of the segment perpendicular to the line with one endpoint on the line and the other on the point.  This means the altitude from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/b/cfbb4c52e011ceed6e813118a7d59e931af444a2.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AD}$" width="29" height="15" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />, so the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/e/5be4d56eac13726f9b91a0bf520128539abb449f.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ADP$" width="59" height="13" /> is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/d/f0d0776a63e2cefa83f2aa9d100248b9880ea5f0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac12\cdot AD\cdot x=\frac72x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="126" height="37" />.  Similarly, the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/4/0d4056be5ba6e4107a1691bc996ad3722689fe52.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BCQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="58" height="17" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/4/a94fc88bc324a2aff80095ff77023ff68618da08.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac12\cdot BC\cdot x=\frac52x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="126" height="37" />.  The altitude of the trapezoid is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/c/2ec701493fa72dda5d6747fe14f30e7b1c2fb6c4.png" class="latex" alt="$2x$" width="19" height="12" />, because it is the sum of the distances from either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/0/ce0bfc603f9a044e308b1a1fdadca917e81ad2dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CD}$" width="30" height="15" />.  This means the area of trapezoid <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/b/1eb5bee5337c05605866ee35e06b717be149cd08.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac12(AB+CD)\cdot2x=\frac12(11+19)\cdot2x=30x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="335" height="37" />.  Now, the area of hexagon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/9/219964a919925d926160d1e59b02ef06b8f439ef.png" class="latex" alt="$ABQCDP$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="86" height="16" /> is the area of trapezoid <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" />, minus the areas of triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/4/984cc6b7903307691254f8e66fc45da2018a1d58.png" class="latex" alt="$ADP$" width="43" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/1/ac1c6112376b5d18ec9bcc888a024b6837279f51.png" class="latex" alt="$BCQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="16" />.  This is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/7/c972622892d701bc8a1ba219911571251d907e14.png" class="latex" alt="$30x-\frac72x-\frac52x=24x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="172" height="37" />.  Now it remains to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />.

</p><p>We let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> be the feet of the altitudes of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, respectively, to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/0/ce0bfc603f9a044e308b1a1fdadca917e81ad2dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CD}$" width="30" height="15" />.  We define <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/b/dab7d579d807754eafabb6f926039279f2a80f27.png" class="latex" alt="$r=RD$" width="62" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/b/c7b52ce21f7ae6ab3a684bf22b72712ebc6a141a.png" class="latex" alt="$s=SC$" width="59" height="12" />.  We know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/3/a93edcbbd832492443379eb87b27845e3b5e79e5.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=RS$" width="78" height="13" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/c/cec5a2d4fe7a856324063755ea3c3c45bf0c16e1.png" class="latex" alt="$RS=11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="67" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/3/52316ff38522c810d1d424bfa9db991badfb7bac.png" class="latex" alt="$r+s=19-11=8$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="155" height="14" />.  By the Pythagorean Theorem on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/2/192e60a9d478ed55f2982ceb7484f1a3e7a871e5.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ADR$" width="59" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/1/9019415c0901d95b3dada17e7c384cf7b166ee94.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BCS$" width="57" height="13" />, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/4/744f475ee22170410d7a3d60aa03aa1291ccd36f.png" class="latex" alt="$r^2+(2x)^2=7^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="118" height="19" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/3/cc34109117c41e210830ca56d8ada1edbd6b2bef.png" class="latex" alt="$s^2+(2x)^2=5^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="118" height="19" />, respectively. We know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/6/196a12a8e1f383c4c636c884772b74e7f23fd058.png" class="latex" alt="$r+s=8$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="72" height="14" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/2/3a2b0bb5c8f02080869f62acfcf7a5b8037a104f.png" class="latex" alt="$r-s=\frac{24}8=3$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="118" height="37" />.  Now we can solve for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> by adding the two equations we just got to see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/b/a9bc8f025463ae8a274758994e0a4650c849dcc3.png" class="latex" alt="$2r=11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="59" height="12" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/a/2eaec9ffd63d83f915d77da190f5ffa836f96b74.png" class="latex" alt="$r=\frac{11}2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="53" height="37" />.
</p><p>We now solve for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />.  We know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/9/4a9b22cd152712195950883d4688c90e9ad1bcec.png" class="latex" alt="$r^2+(2x)^2=49$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="121" height="19" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/b/33b3f7871b0624fc41c49eb235f1074e24c57c66.png" class="latex" alt="$(2x)^2=49-\left(\frac{11}2\right)^2=\frac{75}4$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="206" height="46" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/1/8d1723baf5e70ae00563adef8bbab967d2245008.png" class="latex" alt="$2x=\frac{5\sqrt3}2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="79" height="41" />.  We multiply both sides of this equation by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/d/75d7264228907f00f4e1b0ac50ec5fe992d289b2.png" class="latex" alt="$24x=30\sqrt3$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="95" height="18" />. <p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/2/6d2fcc526d540edebf20603740558027aea9a556.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] import olympiad; unitsize(0.5cm);  pair A, B, C, D; A = 5*(Cos(120), Sin(120)); B = A + (-11, 0); C = origin + (-19, 0); D = origin;  label(&quot;$A$&quot;, A, dir(30)); label(&quot;$B$&quot;, B, dir(150)); label(&quot;$C$&quot;, C, dir(150)); label(&quot;$D$&quot;, D, dir(30));  pair E, F, G, H; E = bisectorpoint(B, A, D); F = bisectorpoint(A, B, C); G = bisectorpoint(B, C, D); H = bisectorpoint(C, D, A);  pair P, Q; P = extension(A, E, D, H); Q = extension(B, F, C, G);  dot(&quot;$P$&quot;, P, dir(20)); dot(&quot;$Q$&quot;, Q, dir(150));  pair W, X, Y, Z; W = extension(A, P, D, C); X = extension(B, Q, C, D); Y = extension(C, Q, A, B); Z = extension(D, P, A, B);  label(&quot;$W$&quot;, W, dir(100)); label(&quot;$X$&quot;, X, dir(60)); label(&quot;$Y$&quot;, Y, dir(50)); label(&quot;$Z$&quot;, Z, dir(140));  draw(A--W--X--B--Y--C--D--Z--B--C--Y--A--D); [/asy]" width="498" height="128" />
</p><p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/6/a369866301022b329259f1a771e9098fd868b429.png" class="latex" alt="$W, X, Y, Z = \overline{AP} \cap \overline{CD}, \overline{BQ} \cap \overline{CD}, \overline{CQ} \cap \overline{AB}, \overline{DP} \cap \overline{AB}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="443" height="19" /> respectively. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/5/485bb64d1c8ea950404ec2db7aeee4615a68aa34.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle{DCQ} = \angle{BCQ}, \angle{CBQ} = \angle{ABQ}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="280" height="16" /> we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/d/d8dc3b91b26713351eb91ef2569a3ae0e0c273d7.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle{QCB} + \angle{CBQ} = 90 \iff \overline{BX} \perp \overline{CY};$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="319" height="19" /> similarly we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/9/bc90002bde8c882686cf47b314e682ae555f4598.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AW} \perp \overline{DZ}.$" width="90" height="15" /> Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/8/b18928339c213c3e404a0605cf86e27a33c8d970.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BQ}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="29" height="19" /> is both an angle bisector and altitude of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/d/17d173c5bc92fab23ebb96203260b0c385309370.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{CBY}$" width="59" height="13" /> so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/0/cc05f4f28274fa4e2750efc957861bbac63d754a.png" class="latex" alt="$BC = BY.$" width="83" height="12" /> Using the same logic on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/2/802488e18e9dc747477247558616c94017e77a05.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{BCX}$" width="60" height="13" /> gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/9/c29510f65a5b532dc2f8cb0310c027b3c3290351.png" class="latex" alt="$BC = BX \iff BYXC$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="198" height="13" /> is a rhombus; similarly, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/f/41f1ac62d85a3bc646c0d0e78256cdbbd34ab54c.png" class="latex" alt="$ADWZ$" width="62" height="13" /> is a rhombus. Then, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/2/b226b306e376c3bc8429cbd5192cce0147b6b742.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABQCDP] = [ABCD] - \frac{1}{4}\left([BYXC] + [ADWZ]\right) = 15h - \frac{1}{4}(7h + 12h) = 12h$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="632" height="37" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/8/8189a5b5a0917b8c93350827be4038af1839139d.png" class="latex" alt="$h$" width="10" height="12" /> is the height of trapezoid <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/e/4ae54b43c219341de4a6e5919922cf55371e6ee3.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD.$" width="61" height="13" /> Finding <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/8/8189a5b5a0917b8c93350827be4038af1839139d.png" class="latex" alt="$h$" width="10" height="12" /> is the same as finding the altitude to the side of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> in a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/8/ad858b0b7620470e4bcd7987a4f77aef3042219a.png" class="latex" alt="$5-7-8$" width="71" height="12" /> triangle, and using Heron's, the area of such a triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e2c6b25d62e9032c6fa36e6d4020eb5af997617.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{10(5)(3)(2)} = 10 \sqrt{3} = 4h \iff h = \frac{5\sqrt{3}}{2}.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="345" height="41" /> Multiply to get our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/9/3b9b3c63bdb1d3a90124507420ac35bed14a53f4.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\mathrm{B}}.$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="27" height="24" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5">Solution 5</span></h2>
<p>Like above solutions, find out the height of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/9/5391abf30ddfb00f30247ff44548bb3f96cfa6ea.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5 \sqrt{3}}{2}.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="41" height="41" /> Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" /> be the intersection of the line through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/6/9866e3a998d628ba0941eb4fea0666ac391d149a.png" class="latex" alt="$Q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> and parallel to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/4/d3431b737f3671c3c1d717f039984ff101e97404.png" class="latex" alt="$AB,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="32" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" /> the intersection of the line through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> and parallel to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/c/8ecee609050b0744995a1b79ec0fcd162a97441b.png" class="latex" alt="$AB.$" width="32" height="13" /> Angle chasing shows that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" /> is the midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" /> midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/8/f386fc0035258fe8780a97b973f9ea93679abf16.png" class="latex" alt="$AD.$" width="32" height="13" /> Then from midline theorem,  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/9/ce9bc4fede5f41aa63b8fe863d558c8cffe165c2.png" class="latex" alt="$M, Q, N$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="65" height="16" /> are collinear, and likewise for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/2/cd2deec8bf9af79207f20e2bf93d392695bfc3cb.png" class="latex" alt="$N, P, M.$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="66" height="16" /> Thus, the line through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" /> is in fact the midline of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/e/4ae54b43c219341de4a6e5919922cf55371e6ee3.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD.$" width="61" height="13" />
</p><p><br />
Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/9/5095d485d0ea403d7ed4df9bab16a53c66e46341.png" class="latex" alt="$BQ \cap CD = X, AP \cap CD = Y.$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="244" height="16" /> Then, angle chasing shows that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/c/36c4513c51b21ded24a893c497f1c571a10e1b69.png" class="latex" alt="$CQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="28" height="16" /> not only bisects <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/c/32c07a11f1e1068f7ba5f951c2390e97025238a4.png" class="latex" alt="$BX,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="34" height="16" /> but is also perpendicular to it. This makes it a perpendicular bisector. The same is true for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/3/783d500281f8f46167dff3b071c543d42150e67f.png" class="latex" alt="$DP$" width="29" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/d/97d8ca9012a6e5b67843cf720c05aca4b9440672.png" class="latex" alt="$AY.$" width="28" height="13" /> Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/2/2327bde3feb6c03c4c444f3492c7b93d5a13f9c2.png" class="latex" alt="$CX = CB = 5,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="121" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/a/ada3bbeabfc46341eb1c2e2c9b26f0105cb0b887.png" class="latex" alt="$DY = DA = 7.$" width="120" height="13" /> This means <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/f/6cff5d883cad5f55e3359759210b035645e0ecf9.png" class="latex" alt="$XY = 19 - 5 - 7 = 7.$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="173" height="13" /> We can now find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/b/7ab17967324fc2b12349c0610c6c256f4c829162.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="27" height="16" /> as the midline of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/5/2052f0b297261000e3e151421072e4ea4c4f8c76.png" class="latex" alt="$ABXY.$" width="60" height="13" /> Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/3/cf3c58eaee3ef65a49e87e95863ee659c5a92347.png" class="latex" alt="$PQ = \frac{1}{2} (11+7) = 9.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="166" height="37" />
</p><p>Now, the answer is simply finding the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/9/839808168f6a437e9f43d7bb13b972ffa29f30dd.png" class="latex" alt="$ABQP$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="57" height="16" /> plus area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/f/60fb18a8942e282733214e697fffc061b2a81095.png" class="latex" alt="$CDPQ.$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="62" height="16" /> This is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/f/f4f1f0a22cab529fdeac2b24479206e0e9d32cf9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{5 \sqrt{3}}{2} \cdot \frac{11+9}{2} + \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{5 \sqrt{3}}{2} \cdot \frac{19+9}{2}  = \boxed{30 \sqrt{3}}.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="364" height="41" />
</p><p>~ MelonGirl
</p>
