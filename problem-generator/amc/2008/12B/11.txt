<p>A cone-shaped mountain has its base on the ocean floor and has a height of 8000 feet. The top <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/b/5db9e227e8177d26de452c2a2faeb6d45cc53d7b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{8}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> of the volume of the mountain is above water. What is the depth of the ocean at the base of the mountain in feet?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/f/5af32aa6247103bf6afbccc5ee9d67048f5a9a96.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 4000 \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 2000(4-\sqrt{2}) \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 6000 \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 6400 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 7000$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="554" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>In a cone, radius and height each vary inversely with increasing height (i.e. the radius of the cone formed by cutting off the mountain at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/e/b8e0d2a14866c3200683829344e1c82d0855366d.png" class="latex" alt="$4,000$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="43" height="16" /> feet is half that of the original mountain). Therefore, volume varies as the inverse cube of increasing height (expressed as a percentage of the total height of cone):
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/b/44b35af3cbdefafb894f57408f5ea025e14bb3ff.png" class="latex" alt="$V_I\times \text{Height}^3 = V_N$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="142" height="18" />
</p><p>Plugging in our given condition, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/f/55fd6cad6091f73b009691c0621cb544de73fcfb.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{8} = \text{Height}^3 \Rightarrow \text{Height} = \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="204" height="37" />.  
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/5/fb5cbbe058dfeb5683c751756473adadabab149d.png" class="latex" alt="$8000\cdot\frac{1}{2}=4000 \Rightarrow \boxed{\textbf{A}}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="177" height="37" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Faster_Solution">Faster Solution</span></h2>
<p>The volume of the cone above water is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/8/5d88cd982056c4509a72b6c926e0ada698a4786f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac 1 8$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> that of the entire cone (mountain). These cones are obviously similar so the radius and height of the small cone must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/e/23ef706d5de513df95c2c5a56b4c2b2fc9b7c835.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt[3]{\frac1 8}= \frac 1 2$" style="vertical-align: -14px" width="67" height="43" /> that of the large one. Because the height of the large cone is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/7/5a7d4eb637d47b95bbf234703443464f33712ee9.png" class="latex" alt="$8000$" width="35" height="12" /> the height of the small cone is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/0929ed034079c23455c385741744274c1f69b61b.png" class="latex" alt="$4000$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="13" />. Thus the depth of the water is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/e/07ef65f11c4e570245874fe24a5346e5a340ad3a.png" class="latex" alt="$8000-4000 = 4000 \Rightarrow\boxed{\text{A}}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="208" height="23" /> - AOPqghj
</p>
