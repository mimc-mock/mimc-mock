<p>An equilateral triangle of side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> is completely filled in by non-overlapping equilateral triangles of side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. How many small triangles are required?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/a/c4a65f67e7b2b0ae9dede3bc9953b17b8a7db2b4.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ 10\qquad\mathrm{(B)}\ 25\qquad\mathrm{(C)}\ 100\qquad\mathrm{(D)}\ 250\qquad\mathrm{(E)}\ 1000$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="423" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The area of the large triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/f/95f426fe9394b7519e6bb485eb08f649caa5bb93.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{10^2\sqrt3}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="52" height="41" />, while the area of each small triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/c/dcce9a3682d3f5044bacc68d8057f73c75b068fc.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1^2\sqrt3}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="43" height="41" />. Dividing these two quantities results in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />, therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/0/d00e87bc26843df2c3a4e325d87b1ad26dd14152.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{100} \mathrm{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="64" height="23" /> small triangles can fill the large one without overlap.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2-4">Solution 2-4</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/6/2b6049d158759587caa572b1d8911fc0deb0b10b.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(0.5cm); defaultpen(0.8); for (int i=0; i&lt;10; ++i) { draw( (i*dir(60)) -- ( (10,0) + (i*dir(120)) ) ); } for (int i=0; i&lt;10; ++i) { draw( (i*dir(0)) -- ( 10*dir(60) + (i*dir(-60)) ) ); } for (int i=0; i&lt;10; ++i) { draw( ((10-i)*dir(60)) -- ((10-i)*dir(0)) ); } [/asy]" width="238" height="208" />
</p><p>The number of triangles is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/2/832ca3243340669d4a5b39542d03c7c2534fa530.png" class="latex" alt="$1+3+\dots+19 = \boxed{100}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="187" height="23" />.
</p><p>Also, another way to do it is to notice that as you go row by row (from the bottom), the number of triangles decrease by 2 from 19, so we have:
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/a/63a6ef0b753898683ee91f33ea9c71e981bccc26.png" class="latex" alt="$19+17+15...+3+1 = \frac{19+1}{2}\cdot 10 = \boxed{100}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="349" height="37" />
</p><p>A fourth solution is to notice that the small triangles are similar to the large triangle as they are both equilateral. Therefore, the ratio of their areas is the square of the ratio of their side lengths.  Hence the ratio of their areas is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/7/f576707cb28360ce8b95143ea7517bb3b634758f.png" class="latex" alt="$(1/10)^2=1/100$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="126" height="19" />, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/b/43bc38a3aa247c41492f0162a7f4b8cc4ef1c108.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{100}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="39" height="23" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5">Solution 5</span></h2>
<p>The side length of the large equilateral triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. The number of equilateral triangles with side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> that can fill it is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/6/db61fd75a1a8bb6fa2b202b6b73daf9b4c9838d0.png" class="latex" alt="$10^2$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="24" height="15" />. This is equal to the sum of the first <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> odd integers. The sum of the first <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> odd integers is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/0/6006bba637eef443381fbaff768fa62d9ff41e62.png" class="latex" alt="$n^2$" width="17" height="15" />. In general, an equilateral triangle with side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> can be filled by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/0/6006bba637eef443381fbaff768fa62d9ff41e62.png" class="latex" alt="$n^2$" width="17" height="15" /> triangles. The answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/d/02dc3c48a9c90d6f5ae4c0c5ed11a668c0de0c16.png" class="latex" alt="$100\ \mathrm{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="58" height="18" />.
</p><p>~mobius247
</p>
