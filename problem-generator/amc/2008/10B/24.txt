<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<ul><li>Note: This solution requires the use of cyclic quadrilateral properties but could be a bit time-consuming during the contest.</li></ul>
<p>To start off, draw a diagram like in solution one and label the points. Create lines <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/5/12517bbc31cb9026c71299b7a0d232d07e60f284.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AC}$" width="28" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/7/4373e7b4be983c0315e82e1fda1df66ed34dd7fb.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BD}$" width="30" height="15" />. We can call their intersection point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/5/ce58e4af225c93d08606c26554caaa5ae32edeba.png" class="latex" alt="$Y$" width="14" height="12" />. Note that triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/a/d7ada763e99852a6e44a15d46cce3c3709a944d5.png" class="latex" alt="$BCD$" width="44" height="12" /> is an isosceles triangle so angles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/4/fd43b1261ea6db8d43a9bdbd28f90cf6f23617d4.png" class="latex" alt="$CDB$" width="44" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/d/87da4992deed43fafdcdf6879ffb693fd6414995.png" class="latex" alt="$CBD$" width="44" height="12" /> are each <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> degrees. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> equals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" />, angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/d/2edcdc0d73ef64ec441f60a3a8dccdbd10aa61a5.png" class="latex" alt="$BAC$" width="42" height="13" /> equals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/9/b79030abeb688469b5ffc2e1c4e37fee225a529e.png" class="latex" alt="$55$" width="17" height="12" /> degrees, thus making angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/e/0eed3d8693577f26f7643328c1ae4b1ae070ac82.png" class="latex" alt="$AYB$" width="42" height="13" /> equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/2/302e99c1c7cd91a6ae371139e5142ccef5977dca.png" class="latex" alt="$60$" width="17" height="12" /> degrees. We can also find out that angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/4/554eb00958a95f255a2e7862c030aebf029a4e78.png" class="latex" alt="$CYB$" width="43" height="12" /> equals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/a/aaa4de95cc6371d125d5cf773cdd9f9913dd1d8e.png" class="latex" alt="$120$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> degrees. 
</p><p>Extend <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/0/ce0bfc603f9a044e308b1a1fdadca917e81ad2dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{CD}$" width="30" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> and let their intersection be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" />. Since angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/1/ef14ba8fc495f2a5750a5ed394ebf75a28641658.png" class="latex" alt="$BEC$" width="43" height="12" /> plus angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/4/554eb00958a95f255a2e7862c030aebf029a4e78.png" class="latex" alt="$CYB$" width="43" height="12" /> equals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/b/5cb1b41850ab676e4d1cf15c69b1609af4befbc5.png" class="latex" alt="$180$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> degrees, quadrilateral <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/f/43f78ace825727df4005468cd1dc47e027e0c86d.png" class="latex" alt="$YCEB$" width="57" height="12" /> is a cyclic quadrilateral. 
</p><p>Next, draw a line from point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/5/ce58e4af225c93d08606c26554caaa5ae32edeba.png" class="latex" alt="$Y$" width="14" height="12" /> to point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" />. Since angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/0/8e05997706bbfdfbe064b06b1120b92044a8bce6.png" class="latex" alt="$YBC$" width="43" height="12" /> and angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/b/d8b1e5305f63388fbe94b9751c75e5f92f74f59b.png" class="latex" alt="$YEC$" width="43" height="12" /> point to the same arc, angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/b/d8b1e5305f63388fbe94b9751c75e5f92f74f59b.png" class="latex" alt="$YEC$" width="43" height="12" /> is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> degrees. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/c/7acd05ac9dd6205d9f6a653c2a15c54d3e128705.png" class="latex" alt="$EYD$" width="44" height="12" /> is an isosceles triangle (based on angle properties) and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/3/f93cbf922c81ba26d6d1a76f4a9377514baf2ce4.png" class="latex" alt="$YAE$" width="42" height="13" /> is also an isosceles triangle, we can find that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/1/361c2accf6a180b8c32fa6241b1004d0128b68d0.png" class="latex" alt="$YAD$" width="43" height="13" /> is also an isosceles triangle. Thus, each of the other angles is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/2/952d955230af4cafc5585c7591a72fb3d00fedc8.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{180-120}{2}=30$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="122" height="37" /> degrees. Finally, we have angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/5/7e538978649c14e1616fac744e983b5981d0ea8e.png" class="latex" alt="$BAD$" width="43" height="13" /> equals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/3/4a37e3befe54507fb95ef94e2902b07b3505024b.png" class="latex" alt="$30+55=\boxed{85}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="112" height="23" /> degrees.
</p><p>~Minor edits by BakedPotato66

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>First, connect the diagonal <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/4/204a6f5a6757f5da4c1b3035ad8d806769744e63.png" class="latex" alt="$DB$" width="29" height="12" />, then, draw line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/5/6d5c9afa92ae3180f02754c177e530d9d0f8b4d4.png" class="latex" alt="$DE$" width="29" height="12" /> such that it is congruent to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/e/27e525b776c49bbe7d0ae66cfc7ab8f58f40624e.png" class="latex" alt="$DC$" width="29" height="12" /> and is parallel to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" />. Because triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/5/cf51b47182ee2e945de88c04f653d01d4919d104.png" class="latex" alt="$DCB$" width="44" height="12" /> is isosceles and angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/5/cf51b47182ee2e945de88c04f653d01d4919d104.png" class="latex" alt="$DCB$" width="44" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/f/f1f38f77d3125c2d6d994b9887de9c45b61ef44b.png" class="latex" alt="$170^\circ$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="33" height="13" />, the angles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/4/fd43b1261ea6db8d43a9bdbd28f90cf6f23617d4.png" class="latex" alt="$CDB$" width="44" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/d/87da4992deed43fafdcdf6879ffb693fd6414995.png" class="latex" alt="$CBD$" width="44" height="12" /> are both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/b/32b6a374f5da5aec982fb8ab220bd2f2df89b0ed.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{180-170}{2} = 5^\circ$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="120" height="37" />. Because angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/a/31adfcb608f857dff15cc5fa7ea0c327e8dc65ba.png" class="latex" alt="$70^\circ$" width="24" height="13" />, we get angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/a/a6adfd3ad27a2dddeb0af7131d73c33c86991e93.png" class="latex" alt="$ABD$" width="43" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/8/ee8be1368ce8d14298fcb69510c04460d362b23e.png" class="latex" alt="$65^\circ$" width="24" height="13" />. Next, noticing parallel lines <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/5/6d5c9afa92ae3180f02754c177e530d9d0f8b4d4.png" class="latex" alt="$DE$" width="29" height="12" /> and transversal <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/4/204a6f5a6757f5da4c1b3035ad8d806769744e63.png" class="latex" alt="$DB$" width="29" height="12" />, we see that angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/e/96e81e1599a08776bb0799f73128f99332c1d78f.png" class="latex" alt="$BDE$" width="44" height="12" /> is also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/8/ee8be1368ce8d14298fcb69510c04460d362b23e.png" class="latex" alt="$65^\circ$" width="24" height="13" />, and subtracting off angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/4/fd43b1261ea6db8d43a9bdbd28f90cf6f23617d4.png" class="latex" alt="$CDB$" width="44" height="12" /> gives that angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/b/b8b38900100755bb7ceb651e8d20b173fa9e3001.png" class="latex" alt="$EDC$" width="44" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/f/29fa0a9949f018e121b2093216dd5db13d3abef0.png" class="latex" alt="$60^\circ$" width="24" height="13" />.
</p><p>Now, because we drew <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/f/e7ff09c51622b7a3b1e2883066ca4f8774d71dc9.png" class="latex" alt="$ED = DC$" width="84" height="12" />, triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/a/b7a9191aefcf2eb237d1999486083ed78f6d515f.png" class="latex" alt="$DEC$" width="44" height="12" /> is equilateral. We can also conclude that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/4/f74e01223dfa8777616e35ebc7d8a1c9521268bd.png" class="latex" alt="$EC=DC=CB$" width="135" height="12" /> meaning that triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/b/ecb4cd2a13596695c67bb16257a68f9cd006073d.png" class="latex" alt="$ECB$" width="43" height="12" /> is isosceles, and angles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/6/3d6cc967cfad5ef4e31600bf87510379a566e9bb.png" class="latex" alt="$CBE$" width="43" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/2/f52863fdb7628708d5fe508796064193c3aa12b0.png" class="latex" alt="$CEB$" width="43" height="12" /> are equal.
</p><p>Finally, we can set up our equation. Denote angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/5/7e538978649c14e1616fac744e983b5981d0ea8e.png" class="latex" alt="$BAD$" width="43" height="13" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/6/7a66181795ab222ad9ff38cb534dead38e5a47de.png" class="latex" alt="$x^\circ$" width="16" height="13" />. Then, because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/8/a68eca3ba7f3e3a5f5b07a373f664d66e17f9d91.png" class="latex" alt="$ABED$" width="58" height="13" /> is a parallelogram, the angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/0/a00d9600453b5f79a8ea20606ca8f0a538bac5e9.png" class="latex" alt="$DEB$" width="44" height="12" /> is also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/6/7a66181795ab222ad9ff38cb534dead38e5a47de.png" class="latex" alt="$x^\circ$" width="16" height="13" />. Then, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/2/f52863fdb7628708d5fe508796064193c3aa12b0.png" class="latex" alt="$CEB$" width="43" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/c/86cfaf0a44e61c16553555ec9d614dc356a7f7bb.png" class="latex" alt="$(x-60)^\circ$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="70" height="18" />. Again because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/8/a68eca3ba7f3e3a5f5b07a373f664d66e17f9d91.png" class="latex" alt="$ABED$" width="58" height="13" /> is a parallelogram, angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/8/fd85454c0ce4000c0e63b4a1c4a12ef6f8b56e9d.png" class="latex" alt="$ABE$" width="42" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/9/4e902510dbcdccbf0d866093439244571b6ca910.png" class="latex" alt="$(180-x)^\circ$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="80" height="18" />. Subtracting angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> gives that angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/6/3d6cc967cfad5ef4e31600bf87510379a566e9bb.png" class="latex" alt="$CBE$" width="43" height="12" /> equals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/4/0741cb82d67e95c99e60d16d909e0ec7e05f8ece.png" class="latex" alt="$(110-x)^\circ$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="80" height="18" />. Because angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/6/3d6cc967cfad5ef4e31600bf87510379a566e9bb.png" class="latex" alt="$CBE$" width="43" height="12" /> equals angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/2/f52863fdb7628708d5fe508796064193c3aa12b0.png" class="latex" alt="$CEB$" width="43" height="12" />, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/f/01fa4b5d2675f2c894d513b672bf345b5a788893.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[x-60=110-x\]" width="134" height="13" />, solving into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/7/f973a71e52817b3011581f86e0b8908351eb52ab.png" class="latex" alt="$x=\boxed{85^\circ}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="72" height="24" />.
