<p>How many non-<a href="/wiki/index.php/Similar" class="mw-redirect" title="Similar">similar</a> triangles have angles whose degree measures are distinct positive integers in <a href="/wiki/index.php/Arithmetic_progression" class="mw-redirect" title="Arithmetic progression">arithmetic progression</a>? 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/d/bdd1cce8256c82d17e8f809e7ff76bd465c02776.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 0\qquad\textbf{(B) } 1\qquad\textbf{(C) } 59\qquad\textbf{(D) } 89\qquad\textbf{(E) } 178\qquad$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="425" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>The sum of the angles of a triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/b/5cb1b41850ab676e4d1cf15c69b1609af4befbc5.png" class="latex" alt="$180$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> degrees. For an arithmetic progression with an odd number of terms, the middle term is equal to the average of the sum of all of the terms, making it <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/7/2/6724e1f344bb3eb8ab3959e723d0523578fbb57c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{180}{3} = 60$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="73" height="37" /> degrees. The minimum possible value for the smallest angle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> and the highest possible is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/e/afe61e5b80572ad5a200a0d9cd354ce12a8c7800.png" class="latex" alt="$59$" width="17" height="12" /> (since the numbers are distinct), so there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/6/6c64ae31b324e697d36ab230bd1f994f7b11b9d3.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) }59}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="66" height="30" /> possibilities.
</p>
