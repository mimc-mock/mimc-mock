<p>A number of linked rings, each <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> cm thick, are hanging on a peg. The top ring has an outside diameter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/3/f8366cd6196dd8a9da6d38a3e9eafb109e99d53e.png" class="latex" alt="$20$" width="17" height="12" /> cm. The outside diameter of each of the outer rings is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> cm less than that of the ring above it. The bottom ring has an outside diameter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> cm. What is the distance, in cm, from the top of the top ring to the bottom of the bottom ring?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/b/75b3e762b96f49323740d90a83fdb1339789180e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(7cm); pathpen = linewidth(0.7); D(CR((0,0),10)); D(CR((0,0),9.5)); D(CR((0,-18.5),9.5)); D(CR((0,-18.5),9)); MP(&quot;$\vdots$&quot;,(0,-31),(0,0)); D(CR((0,-39),3)); D(CR((0,-39),2.5)); D(CR((0,-43.5),2.5)); D(CR((0,-43.5),2)); D(CR((0,-47),2)); D(CR((0,-47),1.5)); D(CR((0,-49.5),1.5)); D(CR((0,-49.5),1.0));  D((12,-10)--(12,10)); MP(&#39;20&#39;,(12,0),E); D((12,-51)--(12,-48)); MP(&#39;3&#39;,(12,-49.5),E);[/asy]" width="148" height="332" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/6/aa6459ccc9fd6e39b0316f7caf4db43a04046004.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 171\qquad\textbf{(B) } 173\qquad\textbf{(C) } 182\qquad\textbf{(D) } 188\qquad\textbf{(E) } 210\qquad$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="479" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The inside diameters of the rings are the positive integers from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/7/5e7a1b6098a8c98b8e4adae526aeef4b91712620.png" class="latex" alt="$18$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />.  The total distance needed is the sum of these values plus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> for the top of the first ring and the bottom of the last ring. Using the formula for the sum of an <a href="/wiki/index.php/Arithmetic_series" class="mw-redirect" title="Arithmetic series">arithmetic series</a>, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/0/820a14d734ec4163ca7028761cc482f869f37298.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{18 \cdot 19}{2} + 2 = \boxed{\textbf{(B) }173}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="184" height="38" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Alternatively, the sum of the consecutive integers from 3 to 20 is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/c/d9c64de6e13f7680458b39641f7be0cf2005045d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}(18)(3+20) = 207$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="160" height="37" />.  However, the 17 <a href="/wiki/index.php/Intersection" title="Intersection">intersections</a> between the rings must be subtracted, and we also get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/c/76c8a2d702906f58de5f50f564c3d83691d67743.png" class="latex" alt="$207 - 2(17) = \boxed{\textbf{(B) }173}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="190" height="30" />.
</p>
