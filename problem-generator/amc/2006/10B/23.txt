<p>A triangle is partitioned into three triangles and a quadrilateral by drawing two lines from vertices to their opposite sides. The areas of the three triangles are 3, 7, and 7, as shown. What is the area of the shaded quadrilateral?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/7/cf791fd7b612d29ad4afcad408d1c4e8e0c60790.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(1.5cm); defaultpen(.8);  pair A = (0,0), B = (3,0), C = (1.4, 2), D = B + 0.4*(C-B), Ep = A + 0.3*(C-A); pair F = intersectionpoint( A--D, B--Ep );  draw( A -- B -- C -- cycle ); draw( A -- D ); draw( B -- Ep ); filldraw( D -- F -- Ep -- C -- cycle, mediumgray, black );  label(&quot;$7$&quot;,(1.25,0.2)); label(&quot;$7$&quot;,(2.2,0.45)); label(&quot;$3$&quot;,(0.45,0.35)); [/asy]" width="215" height="145" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/0/f40e9555fe1d9b1e0aa46afd86b03cad2b23cc77.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A) \ } 15\qquad \mathrm{(B) \ } 17\qquad \mathrm{(C) \ } \frac{35}{2}\qquad \mathrm{(D) \ } 18\qquad \mathrm{(E) \ } \frac{55}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="394" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Label the points in the figure as shown below, and draw the segment <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/d/07d29465728e92952374a857beaee91665d8dbd9.png" class="latex" alt="$CF$" width="28" height="12" />. This segment divides the quadrilateral into two triangles, let their areas be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/f/1ef409b8d310b1b022e55175de0f56a987435bbd.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(2cm); defaultpen(.8);  pair A = (0,0), B = (3,0), C = (1.4, 2), D = B + 0.4*(C-B), Ep = A + 0.3*(C-A); pair F = intersectionpoint( A--D, B--Ep );  draw( A -- B -- C -- cycle ); draw( A -- D ); draw( B -- Ep ); filldraw( D -- F -- Ep -- C -- cycle, mediumgray, black );  label(&quot;$7$&quot;,(1.45,0.15)); label(&quot;$7$&quot;,(2.2,0.45)); label(&quot;$3$&quot;,(0.45,0.35));  draw( C -- F, dashed );  label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,SW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,SE); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,N); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,NE); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,Ep,NW); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,F,S);  label(&quot;$x$&quot;,(1,1)); label(&quot;$y$&quot;,(1.6,1)); [/asy]" width="328" height="232" />
</p><p>Since <a href="/wiki/index.php/Triangle" title="Triangle">triangles</a> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/8/848339679da78d0867f8392f691c81f0d45c81fe.png" class="latex" alt="$AFB$" width="42" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/9/439f49d17a22405b40790e923c63559f6a026399.png" class="latex" alt="$DFB$" width="44" height="12" /> share an <a href="/wiki/index.php/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> and have equal area, their bases must be equal, hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/4/a24dd1bd1438ef123e059f6e9178721c035f6f35.png" class="latex" alt="$AF=DF$" width="81" height="13" />.
</p><p>Since triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/9/419dea12de38c0f719dc92765204a7ccb24cd27f.png" class="latex" alt="$AFC$" width="42" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/6/d7688a32dd8ca72b8d925b3ecb6d39edb2b33e9b.png" class="latex" alt="$DFC$" width="44" height="12" /> share an altitude from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> and their respective bases are equal, their areas must be equal, hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/7/207861a9d2228b7c84ec01c463ac2e2c90295b76.png" class="latex" alt="$x+3=y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="75" height="16" />. 
</p><p>Since triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/d/ccd67c4d1463073f422f24bacfd3af2b14bdf014.png" class="latex" alt="$EFA$" width="42" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/8/068be40c44c2fb63ce8085751c281210281a3989.png" class="latex" alt="$BFA$" width="42" height="13" /> share an altitude from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and their respective areas are in the ratio <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/f/8bff28fddf20c44b02e914be2590b2e309f2b964.png" class="latex" alt="$3:7$" width="33" height="12" />, their bases must be in the same ratio, hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/f/d2ff134d367700f51a70ab6d8ed2e14354243ec8.png" class="latex" alt="$EF:FB = 3:7$" width="130" height="12" />.
</p><p>Since triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/a/d1aa64bffb491dd06f6c006a88358b19687b2f67.png" class="latex" alt="$EFC$" width="42" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/d/efdd26b7cbfad505c1adbdfccc28d3ab1eb91872.png" class="latex" alt="$BFC$" width="43" height="12" /> share an altitude from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> and their respective bases are in the ratio <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/f/8bff28fddf20c44b02e914be2590b2e309f2b964.png" class="latex" alt="$3:7$" width="33" height="12" />, their areas must be in the same ratio, hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/1/9a1bc25c0078a91a62c01962169c037bb436d8a9.png" class="latex" alt="$x:(y+7) = 3:7$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="138" height="18" />, which gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/2/0a246a1e5f4f9a280f1f477117434b75d7506124.png" class="latex" alt="$7x = 3(y+7)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="106" height="18" />.
</p><p>Substituting <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/5/d95bcce6dc731c9ef8f3e99f6fae06aac36cc5c7.png" class="latex" alt="$y=x+3$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="75" height="16" /> into the second equation we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/6/6467d8c06f61af0fdee59ea873eab2608d486b03.png" class="latex" alt="$7x = 3(x+10)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="116" height="18" />, which solves to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/e/afea8b725c42f5d0d9fa0e9ac3f86e8a67e79da2.png" class="latex" alt="$x=\frac{15}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="55" height="37" />. Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/6/796b88770627043462ca9d10535196f805ccace4.png" class="latex" alt="$y=x+3 = \frac{15}{2}+3 = \frac{21}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="198" height="37" />, and the total area of the quadrilateral is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/6/416b9db2dabcdff8011b655bffb0e57bf8756820.png" class="latex" alt="$x+y = \frac{15}{2}+\frac{21}{2} = \boxed{\textbf{(D) }18}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="223" height="37" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Connect points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />. Triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/d/ccd67c4d1463073f422f24bacfd3af2b14bdf014.png" class="latex" alt="$EFA$" width="42" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/6/5b66ed8804598d6133c4f90f551e73be8b215f4d.png" class="latex" alt="$FAB$" width="41" height="13" /> share an altitude and their areas are in the ratio <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/f/8bff28fddf20c44b02e914be2590b2e309f2b964.png" class="latex" alt="$3:7$" width="33" height="12" />. Their bases, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/2/c128243ea8125d0bb007b8132dcfa44461df73a7.png" class="latex" alt="$EF$" width="28" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/9/59910c059976e19ba94be84e4dab4701a119d5ba.png" class="latex" alt="$FB$" width="28" height="12" />, must be in the same <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/f/8bff28fddf20c44b02e914be2590b2e309f2b964.png" class="latex" alt="$3:7$" width="33" height="12" /> ratio. 
</p><p>Triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/7/987d57e2a07f5c9a3845ccc44680130c787ba0fd.png" class="latex" alt="$EFD$" width="43" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/2/a4280b51205a516de3a891f86968342518b9a806.png" class="latex" alt="$FBD$" width="44" height="12" /> share an altitude and their bases are in a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/f/8bff28fddf20c44b02e914be2590b2e309f2b964.png" class="latex" alt="$3:7$" width="33" height="12" /> ratio. Therefore, their areas are in a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/f/8bff28fddf20c44b02e914be2590b2e309f2b964.png" class="latex" alt="$3:7$" width="33" height="12" /> ratio and the area of triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/7/987d57e2a07f5c9a3845ccc44680130c787ba0fd.png" class="latex" alt="$EFD$" width="43" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />.
</p><p>Triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/5/dc5309b25510f426ca00c957623bd0396c79e4c3.png" class="latex" alt="$CED$" width="44" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/1/291a26605884e355c23c524c392c07ad49e5df5a.png" class="latex" alt="$DEA$" width="43" height="13" /> share an altitude. Therefore, the ratio of their areas is equal to the ratio of bases <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/c/13c970cbf5e2e7029089517d5e8efbb8cade6d8a.png" class="latex" alt="$CE$" width="28" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/9/2d9456e0361813641f4ce80980839e9038fe8f7b.png" class="latex" alt="$EA$" width="28" height="13" />. The ratio is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/7/0c7b397e56d5e0fd82eb525a256c62fb4476a908.png" class="latex" alt="$A:(3+3) \Rightarrow A:6$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="149" height="18" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> is the area of triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/5/dc5309b25510f426ca00c957623bd0396c79e4c3.png" class="latex" alt="$CED$" width="44" height="12" />
</p><p>Triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/2/f52863fdb7628708d5fe508796064193c3aa12b0.png" class="latex" alt="$CEB$" width="43" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/3/0e30e7dec97a3f5e84e0de8a12e3056c6c9a77fc.png" class="latex" alt="$EAB$" width="42" height="13" /> also share an altitude. The ratio of their areas is also equal to the ratio of bases <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/c/13c970cbf5e2e7029089517d5e8efbb8cade6d8a.png" class="latex" alt="$CE$" width="28" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/9/2d9456e0361813641f4ce80980839e9038fe8f7b.png" class="latex" alt="$EA$" width="28" height="13" />. The ratio is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/1/801c0c21a0bd8d3897c93380350f038cb3a368c1.png" class="latex" alt="$(A+3+7):(3+7) \Rightarrow (A+10):10$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="289" height="18" />
</p><p>Because the two ratios are equal, we get the equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/e/56ee0a051823e3b824b962d6c420934817b4ebac.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{A}{6} = \frac {A+10}{10} \Rightarrow 10A = 6A+60 \Rightarrow A = 15$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="331" height="38" />. We add the area of triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/e/f6ee867c86cc90974d089043903a0b1c02291e6a.png" class="latex" alt="$EDF$" width="44" height="12" /> to get that the total area of the quadrilateral is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/b/81b2f4d8afb3d12f67ca7d804a3b24167205d534.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) }18}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="65" height="28" />.
</p><p>~Zeric Hang
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>We use mass points (similar to above). Let the triangle be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> with cevians (lines to opposite side) from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />. Let the points opposite <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> respectively and the intersection as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />. 
</p><p>Assign masses of 1 at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/4/a94eec6aacb20224c9cb4d52e3ed264de9b2582d.png" class="latex" alt="$[BPC] = [DPC]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="129" height="18" />. Then the mass at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> is 2. To find masses at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />, we let the mass at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> be x and the mass at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> be y. Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/e/8de9651fe8f255dc573c8d30cacf95b916844067.png" class="latex" alt="$3x = 7y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="61" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/7/7d75a835b4b3204758dcbe4f781f45d736183a2d.png" class="latex" alt="$y = \frac{3}{7}x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="57" height="37" />. Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/4/c94afe5bc9c7cdd327f3d332144a0126eeba8fdf.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{10}{7}x = 2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="65" height="37" /> since we add the masses for the fulcrum mass, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/8/69844c5096755896548ba0b87ed4db86af7436c6.png" class="latex" alt="$x = \frac{7}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="46" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/b/5ab3d37f9012b232f0f93dc7989c76000d0cce72.png" class="latex" alt="$y = \frac{3}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="45" height="37" />. 
</p><p>To calculate the mass at a, it is merely <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/e/efef3ebdb586504d0316a1d82865f835cf5de7d0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{7}{5} - 1 = \frac{2}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="80" height="37" /> which means <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/f/f5f07481396e51bd0f6f4bbbb0b350036ada26f8.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{[BCF]}{[ACF]} = \frac{2}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="93" height="43" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/1/e61708223dcbc8dcb6cbf401e79234805172de63.png" class="latex" alt="$[ACF] = 25$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="94" height="18" />. It is easy to see the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/b/81b2f4d8afb3d12f67ca7d804a3b24167205d534.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D) }18}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="65" height="28" />.
</p>
