<p>Suppose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> are positive integers with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/d/22d61749698f06d583904f9b6592020c685d91b7.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b+c=2006$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="129" height="14" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/b/92b10fe52fc9aad9b96806cddd894c0134f4ef87.png" class="latex" alt="$a!b!c!=m\cdot 10^n$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="120" height="13" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> are integers and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> is not divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. What is the smallest possible value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/8/8387686fb1b4ff7f52dc91314b2269c0f30f4239.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ 489 \qquad \mathrm{(B)}\ 492  \qquad \mathrm{(C)}\ 495 \qquad \mathrm{(D)}\ 498 \qquad \mathrm{(E)}\ 501$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="432" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The power of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> for any factorial is given by the well-known algorithm 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/0/f80b47a77cf7ef187698dba7dd44c34edc1a490d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\left\lfloor \frac n{5}\right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac n{25}\right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac n{125}\right\rfloor + \cdots\]" width="207" height="34" />
It is rational to guess numbers right before powers of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> because we won't have any extra numbers from higher powers of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />. As we list out the powers of 5, it is clear that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/4/a0460066a33ea3495286199cef1e272078616816.png" class="latex" alt="$5^{4}=625$" width="67" height="15" /> is less than 2006 and  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/d/48d9c538583f77179026c8683b5ff0654c41e8e5.png" class="latex" alt="$5^{5}=3125$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="76" height="15" /> is greater. Therefore, set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> to be 624. Thus, c is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/6/4866bf1b75671f262e03e2d006fcb7937ab94fa9.png" class="latex" alt="$2006-(624\cdot 2)=758$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="173" height="18" />. Applying the algorithm, we see that our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/5/3a572fe44038a7b769816308ddda579e6e7fcc78.png" class="latex" alt="$152+152+188= \boxed{492}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="189" height="23" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Clearly, the power of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> that divides <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/f/63f344e7d8b14a7886fc7fb72da53cd0a3271dcc.png" class="latex" alt="$n!$" width="14" height="13" /> is larger or equal than the power of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> which divides
it. Hence we are trying to minimize the power of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> that will divide <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/a/98ae5b2d1456aad3cba53d13e86969c755111bc9.png" class="latex" alt="$a!b!c!$" width="39" height="13" />.
</p><p>Consider <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/5/645ba12dbf4a999f21a0289bfdddb92acf1c9067.png" class="latex" alt="$n! = 1\cdot 2 \cdot \dots \cdot n$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="129" height="13" />. Each fifth term is divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, each <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/6/66644fd9f411299c3c1eabf151b5f601c9ef6c21.png" class="latex" alt="$25$" width="17" height="12" />-th one
by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/6/66644fd9f411299c3c1eabf151b5f601c9ef6c21.png" class="latex" alt="$25$" width="17" height="12" />, and so on. Hence the total power of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> that divides <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/b/debe69462f8cc99d226994ee4007867a6e67e887.png" class="latex" alt="$\left\lfloor \frac n{5}\right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac n{25}\right\rfloor + \cdots$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="138" height="33" />. (For any <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> only finitely many terms in the sum
are
non-zero.)
</p><p>In our case we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/6/f16861488f75f0100474124f711e8e0eb21a58af.png" class="latex" alt="$a&lt;2006$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="69" height="13" />, so the largest power of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> that will be less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is at most
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/4/3348cb621c1e743dadf3c6625cc24f8fc9cf15e8.png" class="latex" alt="$5^4 = 625$" width="67" height="15" />. Therefore the power of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> that divides <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/8/dc80ce189c35cf4877ca2f60fa66815ab167a6a3.png" class="latex" alt="$a!$" width="13" height="13" /> is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/0/110ffe3055a358427bdd18e84cf936000131fc3b.png" class="latex" alt="$\left\lfloor \frac a{5}\right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac a{25}\right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac a{125}\right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac a{625}\right\rfloor$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="236" height="34" />. The same
is true for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" />.
</p><p>Intuition may now try to lure us to split <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/2/d1216dd3855e1bb88b6136b4ce49c58b57747417.png" class="latex" alt="$2006$" width="35" height="12" /> into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/b/6ebd1e0356cfe09c0d9b5459d6f1542657bb5891.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b+c$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="70" height="14" /> as evenly as possible, giving
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/2/b32e298edab1af3d0641276b5adea03cf7786ac1.png" class="latex" alt="$a=b=669$" width="92" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/7/e479459abe2c94b1480742e9b35f56381b51214c.png" class="latex" alt="$c=668$" width="58" height="12" />. However, this solution is not optimal.
</p><p>To see how we can do better, let's rearrange the terms as follows:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/6/d36ce4dfc0769d4a11faf9c4c7067e13aac40dd3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} \text{result} &amp; = \Big\lfloor \frac a{5}\Big\rfloor + \Big\lfloor \frac b{5}\Big\rfloor + \Big\lfloor \frac c{5}\Big\rfloor \\ &amp; + \Big\lfloor \frac a{25}\Big\rfloor + \Big\lfloor \frac b{25}\Big\rfloor + \Big\lfloor \frac c{25}\Big\rfloor \\ &amp; + \Big\lfloor \frac a{125}\Big\rfloor + \Big\lfloor \frac b{125}\Big\rfloor + \Big\lfloor \frac c{125}\Big\rfloor \\ &amp; + \Big\lfloor \frac a{625}\Big\rfloor + \Big\lfloor \frac b{625}\Big\rfloor + \Big\lfloor \frac c{625}\Big\rfloor \end{align*}" width="254" height="169" />
</p><p>The idea is that the rows of the above equation are roughly equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/7/387948a019c3c3a2646602e8043fba93bc711850.png" class="latex" alt="$\left\lfloor \frac n{5}\right\rfloor$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="30" height="33" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/e/44e6ca61cb98e579902c9177572cfa4574399414.png" class="latex" alt="$\left\lfloor \frac n{25}\right\rfloor$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="37" height="33" />, etc.
</p><p>More precisely, we can now notice that for any positive integers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/e/0fe29569b76eadcef569c25a70ebda35e1fd43e1.png" class="latex" alt="$a,b,c,k$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="59" height="16" /> we can write <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/b/a5b29b358e825defa3e11b7d903d43ec31e5909a.png" class="latex" alt="$a,b,c$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="41" height="16" /> in the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/9/7990082f76bfd96e36a75600d044cb14701b44d9.png" class="latex" alt="$a=a_0k + a_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="98" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/4/414470c68ddac54a614885759d6e7e2cd59b40df.png" class="latex" alt="$b=b_0k+b_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="93" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/4/0c4c1dcdf8c34ef893107e71740b7d5be7b200c0.png" class="latex" alt="$c=c_0k + c_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="93" height="15" />, where all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/f/7cf9a3b01d5661cf64ee8b1988aef6616b0c9d37.png" class="latex" alt="$a_i,b_i,c_i$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="57" height="16" /> are integers and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/c/1cc77c8e11bf279aaeeb5cf4962d0e8f66f6bbee.png" class="latex" alt="$0\leq a_1,b_1,c_1&lt;k$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="131" height="16" />.
</p><p>It follows that
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/c/2/1c2fda95660c185cd8dac069be5dd43892d5276a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\Big\lfloor \frac a{k}\Big\rfloor + \Big\lfloor \frac b{k}\Big\rfloor + \Big\lfloor \frac c{k}\Big\rfloor = a_0+b_0+c_0\]" width="253" height="37" />
and
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/6/126774d7a6b09b1310d9a9dc03931325961498db.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\Big\lfloor \frac {a+b+c}k\Big\rfloor = a_0 + b_0 + c_0 + \Big\lfloor \frac {a_1+b_1+c_1}k\Big\rfloor \leq a_0 + b_0 + c_0 + 2\]" width="475" height="37" />
</p><p>Hence we get that for any positive integers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/e/0fe29569b76eadcef569c25a70ebda35e1fd43e1.png" class="latex" alt="$a,b,c,k$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="59" height="16" /> we have
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/1/341d9f17bcb55c1086aafae4435dc96ead2fdbc1.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\Big\lfloor \frac a{k}\Big\rfloor + \Big\lfloor \frac b{k}\Big\rfloor + \Big\lfloor \frac c{k}\Big\rfloor \quad \geq \quad \Big\lfloor \frac {a+b+c}k\Big\rfloor - 2\]" width="319" height="37" />
</p><p>Therefore for any <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/b/a5b29b358e825defa3e11b7d903d43ec31e5909a.png" class="latex" alt="$a,b,c$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="41" height="16" /> the result is at least <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/7/c6724267f41c17e918eabc5046e2b89a95588522.png" class="latex" alt="$\left\lfloor \frac n{5}\right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac n{25}\right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac n{125}\right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac n{625}\right\rfloor - 8 = 401 + 80 + 16 + 3 - 8 = 500 - 8 = 492$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="601" height="34" />.
</p><p>If we now show how to pick <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/b/a5b29b358e825defa3e11b7d903d43ec31e5909a.png" class="latex" alt="$a,b,c$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="41" height="16" /> so that we'll get the result <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/e/49e515df0b582548c914cc0ffe1f78da177a5cfa.png" class="latex" alt="$492$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />, we will be done.
</p><p>Consider the row with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/1/15117013d1b9a82a24e57d2e8d7ffa4b5df7bb85.png" class="latex" alt="$625$" width="26" height="12" /> in the denominator. We need to achieve sum <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> in this row,
hence we need to make two of the numbers smaller than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/5/1/15117013d1b9a82a24e57d2e8d7ffa4b5df7bb85.png" class="latex" alt="$625$" width="26" height="12" />. Choosing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/9/f395c7c5ea8a92a5cd4f2a04e534c4c3af62c787.png" class="latex" alt="$a=b=624$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="93" height="13" />
does this, and it will give us the largest possible remainders for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> in
the other three rows, so this is a pretty good candidate. We can compute
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/0/f20b850618e38e771ba3d2e5b8038a03c505d1fe.png" class="latex" alt="$c=2006-a-b=758$" width="181" height="12" /> and verify that this triple gives the desired result <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad298069138eb85bcee3bc1f2d3cc7794ea47b17.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{492}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="39" height="23" />.
</p>
