<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> be the set of all point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/3/e53b1fe25be1c679117fb44a6a886fe1247d189a.png" class="latex" alt="$(x,y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="40" height="18" /> in the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Coordinate_plane" class="mw-redirect" title="Coordinate plane">coordinate plane</a> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/1/3b1cbc72f1dfb1393826f9228576d89bbc365f6f.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \le x \le \frac{\pi}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="81" height="33" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/e/56efd0c8a0248bf37fbf3c9810a7a827fbd626d5.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \le y \le \frac{\pi}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="80" height="33" />.  What is the area of the subset of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> for which <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/f/dbfe47d3cebb8cec2ec7292989a766db924380a7.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sin^2x-\sin x \sin y + \sin^2y \le \frac34?\]" width="248" height="38" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/2/5a2abb4f0c8241dbaedb73e7a48b1c543d761d6f.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ \dfrac{\pi^2}{9} \qquad \mathrm{(B)}\ \dfrac{\pi^2}{8} \qquad \mathrm{(C)}\ \dfrac{\pi^2}{6} \qquad \mathrm{(D)}\ \dfrac{3\pi^2}{16} \qquad \mathrm{(E)}\ \dfrac{2\pi^2}{9}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="426" height="39" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>We start out by solving the equality first.
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/e/90e429c50ff7d2d430f4f360e6277084f9ab5873.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} \sin^2x - \sin x \sin y + \sin^2y &amp;= \frac34 \\ \sin x &amp;= \frac{\sin y \pm \sqrt{\sin^2 y - 4 ( \sin^2y - \frac34 ) }}{2} \\  &amp;= \frac{\sin y \pm \sqrt{3 - 3 \sin^2 y }}{2} \\  &amp;= \frac{\sin y \pm \sqrt{3 \cos^2 y }}{2} \\  &amp;= \frac12 \sin y \pm \frac{\sqrt3}{2} \cos y \\ \sin x &amp;= \sin (y \pm \frac{\pi}{3}) \end{align*}" width="468" height="279" />
We end up with three lines that matter: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/a/d4a4f05367391c0d8b7012af2c77f31349a0de52.png" class="latex" alt="$x = y + \frac\pi3$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="80" height="33" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/7/e5747262239b6bd63701c90e7da1378d12d3ccbb.png" class="latex" alt="$x = y - \frac\pi3$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="80" height="33" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/8/d48708f4172a69288665d680735d6e6545ec1483.png" class="latex" alt="$x = \pi - (y + \frac\pi3) = \frac{2\pi}{3} - y$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="208" height="37" />.  We plot these lines below.
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/2/0b260cfae48ccfba4d3965e319cf0157f545541d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(5cm); D((0,0)--(3,0)--(3,3)--(0,3)--cycle); D((1,-0.1)--(1,0.1)); D((2,-0.1)--(2,0.1)); D((-0.1,1)--(0.1,1)); D((-0.1,2)--(0.1,2)); D((2,0)--(3,1)--(1,3)--(0,2)); MP(&quot;\frac{\pi}{6}&quot;, (1,0), plain.S); MP(&quot;\frac{\pi}{3}&quot;, (2,0), plain.S); MP(&quot;\frac{\pi}{2}&quot;, (3,0), plain.S); MP(&quot;\frac{\pi}{6}&quot;, (0,1), plain.W); MP(&quot;\frac{\pi}{3}&quot;, (0,2), plain.W); MP(&quot;\frac{\pi}{2}&quot;, (0,3), plain.W); [/asy]" width="228" height="238" />
Note that by testing the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/f/7df738db118b72498bdc1115bf57649acb94e932.png" class="latex" alt="$(\pi/6,\pi/6)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="79" height="18" />, we can see that we want the area of the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Pentagon" title="Pentagon">pentagon</a>.  We can calculate that by calculating the area of the square and then subtracting the area of the 3 triangles. (Note we could also do this by adding the areas of the isosceles triangle in the bottom left corner and the rectangle with the previous triangle's hypotenuse as the longer side.)
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/6/9960974f3e746e6c6a8f09944e77b21d0bb7d877.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} A &amp;= \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)^2 - 2 \cdot \frac12 \cdot \left(\frac{\pi}{6}\right)^2 - \frac12 \cdot \left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right)^2 \\ &amp;= \pi^2 \left ( \frac14 - \frac1{36} - \frac1{18}\right ) \\ &amp;= \pi^2 \left ( \frac{9-1-2}{36} \right ) = \boxed{\text{(C)}\ \frac{\pi^2}{6}} \end{align*}" width="286" height="143" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We can write the given equation as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/2/2725e0e8e00d3e8fb9e55ba36990d56b95d62e75.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sin^3x + \sin^3y \le \frac{3}{4}(\sin x + \sin y).\]" width="255" height="37" /> Note that when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/3/2d348bde3e15456e71734dc2c56fc7425c95927f.png" class="latex" alt="$x = 0$" width="43" height="12" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/8/06831c9585762a83a9bca2dcc3c802d9d8379523.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin y \le \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="89" height="43" /> which implies <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/5/2051f91d701f0870ea9778aade3dacb5af2517fc.png" class="latex" alt="$y \le \frac{\pi}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="51" height="34" />. Similary we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/e/36e0c50b9f10fba2c65d38f55b6d1c97d8c666bc.png" class="latex" alt="$x \le \frac{\pi}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="51" height="34" /> when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/0/7a06334068417b1781d14e8b28af827907961eb3.png" class="latex" alt="$y = 0$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="16" />. Then we see what happens at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/4/d04f02535ba0d4d2e88dd26b9ce36c7a24cd7057.png" class="latex" alt="$x = \frac{\pi}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="48" height="33" />. Clearly at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/4/d04f02535ba0d4d2e88dd26b9ce36c7a24cd7057.png" class="latex" alt="$x = \frac{\pi}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="48" height="33" />, we see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/e/95e474f06f50ff7556c969506eaf6702943934a1.png" class="latex" alt="$y \le \frac{\pi}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="51" height="34" />. By symmetry we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/f/73f70e90a005447dcda477ff61799bd9a211c68e.png" class="latex" alt="$x \le \frac{\pi}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="51" height="34" /> when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/0/5e0f63cc83c328a50cc2c1cc9335d34bf05b8e3f.png" class="latex" alt="$y = \frac{\pi}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="51" height="34" />. So we get a graph like 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/2/0b260cfae48ccfba4d3965e319cf0157f545541d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(5cm); D((0,0)--(3,0)--(3,3)--(0,3)--cycle); D((1,-0.1)--(1,0.1)); D((2,-0.1)--(2,0.1)); D((-0.1,1)--(0.1,1)); D((-0.1,2)--(0.1,2)); D((2,0)--(3,1)--(1,3)--(0,2)); MP(&quot;\frac{\pi}{6}&quot;, (1,0), plain.S); MP(&quot;\frac{\pi}{3}&quot;, (2,0), plain.S); MP(&quot;\frac{\pi}{2}&quot;, (3,0), plain.S); MP(&quot;\frac{\pi}{6}&quot;, (0,1), plain.W); MP(&quot;\frac{\pi}{3}&quot;, (0,2), plain.W); MP(&quot;\frac{\pi}{2}&quot;, (0,3), plain.W); [/asy]" width="228" height="238" />
Thus the area of what we are interested in is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/9/c1914a5b3b2289b34efa941949bce0d35066230a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{\pi^2}{4} - (\frac{\pi^2}{18} + \frac{\pi^2}{36}) = \frac{\pi^2}{6}.\]" width="174" height="39" />
~coolmath_2018
</p>
