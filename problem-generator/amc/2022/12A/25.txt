<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Suppose that with a pair <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/6/1a657e3281a7b500d91685cef510232a9171613f.png" class="latex" alt="$(a_i,b_i)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="49" height="18" /> the circle is an excircle. Then notice that the hypotenuse must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/3/3a389ef89e8b32559b68489793792953deee7e2d.png" class="latex" alt="$(r-x)+(r-y)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="134" height="20" />, so it must be the case that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/8/318006245ea64db3eb143dd0ca030c990251e8bf.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[a_i^2+b_i^2=(2r-a_i-b_i)^2.\]" width="194" height="20" /> Similarly, if with a pair <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/6/1a657e3281a7b500d91685cef510232a9171613f.png" class="latex" alt="$(a_i,b_i)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="49" height="18" /> the circle is an incircle, the hypotenuse must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/7/977bc30ebf806b817d29cee9caf69373153bc921.png" class="latex" alt="$(x-r)+(y-r)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="134" height="20" />, leading to the same equation. 
</p><p>Notice that this equation can be simplified through SFFT to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/c/30cc512e61fcff33dab79b7a9fc93a6933b262d3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(a_i-2r)(b_i-2r)=2r^2.\]" width="188" height="20" /> Thus, we want the smallest <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> such that this equation has at least <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/0/820a20de2847dfdc1bc5cffc8d904933150032bd.png" class="latex" alt="$14$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> distinct pairs <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/6/1a657e3281a7b500d91685cef510232a9171613f.png" class="latex" alt="$(a_i,b_i)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="49" height="18" /> for which this holds. The obvious choice to check is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/1/c811dd13665fdb745e87cd31c76c996740023531.png" class="latex" alt="$r=6$" width="41" height="12" />. In this case, since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/0/4e0bf87f1a0ea05072192c9f243a3355625dd340.png" class="latex" alt="$2r^2=2^3\cdot 3^2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="98" height="17" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> positive factors, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> pairs, and we get another two if the factors are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/4/584cb7bc5ce82ee48c7c9cdb126d115abbc5e19f.png" class="latex" alt="$-8,-9$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="56" height="17" /> or vice versa. One can check that for smaller values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />, we do not even get close to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/0/820a20de2847dfdc1bc5cffc8d904933150032bd.png" class="latex" alt="$14$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> possible pairs.
</p><p>When <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/1/c811dd13665fdb745e87cd31c76c996740023531.png" class="latex" alt="$r=6$" width="41" height="12" />, the smallest possible <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" />-value is clearly when the factors are negative. When this occurs, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/6/896abb2ac640a1e9ef3e333395d1695f71d2e049.png" class="latex" alt="$a_i=4, b_i=3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="105" height="18" /> (or vice versa), so the mimimal <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />. The largest possible <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" />-value occurs when the largest of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/2/412787c048e28774dc63fc27db42dc52ca858de7.png" class="latex" alt="$a_i$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="14" height="10" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/d/94d9565abaadf04609a2e9941aa2d20b0a299b8a.png" class="latex" alt="$b_i$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="12" height="15" /> are maximized. This occurs when the factors are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/d/94db15b430439ad5b3c80bfacfef0c6499ede2ee.png" class="latex" alt="$72$" width="17" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, leading to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/1/df1facf2d5979419d072771f1127f6a8f6c4b457.png" class="latex" alt="$a_i=84, b_i=13$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="123" height="18" /> (or vice-versa), leading to a maximal <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/e/7fea07b9bf739f8e420081f31186264b91ced2ce.png" class="latex" alt="$85$" width="17" height="12" />.
</p><p>Hence the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/5/535e01342d0f396b515529d8eb1c0ba6050eecd7.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{85}5=\boxed{17}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="78" height="38" />.
</p><p>~ bluelinfish

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Case 1: The tangent and the origin are on the opposite sides of the circle.
</p><p>In this case, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/8/078dd5bb8c0e86fe4556d378267fd4d635d351d2.png" class="latex" alt="$a, b &gt; 2r$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="69" height="18" />.
</p><p>We can easily prove that
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/d/1fd111d5ba78eef050e31cde44f28f35ba23e9a2.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[a + b - 2 r = c . \hspace{1cm} (1)\]" width="185" height="18" />
</p><p>Recall that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/5/58574d57431aa17960974d106d330fcad5a9ab73.png" class="latex" alt="$c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="102" height="18" />.
</p><p>Taking square of (1) and reorganizing all terms, (1) is converted as
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/9/4699309d43b90942323ba0269bd790f972b86a5b.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\left( a - 2 r \right) \left( b - 2 r \right) = 2 r^2 .\]" width="180" height="20" />
</p><p><br />
Case 2: The tangent and the origin are on the opposite sides of the circle.
</p><p>In this case, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/d/25db794e67d1c99f2f9af980c2c8b937733c0836.png" class="latex" alt="$0 &lt; a, b &lt; r$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="94" height="18" />.
</p><p>We can easily prove that
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/a/f8a717cfe7d761ff318a5957450c67646c80c742.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[2 r - a - b  = c . \hspace{1cm} (2)\]" width="185" height="18" />
</p><p>Recall that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/5/58574d57431aa17960974d106d330fcad5a9ab73.png" class="latex" alt="$c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="102" height="18" />.
</p><p>Taking square of (2) and reorganizing all terms, (2) is converted as
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/9/4699309d43b90942323ba0269bd790f972b86a5b.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\left( a - 2 r \right) \left( b - 2 r \right) = 2 r^2 .\]" width="180" height="20" />
</p><p>Putting both cases together, for given <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />, we look for solutions of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" /> satisfying
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/7/867b085f01b83cf42506a0425296919315bafa5e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\left( a - 2 r \right) \left( b - 2 r \right) = 2 r^2 ,\]" width="180" height="20" />
with either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/8/078dd5bb8c0e86fe4556d378267fd4d635d351d2.png" class="latex" alt="$a, b &gt; 2r$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="69" height="18" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/d/25db794e67d1c99f2f9af980c2c8b937733c0836.png" class="latex" alt="$0 &lt; a, b &lt; r$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="94" height="18" />.
</p><p>Now, we need to find the smallest <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />, such that the number of feasible solutions of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/e/28e24c43456d95b5ab8d4f2f8f85a644e98b7ed3.png" class="latex" alt="$(a, b)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> is at least 14.
</p><p>For equation
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/a/fba44e90132e7cb9702e53b47aa80b6650442bfa.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[uv = 2 r^2 ,\]" width="73" height="19" />
we observe that the R.H.S. is a not a perfect square. Thus, the number of positive <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/1/e4162d79742879fcd196374e6e48a3f896087872.png" class="latex" alt="$(u, v)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="40" height="18" /> is equal to the number of positive divisors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/c/5ac6d739dbf9ebf10333dea593c8c26ebd07b5ce.png" class="latex" alt="$2 r^2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="27" height="17" />.
</p><p>Second, for each feasible positive solution <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/1/e4162d79742879fcd196374e6e48a3f896087872.png" class="latex" alt="$(u, v)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="40" height="18" />, its opposite <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/9/f49c3b87967233e2c8116c13a514b3fa500c74af.png" class="latex" alt="$(-u, -v)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="72" height="20" /> is also a solution. However, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/7/0d7cc0aaa3c34fd4a50a506fb22de40dfff3ed09.png" class="latex" alt="$(u,v)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="40" height="18" /> corresponds to a feasible solution if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/e/28e24c43456d95b5ab8d4f2f8f85a644e98b7ed3.png" class="latex" alt="$(a, b)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/f/29ff580c5792d732c7ebf7c0c04b136cf521fb1f.png" class="latex" alt="$a = u + 2r$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="86" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/7/077ba1098c11b4f7e89e59979c1fce00b021f44c.png" class="latex" alt="$b = v + 2r$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="83" height="16" />, but <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/9/f49c3b87967233e2c8116c13a514b3fa500c74af.png" class="latex" alt="$(-u, -v)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="72" height="20" /> may not lead to a feasible solution if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/e/28e24c43456d95b5ab8d4f2f8f85a644e98b7ed3.png" class="latex" alt="$(a, b)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" /> with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/b/52b82e9118d220dc242a88e2127ea4a4fe884be6.png" class="latex" alt="$a = 2 r - u$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="86" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/3/f03505dfdc50e82166d866bd7da10ef727e1219c.png" class="latex" alt="$b = 2 r - v$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="83" height="16" />.
</p><p>Recall that we are looking for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> that leads to at least 14 solutions.
Therefore, the above observations imply that we must have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />, such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/c/5ac6d739dbf9ebf10333dea593c8c26ebd07b5ce.png" class="latex" alt="$2 r^2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="27" height="17" /> has least 7 positive divisors.
</p><p>Following this guidance, we find the smallest <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> is 6. This leads to the following solutions:
