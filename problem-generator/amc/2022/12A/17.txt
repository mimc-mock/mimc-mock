<p>Suppose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is a real number such that the equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/1/6514e5c2f1b5f00055a46e13a0f7bc3a8b3ed885.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[a\cdot(\sin{x}+\sin{(2x)}) = \sin{(3x)}\]" width="235" height="18" />

The set of all such <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> that can be written
in the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/3/753fb68fe13dda58b70a340f28da137c751bdbf9.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(p,q) \cup (q,r),\]" width="102" height="18" />

What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/8/d08ff3681d6992f69ee41e21c7237c89f970ac49.png" class="latex" alt="$p+q+r$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="71" height="14" />?

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We are given that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/5/4a5b1fb3aa6b19bbef2964c332a42de9855fd799.png" class="latex" alt="$a\cdot(\sin{x}+\sin{(2x)})=\sin{(3x)}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="239" height="20" />
</p><p>Using the sine double angle formula combine with the fact that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/7/aa778bf978dda1a3ddfa849122dd75336d117067.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{(3x)} = \sin{x}\cdot(4\cos^2{x}-1)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="237" height="21" />, which can be derived using sine angle addition with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/b/b2b9ff9ae3c91102bcee5e2426fe9702a69b1011.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{(2x + x)}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="93" height="20" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/7/e379cc785115c8b79d1899b7bc57e218f2ef0932.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[a\cdot(\sin{x}+2\sin{x}\cos{x})=\sin{x}\cdot(4\cos^2{x}-1)\]" width="357" height="20" />
Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/6/64656f53977b7d71e661ab661ef9ac0e97c256c1.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{x} \ne 0$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="71" height="18" /> as it is on the open interval <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/b/9dbd13d255bcea5d73d3fb36e7f92ebc9d314db8.png" class="latex" alt="$(0, \pi)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="41" height="18" />, we can divide out <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/1/f41470508df2ceb87f97c7e939589024bceb16e1.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{x}$" width="35" height="12" /> from both sides, leaving us with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/1/a712a8ab5fa9370a99cd9d581353ccd2ca48c440.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[a\cdot(1+2\cos{x})=4\cos^2{x}-1\]" width="229" height="20" />
Now, distributing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> and rearranging, we achieve the equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/1/311abaac7ad0642361a2b90158b48751bb6f4354.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[4\cos^2{x} - 2a\cos{x} - (1+a) = 0\]" width="248" height="20" /> which is a quadratic in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/9/3c9cfae39cb378f17672726fb1de7705b94a6da4.png" class="latex" alt="$\cos{x}$" width="37" height="8" />.
</p><p>Applying the quadratic formula to solve for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/9/3c9cfae39cb378f17672726fb1de7705b94a6da4.png" class="latex" alt="$\cos{x}$" width="37" height="8" />, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/2/a728e0aa90abca620fbe1fd5bca1bf9328fba5b5.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\cos{x} =\frac{2a\pm\sqrt{4a^2+4(4)(1+a)}}{8}\]" width="258" height="41" /> and expanding the terms under the radical, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/6/5f6c28419e76045f056262025047e2d585b3fa10.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\cos{x} =\frac{2a\pm\sqrt{4a^2+16a+16}}{8}\]" width="236" height="40" />
Factoring, since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/e/6eec13d402177894819e25e5a528465ec659baae.png" class="latex" alt="$4a^2+16a+16 = (2a+4)^2$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="214" height="21" />, we can simplify our expression even further to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/0/2701df7aaeae25ad5b4ca77333c8fca618b3044d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\cos{x} =\frac{a\pm(a+2)}{4}\]" width="150" height="38" />
</p><p>Now, solving for our two solutions,  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/e/30e5fbdf2baef6adfb7afc2bad37264c14f25e49.png" class="latex" alt="$\cos{x} = -\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="91" height="38" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/6/96699243fd5810906ec792f6c4fa32d62748a0ab.png" class="latex" alt="$\cos{x} = \frac{a+1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="109" height="38" />.
</p><p>Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/e/30e5fbdf2baef6adfb7afc2bad37264c14f25e49.png" class="latex" alt="$\cos{x} = -\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="91" height="38" /> yields a solution that is valid for all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" />, that being <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/f/45f2fd965805ffdf82d87289feebfe7a066b91ef.png" class="latex" alt="$x = \frac{2\pi}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="61" height="38" />, we must now solve for the case where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/4/97439a3cb35d6366b2b48331bc36d5bb25cf1839.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a+1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="43" height="37" /> yields a valid value.
</p><p>As <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/0/87019fbc3cc63480a5ba09a7f5f849f631de7a73.png" class="latex" alt="$x\in (0, \pi)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="77" height="20" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/d/37d044671dff71ee797e0c0b69fffa5e85453455.png" class="latex" alt="$\cos{x}\in (-1, 1)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="116" height="20" />, and therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/0/920406c5b015e2cf0f34d360f0dbd34f4e2fb7a0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a+1}{2}\in (-1, 1)$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="124" height="38" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/b/4db6eb5b2af6d802bdd358693f1620d5dbaf21c6.png" class="latex" alt="$a\in(-3,1)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="88" height="20" />.
</p><p>There is one more case we must consider inside this interval though, the case where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/7/62786145b090ff4897077ebea937cc54535b1e10.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a+1}{2} = -\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="98" height="38" />, as this would lead to a double root for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/9/3c9cfae39cb378f17672726fb1de7705b94a6da4.png" class="latex" alt="$\cos{x}$" width="37" height="8" />, yielding only one valid solution for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />. Solving for this case, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/c/66ce17754e2fee6f9e17faae5aee1c59462933e0.png" class="latex" alt="$a \ne -2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="59" height="18" />.
</p><p>Therefore, combining this fact with our solution interval, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/0/420a07b9562ef0dd0bbf38bb005ac558e583b7b6.png" class="latex" alt="$a\in(-3, -2) \cup (-2, 1)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="177" height="20" />, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/5/3556324278fb89ba520dd02e3d8f119466a31583.png" class="latex" alt="$-3-2+1 = \boxed{\textbf{(A) } {-}4}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="182" height="30" />.
</p><p>- DavidHovey

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Use the sum to product formula to obtain <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/e/62e494457fe877055e73395e3ad3d8bed4f77dd3.png" class="latex" alt="$2a\cdot\sin{\frac{3x}{2}}\cos{\frac{x}{2}}=\sin{3x}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="196" height="38" />. Use the double angle formula on the RHS to obtain <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/a/c9ae3c7d91e9f6c873d329a430b62d7d7c8f09fe.png" class="latex" alt="$a\cdot\sin{\frac{3x}{2}}\cos{\frac{x}{2}}=\sin{\frac{3x}{2}}\cos{\frac{3x}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="245" height="38" />. From here, it is obvious that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/c/85c0a0bc6cbf65c1a64b7c907716f335ed011177.png" class="latex" alt="$x=\frac{2\pi}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="57" height="37" /> is always a solution, and thus we divide by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/7/b37b477a22360e3d81415de63fbd1e28353318e6.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{\frac{3x}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="51" height="38" /> to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/a/8ea7482705923e3202b5786d40921d69629bf9be.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[a\cdot\cos{\frac{x}{2}}=\cos{\frac{3x}{2}}\]" width="137" height="37" /> We wish to find all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> such that there is at least one more solution to this equation distinct from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/c/85c0a0bc6cbf65c1a64b7c907716f335ed011177.png" class="latex" alt="$x=\frac{2\pi}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="57" height="37" />. Letting <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/a/01adb5172afbd0de886d647cc9b76c6efa91603f.png" class="latex" alt="$y=\cos{\frac{x}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="77" height="34" />, and noting that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/f/69f8b29010d2f528d42ea916f22571854cecf2db.png" class="latex" alt="$\cos{\frac{3x}{2}}=4y^3-3y$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="144" height="38" />, we can rearrange our equation to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/4/50454ab236bccee705b509e92e45a68c8dca2165.png" class="latex" alt="$4y^3=y(3+a)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="117" height="21" /> The smallest value <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/8/ec89f09c84538ec43930ca5d8e5e0957e2000822.png" class="latex" alt="$y=0$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="16" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/c/f2ca003a7da0de4994b4733e203b74ff52d42553.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi$" width="10" height="8" />, which is not in our domain so we divide by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> to obtain <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/0/bb0125b0585cc06668085b2a70ec65a3ca7fc75d.png" class="latex" alt="$4y^2=a+3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="93" height="20" />. By the trivial inequality, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/6/756a604aaf08c6d481f2eeda2bea66891068f5ba.png" class="latex" alt="$a+3\ge{0}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="76" height="16" />. Furthermore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/f/0afd84fc1e7b2b81858563631a3348cc7bba8ae1.png" class="latex" alt="$y\neq{0}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="42" height="17" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/f/1ffd1cbd4c86c6bf2393821c720c80b0c8558048.png" class="latex" alt="$a+3&gt;0$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="76" height="15" />. Also, if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/a/caa5b61af3eb613f029161d897e1a7bc1e301ebd.png" class="latex" alt="$a=-2$" width="56" height="12" />, then the solution to this equation would be shared with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/c/85c0a0bc6cbf65c1a64b7c907716f335ed011177.png" class="latex" alt="$x=\frac{2\pi}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="57" height="37" />, so there would only be one distinct solution. Finally, because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/b/22b5ccd436d776b90a1db791d3bb0385da3788cc.png" class="latex" alt="$y\le{1}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="15" /> due to the restrictions of a sine wave, and that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/7/4a70d77c0aead032f14411a8299629f7617f660a.png" class="latex" alt="$y\neq{1}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="45" height="18" /> due to the restrictions on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/6/d46bb693c803a9d34a8776528416ccf61f861408.png" class="latex" alt="$-3&lt;a&lt;1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="92" height="15" /> with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/2/2328c544dc1de84530a5c172cac796c9cb549355.png" class="latex" alt="$a\neq{-2}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="59" height="18" />. Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/b/f3bc147ceb82295aedfdd7cbf1e2d72a78d27b3a.png" class="latex" alt="$p=-3,q=-2, r=1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="173" height="17" />, so our final answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/c/eec52603a9da72c2338614c90c60664c7a5dbaca.png" class="latex" alt="$-3+(-2)+1=\boxed{\textbf{(A) } {-}4}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="210" height="30" />. 
</p><p>~sigma
</p>
