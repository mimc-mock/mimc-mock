<p>Regular hexagon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/2/8029902caf855d940e465d04ff40101d8850c3e0.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCDEF$" width="86" height="13" /> has side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/2/6e28ce12d49d39f160d5a0ef54077fc98e4b9d2b.png" class="latex" alt="$G$" width="14" height="12" /> be the midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" />, and let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/9/b1902d279ba37d60bdce4e0e987b7cd19d48974e.png" class="latex" alt="$H$" width="16" height="12" /> be the midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/9/699784c989c3db382520e6f92819e062d239f504.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{DE}$" width="30" height="15" />. What is the perimeter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/6/426be5d42d4faf0c0d1877bd9dcc71b7b0c536ce.png" class="latex" alt="$GCHF$" width="59" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/5/4d52243a3b6fdaf37278ba2aa723b9637e13b6bb.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 4\sqrt3 \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 8 \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 4\sqrt5 \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 4\sqrt7 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 12$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="433" height="23" />
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Let the center of the hexagon be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/4/e44c01c0ed926b86414a0fbedc2fe1d2eaa9b871.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle AOB$" width="58" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/7/41752e67cd033c961edd7532c9376bc82296d255.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BOC$" width="59" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/4/8f44214e5c66155bbcf0dd1b466ad394037eb30f.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle COD$" width="60" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/b/fbb66762ba207e5ab9f45a04d174092eed82b212.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DOE$" width="60" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/9/899899a2f10f1ac00c700d284d6530bd71dbac89.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle EOF$" width="59" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/f/3/6f365881ce358dd7812173466fe07b105d424a3a.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle FOA$" width="58" height="13" /> are all equilateral triangles with side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />. Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/7/7d701d0cd3d4d04f9be1f18d493263287024d4f1.png" class="latex" alt="$CO = 2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="64" height="14" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/f/ebf4e5f7b583d454ae56c62da792c5afe42d1d1b.png" class="latex" alt="$GO = \sqrt{AO^2 - AG^2} = \sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="211" height="21" />. By symmetry, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/f/60f8b05aee8cd78734973952b489d44747182915.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle COG = 90^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="108" height="15" />. Thus, by the Pythagorean theorem, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/c/fbcfbc6493bb1b840e6ae7f1e90b2d2b0566be2d.png" class="latex" alt="$CG = \sqrt{2^2 + \sqrt{3}^2} = \sqrt{7}$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="191" height="35" />. Because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/e/01eb3e922a21ecfc84b21a9c4f8331f37b64a53e.png" class="latex" alt="$CO = OF$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="83" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/a/37ad82bc0e26dc2378bdbf8be954a9579a70aa26.png" class="latex" alt="$GO = OH$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="86" height="14" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/2/e2215ceb308ae9543466006d205cb7176e12d5d3.png" class="latex" alt="$CG = HC = FH = GF = \sqrt{7}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="242" height="21" />. Thus, the solution to our problem is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/d/cfdcfe7c76e52d784f0cc4fa16f15d8aa41fd7bc.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{7} + \sqrt{7} + \sqrt{7} + \sqrt{7} = \boxed{\textbf{(D)}\ 4\sqrt7}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="269" height="35" />.
</p><p>~mathboy100
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Consider triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/0/8600e69a900b0105eb63e8712ffca58544004c00.png" class="latex" alt="$AFG$" width="42" height="13" />. Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/e/4debd7b74e3a5cb6a67f3f13da34422fa2c9132a.png" class="latex" alt="$AF = 2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="63" height="14" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf3240290de505b33df678155447f52a76c92e3.png" class="latex" alt="$AG = 1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="63" height="14" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/4/904f5214ae2f2a0f7ca4873c1105b5d2755d5e27.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle GAF = 120 ^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="116" height="15" /> because it is an interior angle of a regular hexagon. (See note for details.) 
</p><p>By the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Law_of_Cosines" title="Law of Cosines">Law of Cosines</a>, we have: 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/5/6353766afb2e79fa99db4fa4d31b7e86324f6be5.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} FG^2 &amp;= AG^2 + AF^2 - 2 \cdot AG \cdot AF \cdot \cos \angle GAF \\ FG^2 &amp;= 1^2 + 2^2 - 2 \cdot 1 \cdot 2 \cdot \cos 120 ^{\circ} \\ FG^2 &amp;= 5 + 4 \cdot \left( \frac 12 \right) \\ FG^2 &amp;= 7 \\ FG &amp;= \sqrt 7. \end{align*}" width="361" height="151" />
By <a href="/wiki/index.php/SAS_Congruence" class="mw-redirect" title="SAS Congruence">SAS Congruence</a>, triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/0/8600e69a900b0105eb63e8712ffca58544004c00.png" class="latex" alt="$AFG$" width="42" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/f/9bfb5ceb2d5b4b942fdff979d643672f8f3f07a3.png" class="latex" alt="$BCG$" width="43" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/7/fd78c090cec1b246eed7f6bfacc30bef0e349e3b.png" class="latex" alt="$CDH$" width="46" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/f/4df8099148338e39565d6aa814f9749b9dc56782.png" class="latex" alt="$EFH$" width="45" height="12" /> are congruent, and by <a href="/wiki/index.php/CPCTC" title="CPCTC">CPCTC</a>, quadrilateral <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/6/426be5d42d4faf0c0d1877bd9dcc71b7b0c536ce.png" class="latex" alt="$GCHF$" width="59" height="12" /> is a rhombus. Therefore, the perimeter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/6/426be5d42d4faf0c0d1877bd9dcc71b7b0c536ce.png" class="latex" alt="$GCHF$" width="59" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/6/e56711b75ea81ec62e7001fec3b67a232d12f599.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \cdot FG = \boxed{\textbf{(D)}\ 4\sqrt7}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="156" height="35" />.
</p><p>Note: The sum of the interior angles of any polygon with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> sides is given by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/4/0542335cc70e3f33841b1bd0f21d1b266fb4e4f1.png" class="latex" alt="$180 ^{\circ} (n - 2)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="93" height="20" />. Therefore, the sum of the interior angles of a hexagon is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/8/c48b7794d930fdf430908c3efd68b28675d2bafa.png" class="latex" alt="$720 ^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="37" height="15" />, and each interior angle of a regular hexagon measures <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/6/b56d363ed0bad6f688bb472c21c0b14b65599111.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{720 ^{\circ}}{6} = 120 ^{\circ}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="100" height="39" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>We use a coordinates approach. Letting the origin be the center of the hexagon, we can let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/0/3e084682e4b01eeb80515403327e645fc95a7b44.png" class="latex" alt="$A = (-1, \sqrt{3}), B = (1, \sqrt{3}), C = (2, 0), D = (1, -\sqrt{3}), E = (-1, -\sqrt{3}), F = (-2, 0).$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="653" height="24" /> Then, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/3/24397a3582fe3332d26758d810d613a1f6ceec10.png" class="latex" alt="$G = (0, \sqrt{3})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="96" height="24" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/6/d662d23f96916d3e6ccaa3da10ea13a6c523a335.png" class="latex" alt="$H = (0, -\sqrt{3}).$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="117" height="24" />
</p><p>We use the distance formula four times to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/0/41002537e64f81eda39f3d87cfbfe27c2ff87c86.png" class="latex" alt="$CH, HF, FG, \text{ and } GC.$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="184" height="18" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/1/e11d24dcb38768c27229fdd784189196d87330a0.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{alignat*}{8} CH^2 &amp;= (2-0)^2 + (0-(-\sqrt{3}))^2 &amp;&amp;= 7 &amp;&amp;\rightarrow CH &amp;&amp;= \sqrt{7}, \\ HF^2 &amp;= (0-(-2))^2 + (-\sqrt{3}-0)^2 &amp;&amp;= 7 &amp;&amp;\rightarrow HF &amp;&amp;= \sqrt{7}, \\ FG^2 &amp;= (-2-0)^2 + (0-\sqrt{3})^2 &amp;&amp;= 7 &amp;&amp;\rightarrow FG &amp;&amp;= \sqrt{7}, \\ GC^2 &amp;= (0-2)^2 + (\sqrt{3}-0)^2 &amp;&amp;= 7 &amp;&amp;\rightarrow GC &amp;&amp;= \sqrt{7}. \end{alignat*}" width="411" height="111" />
Thus, the perimeter of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/e/90ee35c9715850abb6ee17ff0a36e8fcf9d0ddb1.png" class="latex" alt="$GCHF = CH + HF + FG + GC = \sqrt{7} + \sqrt{7} + \sqrt{7} + \sqrt{7} = \boxed{\textbf{(D)}\ 4\sqrt7}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="563" height="35" />.
</p><p>~sirswagger21
</p><p>Note: the last part of this solution could have been simplified by noting that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/a/fea2a38ef000e7cd6594958c81f2d889f7ee51bd.png" class="latex" alt="$CH = HF = FG = GC = \sqrt{7}.$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="247" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>Note that triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/7/c171142fadbd15b2d28e52a086258ddc5f6bd58a.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{GAF}, \triangle{FEH}, \triangle{HDC},$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="205" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/2/2426760c321e3aa0c024f89345c89ff01fe0606c.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{CBG}$" width="59" height="13" /> are all congruent, since they have side lengths of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> and an included angle of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/1/e91d2ef1d21375fc376ebbc6dda11f702fc15cbd.png" class="latex" alt="$120^{\circ}.$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="38" height="13" /> 
</p><p>By the Law of Cosines, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/c/38cd720ea801002aa7f9dc4bea43621581c1e30b.png" class="latex" alt="$FG=\sqrt{1^2+2^2-2\cdot{1}\cdot{-\frac{1}{2}}}=\sqrt{7}.$" style="vertical-align: -15px" width="275" height="45" />
</p><p>Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/b/c6b757a7d7fd6a4a9680de253db3487acd20b47c.png" class="latex" alt="$FG+GC+CH+HF=4\cdot{FG}=\boxed{\textbf{(D)}\ 4\sqrt7}.$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="371" height="35" />
</p><p>-Benedict T (countmath1)
</p>
