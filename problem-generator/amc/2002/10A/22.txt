<p>A set of tiles numbered 1 through 100 is modified repeatedly by the following operation: remove all tiles numbered with a <a href="/wiki/index.php/Perfect_square" title="Perfect square">perfect square</a>, and renumber the remaining tiles consecutively starting with 1. How many times must the operation be performed to reduce the number of tiles in the set to one?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/4/bf4da4caa41a981cf84d5b220a56f56fb5cb62a4.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{(A)}\ 10 \qquad \text{(B)}\ 11 \qquad \text{(C)}\ 18 \qquad \text{(D)}\ 19 \qquad \text{(E)}\ 20$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="375" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The pattern is quite simple to see after listing a couple of terms.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/0/ed01d0466fe814594e0fe01d63f42273ea143554.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\begin{tabular}{|r|r|r|} \hline \#&amp;\text{Removed}&amp;\text{Left}\\ \hline 1&amp;10&amp;90\\ 2&amp;9&amp;81\\ 3&amp;9&amp;72\\ 4&amp;8&amp;64\\ 5&amp;8&amp;56\\ 6&amp;7&amp;49\\ 7&amp;7&amp;42\\ 8&amp;6&amp;36\\ 9&amp;6&amp;30\\ 10&amp;5&amp;25\\ 11&amp;5&amp;20\\ 12&amp;4&amp;16\\ 13&amp;4&amp;12\\ 14&amp;3&amp;9\\ 15&amp;3&amp;6\\ 16&amp;2&amp;4\\ 17&amp;2&amp;2\\ 18&amp;1&amp;1\\ \hline \end{tabular}\]" width="173" height="381" />
</p><p>Thus, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/7/1f731aad5c1cdc3c3b70667b3e1285045107cab2.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\text{(C) } 18}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="60" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Given <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/0/6006bba637eef443381fbaff768fa62d9ff41e62.png" class="latex" alt="$n^2$" width="17" height="15" /> tiles, a step removes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> tiles, leaving <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/5/0a5dcc157a1564c55ef9cb3c70f05b8473bf1a09.png" class="latex" alt="$n^2 - n$" width="51" height="15" /> tiles behind. Now, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/0/e/c0ee08b0432bf992cd453e23ca55276b626b6a36.png" class="latex" alt="$(n-1)^2 = n^2 - n + (1-n) &lt; n^2 - n &lt; n^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="335" height="19" />, so in the next step <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/d/4bdca93962a2beaffc172ca394e04fe05bd74ffd.png" class="latex" alt="$n-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="41" height="12" /> tiles are removed. This gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/1/aa173ed67884b1744df1fea61ab1be271f51a1ea.png" class="latex" alt="$(n^2 - n) - (n-1) = n^2 - 2n + 1 = (n-1)^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="347" height="19" />, another perfect square.
</p><p>Thus each two steps we cycle down a perfect square, and in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/7/177bc2ae20bc3e3c012b07e02584b35e319812e8.png" class="latex" alt="$(10-1)\times 2 = 18$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="136" height="18" /> steps, we are left with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> tile, hence our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/7/1f731aad5c1cdc3c3b70667b3e1285045107cab2.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\text{(C) } 18}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="60" height="28" />.
</p>
