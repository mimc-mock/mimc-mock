<p>Using the digits 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, and 9, form 4 two-digit prime numbers, using each digit only once. What is the sum of the 4 prime numbers?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/4/5441ea7a787a81c3a783a0bbf51eed4810128ccd.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{(A)}\ 150 \qquad \text{(B)}\ 160 \qquad \text{(C)}\ 170 \qquad \text{(D)}\ 180 \qquad \text{(E)}\ 190$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="421" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Since a multiple-digit prime number is not divisible by either 2 or 5, it must end with 1, 3, 7, or 9 in the units place. The remaining digits given must therefore appear in the tens place. Hence our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/2/442c2b582db23db64c3ba70201cee8376e5eb3c6.png" class="latex" alt="$20 + 40 + 50 + 60 + 1 + 3 + 7 + 9 = 190\Rightarrow\boxed{(E)= 190}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="436" height="29" />.
</p><p>(Note that we did not need to actually construct the primes. If we had to, one way to match the tens and ones digits to form four primes is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/1/9a1b2497e7b125cd721bab4c6e2385f954fe35cb.png" class="latex" alt="$23$" width="17" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/a/3ba6cdde56f366d628a7a35826f98523dbe58bd3.png" class="latex" alt="$41$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/e/afe61e5b80572ad5a200a0d9cd354ce12a8c7800.png" class="latex" alt="$59$" width="17" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/a/7fa7247f4d29d95459cbf6f238007a1b1090ebea.png" class="latex" alt="$67$" width="18" height="12" />.)
</p><p>(You could also guess the numbers, if you have a lot of spare time.)
</p>
