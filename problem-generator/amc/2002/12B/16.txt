<p>Juan rolls a fair regular <a href="/wiki/index.php/Octahedron" title="Octahedron">octahedral</a> die marked with the numbers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" />. Then Amal rolls a fair six-sided die. What is the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Probability" title="Probability">probability</a> that the product of the two rolls is a multiple of 3?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/f/25f84d0e7d4e45e4a61bae273451ffbdc314986f.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ \frac 1{12} \qquad\mathrm{(B)}\ \frac 13 \qquad\mathrm{(C)}\ \frac 12 \qquad\mathrm{(D)}\ \frac 7{12} \qquad\mathrm{(E)}\ \frac 23$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="379" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>On both dice, only the faces with the numbers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/e/22e504db36a22a9193be7ecfb4b10e983cfaf430.png" class="latex" alt="$3,6$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="25" height="16" /> are divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/6/e669ff927770c85216d692bbeb0ccac905319e4a.png" class="latex" alt="$P(a) = \frac{2}{8} = \frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="111" height="37" /> be the probability that Juan rolls a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> or a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/d/85d51719dedbc8953f9d633e0af22d6447933cee.png" class="latex" alt="$P(b) = \frac{2}{6} = \frac 13$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="109" height="37" /> that Amal does. By the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Principle_of_Inclusion-Exclusion" title="Principle of Inclusion-Exclusion">Principle of Inclusion-Exclusion</a>,
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/2/cc29f83d1e2747949dccdb55b4eb258c113092a4.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[P(a \cup b) = P(a) + P(b) - P(a \cap b) = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{2} \Rightarrow \mathrm{(C)}\]" width="477" height="37" />
</p><p>Alternatively, the probability that Juan rolls a multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c339afa03e060d959e4942be7522c0c992f4c41c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="11" height="37" />, and the probability that Juan does not roll a multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> but Amal does is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/4/384517d227788002bd7523cc924210830ad80bd5.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(1 - \frac{1}{4}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="132" height="43" />. Thus the total probability is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/8/798406b53d9f5a5f0c9f90bb1af7ade2885afd2e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac 14 + \frac 14 = \frac 12$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="84" height="37" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>The probability that neither Juan nor Amal rolls a multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/c/74cddeee72381b5d24db69b6520f8dc8947c09b2.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{6}{8} \cdot \frac{4}{6} = \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="75" height="37" />; using <a href="/wiki/index.php/Complementary_counting" title="Complementary counting">complementary counting</a>, the probability that at least one does is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/8/c683ad28a371dd556e427f7b9b00190c4a3abd5d.png" class="latex" alt="$1 - \frac 12 = \frac 12 \Rightarrow \mathrm{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="135" height="37" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h3>
<p>The product will be a multiple of 3 if and only if at least one of the two rolls is a 3 or a 6. The probability that Juan rolls 3 or 6 is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/e/cee1990d926d2ac8950bc3ef422b7da6568fd6e5.png" class="latex" alt="$2/8 = 1/4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="78" height="18" />. The probability that Juan does not roll 3 or 6, but Amal does is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/0/c60a5d15fc59608720165d5b583a413365654cb4.png" class="latex" alt="$(3/4) (1/3) = 1/4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="134" height="18" />. Thus, the probability that the product of the rolls is a multiple of 3 is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/2/bc2b91cd826408c90c3ae943e632a2dea937cb89.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{4} = \boxed{\frac{1}{2}}.\]" width="98" height="48" />
~aopsav (Credit to AoPS Alcumus)
</p>
