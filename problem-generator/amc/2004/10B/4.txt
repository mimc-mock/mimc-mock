<p>A standard six-sided die is rolled, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> is the product of the five numbers that are visible. What is the largest number that is certain to divide <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/b/b5b218575044061478d850f37ac81366391fc210.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A) \ } 6 \qquad \mathrm{(B) \ } 12 \qquad \mathrm{(C) \ } 24 \qquad \mathrm{(D) \ } 144\qquad \mathrm{(E) \ } 720$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="396" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The product of all six numbers is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/2/95246be1744710288c10397eb46a97bb6778e774.png" class="latex" alt="$6!=720$" width="65" height="13" />. The products of numbers that can be visible are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/e/cce832c5bb1f768629b08255a609f0e4aecc4126.png" class="latex" alt="$720/1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="44" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/0/880c0706e5e6e38885b4cce442b86996486caa20.png" class="latex" alt="$720/2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="44" height="18" />, ..., <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/4/394797d8ec4593a79d0f2a91326af830af069d7c.png" class="latex" alt="$720/6$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="44" height="18" />.
The answer to this problem is their greatest common divisor -- which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/6/9f6eb4da81c9ca1a5955be01bef537224c654707.png" class="latex" alt="$720/L$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="48" height="18" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/9/859ccf4cd60c7bc6b8fa1afc9a42dc811a826d6f.png" class="latex" alt="$L$" width="12" height="12" /> is the least common multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/5/615346eb2f9dc275d58727bfdb39c34676691128.png" class="latex" alt="$\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="112" height="18" />.
Clearly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/3/ee3efa4cac62865ee0cf82e99f8492f6a6a61b40.png" class="latex" alt="$L=60$" width="54" height="12" /> and the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/b/8db8c59ffa3384f78872ba96c50d817aa09adc4c.png" class="latex" alt="$720/60=\boxed{\mathrm{(B)}\ 12}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="141" height="29" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Clearly, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> cannot have a prime factor other than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />.
</p><p>We can not guarantee that the product will be divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, as the number <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> can end on the bottom.
</p><p>We can guarantee that the product will be divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> (one of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> will always be visible), but not by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/9/819068968ed60608a91d0c3921d6296964660e9d.png" class="latex" alt="$3^2$" width="15" height="15" />.
</p><p>Finally, there are three even numbers, hence two of them are always visible and thus the product is divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/a/32a35631e1e126934ead6d2af161e9d250827bbb.png" class="latex" alt="$2^2$" width="15" height="15" />. This is the most we can guarantee, as when the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> is on the bottom side, the two visible even numbers are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />, and their product is not divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/6/3f6a4ab443c94f13ae3ad0efbc8c6fa07b58a930.png" class="latex" alt="$2^3$" width="15" height="15" />.
</p><p>Hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/2/a1247cf60a02e1285f99d1f50e33874031ff4bd4.png" class="latex" alt="$P=3\cdot2^2=\boxed{\mathrm{(B)}\ 12}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="164" height="29" />.
</p>
