<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The area of the small circle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/c/f2ca003a7da0de4994b4733e203b74ff52d42553.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi$" width="10" height="8" />. We can add it to the shaded region, compute the area of the new region, and then subtract the area of the small circle from the result.
</p><p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> be the intersections of the two large circles. Connect them to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> to get the picture below:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/9/e490ff773fb3216ee3ebc43d93ebd8933c748ded.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(1.5cm); defaultpen(0.8);  pair O=(0,0), A=(0,1), B=(0,-1); path bigc1 = Circle(A,2), bigc2 = Circle(B,2), smallc = Circle(O,1);  pair[] P = intersectionpoints(bigc1, bigc2); filldraw( arc(A,P[0],P[1])--arc(B,P[1],P[0])--cycle, lightgray, black ); draw(bigc1); draw(bigc2);  dot(A); dot(B); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,N); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,S); /* dot(O);  unfill(smallc); draw(smallc);  draw( O--dir(30) ); draw( A--(A+2*dir(30)) ); draw( B--(B+2*dir(210)) );  label(&quot;$1$&quot;, O--dir(30), N ); label(&quot;$2$&quot;, A--(A+2*dir(30)), N ); label(&quot;$2$&quot;, B--(B+2*dir(210)), S ); */  label(&quot;$C$&quot;,P[0],W); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,P[1],E); draw( P[0]--A--P[1] ); draw( P[0]--B--P[1] ); draw( A--B ); [/asy]" width="295" height="428" />
</p><p>We can see that the triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/5/f75d6d58b03031bd7cb52a7ca886a8ddf369d21c.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD$" width="59" height="13" /> are both equilateral with side <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />.
</p><p>Take a look at the lower circle. The angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/f/29fa0a9949f018e121b2093216dd5db13d3abef0.png" class="latex" alt="$60^\circ$" width="24" height="13" />, thus sector <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/4/f34b3cf20be89133434c1a52cb3bbc3342a16664.png" class="latex" alt="$1/6$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" /> of the circle. The same is true for sector <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/a/a6adfd3ad27a2dddeb0af7131d73c33c86991e93.png" class="latex" alt="$ABD$" width="43" height="13" /> of the lower circle, and sectors <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/9/459b39e026037707282c95dfdbfd6dc64c855a16.png" class="latex" alt="$CAB$" width="42" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/5/7e538978649c14e1616fac744e983b5981d0ea8e.png" class="latex" alt="$BAD$" width="43" height="13" /> of the upper circle. 
</p><p>If we now sum the areas of these four sectors, we will almost get the area of the new shaded region - except that each of the two equilateral triangles will be counted twice. The area of an equilateral triangle given its side, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/7/547263eb631ff2f1ef193c447d92ecb966213e1f.png" class="latex" alt="$s,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="12" height="11" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/6/686046dda644f045db2534ac661bf0bc3b430af4.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt3}4s^2.$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="47" height="41" />
</p><p>Therefore, the area of the new shaded region is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/0/8d077f44b1d6a4092f4cf1842d11b86e31feafe4.png" class="latex" alt="$4\cdot \left( \frac 16 \cdot \pi\cdot 2^2 \right) - 2 \cdot \left( 4 \cdot \frac{\sqrt3}4 \right) = \frac 83 \pi - 2\sqrt 3$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="342" height="53" />. Lastly, we must subtract the area of the circle that we added earlier, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/c/f2ca003a7da0de4994b4733e203b74ff52d42553.png" class="latex" alt="$\pi$" width="10" height="8" />, and we get
