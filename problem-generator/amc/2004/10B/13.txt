<p>In the United States, coins have the following thicknesses: penny, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/a/6ba04c0269a26aebf635b1f82739f740ba40ed37.png" class="latex" alt="$1.55$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="31" height="13" /> mm; nickel, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/a/93ac9349f41267974c9a27522563a714ad027e36.png" class="latex" alt="$1.95$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="31" height="13" /> mm; dime, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/3/97306b985de6c8e82811f9e1717c0e64d2bd7fbd.png" class="latex" alt="$1.35$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="31" height="13" /> mm; quarter, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/a/90ab24f6f4655d2c0ca3284a9c75f2756049ee4c.png" class="latex" alt="$1.75$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="31" height="13" /> mm. If a stack of these coins is exactly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/0/820a20de2847dfdc1bc5cffc8d904933150032bd.png" class="latex" alt="$14$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="12" /> mm high, how many coins are in the stack?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/4/cb4e7b77220102e2127bbe7499e63588d3f7ef17.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A) \ } 7 \qquad \mathrm{(B) \ } 8 \qquad \mathrm{(C) \ } 9 \qquad \mathrm{(D) \ } 10 \qquad \mathrm{(E) \ } 11$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="359" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>All numbers in this solution will be in hundredths of a millimeter.
</p><p>The thinnest coin is the dime, with thickness <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/e/d8efc4595ab8d0d7d43d5ec1988b1b7dc8ecc7fc.png" class="latex" alt="$135$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" />. A stack of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> dimes has height <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/c/57c598d169f0191944e719d1fa3238b0d0a38dcf.png" class="latex" alt="$135n$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="37" height="13" />.
</p><p>The other three coin types have thicknesses <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/0/290a5eeae70aec9d0d8ba02dd3465ea57e43c9ad.png" class="latex" alt="$135+20$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="67" height="14" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/9/da98ff5546273fc8ae0833117a12e84f121e748c.png" class="latex" alt="$135+40$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="67" height="14" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/0/270a85e880f55f24f86979c461610e8be667e078.png" class="latex" alt="$135+60$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="67" height="14" />. By replacing some of the dimes in our stack by other, thicker coins, we can clearly create exactly all heights in the set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/8/0a8b76a545d02a72324cf9f58d97b076ddcf878f.png" class="latex" alt="$\{135n, 135n+20, 135n+40, \dots, 195n\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="307" height="18" />.
</p><p>If we take an odd <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, then all the possible heights will be odd, and thus none of them will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/7/2d751cd0b479bceb949355d9a385b9b0fcddaa3b.png" class="latex" alt="$1400$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="13" />. Hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> is even.
</p><p>If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/7/6b781ecb692e190a0b96f3fb8b52653f6f21615a.png" class="latex" alt="$n&lt;8$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="13" /> the stack will be too low and if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/3/9c3b4036d87cbbed0983403a6adbb86fe1412efe.png" class="latex" alt="$n&gt;10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="52" height="13" /> it will be too high. Thus we are left with cases <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/4/1d4f171162b090bab7ce5dfa1c58715850530ba5.png" class="latex" alt="$n=8$" width="43" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/c/dcc1b21210db29332b1a7d9ce088fd457f23aeee.png" class="latex" alt="$n=10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="52" height="13" />.
</p><p>If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/c/dcc1b21210db29332b1a7d9ce088fd457f23aeee.png" class="latex" alt="$n=10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="52" height="13" /> the possible stack heights are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/a/9fa8239e0639bd68471cbaa2a98fe9185ed4d119.png" class="latex" alt="$1350,1370,1390,\dots$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="153" height="16" />, with the remaining ones exceeding <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/7/2d751cd0b479bceb949355d9a385b9b0fcddaa3b.png" class="latex" alt="$1400$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="13" />.
</p><p>Therefore there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/e/5ce10d8b85a2bbad80aeed87a77c56aa145fa22f.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\mathrm{(B)\ }8}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="53" height="29" /> coins in the stack.
</p><p>Using the above observation we can easily construct such a stack. A stack of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> dimes would have height <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/1/ec12182aa7ebf3e1aaa33004aad13b24037e2ab6.png" class="latex" alt="$8\cdot 135=1080$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="109" height="13" />, thus we need to add <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/6/0362f8decbd5131e34fd0619f907ecb8be327718.png" class="latex" alt="$320$" width="26" height="12" />.
This can be done for example by replacing five dimes by nickels (for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/4/0045a1057adbf1f16bcf246dc5d50c8bfa2e0fa5.png" class="latex" alt="$+60\cdot 5 = +300$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="119" height="14" />), and one dime by a penny (for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/8/a2876c6ae005be23098b5c730b15313fe703a828.png" class="latex" alt="$+20$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="31" height="14" />).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/c/acc0e1defcd11c8124bf369766eb60897df6ac9d.png" class="latex" alt="$p,n,d$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="46" height="16" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/1/0615acc3725de21025457e7d6f7694dab8e2f758.png" class="latex" alt="$q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="8" height="11" /> be the number of pennies, nickels, dimes, and quarters used in the stack. 
</p><p>From the conditions above, we get the following equation:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/0/e90cd126f01ba1b511e60298c2b287dc3dbcdd3a.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[155p+195n+135d+175q=1400.\]" width="276" height="16" />
</p><p>Then we divide each side by five to get
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/2/252854c806f1bf247a6f731481661e6e500be9db.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[31p+39n+27d+35q=280.\]" width="231" height="16" />
</p><p>Writing both sides in terms of mod 4, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/9/9f91f3d3efadecd9133eb24bc2ae9e50704b3c17.png" class="latex" alt="$-p-n-d-q \equiv 0  \pmod 4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="223" height="18" />. 
</p><p>This means that the sum <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/c/f7c7557d8f5652aed7a974052c11281b41e259f1.png" class="latex" alt="$p+n+d+q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="105" height="16" /> is divisible by 4. Therefore, the answer must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/e/a7ef8595c8039667a688f1f4512e6f3bb87f7595.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{(B)\,\, 8}.$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="59" height="29" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Note">Note</span></h2>
<p>We can easily add up <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/3/9a30142e8532294a0a125171e1506cb32171befc.png" class="latex" alt="$1.55\text{\ mm}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="65" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/8/02819482157c7b5eb295b60e31fb58d1994ad677.png" class="latex" alt="$1.95\text{\ mm}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="65" height="13" /> to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/e/28e4117f7e90067290b39c1ae3d349ef6d825f83.png" class="latex" alt="$3.50\text{\ mm}$" width="65" height="12" />. We multiply that by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/2/7223f840af98b5886fb73c4209d87dc27588da3e.png" class="latex" alt="$14\text{\ mm}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="51" height="12" />. Since this works and it requires 8 coins, the answer is clearly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/e/5ce10d8b85a2bbad80aeed87a77c56aa145fa22f.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\mathrm{(B)\ }8}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="53" height="29" />.
</p><p>Similarly, we can simply take <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> quarters to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/a/f5ab2600b1a9ba810c8965369dcc6a7a2491ef12.png" class="latex" alt="$8\cdot 1.75=14$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="97" height="13" />.
</p>
