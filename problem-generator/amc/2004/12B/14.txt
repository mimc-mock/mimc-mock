<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>The triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> is clearly a right triangle, its area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/0/eb0e3ca013d74edb584fb3d07c54befc16477e98.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5\cdot 12}2 = 30$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="86" height="37" />. If we knew the areas of triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/e/fee44be423061ad897de8afdfec8e109ecfbfcd3.png" class="latex" alt="$AMJ$" width="45" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/7/487e577b9718396fabfcdaefbbeaeff31d99d686.png" class="latex" alt="$BNK$" width="48" height="12" />, we could subtract them to get the area of the pentagon.
</p><p>Draw the height <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/2/4d21d821b4d355d89a283fb4770140d1497d26c1.png" class="latex" alt="$CL$" width="26" height="12" /> from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> onto <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" />. As <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/e/dde1774a06facbf7b3c05e82bc4ed9bd41cd786a.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=13$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="70" height="13" /> and the area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/1/651ba418a810d971dbc8a326d49a71fe1541fae0.png" class="latex" alt="$30$" width="17" height="12" />, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/5/785429a2dbc4e2d0bf27608e226c62e7e4c2579a.png" class="latex" alt="$CL=\frac{60}{13}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="71" height="38" />. The situation is shown in the picture below:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/5/5c537489018db33f3318c7e0d03fee0274596fd2.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(0.5cm); defaultpen(0.8); pair C=(0,0), A=(0,5), B=(12,0), M=(0,4), N=(4,0); pair J=intersectionpoint(A--B, M--(M+rotate(90)*(B-A)) ); pair K=intersectionpoint(A--B, N--(N+rotate(90)*(B-A)) ); pair L=intersectionpoint(A--B, C--(C+rotate(90)*(B-A)) ); draw( A--B--C--cycle ); draw( M--J ); draw( N--K ); draw( C--L, dashed ); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,NW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,SE); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,SW); label(&quot;$M$&quot;,M,SW); label(&quot;$N$&quot;,N,S); label(&quot;$J$&quot;,J,NE); label(&quot;$K$&quot;,K,NE); label(&quot;$L$&quot;,L,NE); [/asy]" width="335" height="162" />
</p><p>Now note that the triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/e/fee44be423061ad897de8afdfec8e109ecfbfcd3.png" class="latex" alt="$AMJ$" width="45" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/5/2e5b5738cd00074445ee860204467f1665fe9810.png" class="latex" alt="$ACL$" width="40" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/c/d4c5ccc25c8a1d4db3bbbf24ba4f006f9c63d493.png" class="latex" alt="$CBL$" width="41" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/d/53d0a16c54c9dfb5c885db10cc532ff42f4decf9.png" class="latex" alt="$NBK$" width="48" height="12" /> all have the same angles and therefore they are similar. We already know some of their sides, and we will use this information to compute their areas. Note that if two polygons are similar with ratio <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />, their areas have ratio <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/5/0e5c83cc0c639fc2ed843bbe2d3d209fb478b104.png" class="latex" alt="$k^2$" width="16" height="15" />. We will use this fact repeatedly.
Below we will use <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/6/c7608a3e97e44fc5bcf0cd62a1f7dfc0a0a2e7d4.png" class="latex" alt="$[XYZ]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="53" height="18" /> to denote the area of the triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/2/0526e1f5ad1412706cf938657f754604e672a671.png" class="latex" alt="$XYZ$" width="44" height="12" />.
</p><p>We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/e/46e62faffe744603bf06aef1b65d0288ed6be295.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{CL}{BC} = \frac{60/13}{12} = \frac 5{13}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="151" height="39" />, hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/0/6b05eca7817305831f89c8c32b095fc744d0f3ce.png" class="latex" alt="$[ACL] = \frac{ 25[ABC] }{169} = \frac{750}{169}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="203" height="39" />.
</p><p>Also, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/4/954551da0a07ed463bbe980cf9c78e446ac98542.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{CL}{AC} = \frac{60/13}5 = \frac{12}{13}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="150" height="39" />, hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/d/bad2c53ab712a9f04b729fc778a0a5c8ab76af1f.png" class="latex" alt="$[CBL] = \frac{ 144[ABC] }{169} = \frac{4320}{169}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="222" height="39" />.
</p><p>Now for the smaller triangles:
</p><p>We know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/b/31b5d9cac312311cdb85a3cd86c12012bb3a0969.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{AM}{AC}=\frac 15$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="73" height="38" />, hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/9/af91c0d6d38fd841753aeb4ad6a1d2c935a07d86.png" class="latex" alt="$[AMJ] = \frac{[ACL]}{25} = \frac{30}{169}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="187" height="39" />. 
</p><p>Similarly, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/6/8d64c9ac436697180f6021006c0963a35a1042b1.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{BN}{BC}=\frac 8{12} = \frac 23$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="117" height="38" />, hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/b/05bc3ab44774189bb5da99a6a8bf30767fcb0b84.png" class="latex" alt="$[NBK] = \frac{4[CBL]}9 = \frac{1920}{169}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="209" height="39" />.
</p><p>Finally, the area of the pentagon is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/2/952217d2a66b1eca2031cd310956f6b6cf30fdd3.png" class="latex" alt="$30 - \frac{30}{169} - \frac{1920}{169} = \boxed{\frac{240}{13}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="201" height="48" />.

</p><p>Triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/6/d56ff04c869ff94a1bf8c595e28f8351af60aeeb.png" class="latex" alt="$MCN$" width="50" height="12" /> has area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/2/9820ffc3516b8756f7e31718e300435d9a45608c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}(4)(4)=8$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="92" height="37" />, and the total area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/e/0fe007fb8c6581231d0e43fff77b109cf3f97090.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{104+136}{13}=\boxed{\frac{240}{13}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="148" height="48" />.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h3>
<p>Because triangle ABC, triangle NBK, and triangle AMJ are similar right triangles whose hypotenuses are in the ratio 13&#160;: 8&#160;: 1, their areas are in
the ratio 169&#160;: 64&#160;: 1. The area of triangle ABC is 1/2 (12)(5) = 30, so the areas of triangle NBK and triangle AMJ are (64/169) (30) and (1/169)(30), respectively. Thus the area of pentagon CMJKN is (1 −  64/169 - 1/169)(30)= <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/1/941374482b822c967a2fc9f36a58194007f9f20a.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\mathbf{(D)}240/13}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="96" height="29" />

</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5">Solution 5</span></h3>
<p>We fake-solve. Draw a perpendicular line to side <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/3/3e3a4ec0df571e719bd3f1d7e00bf71fc55ba2b9.png" class="latex" alt="$CB$" width="28" height="12" /> that goes through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" />. Similarly, draw a perpendicular line to side <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/7/a179ff2638e4799cadd820db205c2beff6299ce9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC$" width="27" height="13" /> that goes through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" />. Let them intersect at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" />. It follows that quadrilateral <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/7/4c7c6bd6400d3eb5f5407259b13a480ed994bb70.png" class="latex" alt="$MONC$" width="64" height="12" /> is a square with side <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> and thus area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/5/9a5b4928c8fe50ce3c2428da3bee3505e891b788.png" class="latex" alt="$16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. Now, draw a perpendicular from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/b/abb5588023cfa1ac14643e9778699f03eecc57a3.png" class="latex" alt="$J$" width="12" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/e/4fe8176d34b11c58cdf7b40a9decdb390f934318.png" class="latex" alt="$MO$" width="33" height="12" /> and let them intersect at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/a/3ead47fb9fb4a4c273feee398f72ff2a09702b84.png" class="latex" alt="$Z$" width="13" height="12" />. There is a obvious pair of congruent triangles. Fill in the gap. Hence, we see that the area of the pentagon is definitely greater than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/5/9a5b4928c8fe50ce3c2428da3bee3505e891b788.png" class="latex" alt="$16$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. How much greater? Well, if we let the intersection of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/8/ed896c2a84b46b7c155c647a5196ed60b2473bb3.png" class="latex" alt="$NO$" width="30" height="12" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" />, we can approximate that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/d/dbd1be9f93c82ae8fc066fc1041d74fec0ad51e8.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle NXK$" width="65" height="13" /> has an area greater than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />. So we place bets on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/1/941374482b822c967a2fc9f36a58194007f9f20a.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\mathbf{(D)}240/13}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="96" height="29" /> which is correct!
</p><p>This would be much easier to show and more convincing with a diagram but idk how to upload GeoGebra to AoPSwiki.
</p><p>Solution by franzliszt
