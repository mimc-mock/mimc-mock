<p>A student must choose a program of four courses from a menu of courses consisting of English, Algebra, Geometry, History, Art, and Latin. This program must contain English and at least one mathematics course. In how many ways can this program be chosen?
</p><p><br />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/f/f8f95af3b537ae2fbba434273af38de0d9f27abc.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 6\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 8\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 9\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 12\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 16$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="366" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>Let us split this up into two cases.
</p><p>Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />: The student chooses both algebra and geometry.
</p><p>This means that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> courses have already been chosen.  We have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> more options for the last course, so there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> possibilities here.  
</p><p>Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />: The student chooses one or the other.  
</p><p>Here, we simply count how many ways we can do one, multiply by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, and then add to the previous.  
</p><p>Assume the mathematics course is algebra.  This means that we can choose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> of History, Art, and Latin, which is simply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/8/228972e595029a9fda0a647174b1bf501bb580b2.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{3}{2} = 3$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="70" height="53" />.  If it is geometry, we have another <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> options, so we have a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> options if only one mathematics course is chosen.
</p><p>Thus, overall, we can choose a program in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/d/93d12ee447f2cb6336b32362dfd668288b0db06a.png" class="latex" alt="$6 + 3 = \boxed{\textbf{(C) }9}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="120" height="28" /> ways
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>We can use complementary counting. Since there must be an English class, we will add that to our list of classes leaving <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> remaining spots for rest of classes. We are also told that there needs to be at least one math class. This calls for complementary counting. The total number of ways of choosing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> classes out of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/c/bfcd215213ab7732eefd26c63bb970d3cde42d94.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{5}3$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" />. The total number of ways of choosing only non-mathematical classes is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/a/c2ad9e8d14fb1c77568cf90b6a741433278be67f.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{3}3$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" />. Therefore the amount of ways you can pick classes with at least one math class is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/b/6aba5bc8c5757da3a44c09a06f486f9f1a3c3171.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{5}3-\binom{3}3=10-1=\boxed{\textbf{(C) }9}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="250" height="53" /> ways.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h3>
<p>Similar to Solution 1, note that for Case 1 of solution the answer is simply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/2/db294f4177f5964a4a2fe79271d38586997d576a.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{4}2$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" /> and for the second case it is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/2/7329d5cf2c1608b13663b3cfa2d837ad0f44d80c.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{3}2$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" />  hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/6/e766b969792e79bb4da826a4f74df803853edbda.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) }9}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="55" height="28" /> ways
</p>
