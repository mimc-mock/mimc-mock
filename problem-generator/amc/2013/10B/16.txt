<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Let us use mass points:
Assign <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> mass <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />.  Thus, because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> is the midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> also has a mass of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />.  Similarly, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> has a mass of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />.  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> each have a mass of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> because they are between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> respectively.  Note that the mass of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> is twice the mass of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, so AP must be twice as long as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/d/b9d6394023b7f71962bce796b61343dcc276679c.png" class="latex" alt="$PD$" width="29" height="12" />.  PD has length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/b/e3bb4eeb347e74e025fd175c22f1f7275af0555e.png" class="latex" alt="$AP$" width="27" height="13" /> has length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc56862b1fdb71204894da278e42ef75de73a40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AD$" width="28" height="13" /> has length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />. Since the diagonals of quadrilaterals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/25519bc6e5a2d5863af7d105937565a5b9956967.png" class="latex" alt="$AEDC$" width="57" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc56862b1fdb71204894da278e42ef75de73a40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AD$" width="28" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/c/13c970cbf5e2e7029089517d5e8efbb8cade6d8a.png" class="latex" alt="$CE$" width="28" height="12" />, are perpendicular, the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/25519bc6e5a2d5863af7d105937565a5b9956967.png" class="latex" alt="$AEDC$" width="57" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/6/2769ff81fb5056d9a4d4c7bb604a36579d88d531.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{6 \times 4.5}{2}=\boxed{\textbf{(B)} 13.5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="155" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Note that triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/0/8b0e475dc462398a8d537e8640542c80d22a8e98.png" class="latex" alt="$DPE$" width="44" height="12" /> is a right triangle, and that the four angles (angles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/a/91ac6f388de0ba6ea20f295417fb6c43c26ef472.png" class="latex" alt="$APC, CPD, DPE,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="149" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/f/dcff1cbc94e26e9f9add8cc782c254c72de62b62.png" class="latex" alt="$EPA$" width="42" height="13" />) that have point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> are all right angles. Using the fact that the centroid (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />) divides each median in a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/3/0b3714d1977e2e6fa2c03536bd04eabab371fd18.png" class="latex" alt="$2:1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="32" height="12" /> ratio, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/a/a9a021431716720da106ccde2a8e687003cbdfbf.png" class="latex" alt="$AP=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="61" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/6/216d78a3f3c239d8aa9fcbf33d503fa0bd46b1e8.png" class="latex" alt="$CP=3$" width="61" height="12" />. Quadrilateral <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/5/25519bc6e5a2d5863af7d105937565a5b9956967.png" class="latex" alt="$AEDC$" width="57" height="13" /> is now just four right triangles. The area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/2/e42a573539faa1d1b3b734fd078887389ca4be50.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{4\cdot 1.5+4\cdot 3+3\cdot 2+2\cdot 1.5}{2}=\boxed{\textbf{(B)} 13.5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="321" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>From the solution above, we can find that the lengths of the diagonals are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/b/0fbe18900c777778ad24c84cb3d99df7032b4fd3.png" class="latex" alt="$4.5$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="22" height="13" />. Now, since the diagonals of AEDC are perpendicular, we use the area formula to find that the total area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/0/0c037f56326f7908d4f8c2c8276655a9ee12e93e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{6\times4.5}{2} = \frac{27}{2} = 13.5 = \boxed{\textbf{(B)} 13.5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="258" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>From the solutions above, we know that the sides CP and AP are 3 and 4 respectively because of the properties of medians that divide cevians into 1:2 ratios. We can then proceed to use the heron's formula on the middle triangle EPD and get the area of EPD as 3/2, (its simple computation really, nothing large). Then we can find the areas of the remaining triangles based on side and ratio length of the bases.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_5">Solution 5</span></h2>
<p>We know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/6/106180ce9be5be184099d1a41847420e3e54a905.png" class="latex" alt="$[AEDC]=\frac{3}{4}[ABC]$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="156" height="38" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/4/1043f5e59326b5780921c5188bb58e12e62fe895.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABC]=3[PAC]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="136" height="18" /> using median properties. So Now we try to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/a/f1a8a7893684d6568f7d1de024258b8b62ce1962.png" class="latex" alt="$[PAC]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="50" height="18" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/0/500d1abd22c267bd76ea301e288ea237575fe789.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle PAC\sim \triangle PDE$" width="142" height="13" />, then the side lengths of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/e/d9e2286f54a8da97297935452f9b0ecc9069d9a8.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle PAC$" width="58" height="13" /> are twice as long as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/a/85a66de014aae7c9892d12234872ccc7c10c9638.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle PDE$" width="60" height="13" /> since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> are midpoints. Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a05f2cd19e3b013d35c07c0eb75db1793047f96f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{[PAC]}{[PDE]}=2^2=4$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="132" height="43" />. It suffices to compute <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/3/c8354027abfbcc708e1b46d5c16be1e40a2f20f0.png" class="latex" alt="$[PDE]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="52" height="18" />. Notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/f/e4f29009018f4fa8ceab8c0fd955c4e1d5dc33ab.png" class="latex" alt="$(1.5, 2, 2.5)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="84" height="18" /> is a Pythagorean Triple, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/4/ad4176e839a5dd74451b92dd57ca8d0146263717.png" class="latex" alt="$[PDE]=\frac{1.5\times 2}{2}=1.5$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="184" height="37" />. This implies <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/7/957a288d46af99d611338c28989321ba80e205d2.png" class="latex" alt="$[PAC]=1.5\cdot 4=6$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="154" height="18" />, and then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/6/4f6ed2206626842ec54eb182c1346f065b266924.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABC]=3\cdot 6=18$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="150" height="18" />. Finally, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/e/8ee8a48d9f420def8dac0222630ce6454d9e85f0.png" class="latex" alt="$[AEDC]=\frac{3}{4}\times 18=\boxed{13.5}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="214" height="38" />.
</p><p>~CoolJupiter
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_6">Solution 6</span></h2>
<p>As from Solution 4, we find the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/4/58422c70280d3ce4de929d3959c0eb96a794da86.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DPE$" width="60" height="13" /> to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/d/cfdbd19877c13d1755e01b54ba640f512c883a34.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{3}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. Also, because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/9/b69d33a2af16c5041f7ae2cab3674dd8bcea1c23.png" class="latex" alt="$DE || AC$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> is similar to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/5/285efce7fabaa74905734ef9cfbc787ad09526f7.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{DBE}$" width="60" height="13" /> with side length ratio <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/3/0b3714d1977e2e6fa2c03536bd04eabab371fd18.png" class="latex" alt="$2:1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="32" height="12" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/b/edb20df9f4016a15bd68c2c0286c8b5e540ced58.png" class="latex" alt="$AC=5$" width="60" height="13" /> and the altitude perpendicular to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/c/4ecf002dbb2c81eb01c9d945f6c7fa95014c4a50.png" class="latex" alt="$AC = \frac{12}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="72" height="37" />.
