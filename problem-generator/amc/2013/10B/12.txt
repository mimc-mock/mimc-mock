<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> be the set of sides and diagonals of a regular pentagon. A pair of elements of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> are selected at random without replacement. What is the probability that the two chosen segments have the same length?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/d/0ed223e2011e6a74141e93a150e73c0324abb676.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }\frac{2}5\qquad\textbf{(B) }\frac{4}9\qquad\textbf{(C) }\frac{1}2\qquad\textbf{(D) }\frac{5}9\qquad\textbf{(E) }\frac{4}5$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="371" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions">Solutions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>In a regular pentagon, there are 5 sides with the same length and 5 diagonals with the same length. Picking an element at random will leave 4 elements with the same length as the element picked, with 9 total elements remaining. Therefore, the probability is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/5/8e5e6424f3365fbf10b268ddf362056d9a853fd7.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B) }\frac{4}{9}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="59" height="48" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>Alternatively, we can divide this problem into two cases.
Case 1: Side;
In this case, there is a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/b/a4b8722a423c09cc333298318a89ce22d2bf90d9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5}{10}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="38" /> chance of picking a side, and a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/b/14bd331780e3aa8eb581193b53ca357bfae6f21d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{4}{9}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> chance of picking another side.
Case 2: Diagonal;
This case is similar to the first, for again, there is a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/b/a4b8722a423c09cc333298318a89ce22d2bf90d9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{5}{10}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="38" /> chance of picking a diagonal, and a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/b/14bd331780e3aa8eb581193b53ca357bfae6f21d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{4}{9}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> chance of picking another diagonal.
</p><p>Summing these cases up gives us a probability of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/5/8e5e6424f3365fbf10b268ddf362056d9a853fd7.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B) }\frac{4}{9}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="59" height="48" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h3>
<p>Another way to do this is to use combinations.
We know that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/a/b4a36d045406715079df9fc3e9053bd327aa03cc.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{10}{2} = 45$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="88" height="53" /> ways to select two segments. The ways in which you get 2 segments of the same length are if you choose two sides, or two diagonals. Thus, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/a/aaaa6f591cc90dae3932e4602aec5bbe488b612c.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \times \binom{5}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="64" height="53" /> = 20 ways in which you end up with two segments of the same length. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/7/757b2cd68d8136a9f345a233ff79ad1892f84805.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{20}{45}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="20" height="38" /> is equivalent to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/5/8e5e6424f3365fbf10b268ddf362056d9a853fd7.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B) }\frac{4}{9}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="59" height="48" />.
</p>
