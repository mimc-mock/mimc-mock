<p>Rabbits Peter and Pauline have three offspring—Flopsie, Mopsie, and Cotton-tail. These five rabbits are to be distributed to four different pet stores so that no store gets both a parent and a child. It is not required that every store gets a rabbit. In how many different ways can this be done?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/f/78fccdf6b49d5b9b57000807d6980b5893328479.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)} \ 96 \qquad  \textbf{(B)} \ 108 \qquad  \textbf{(C)} \ 156 \qquad  \textbf{(D)} \ 204 \qquad  \textbf{(E)} \ 372$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="430" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>There are two possibilities regarding the parents.
</p><p>1) Both are in the same store. In this case, we can treat them both as a single bunny, and they can go in any of the 4 stores. The 3 baby bunnies can go in any of the remaining 3 stores. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/1/e716f45db80b8646352cce7d1549f4a3fc33fb9b.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \cdot 3^3 = 108$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="89" height="15" /> combinations.
</p><p><br />
2) The two are in different stores. In this case, one can go in any of the 4 stores, and the other can go in any of the 3 remaining stores. The 3 baby bunnies can each go in any of the remaining 2 stores. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/6/b86e55fbdf8ea64a703e081e3a6f9e3735115c11.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \cdot 3 \cdot 2^3 = 96$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="102" height="15" /> combinations.
</p><p>Adding up, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/b/86bf433514106005c45b94583a17c0bca4425ca4.png" class="latex" alt="$108 + 96 = 204$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="119" height="14" /> combinations.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We tackle the problem by sorting it by how many stores are involved in the transaction.
</p><p>1) 2 stores are involved. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/0/f401413a5807bd29f485f438f9b97173ecd58351.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{4}{2} = 6$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="70" height="53" /> ways to choose which of the stores are involved and 2 ways to choose which store recieves the parents. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/5/785806dee2edf4f47b6b15f6f4beb572b97b9de3.png" class="latex" alt="$6 \cdot 2 = 12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="73" height="13" /> total arrangements.
</p><p><br />
2) 3 stores are involved. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/3/a63c89d04c2e7eabe943f7c86542d4c892414665.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{4}{3} = 4$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="70" height="53" /> ways to choose which of the stores are involved. We then break the problem down to into two subsections - when the parents and grouped together or sold separately.
</p><p><br />
<b>Separately:</b>
All children must be in one store. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/5/6859713adc973d3fbe97fafef9ce829757e54b12.png" class="latex" alt="$3!$" width="12" height="13" /> ways to arrange this. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> ways in total.
</p><p><b>Together:</b>
Both parents are in one store and the 3 children are split between the other two. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/2/50257e1113399bb38a23519393448f182d5f410d.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{3}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" /> ways to split the children and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/5/6859713adc973d3fbe97fafef9ce829757e54b12.png" class="latex" alt="$3!$" width="12" height="13" /> ways to choose to which store each group will be sold. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/2/5429586b4ffcd2d899a768a4f8f8f73b8b1b6e23.png" class="latex" alt="$3! \cdot \binom{3}{2} = 18$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="106" height="53" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/60153dfa25cb496cda1a2d0ffae1e1736413b634.png" class="latex" alt="$(6 + 18) \cdot 4 = 96$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="127" height="18" /> total arrangements.
</p><p><br />
3) All 4 stores are involved. We break down the problem as previously shown.
</p><p><br />
<b>Separately:</b>
All children must be split between two stores. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/a/41a904b05a212869748661e42154aa5f0c622f08.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{3}{2} = 3$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="70" height="53" /> ways to arrange this. We can then arrange which group is sold to which store in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/a/2da866442e69c040daf44260b4728d37e98de313.png" class="latex" alt="$4!$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="12" height="13" /> ways. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/6018575ae59158b5acd56d9c8f6ec604f10f7f4c.png" class="latex" alt="$4! \cdot 3 = 72$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="78" height="13" />.
</p><p><b>Together:</b>
Both parents are in one store and the 3 children are each in another store. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/2/482b0fba62fc7502e8b9fb930374b8f096a3a940.png" class="latex" alt="$4! = 24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="56" height="13" /> ways to arrange this.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/0/850f4b72d35c3113b06fef00023e992e8084bd0a.png" class="latex" alt="$24 + 72 = 96$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="100" height="14" /> total arrangements.
</p><p><br />
<b>Final Answer:</b>
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7dde00d66924d0887f5834f7cd1e064a87e7702.png" class="latex" alt="$12 + 96 + 96 = \boxed{\textbf{(D)} \: 204}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="195" height="28" />.
</p>
