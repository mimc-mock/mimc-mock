<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> be the set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/b/1ab9fc2533c4c85549baa0fd13ca1d94e7b03cd7.png" class="latex" alt="$\{1,2,3,...,19\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="110" height="18" />. For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/3/c735d6a627b91150d3ebdeae431fc2f86df26b28.png" class="latex" alt="$a,b \in S$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="59" height="16" />, define <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/f/a3f681ed361802f316f3fd5db54858dbf51778e1.png" class="latex" alt="$a \succ b$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="42" height="13" /> to mean that either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f50ae347f36db34352cee98e1521350c7a6d944c.png" class="latex" alt="$0 &lt; a - b \le 9$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="105" height="15" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/0/32061883d061a38ad81813ee8ef09cecd1c7e978.png" class="latex" alt="$b - a &gt; 9$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="72" height="13" />. How many ordered triples <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/3/c73ed093cc73a8d9a14185fa525e7067e5c3725d.png" class="latex" alt="$(x,y,z)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="58" height="18" /> of elements of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> have the property that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/7/8e73253016df5627fefc1396ed238bfb394c8b30.png" class="latex" alt="$x \succ y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="43" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/b/5/7b5bef0304989fa381a6e87ce2b36a4127911256.png" class="latex" alt="$y \succ z$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/1/521207eb6f4c6d36d3e4022afc80904754ac8616.png" class="latex" alt="$z \succ x$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="43" height="11" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/8/028235c384d01d8f5dea69cfa2da85ab5ecc289f.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)} \ 810 \qquad  \textbf{(B)} \ 855 \qquad  \textbf{(C)} \ 900 \qquad  \textbf{(D)} \ 950 \qquad  \textbf{(E)} \ 988$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="439" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Imagine that the 19 numbers are just 19 persons sitting evenly around a circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />; each of them is facing to the center. 
</p><p>One may check that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/7/8e73253016df5627fefc1396ed238bfb394c8b30.png" class="latex" alt="$x \succ y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="43" height="13" /> if and only if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> is one of the 9 persons on the left of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/8/aa866dc31ff99cac53235df5545377e6a280bf1b.png" class="latex" alt="$y \succ x$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="13" /> if and only if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> is one of the 9 persons on the right of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />. Therefore, "<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/7/8e73253016df5627fefc1396ed238bfb394c8b30.png" class="latex" alt="$x \succ y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="43" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/b/5/7b5bef0304989fa381a6e87ce2b36a4127911256.png" class="latex" alt="$y \succ z$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/1/521207eb6f4c6d36d3e4022afc80904754ac8616.png" class="latex" alt="$z \succ x$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="43" height="11" />" implies that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/0/d20258310be3455daf8318274224a9d1d28fb6a3.png" class="latex" alt="$x, y, z$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="11" /> cuts the circumference of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> into three arcs, each of which has no more than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> numbers sitting on it (inclusive).
</p><p>We count the complement: where the cut generated by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/3/c73ed093cc73a8d9a14185fa525e7067e5c3725d.png" class="latex" alt="$(x,y,z)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="58" height="18" /> has ONE arc that has more than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> persons sitting on. Note that there can only be one such arc because there are only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/b/efbfe273946ff9f971c5eab331ca39caa6fdf7f4.png" class="latex" alt="$19$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> persons in total.
</p><p>Suppose the number of persons on the longest arc is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/0/110330a0a87f802d3610749d57f1afaf5a09ec69.png" class="latex" alt="$k&gt;10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="52" height="13" />. Then two places of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/c/accc80fdf164cef264f56a82b6f9f6add320fe05.png" class="latex" alt="$x,y,z$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="11" /> are just chosen from the two end-points of the arc, and there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/f/ccf5ae774ea982fc8f16b40db23c3902dec83f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$19-k$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="50" height="13" /> possible places for the third person. Once the three places of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/c/accc80fdf164cef264f56a82b6f9f6add320fe05.png" class="latex" alt="$x,y,z$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="11" /> are chosen, there are three possible ways to put <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/c/accc80fdf164cef264f56a82b6f9f6add320fe05.png" class="latex" alt="$x,y,z$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="11" /> into them clockwise. Also, note that for any <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/0/110330a0a87f802d3610749d57f1afaf5a09ec69.png" class="latex" alt="$k&gt;10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="52" height="13" />, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/b/efbfe273946ff9f971c5eab331ca39caa6fdf7f4.png" class="latex" alt="$19$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> ways to choose an arc of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />. Therefore the total number of ways (of the complement) is 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/e/25efcaa790c44863401a29b60bc04a11d1aea380.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum_{k=11}^{18} 3\cdot 19 \cdot (19-k) = 3\cdot 19 \cdot (1+\cdots+8) = 3\cdot 19\cdot 36\]" width="422" height="50" />
</p><p>So the answer is
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/0/d20bc97c03ec0038ec40124df805e17d3b337d92.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[3\cdot \binom{19}{3} - 3\cdot 19\cdot 36 = 3\cdot 19 \cdot (51 - 36) = 855\]" width="363" height="53" />
</p><p>NOTE: this multiple-choice problem can be done even faster -- after we realized the fact that each choice of the three places of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/c/accc80fdf164cef264f56a82b6f9f6add320fe05.png" class="latex" alt="$x,y,z$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="11" /> corresponds to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> possible ways to put them in, and that each arc of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/0/110330a0a87f802d3610749d57f1afaf5a09ec69.png" class="latex" alt="$k&gt;10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="52" height="13" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/b/efbfe273946ff9f971c5eab331ca39caa6fdf7f4.png" class="latex" alt="$19$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> equitable positions, it is evident that the answer should be divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/a/25a1d2b26247509c01ec4eb41ea3b3c775353ce1.png" class="latex" alt="$3\cdot 19$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="39" height="13" />,  which can only be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/d/b7d05977e249669c8458358830f45ae647f965f4.png" class="latex" alt="$855$" width="26" height="12" /> from the five choices.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>First, we can find out that the only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/0/d20258310be3455daf8318274224a9d1d28fb6a3.png" class="latex" alt="$x, y, z$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="11" /> that satisfy the conditions in the problem are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/d/9ad5e4d30650280d29f016aae71d61e811467690.png" class="latex" alt="$(1 \leq z-y \leq 9 , 1 \leq y-x \leq 9 , z-x \geq 10)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="329" height="18" /> , <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/d/78df151a1b303b8ec4e49fbb325860b7e4cb4e98.png" class="latex" alt="$(1 \leq y-x \leq 9 , 1 \leq x-z \leq 9 , y-z \geq 10)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="329" height="18" /> , and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/0/720728c3ec76cc4c81ae02c86e5f310d9b8ce7bd.png" class="latex" alt="$(1 \leq x-z \leq 9 , 1 \leq z-y \leq 9 , x-y \geq 10)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="329" height="18" />. 
</p><p>Consider the 1st set of conditions for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/0/d20258310be3455daf8318274224a9d1d28fb6a3.png" class="latex" alt="$x, y, z$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="11" />. We get that there are
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/3/b932a212472a23e42428ac119b9d85896a9e76e7.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[45\cdot 10 - \sum_{k=1}^{9} \binom{k+1}{2} = 285\]" width="220" height="53" />
</p><p>cases for the first set of conditions.
</p><p>Since the 2nd and 3rd set of conditions are simply rotations of the 1st set, the total number of cases is
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/4/7d4d990cddcf4707a4ac2ff8aded51d856e145b7.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[3\cdot 285 = \fbox{(B)855}\]" width="134" height="28" />
</p>
