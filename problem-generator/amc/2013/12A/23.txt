<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> is a square of side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/1/db1a5e916bf39ad89d64ec678eb7184005e4653a.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{3} + 1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="54" height="18" />. Point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> is on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/5/12517bbc31cb9026c71299b7a0d232d07e60f284.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AC}$" width="28" height="15" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/b/adb73ff17fdbfa66ccfc367a85353a150c583303.png" class="latex" alt="$AP = \sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="76" height="18" />. The square region bounded by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> is rotated <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/7/6a7dfa8a2a90d521b2b03f5b82a1fb9f85dce88c.png" class="latex" alt="$90^{\circ}$" width="24" height="13" /> counterclockwise with center <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" />, sweeping out a region whose area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/6/0269723d2f648172de4c185015d2e317e6fece0a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{c} (a \pi + b)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="76" height="37" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> are positive integers and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/e/25e152853b879e16286aca8f620e52bf396ee1ea.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{gcd}(a,b,c) = 1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="112" height="18" />. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/d/2cdfeae33c1e09de6207d446beb72396c04be481.png" class="latex" alt="$a + b + c$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="70" height="14" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/0/2f0c65e345628c16cc73b4ab1cfabd9cdb4e3c71.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)} \ 15 \qquad \textbf{(B)} \ 17 \qquad \textbf{(C)} \ 19 \qquad \textbf{(D)} \ 21 \qquad \textbf{(E)} \ 23$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="394" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>We first note that diagonal <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/5/12517bbc31cb9026c71299b7a0d232d07e60f284.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AC}$" width="28" height="15" /> is of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/3/8/a383b879a8bc376d66858292adf8d8b901670846.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{6} + \sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="71" height="18" />. It must be that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/1/b41699a79262163c0df579406c575f506f6dfb97.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AP}$" width="28" height="15" /> divides the diagonal into two segments in the ratio <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/7/6c77cf4b63af80bfbc6a9ad9b4b9e8e549e77d78.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. It is not difficult to visualize that when the square is rotated, the initial and final squares overlap in a rectangular region of dimensions <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/5/e455d80f4657b1112ba302a6e2c42e9c81f780ee.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" /> by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/1/db1a5e916bf39ad89d64ec678eb7184005e4653a.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{3} + 1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="54" height="18" />. The area of the overall region (of the initial and final squares) is therefore twice the area of the original square minus the overlap, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/a/edae1e682f6ef7ab0e1135e7f86425f1bfde9fea.png" class="latex" alt="$2 (\sqrt{3} + 1)^2 - 2 (\sqrt{3} + 1) = 2 (4 + 2 \sqrt{3}) - 2 \sqrt{3} - 2 = 6 + 2 \sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="475" height="21" />.
</p><p><br />
The area also includes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> circular segments. Two are quarter-circles centered at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> of radii <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/4/2c4dc1409208ff6a3039f4871d85c16763334983.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" /> (the segment bounded by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/3/be30a7d4898ac6bc1f824ca3f331a7fdb5ed617d.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{PA}$" width="28" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/7/987c69feb8169ce16fed5ab2769e0157c404982f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{PA&#39;}$" width="32" height="15" />) and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/f/0af834b481b16223a0f04fa9a05f4f8a581e0182.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{6}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" /> (that bounded by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/9/a894a620b157358dab619a863fdea6c368d3d0cb.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{PC}$" width="28" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/2/9d2be1f40718954ff67a000ef0500cc4154a7a4a.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{PC&#39;}$" width="33" height="15" />). Assuming <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> is the bottom-left vertex and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> is the bottom-right one, it is clear that the third segment is formed as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> swings out to the right of the original square [recall that the square is rotated counterclockwise], while the fourth is formed when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> overshoots the final square's left edge. To find these areas, consider the perpendicular from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/3/e33fe7d65facd8868f58b6e94ddc7f153a5a3f9f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BC}$" width="29" height="15" />. Call the point of intersection <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" />. From the previous paragraph, it is clear that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/8/8089b39c86b5a16fbf2758d18355f3ed70433046.png" class="latex" alt="$PE = \sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="77" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/d/7edf3afba191eb9958ab78c986da35e33e1681b3.png" class="latex" alt="$BE = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="61" height="13" />. This means <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/f/94f1f31ee018e9b09652ed4f0259a7733b2552f9.png" class="latex" alt="$PB = 2$" width="61" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> swings back inside edge <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/3/e33fe7d65facd8868f58b6e94ddc7f153a5a3f9f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BC}$" width="29" height="15" /> at a point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> unit above <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> (since it left the edge <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> unit below). The triangle of the circular sector is therefore an equilateral triangle of side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, and so the angle of the segment is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/d/08d5c45388de5c75af641c046d466de3592ffa98.png" class="latex" alt="$60^{\circ}$" width="24" height="13" />. Imagining the process in reverse, it is clear that the situation is the same with point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />.
</p><p><br />
The area of the segments can be found by subtracting the area of the triangle from that of the sector; it follows that the two quarter-segments have areas <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/c/3bc68fdb97dbbb8496d81e3954b98736905843ab.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4} \pi (\sqrt{2})^2 - \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{2} \sqrt{2} = \frac{\pi}{2} - 1$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="223" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/6/8a60b19057af915339e75e6c9b26ab97da8144e6.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4} \pi (\sqrt{6})^2 - \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{6} \sqrt{6} = \frac{3 \pi}{2} - 3$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="233" height="38" />. The other two segments both have area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/b/44b6ba073e9f80b796adcb23962e80a2ba4b24f0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{6} \pi (2)^2 - \frac{(2)^2 \sqrt{3}}{4} = \frac{2 \pi}{3} - \sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="231" height="41" />.
</p><p><br />
The total area is therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/7/3178c4cd156af7d411c6f2de5b4cd0ead043b1eb.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(6 + 2 \sqrt{3}) + (\frac{\pi}{2} - 1) + (\frac{3 \pi}{2} - 3) + 2 (\frac{2 \pi}{3} - \sqrt{3})\]" width="366" height="37" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/0/480c559c154098b344616d139306092e54a6a5f9.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[= 2 + 2 \sqrt{3} + 2 \pi + \frac{4 \pi}{3} - 2 \sqrt{3}\]" width="227" height="37" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/7/fb729d5f02f29cf9bc10d6cfdaef871a91cb1d3e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[= \frac{10 \pi}{3} + 2\]" width="82" height="37" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/3/2b3cab9945611ff8b330a03e14d7c06b38172350.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[= \frac{1}{3} (10 \pi + 6)\]" width="104" height="37" />
</p><p><br />
Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/6/7d6cc5bbcaca2ea70d1998da025c3f0873bb8562.png" class="latex" alt="$a = 10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="51" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/0/b4072cb7de18361e960cfe836e16456d1dd1ae2e.png" class="latex" alt="$b = 6$" width="40" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/4/364e73f027e00a6cc83380babf2678597bba5df9.png" class="latex" alt="$c = 3$" width="40" height="12" />, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/a/a0ad722cc6b9b51e0d516fe45f88ea7b10bd33b4.png" class="latex" alt="$a + b + c = 10 + 6 + 3 = \boxed{\textbf{(C)} \ 19}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="263" height="28" />.
</p>
