What is the area of the rectangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/9/5e91fa584790bd73c21e57d1c0bdc5199237b961.png" class="latex" alt="$ABEF$" width="57" height="13" />?

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Here is an easy way to look at this, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/f/36f73fc1312ee0349b3f3a0f3bd9eb5504339011.png" class="latex" alt="$p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" /> is the perimeter, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Apothem" title="Apothem">apothem</a>:
</p><p>Area of Octagon: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/1/de131f1f0a0d551897d178eccf881254a908729a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{ap}{2}=1$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="55" height="33" />.
</p><p>Area of Rectangle: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/a/ccaa941dcbae69d1493febaac370adf0ceeb1b02.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{p}{8}\times 2a=\dfrac{2ap}{8}=\frac{ap}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="155" height="37" />.
</p><p>You can see from this that the octagon's area is twice as large as the rectangle's area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/5/e1521e0827b3737d6bd56920ff13324b3b97f8bf.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{1}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="59" height="48" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/a/a8a9e9c63ae7f93d28c6a8e83ab8b76c83a6f147.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(1cm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt)+fontsize(8pt)); pair C=dir(22.5), B=dir(67.5), A=dir(112.5), H=dir(157.5), G=dir(202.5), F=dir(247.5), E=dir(292.5), D=dir(337.5); draw(A--B--C--D--E--F--G--H--cycle); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,NNW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,NNE); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,ENE); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,ESE); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,E,SSE); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,F,SSW); label(&quot;$G$&quot;,G,WSW); label(&quot;$H$&quot;,H,WNW);  draw(A--E--F--B--C--G--H--D); draw(A--E--F--B--A,blue); draw(A--F--E--B--A,red); [/asy]" width="125" height="118" />
</p><p>Here is a less complicated way than that of the user below. If you draw a line segment from each vertex to the center of the octagon and draw the rectangle ABEF (in red), you can see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> of the triangles (in blue) share the same base and height with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/4/4a4a7df1100efe353cc612d5d1c289c22d5bf8c1.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> the rectangle. Therefore, the rectangle's area is the same as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/8/638ee7316e2377ea24df5d33afe4ead7a45fee2e.png" class="latex" alt="$2\cdot2$" width="30" height="12" /> of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> triangles, and is  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/5/e1521e0827b3737d6bd56920ff13324b3b97f8bf.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{1}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="59" height="48" /> the area of the octagon.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/3/a83fa1ab65b5ad398b3743d61186fe7a87c34209.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(1cm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt)+fontsize(8pt)); pair C=dir(22.5), B=dir(67.5), A=dir(112.5), H=dir(157.5), G=dir(202.5), F=dir(247.5), E=dir(292.5), D=dir(337.5); draw(A--B--C--D--E--F--G--H--cycle); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,NNW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,NNE); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,ENE); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,ESE); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,E,SSE); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,F,SSW); label(&quot;$G$&quot;,G,WSW); label(&quot;$H$&quot;,H,WNW);  draw(A--D--E--H--G--B--C--F); [/asy]" width="125" height="118" />
Drawing lines <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/5/bc56862b1fdb71204894da278e42ef75de73a40b.png" class="latex" alt="$AD$" width="28" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/2/3f2ef2fa9310285ea0bc63937e3bf83c99168a04.png" class="latex" alt="$BG$" width="28" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/d/07d29465728e92952374a857beaee91665d8dbd9.png" class="latex" alt="$CF$" width="28" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/b/90b19c0bfdec36082e8da6e7ba3a3149b69fa63c.png" class="latex" alt="$EH$" width="31" height="12" />, we can see that the octagon is comprised of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> square, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> rectangles, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> triangles. The triangles each are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/2/22231a5cdd4fe05a11f4b577e3b81221f3017a85.png" class="latex" alt="$45-45-90$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="98" height="13" /> triangles, and since their diagonal is length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />, each of their sides is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/9/7/a97a62890d87757ecbbfa2c66894efdc16ca4f8b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="38" height="41" />. The area of the entire figure is, likewise, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/b/76b2878564ab19b80abb21ba964abe90fe120846.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2$" width="16" height="15" /> (the square)<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/c/3fc2587d106d60603b58c61aed8888075e7440ab.png" class="latex" alt="$+4x^2\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="67" height="41" /> (the 4 rectangles)<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/8/0d8c5d514ad292dc3e1dfb94e40ea9b67f6273a0.png" class="latex" alt="$+2\cdot(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}x)^2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="95" height="41" /> (the triangles), which simplifies to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/c/3ac433b651c82a32c4acf5bfe11bcc89cd32eba8.png" class="latex" alt="$2x^2 + 2\sqrt{2}x^2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="99" height="18" />. The area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/9/5e91fa584790bd73c21e57d1c0bdc5199237b961.png" class="latex" alt="$ABEF$" width="57" height="13" /> is just <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/6/cb69cd00d15ce0ee6aa93b9072e7db7e76c8faf4.png" class="latex" alt="$x(x+\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{2}x)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="103" height="41" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/b/76b2878564ab19b80abb21ba964abe90fe120846.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2$" width="16" height="15" /> + <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/1/e71ff3655ae7479788de14fa5050165b4204ce28.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="42" height="18" />, which we can see is the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/4/cc40b39fdfc16c2f7849255417487c046bb9399b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{ABCDEFGH}{2} =  \boxed{\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{1}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="205" height="48" /> the area of the octagon.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>First, we are going to divide the diagram. Now we need to find the ratio of the area of the rectangle to the area of the trapezoid. 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/1/5/c153839e87140be8666011f784d5c1564b31f2d8.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(1cm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt)+fontsize(8pt)); pair C=dir(22.5), B=dir(67.5), A=dir(112.5), H=dir(157.5), G=dir(202.5), F=dir(247.5), E=dir(292.5), D=dir(337.5); draw(A--B--C--D--E--F--G--H--cycle); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,NNW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,NNE); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,ENE); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,ESE); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,E,SSE); label(&quot;$F$&quot;,F,SSW); label(&quot;$G$&quot;,G,WSW); label(&quot;$H$&quot;,H,WNW);draw(A--F);draw(B--E);draw(D--G);draw(C--H);[/asy]" width="125" height="118" />
</p><p>The area of a trapezoid is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/3/993ba573c8add83425365bc66f1544732ae7073e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{b_1 + b_2}{2}h$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="66" height="37" />
Note that the trapezoid is made out of 2 45-45-90 triangles and a rectangle, and that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/f/2ff7577f4acd036e4f48bed742c91c9b1416c795.png" class="latex" alt="$AH=FG=1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="118" height="14" />. By realizing that, the area of the trapezoid is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/8/aa88cab81d7774d69631053f496bedaca564331f.png" class="latex" alt="$(\frac{2+\sqrt{2}}{2})(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="111" height="43" />). To make this product easier, note there is two trapezoids, so the new product is now this, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/9/9d994094c9f138ee1438ff91c27309e3101b0743.png" class="latex" alt="$(\frac{2+\sqrt{2}}{2})(\sqrt{2}) = \sqrt{2} + 1$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="194" height="43" /> 
</p><p>Notice how the rectangle has side lengths <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/7/fc749b0e7d47fb1f19fa539c653c3cdf3a15bd2c.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{2}+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="54" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, so it's area is also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/7/fc749b0e7d47fb1f19fa539c653c3cdf3a15bd2c.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{2}+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="54" height="18" />.
</p><p>The ratio of the area of rectangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/9/5e91fa584790bd73c21e57d1c0bdc5199237b961.png" class="latex" alt="$ABEF$" width="57" height="13" /> to the two trapezoids is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/c/04c24ac2a2a3d8ab9f3f0f63c567f2a56164c670.png" class="latex" alt="$1:1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="32" height="12" />, meaning they share half the area of the octogon. Since the area of the octogon is 1, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/3/a63588d48cc9015ddcfc1560cf86e98b33b51972.png" class="latex" alt="$\therefore$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="11" height="10" /> the area of the rectangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/5/e1521e0827b3737d6bd56920ff13324b3b97f8bf.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{1}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="59" height="48" />.
