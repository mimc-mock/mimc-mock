<p>The sum of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> consecutive even integers is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> less than the sum of the first <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> consecutive odd counting numbers. What is the smallest of the even integers?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/a/9aa4665375421b9a5e4816eb7d03740543a985fd.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } 6 \qquad\textbf{(B) } 8 \qquad\textbf{(C) } 10 \qquad\textbf{(D) } 12 \qquad\textbf{(E) } 14$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="380" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>It is a well-known fact that the sum of the first <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> odd numbers is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/0/6006bba637eef443381fbaff768fa62d9ff41e62.png" class="latex" alt="$n^2$" width="17" height="15" />.  This makes the sum of the first <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> odd numbers equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/e/05e228ac5bee7b9ffb60cacf50f3f2c7c31f8038.png" class="latex" alt="$64$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="18" height="13" />.
</p><p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> be equal to the smallest of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> even integers.  Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/9/1b959c199967b855702c0dbce5bdb4868385f2c7.png" class="latex" alt="$(n+2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="54" height="18" /> is the next highest, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/8/b18df58e57821c81bd23f3ae77f7580ed1baf93d.png" class="latex" alt="$(n+4)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="54" height="18" /> even higher, and so on.
</p><p>This sets up the equation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/c/f0c0e845d33a776748c988fccc8815cfdcfdfe9c.png" class="latex" alt="$n+(n+2)+(n+4)+(n+6)+(n+8)+4=64$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="399" height="18" />
</p><p>Now we solve:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/2/842502c8003c93ed53dca618860b689d6544279e.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[5n+24=64\]" width="102" height="14" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/e/3/de3e5e0f25b320e50496de28ad34bd574e697841.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[5n=40\]" width="61" height="13" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/a/2ba83bf8758acefe099680e230795f472acecdae.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[n=8\]" width="43" height="12" />
</p><p>Thus, the smallest integer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/d/95d3424aa300eaba7da2d15094b7a5daaefc0e70.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B) } 8}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="55" height="28" />.
</p>
