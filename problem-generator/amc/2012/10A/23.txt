<p>Adam, Benin, Chiang, Deshawn, Esther, and Fiona have internet accounts.  Some, but not all, of them are internet friends with each other, and none of them has an internet friend outside this group.  Each of them has the same number of internet friends.  In how many different ways can this happen?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/3/913abd130de8e966f472b79e4f0b46fff6692eac.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{(A)}\ 60\qquad\text{(B)}\ 170\qquad\text{(C)}\ 290\qquad\text{(D)}\ 320\qquad\text{(E)}\ 660$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="412" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Note that if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> is the number of friends each person has, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> can be any integer from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, inclusive.
</p><p>One person can have at most 4 friends since they cannot be all friends (stated in the problem).
</p><p>Also note that the cases of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/9/1d96ecc15129c5631ba4a2c6578e91e3d13e3baf.png" class="latex" alt="$n=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/c/39cf3e35a4981583288d2a7c4b34989916fb7360.png" class="latex" alt="$n=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="44" height="12" /> are the same, since a map showing a solution for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/9/1d96ecc15129c5631ba4a2c6578e91e3d13e3baf.png" class="latex" alt="$n=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="12" /> can correspond one-to-one with a map of a solution for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/c/39cf3e35a4981583288d2a7c4b34989916fb7360.png" class="latex" alt="$n=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="44" height="12" /> by simply making every pair of friends non-friends and vice versa.  The same can be said of configurations with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/b/6cb7ae00764b56ee2adf59e78a1ffde9685b80db.png" class="latex" alt="$n=2$" width="43" height="12" /> when compared to configurations of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/3/5635737307f5f0651cced8ee2e6558a426fd27b5.png" class="latex" alt="$n=3$" width="43" height="12" />.  Thus, we have two cases to examine, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/9/1d96ecc15129c5631ba4a2c6578e91e3d13e3baf.png" class="latex" alt="$n=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/b/6cb7ae00764b56ee2adf59e78a1ffde9685b80db.png" class="latex" alt="$n=2$" width="43" height="12" />, and we count each of these combinations twice.
</p><p>(Note: If you aren’t familiar with one-to-one correspondences, think of it like this: the number of ways to choose 4 friends is equal to number of ways to exclude one friend from your friend group. Hence, since the number of ways to choose 1 friend is the same thing as choosing 1 to not be friends with, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/9/1d96ecc15129c5631ba4a2c6578e91e3d13e3baf.png" class="latex" alt="$n=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/c/39cf3e35a4981583288d2a7c4b34989916fb7360.png" class="latex" alt="$n=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="44" height="12" /> have the same number of ways. Similarly, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/b/6cb7ae00764b56ee2adf59e78a1ffde9685b80db.png" class="latex" alt="$n=2$" width="43" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/3/5635737307f5f0651cced8ee2e6558a426fd27b5.png" class="latex" alt="$n=3$" width="43" height="12" /> have the same number of ways as well. ~peelybonehead)
</p><p>For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/9/1d96ecc15129c5631ba4a2c6578e91e3d13e3baf.png" class="latex" alt="$n=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="12" />, if everyone has exactly one friend, that means there must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> pairs of friends, with no other interconnections.  The first person has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> choices for a friend.  There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> people left.  The next person has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> choices for a friend.  There are two people left, and these remaining two must be friends.  Thus, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/9/f1965fae079a9ba2c0726c307070c2355dfcb213.png" class="latex" alt="$15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> configurations with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/9/1d96ecc15129c5631ba4a2c6578e91e3d13e3baf.png" class="latex" alt="$n=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="12" />.
</p><p>For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/b/6cb7ae00764b56ee2adf59e78a1ffde9685b80db.png" class="latex" alt="$n=2$" width="43" height="12" />, there are two possibilities.  The group of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> can be split into two groups of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />, with each group creating a friendship triangle.  The first person has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/f/9bfd1a7ef429b76abe80e3bf7e963b1bc1dc742f.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{5}{2} = 10$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="79" height="53" /> ways to pick two friends from the other five, while the other three are forced together.  Thus, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> triangular configurations.  
</p><p>However, the group can also form a friendship hexagon, with each person sitting on a vertex, and each side representing the two friends that person has.  The first person may be seated anywhere on the hexagon <a href="/wiki/index.php/Without_loss_of_generality" title="Without loss of generality">without loss of generality</a>.  This person has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/b/f/9bfd1a7ef429b76abe80e3bf7e963b1bc1dc742f.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{5}{2} = 10$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="79" height="53" /> choices for the two friends on the adjoining vertices.  Each of the three remaining people can be seated "across" from one of the original three people, forming a different configuration.  Thus, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/9/409403c5e5254b9df734c7f509299c6afae6fc32.png" class="latex" alt="$10 \cdot 3! = 60$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="87" height="13" /> hexagonal configurations, and in total <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/9/aa94edfb9739dcc66dad90f7dd5c148f44a57e2c.png" class="latex" alt="$70$" width="17" height="12" /> configurations for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/b/6cb7ae00764b56ee2adf59e78a1ffde9685b80db.png" class="latex" alt="$n=2$" width="43" height="12" />.
</p><p>As stated before, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/6/3/5635737307f5f0651cced8ee2e6558a426fd27b5.png" class="latex" alt="$n=3$" width="43" height="12" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/9/aa94edfb9739dcc66dad90f7dd5c148f44a57e2c.png" class="latex" alt="$70$" width="17" height="12" /> configurations, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/c/39cf3e35a4981583288d2a7c4b34989916fb7360.png" class="latex" alt="$n=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="44" height="12" /> has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/9/f1965fae079a9ba2c0726c307070c2355dfcb213.png" class="latex" alt="$15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> configurations.  This gives a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/f/51f666be8105dac1e8f4057f86fe3874fd6bbfd9.png" class="latex" alt="$(70 + 15)\cdot 2 = 170$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="145" height="18" /> configurations, which is option <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/f/77f245bf2c8c4a6b22841358fbed8dc86268c39e.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(B)}\ 170}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="73" height="28" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Note">Note</span></h3>
<p>We can also calculate the triangular configurations by applying <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/6/d76433e4d0c6823f0977174308e0f86a258d724e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\binom{6}{3}}{2} = \frac{20}{2}=10$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="115" height="41" /> (Because choosing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />,<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> is the same as choosing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />,<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" />.
</p><p><br />
For the hexagonal configurations, we know that the total amount of combinations is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/2/95246be1744710288c10397eb46a97bb6778e774.png" class="latex" alt="$6!=720$" width="65" height="13" />. However, we must correct for our  overcounting because of rotation and reflection. We have that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/d/3/8d3ae204e8ad2f4e493288942b6753e80a0a0f44.png" class="latex" alt="${720 \over 6 \cdot 2} = 60$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="77" height="37" /> because there <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> rotations of the hexagon and 2 reflections (clockwise and counterclockwise) for each valid way.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Note_2">Note 2</span></h3>
<p>We can also calculate the hexagonal configurations by placing each person at a random vertex (6!) and dividing for rotations (by 6) and reflections (by 2): 
</p>
<pre>                                                <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/d/fad803bcc03005faea684dd23a8a6cb6bfd21438.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{6!}{12}=60$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="64" height="38" />
</pre>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Note_3">Note 3</span></h3>
<p>For the hexagonal configuration, it is essentially congruent to counting the number of paths that start and end with A. From <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> options, then the next one has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, and so on, so we have: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/f/a7f2680cb1b587224a69309ae851ab7695f83ed4.png" class="latex" alt="$5\cdot 4 \hdots 1 \cdot 1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="92" height="14" />, where the final <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> is because the final point needs to reconnect to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />. Thus, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/a/48a578519da397fe10b98efaa61cb1c65ca1b243.png" class="latex" alt="$5! = 120$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="65" height="13" /> ways. Dividng by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> since we overcounted by accounting for both directions, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/6/4e64783baaf985ee8444fdd865b00573a6c2a80a.png" class="latex" alt="$120/2 = \fbox{60}$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="99" height="24" />
</p><p>-bigbrain123
