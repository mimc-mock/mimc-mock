<p>A triangle has area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/1/651ba418a810d971dbc8a326d49a71fe1541fae0.png" class="latex" alt="$30$" width="17" height="12" />, one side of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />, and the median to that side of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" />.  Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/e/52e8ed7a3ba22130ad3984eb2cd413406475a689.png" class="latex" alt="$\theta$" width="8" height="13" /> be the acute angle formed by that side and the median.  What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/1/3b144c5e51935c5f3229f9dced73af7a45486041.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{\theta}$" width="34" height="13" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/1/33140caf195fe4eb90b3df0394d163ff1111009d.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{3}{10}\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{1}{3}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{9}{20}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{2}{3}\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \frac{9}{10}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="394" height="38" />
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/f/0/6f0ff42fa4415e92913383af798361d060587c2f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(5mm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt)+fontsize(10pt)); dotfactor=4;  pair A=(-5,0), B=(5,0), C=(sqrt(45),6), D=(0,0), E=(sqrt(45),0); draw(A--B--C--cycle); draw(D--C); draw(E--C);  pair[] ps={A,B,C,D,E}; dot(ps);  label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,SW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,SE); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,N); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,SW); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,E,S); label(&quot;$9$&quot;,(D--C),NW); label(&quot;$6$&quot;,(E--C)); label(&quot;$10$&quot;,(A--B),SE); [/asy]" width="628" height="342" /></center>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> is the side of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/4/0948661e822f2a953c43a57ac9e40b2734476de4.png" class="latex" alt="$CD$" width="29" height="12" /> is the median of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" />. The altitude of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> because the 0.5(altitude)(base)=Area of the triangle. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/e/52e8ed7a3ba22130ad3984eb2cd413406475a689.png" class="latex" alt="$\theta$" width="8" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/d/96df371e72437f6157c240c6dfe66fdd0dd3f6ac.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle CDE$" width="56" height="12" />. To find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/1/3b144c5e51935c5f3229f9dced73af7a45486041.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{\theta}$" width="34" height="13" />, just use opposite over hypotenuse with the right triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/5/8c5425baeec765203a664dc6bd0f0562f2bf485a.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DCE$" width="60" height="13" />. This is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/3/0e3825e99c84902cf2e0ae47744573c9e2219804.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac69=\boxed{\textbf{(D)}\ \frac23}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="97" height="48" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>It is a well known fact that a median divides the area of a triangle into two smaller triangles of equal area. Therefore, the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/4/03470113b3c2046065cd708bdd3bfe86ea9c5950.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BCD = 15$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="102" height="14" /> in the above figure. Expressing the area in terms of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/1/3b144c5e51935c5f3229f9dced73af7a45486041.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{\theta}$" width="34" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/f/0cf2de28f21f0656742621b538c07a00428a0577.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2} \cdot 5 \cdot 9 \cdot \sin{\theta} = 15$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="147" height="37" />. Solving for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/1/3b144c5e51935c5f3229f9dced73af7a45486041.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{\theta}$" width="34" height="13" /> gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/b/b7b36666e293ddd01ad624c007241eaf5c9403b7.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{\theta} = \frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="70" height="37" />. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/b/55bdade53f471aab49f63d1a734979cc0c636640.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{D}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="27" height="24" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>The area of a triangle with sides <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/3/3834f0cef2a1f4d8524aa5bc8e3faca298550862.png" class="latex" alt="$a, b$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="25" height="16" /> and angle between them <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/e/52e8ed7a3ba22130ad3984eb2cd413406475a689.png" class="latex" alt="$\theta$" width="8" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/0/9207e48ad35deb8b12888e7d4fcde400f1546146.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2} ab \sin{\theta}.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="71" height="37" /> Therefore, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/3/573193e382aacaf699de354460a6293556ee391c.png" class="latex" alt="$30 = \frac{1}{2} (9 \cdot 5) \sin{\theta} + \frac{1}{2} (9 \cdot 5) \sin{(180^{\circ} - \theta)},$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="332" height="37" /> as two angles along the same line must be supplementary. This simplifies to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/4/bd44fab6a75348aee4e5c7822ed25d5d3e64d223.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{\theta} + \sin{(180^{\circ} - \theta)} = \frac{4}{3} = \sin{\theta} + \sin{180^{\circ}}\cos{\theta} - \cos{180^{\circ}}\sin{\theta}.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="505" height="37" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/3/aa358c279706423ca5898426da30e56e02b09412.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \sin{\theta} = \frac{4}{3} \to \sin{\theta} = \frac{2}{3}.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="187" height="37" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/b/55bdade53f471aab49f63d1a734979cc0c636640.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{D}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="27" height="24" />
</p>
