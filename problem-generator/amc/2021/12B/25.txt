<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>First, we find a numerical representation for the number of lattice points in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> that are under the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/5/0f5921ab9aa6e1b8fd089856467856e0bcf9ae9f.png" class="latex" alt="$y=mx.$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="64" height="11" /> For any value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/0/6d0382f3585219947b495ac2262a81b7b0c40db2.png" class="latex" alt="$x,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="14" height="11" /> the highest lattice point under <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/1/0d1fe0afdea68630af2bda225ce5f2978b5ba0be.png" class="latex" alt="$y=mx$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="60" height="11" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/8/6382d3fb1989e03cb1306cc05a8049526420c1ad.png" class="latex" alt="$\lfloor mx \rfloor.$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="46" height="19" /> Because every lattice point from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/7/4472faa561dcd762fcf7216b975779b81819c0a6.png" class="latex" alt="$(x, 1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="40" height="18" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/1/831b2f082f445b0844635a15a9ef0ef1d83494df.png" class="latex" alt="$(x, \lfloor mx \rfloor)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="73" height="19" /> is under the line, the total number of lattice points under the line is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/e/9eec1b3f1b5af8c0bee7bec86b8cbea301324c80.png" class="latex" alt="$\sum_{x=1}^{30}(\lfloor mx \rfloor).$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="85" height="50" />

If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/9/b899aba26cc33f0026125daebb44a60349c999b6.png" class="latex" alt="$300$" width="26" height="12" /> lattice points are below the line, then around <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/1/4e1965f8d70832ded66434c75a7bd52b75090af0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> of the area formed by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> is under the line. By using the formula for a triangle's area, we find that when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/6/936f34c90db47fb936ec13d12b516ff42fd84a56.png" class="latex" alt="$x=30, y \approx 20.$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="116" height="16" /> Solving for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> assuming that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/d/43db8ae6276f0f49dfe890b5701847962ad1354c.png" class="latex" alt="$(30, 20)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="57" height="18" /> is a point on the line, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/c/d6cc904245c732fd0b6d5808f94683f32d7e1b05.png" class="latex" alt="$m = \frac{2}{3}.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="57" height="37" /> Plugging in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/a/5ea4e5513401d6e6a5c7b7e205ff6d6e6f9435ad.png" class="latex" alt="$\sum_{x=1}^{30}(\lfloor mx \rfloor),$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="86" height="50" /> we get:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/c/f0cd2c235724fc0d0759fdc5bccb4824c0811ca0.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum_{x=1}^{30}(\lfloor \frac{2}{3}x \rfloor) = 0 + 1 + 2 + 2 + 3 + \cdots + 18 + 18 + 19 + 20\]" width="442" height="50" />

Thus, we can use arithmetic sequences to calculate the value above:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/c/f0cd2c235724fc0d0759fdc5bccb4824c0811ca0.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sum_{x=1}^{30}(\lfloor \frac{2}{3}x \rfloor) = 0 + 1 + 2 + 2 + 3 + \cdots + 18 + 18 + 19 + 20\]" width="442" height="50" /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/6/946570924268465ccd5b05861d4afb4d2b29899c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[=\frac{20(21)}{2} + 2 + 4 + 6 + \cdots + 18\]" width="250" height="38" /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/4/2b4ea1efbd427527f37b449563dd00c07c77991d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[=210 + \frac{20}{2}\cdot 9\]" width="111" height="37" /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/9/0a9a0cfa085b309dba1baffe5fad606f3cda1cb1.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[=300\]" width="45" height="12" />
</p><p>This means that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/4/7547747b133453d3c209cff6cfbf129aa67a6947.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> is a possible value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/9/269981c02d6475e9e4ac1869e1b063ef377c2847.png" class="latex" alt="$m.$" width="19" height="8" /> Furthermore, it is the lower bound for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/9/269981c02d6475e9e4ac1869e1b063ef377c2847.png" class="latex" alt="$m.$" width="19" height="8" /> This is because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/1/3c1e26b85e05b2ca7631133447461603808710a4.png" class="latex" alt="$y=\frac{2}{3}x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="57" height="37" /> goes through many points (such as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/7/3c7b0c744c5f81b2a21a2d37f34603bf42c05373.png" class="latex" alt="$(21, 14)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="57" height="18" />). If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> was lower, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/1/0d1fe0afdea68630af2bda225ce5f2978b5ba0be.png" class="latex" alt="$y=mx$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="60" height="11" /> would no longer go through some of these points, and there would be less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/9/b899aba26cc33f0026125daebb44a60349c999b6.png" class="latex" alt="$300$" width="26" height="12" /> lattice points under it.
</p><p>Now, we find an upper bound for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/9/269981c02d6475e9e4ac1869e1b063ef377c2847.png" class="latex" alt="$m.$" width="19" height="8" /> Imagine increasing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> slowly and rotating the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/6/c564ab9e8ce2ee0955eb9236ba2925b646479df4.png" class="latex" alt="$y=mx,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="64" height="11" /> starting from the lower bound of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/6/b665fe8bc1f7d93dfa5c63c11eb4e4c9b53e9ae5.png" class="latex" alt="$m=\frac{2}{3}.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="57" height="37" />The upper bound for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/0/f5047d1e0cbb50ec208923a22cd517c55100fa7b.png" class="latex" alt="$m$" width="15" height="8" /> occurs when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/1/0d1fe0afdea68630af2bda225ce5f2978b5ba0be.png" class="latex" alt="$y=mx$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="60" height="11" /> intersects a lattice point again (look at this problem to get a better idea of what's happening: <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="https://artofproblemsolving.com/wiki/index.php/2011_AMC_10B_Problems/Problem_24">https://artofproblemsolving.com/wiki/index.php/2011_AMC_10B_Problems/Problem_24</a>).
</p><p>In other words, we are looking for the first <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/2/aa20e8abac4be2a578975180edaeac9622d0391f.png" class="latex" alt="$m &gt; \frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="51" height="37" /> that is expressible as a ratio of positive integers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/4/7347a1d5586e82c832f61ea799d89945ed2837fc.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{p}{q}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="11" height="36" /> with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/d/81d4a64159d1db48551e386bdde9f043366212ce.png" class="latex" alt="$q \le 30.$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="54" height="16" /> For each <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/7/cf739181b83a537dbba7c8cc183d9f671d9fc030.png" class="latex" alt="$q=1,\dots,30$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="100" height="16" />, the smallest multiple of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/e/20e8bbe4bee7c83c7ff4be58660ff2d03d6a9c42.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{q}$" style="vertical-align: -16px" width="11" height="40" /> which exceeds <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/4/7547747b133453d3c209cff6cfbf129aa67a6947.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/6/e4679549e7b530babe912ece992c089f01c066f6.png" class="latex" alt="$1, \frac{2}{2}, \frac{3}{3}, \frac{3}{4}, \frac{4}{5}, \cdots , \frac{19}{27}, \frac{19}{28}, \frac{20}{29}, \frac{21}{30}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="248" height="38" /> respectively, and the smallest of these is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/6/8161924965230d46a755cc278594a9c4e7038bbe.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{19}{28}.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="25" height="37" />

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>I know that I want about <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/4/7547747b133453d3c209cff6cfbf129aa67a6947.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> of the box of integer coordinates above my line. There are a total of 30 integer coordinates in the desired range for each axis which gives a total of 900 lattice points. I estimate that the slope, m, is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/4/7547747b133453d3c209cff6cfbf129aa67a6947.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. Now, although there is probably an easier solution, I would try to count the number of points above the line to see if there are 600 points above the line. The line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/1/3c1e26b85e05b2ca7631133447461603808710a4.png" class="latex" alt="$y=\frac{2}{3}x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="57" height="37" /> separates the area inside the box so that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/4/7547747b133453d3c209cff6cfbf129aa67a6947.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> of the are is above the line. 
</p><p>I find that the number of coordinates with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/5/4c52e02a35db152495396584bfeae1d0599cd753.png" class="latex" alt="$x=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="12" /> above the line is 30, and the number of coordinates with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/2/69271b422e022ea65ff1f288eddfcfa82afd8ab7.png" class="latex" alt="$x=2$" width="43" height="12" /> above the line is 29. Every time the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/1/3c1e26b85e05b2ca7631133447461603808710a4.png" class="latex" alt="$y=\frac{2}{3}x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="57" height="37" /> hits a y-value with an integer coordinate, the number of points above the line decreases by one. I wrote out the sum of 30 terms in hopes of finding a pattern. I graphed the first couple positive integer x-coordinates, and found that the sum of the integers above the line is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/3/2/03246dd6c1603e280458c482fb2a60f902e96de7.png" class="latex" alt="$30+29+28+28+27+26+26 \ldots+ 10$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="324" height="14" />. The even integer repeats itself every third term in the sum. I found that the average of each of the terms is 20, and there are 30 of them which means that exactly 600 above the line as desired. This give a lower bound because if the slope decreases a little bit, then the points that the line goes through will be above the line. 
</p><p>To find the upper bound, notice that each point with an integer-valued x-coordinate is either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/1/4e1965f8d70832ded66434c75a7bd52b75090af0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/4/7547747b133453d3c209cff6cfbf129aa67a6947.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> above the line. Since the slope through a point is the y-coordinate divided by the x-coordinate, a shift in the slope will increase the y-value of the higher x-coordinates. We turn our attention to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/d/f8da6cdc412b65f85504ada1ab1a9571d6441b81.png" class="latex" alt="$x=28, 29, 30$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="104" height="16" /> which the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/1/3c1e26b85e05b2ca7631133447461603808710a4.png" class="latex" alt="$y=\frac{2}{3}x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="57" height="37" /> intersects at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/4/d64732a20f5a529484a8381cc2ab43934aa4bdd5.png" class="latex" alt="$y= \frac{56}{3}, \frac{58}{3}, 20$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="112" height="37" />. The point (30,20) is already counted below the line, and we can clearly see that if we slowly increase the slope of the line, we will hit the point (28,19) since (28, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/f/e2f4a880f40bcfaaff78f8039c1990de6ddd61b2.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{56}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" />) is closer to the lattice point. The slope of the line which goes through both the origin and (28,19) is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/4/87416ba75086853248efa4663f9c887404534bea.png" class="latex" alt="$y=\frac{19}{28}x$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="66" height="37" />. This gives an upper bound of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/b/13ba53dd15c901de2a5f25fcd02d6247b1f2f2cb.png" class="latex" alt="$m=\frac{19}{28}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="60" height="37" />. 
</p><p>Taking the upper bound of m and subtracting the lower bound yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/b/83bfa725ff7195c1199976f4399c093f18743cac.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{19}{28}-\frac{2}{3}=\frac{1}{84}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="102" height="38" />. This is answer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/8/af8f87e495df41dd6c17bc208d71ddf4f228da21.png" class="latex" alt="$1+84=$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="68" height="14" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/2/af2ea4e01cbc7a6ac9c0db4efa4f66ed8bf2ab65.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(E)} ~85}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="62" height="28" />.

<p>It's easier to calculate the number of lattice points inside a rectangle with vertices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/6/f9603ca3089464e548fc6f1366bc474e7efef8d9.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/4/3/143a69cd701ca40809a540c295b574d7940730c1.png" class="latex" alt="$(p,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="39" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/b/b6b7a40ab0d8178e83fe1b91c511d04b1246059b.png" class="latex" alt="$(p,q)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/5/8c5bea54f1e5a7e6cc80f825c3b5faa5311029c0.png" class="latex" alt="$(0,q)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="42" height="20" />.
