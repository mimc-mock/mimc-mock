<p>A fair <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />-sided die is repeatedly rolled until an odd number appears. What is the probability that every even number appears at least once before the first occurrence of an odd number?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/f/c/0fc4f89821b7d40861ec396c4e4ad74d4390e1aa.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)} ~\frac{1}{120} \qquad\textbf{(B)} ~\frac{1}{32} \qquad\textbf{(C)} ~\frac{1}{20} \qquad\textbf{(D)} ~\frac{3}{20} \qquad\textbf{(E)} ~\frac{1}{6}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="413" height="38" />
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>There is a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/8/538cd221dea6c5076a34c248e8a3113e535bb7be.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac36$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> chance that the first number we choose is even.
</p><p>There is a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/2/ca2297526ede0e6c616802ca72adf69c82518f25.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{5}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> chance that the next number that is distinct from the first is even.
</p><p>There is a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c339afa03e060d959e4942be7522c0c992f4c41c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="11" height="37" /> chance that the next number distinct from the first two is even.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/4/d74bd9e21e487af33e4071b0c515162c102e46ea.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{3}{6} \cdot \frac{2}{5} \cdot \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{20}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="110" height="38" />, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/c/9ac3c7ce19a490e576e236ed4d08dd9cc8cf58b4.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) }\frac{1}{20}}.$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="72" height="48" />
</p><p>~Tucker
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Every set of three numbers chosen from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/5/615346eb2f9dc275d58727bfdb39c34676691128.png" class="latex" alt="$\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="112" height="18" /> has an equal chance of being the first 3 distinct numbers rolled.
</p><p>Therefore, the probability that the first 3 distinct numbers are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/5/b950c0652ed83d7196f75d437f552e495ceb3bbb.png" class="latex" alt="$\{2,4,6\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="60" height="18" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/8/a08411316966cc25832e606e2045bc2465fea09e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{{6 \choose 3}}=\boxed{(C)~\frac{1}{20}}$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="119" height="50" />
</p><p>~kingofpineapplz
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Note that the problem is basically asking us to find the probability that in some permutation of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/9/009a28721d3a620f52220112b7dcc64e7584d300.png" class="latex" alt="$1,2,3,4,5,6$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="94" height="16" /> that we get the three even numbers in the first three spots.
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/6/e26ca46a67d19ce2e5b692103305a083a2361fd7.png" class="latex" alt="$6!$" width="12" height="13" /> ways to order the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> numbers and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/1/621adc5397a13c97f5bc861b5dfc12c65f567421.png" class="latex" alt="$3!(3!)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="40" height="18" /> ways to order the evens in the first three spots and the odds in the next three spots.
</p><p>Therefore the probability is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/b/6/7b6e69409ffb059d5d4188cdb2a123c27e0972b4.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{3!(3!)}{6!} = \frac{1}{20} = \boxed{\textbf{(C)}}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="157" height="38" />.
</p><p><br />
--abhinavg0627
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/4/c/54c51a4acedda645113145d008dbfb50d35f1da3.png" class="latex" alt="$P_n$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" /> denote the probability that the first odd number appears on roll <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> and all our conditions are met. We now proceed with complementary counting. 
</p><p>For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/7/f97c7d98d2756c9e32f33b601de08c1c6be4f244.png" class="latex" alt="$n \le 3$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="43" height="15" />, it's impossible to have all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> evens appear before an odd. Note that for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/4/6448320479ae16ea1238688f50d0cb01db1efee8.png" class="latex" alt="$n \ge 4,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="48" height="15" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/f/24fbd442097805a8e2909d09b2c2ed86d38ecff3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[P_n = \frac {1}{2^{n}} - \frac {1}{2^{n}} \left(\frac{\binom{3}{2}(2^{n-1}-2)+\binom{3}{1}}{3^{n-1}}\right)\]" width="290" height="53" /> since there's a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/6/d86aceafacfd14346fccf36fe50d0770a62d41b4.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {1}{2^{n}}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="20" height="37" /> chance that the first odd appears on roll <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> (disregarding the other conditions) and the other term is subtracting the probability that less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> of the evens show up before the first odd roll. Simplifying, we arrive at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/4/6c4dcc88247edd0c7ad76c6cb822a9351a47fc8f.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[P_n= \frac {1}{2^{n}} - \left(\frac {3(2^{n-1})-3}{2^{n} \cdot 3^{n-1}}\right) = \frac {1}{2^{n}} - \frac {1}{2 \cdot 3^{n-2}} + \frac{1}{2^{n} \cdot 3^{n-2}}.\]" width="434" height="43" />
</p><p>Summing for all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, we get our answer of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/9/b8932c99a1f124e0b519d059d35bb005b81831b1.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\left (\frac {1}{2^{4}} + \frac {1}{2^{5}} + ... \right) - \left (\frac {1}{2 \cdot 3^{2}} + \frac {1}{2 \cdot 3^{3}} + ... \right) + \left (\frac {1}{2^{4} \cdot 3^{2}} + \frac {1}{2^{5} \cdot 3^{3}} + ... \right) = \left (\frac {1}{8} \right) - \left(\frac {\frac {1}{18}}{ \frac{2}{3}} \right) + \left(\frac {\frac {1}{144}}{\frac {5}{6}} \right) = \frac {1}{8} - \frac {1}{12} + \frac{1}{120} = \boxed{\textbf{(C) }\frac{1}{20}.}\]" width="919" height="49" />
</p><p>~ike.chen
</p>
