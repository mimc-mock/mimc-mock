<p>Six ants simultaneously stand on the six <a href="/wiki/index.php/Vertex" title="Vertex">vertices</a> of a regular <a href="/wiki/index.php/Octahedron" title="Octahedron">octahedron</a>, with each ant at a different vertex. Simultaneously and independently, each ant moves from its vertex to one of the four adjacent vertices, each with equal <a href="/wiki/index.php/Probability" title="Probability">probability</a>. What is the probability that no two ants arrive at the same vertex?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/7/177ef7776cf8096e12326add4e26e89563e00797.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ \frac {5}{256} \qquad\mathrm{(B)}\ \frac {21}{1024} \qquad\mathrm{(C)}\ \frac {11}{512} \qquad\mathrm{(D)}\ \frac {23}{1024} \qquad\mathrm{(E)}\ \frac {3}{128}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="470" height="38" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>We approach this problem by counting the number of ways ants can do their desired migration, and then multiple this number by the probability that each case occurs.
</p><p>Let the octahedron be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/2/8029902caf855d940e465d04ff40101d8850c3e0.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCDEF$" width="86" height="13" />, with points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/8/f5892b48148007d75534ee0f7ae639287af08497.png" class="latex" alt="$B,C,D,E$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="82" height="16" /> <a href="/wiki/index.php/Coplanar" title="Coplanar">coplanar</a>. Then the ant from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and the ant from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> must move to plane <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/a/65af420a6e2b903f5295dd8f65f321545f69e44d.png" class="latex" alt="$BCDE$" width="58" height="12" />. Suppose, without loss of generality, that the ant from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> moved to point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />. Then, we must consider three cases.
</p>
<ul><li>Case 1: Ant from point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> moved to point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /></li></ul>
<p>On the plane, points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> are taken. The ant that moves to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> can come from either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />. The ant that moves to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> can come from either <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />. Once these two ants are fixed, the other two ants must migrate to the "poles" of the octahedron, points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" />. Thus, there are two degrees of freedom in deciding which ant moves to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />, two degrees of freedom in deciding which ant moves to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" />, and two degrees of freedom in deciding which ant moves to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />. Hence, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/b/b/bbba4314109554a9f541e17d5675390a058800dd.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \times 2 \times 2=8$" width="104" height="12" /> ways the ants can move to different points.
</p>
<ul><li>Case 2: Ant from point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> moved to point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /></li></ul>
<p>On the plane, points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> are taken. The ant that moves to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> must be from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />, but the ant that moves to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> must also be from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />. The other two ants, originating from points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" />, must move to the poles. Therefore, there are two degrees of freedom in deciding which ant moves to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> and two degrees of freedom in choosing which ant moves to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />. Hence, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/8/e784aacfb2bcd771fdef5ffcaaa0b804c46e5e89.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \times 2=4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="73" height="12" /> ways the ants can move to different points.
</p>
<ul><li>Case 3: Ant from point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> moved to point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /></li></ul>
<p>By symmetry to Case 1, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> ways the ants can move to different points.
</p><p>Given a point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, there is a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/0/b90657ccbb5b3774e0bf42aa847ac05dff822c71.png" class="latex" alt="$8+4+8=20$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="113" height="14" /> ways the ants can move to different points. We oriented the square so that point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> was defined as the point to which the ant from point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> moved. Since the ant from point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> can actually move to four different points, there is a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/d/9dd7af4c108c577f8f5470091d915ceb76ddc285.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \times 20=80$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="91" height="13" /> ways the ants can move to different points.
</p><p>Each ant acts independently, having four different points to choose from. Hence, each ant has probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/8/8f88d403085fe6600059002646348a5f267266d1.png" class="latex" alt="$1/4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="27" height="18" /> of moving to the desired location. Since there are six ants, the probability of each case occuring is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/e/59ec51dd7cf3380146083f00d55ef5ee375d6ec0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4^6} = \frac{1}{4096}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="83" height="37" />. Thus, the desired answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/d/acd790688dd7ffa7bba0cf01e3b4426a53d25597.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{80}{4096}= \boxed{\frac{5}{256}} \Rightarrow \mathrm{(A)}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="161" height="48" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/f/eef0203e65664bd00b5f0325c5c5fcd81bcead8d.png" class="latex" alt="$f(n)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="34" height="18" /> be the number of <a href="/wiki/index.php/Cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Cycle">cycles</a> of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> the can be walked among the vertices of an octahedron. For example, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/3/003fdb0d6edace39b9ce3789476864e120619656.png" class="latex" alt="$f(3)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="32" height="18" /> would represent the number of ways in which an ant could navigate <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> vertices and then return back to the original spot. Since an ant cannot stay still, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/d/82dd6f474fd8d40d5108934acc4ce4f98bfd3c17.png" class="latex" alt="$f(1) = 0$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" />. We also easily see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/7/2171ff409b5540c317b9560a4c15cee8ba3b5749.png" class="latex" alt="$f(2) = 1, f(3) = 2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="142" height="18" />. 
</p><p>Now consider any four vertices of the octahedron. All four vertices will be connected by edges except for one pair. Let’s think of this as a <a href="/wiki/index.php/Square" title="Square">square</a> with one diagonal (from top left to bottom right). EDIT: This part is wrong as if you choose the 4 vertices that have a cross section as a square, there exists no connecting diagonal.  
</p>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/6/666e49bcbf25ed91b3eda62dae34cc3680159376.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] size(30); defaultpen(0.6); pair A = (0,0), B=(5,0), C=(5,5), D=(0,5); draw(A--B--C--D--cycle);  draw(B--D); [/asy]" width="52" height="52" /></center>
<p>Suppose an ant moved across this diagonal; then the ant at the other end can only move across the diagonal (which creates 2-cycle, bad) or it can move to another vertex, but then the ant at that vertex must move to the spot of the original ant (which creates 3-cycle, bad). Thus none of the ants can navigate the diagonal and can either shift clockwise or counterclockwise, and so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/2/ab201fd18662ecefc0c22820a46f6da388c6eae6.png" class="latex" alt="$f(4) = 2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" />.
</p><p>For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/c/7fcff46c5bc563272c7a7936f9ea9263f5f15b81.png" class="latex" alt="$f(6)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="32" height="18" />, consider an ant at the top of the octahedron. It has four choices. Afterwards, it can either travel directly to the bottom, and then it has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> ways back up, or it can travel along the sides and then go to the bottom, of which simple counting gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> ways back up. Hence, this totals <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/5/8c5199a432a07b65006c93579f37d100f31742dc.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \times (2+6) = 32$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="127" height="18" />.
</p><p>Now, the number of possible ways is given by the sum of all possible cycles,
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/5/e65fd0eb8dbb6a962bc099eecf3cb0d37647d6d6.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[a \cdot f(2) \cdot f(2) \cdot f(2) + b \cdot f(2) \cdot f(4) + c \cdot f(3) \cdot f(3) + d \cdot f(6)\]" width="477" height="18" />
</p><p>where the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Coefficient" title="Coefficient">coefficients</a> represent the number of ways we can configure these cycles. To find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" />, fix any face, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> adjacent faces to select from to complete the cycle. From the four remaining faces there are only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> ways to create cycles, hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/9/51955db01f1d3ae46f724e189a1438a0d775657e.png" class="latex" alt="$a = 8$" width="42" height="12" />.
</p><p>To find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/3/8136a7ef6a03334a7246df9097e5bcc31ba33fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$b$" width="8" height="12" />, each cycle of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> faces is distinguished by their common edge, and there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> edges, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/5/a450ff18c9509e2f70ba9f036b8ca9aeb33d49c7.png" class="latex" alt="$b = 12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="49" height="13" />.
</p><p>To find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" />, each three-cycle is distinguished by the vertex, and there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> edges. However, since the two three-cycles are indistinguishable, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/5/4b56d4d1f67e7a5ffc03b4d74c0a0abb5213d78b.png" class="latex" alt="$c = 8/2 = 4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="92" height="18" />.
</p><p>Clearly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad2990977902054f4d1da49fba330ce8139e7859.png" class="latex" alt="$d = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="41" height="13" />. Finally,
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/a/33a8010162aa899b041b3e0073355ddd80212a39.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[8(1)(1)(1) + 12(1)(2) + 4(2)(2) + (32) = 80\]" width="339" height="18" />
</p><p>Each bug has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> possibilities to choose from, so the probability is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/f/65f1904c6476ebe1bdf29b0d70a8f6b9a4880099.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{80}{4^6} = \frac{5}{256}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="76" height="38" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>We use the idea of cycles. 
</p><p>Case 1: Two 3-cycles.
We have 4 ways to divide the faces into cycles. There are 8 faces, and once a face has been chosen, the other three vertices must form the other cycle of length 3. Then there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/a/32a35631e1e126934ead6d2af161e9d250827bbb.png" class="latex" alt="$2^2$" width="15" height="15" /> ways to choose the direction of the cycle.  There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/5/62525c4c24d06e45f4e3322bfc0e9fc7f5039da0.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \cdot 4 = 16.$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="77" height="13" /> 
</p><p>Case 2: Three 2-cycles.
There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/7/3e7a1f54e5eb742563df52e8d0f8a4ee122b4d03.png" class="latex" alt="$4 \cdot 2 = 8$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="64" height="13" /> ways to do so. 
</p><p>Case 3: One 4-cycle, one 2-cycle.
There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/5/875564de45302def688dd4c2ed67984dc752f68f.png" class="latex" alt="$12 \cdot 2 = 24$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="82" height="12" /> ways here because there are 12 different edges for the 2-cycle and we can choose the direction of the 4-cycle. 
</p><p>Case 4: One 6-cycle.
Visualizing the octahedron as a 4-sided pyramid pointing up above one pointing down, we look at the ways an ant can start from the top vertex and visit all vertices without revisiting one along the way and returning to the top. Because the first step must be from the top to the middle square ring, we choose one vertex to move to and will multiply the final result by four.
</p><p>We divide the paths into two sub-cases.
</p><p>Sub-case 1: The ant continues to the bottom vertex.  In this case, the ant has visited three corners of a square, but can not next visit the fourth corner or there will be no way to connect the remaining two vertices of the octahedron.  Thus, the ant must next visit one of the other two vertices, and that selection decides the remainder of the path.  Thus, this sub-case has 2 possible paths.
</p><p>Sub-case 2: The ant continues along the "equator" square ring. By symmetry, there are two choices.  Choose one, and we will multiply the final result by two.  From that second point on the equator, there are two choices.  If it continues to a third point on the equator, there is only one path to complete the cycle.  If it next moves to the bottom vertex, there are two ways to complete the cycle. This gives a total of three possible paths.  Multiplying by 2 gives 6 for this sub-case.
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/3/5935a45514e0abe34e8c94fb78943184874a1e39.png" class="latex" alt="$(2 + 6) \cdot 4 = 32$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="118" height="18" /> ways here. 
</p><p>Overall, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/a/fea5fcc6d66685ee21f722efcd228eb44d38f262.png" class="latex" alt="$80$" width="17" height="12" /> cases.  Answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/c/edcce6c2c10e315ea73a2ccc3ecc768c5148a622.png" class="latex" alt="$80/2^{12} = 5/256$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="119" height="19" />
</p><p>~Williamgolly + Dr. 17
</p>
