<p>Call a number <i>prime-looking</i> if it is <a href="/wiki/index.php/Composite" class="mw-redirect" title="Composite">composite</a> but not divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/a/cba6c40a9735f92383d6b4dfbcd565e7348d682b.png" class="latex" alt="$2, 3,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="30" height="16" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/3/b835f2c6c592edc4583ce996f86bcc0d07ca8da5.png" class="latex" alt="$5.$" width="12" height="12" /> The three smallest prime-looking numbers are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/d/d/9dd37329ee5ff6ef5015c80de793727618e804c5.png" class="latex" alt="$49, 77$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="44" height="16" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/d/7fd34ff50f8bba26a08bc96a69e921daa60f5f12.png" class="latex" alt="$91$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/1/c7116bbc884d2cf49e25754864ae00d9c42d2d90.png" class="latex" alt="$168$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> prime numbers less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/e/6ee927e1332358c96c62c277441c907c4f51057f.png" class="latex" alt="$1000$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="13" />. How many prime-looking numbers are there less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/e/6ee927e1332358c96c62c277441c907c4f51057f.png" class="latex" alt="$1000$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="13" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/4/0/e403d66a7aa3a3805fefe288f35422c625a4887b.png" class="latex" alt="$(\mathrm {A}) \ 100 \qquad (\mathrm {B}) \ 102 \qquad (\mathrm {C})\ 104 \qquad (\mathrm {D}) \ 106 \qquad (\mathrm {E})\ 108$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="432" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>The given states that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/1/c7116bbc884d2cf49e25754864ae00d9c42d2d90.png" class="latex" alt="$168$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> prime numbers less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/e/6ee927e1332358c96c62c277441c907c4f51057f.png" class="latex" alt="$1000$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="13" />, which is a fact we must somehow utilize. Since there seems to be no easy way to directly calculate the number of "prime-looking" numbers, we can apply <a href="/wiki/index.php/Complementary_counting" title="Complementary counting">complementary counting</a>. We can split the numbers from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/e/6ee927e1332358c96c62c277441c907c4f51057f.png" class="latex" alt="$1000$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="35" height="13" /> into several groups: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/4/2e4060022d2114b31a580708a97a030e40def7ec.png" class="latex" alt="$\{1\},$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="31" height="18" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/a/84a79e1a12f54c92e87f4af8fc047b3cc5892054.png" class="latex" alt="$\{\mathrm{numbers\ divisible\ by\ 2 = S_2}\},$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="241" height="18" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/b/88b780050327d60f142c3265463e2f490f61bf07.png" class="latex" alt="$\{\mathrm{numbers\ divisible\ by\ 3 = S_3}\},$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="241" height="18" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/1/3d1ae54d60629a81ab762a77e5379e25c093b979.png" class="latex" alt="$\{\mathrm{numbers\ divisible\ by\ 5 = S_5}\}, \{\mathrm{primes\ not\ including\ 2,3,5}\},$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="480" height="18" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/6/c46383f8c6e0f1f5bd93cff61a30b5087b3cb50a.png" class="latex" alt="$\{\mathrm{prime-looking}\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="142" height="18" />. Hence, the number of prime-looking numbers is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/4/df4ab8b9dba963cfb741b890db2441b4372efa11.png" class="latex" alt="$1000 - (168-3) - 1 - |S_2 \cup S_3 \cup S_5|$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="289" height="18" /> (note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/c/d6c7b10b11b28d1797da01d5bae3015d741450ff.png" class="latex" alt="$2,3,5$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="43" height="16" /> are primes).
</p><p>We can calculate <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/9/f/99f561d9b327b22c4a7709f317d6d8589c0a2474.png" class="latex" alt="$S_2 \cup S_3 \cup S_5$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="94" height="15" /> using the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Principle_of_Inclusion-Exclusion" title="Principle of Inclusion-Exclusion">Principle of Inclusion-Exclusion</a>: (the values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/d/3/1d32cd12dfaf9c3353a7da9a88de9224ff9e0080.png" class="latex" alt="$|S_2| \ldots$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="51" height="18" /> and their intersections can be found quite easily)
</p>
<div style="text-align:center;"><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/c/17c5721db444774f1dd96630410388d954efab60.png" class="latex" alt="$|S_2 \cup S_3 \cup S_5| = |S_2| + |S_3| + |S_5| - |S_2 \cap S_3| - |S_3 \cap S_5| - |S_2 \cap S_5| + |S_2 \cap S_3 \cap S_5|$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="656" height="18" /><br /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/5/49525cc67578276e81303db6e05daa42698d2833.png" class="latex" alt="$= 500 + 333 + 200 - 166 - 66 - 100 + 33 = 734$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="376" height="14" /></div>
<p>Substituting, we find that our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/1/ec18a0d8bd5db483a7fdf297f3745548794c25cb.png" class="latex" alt="$1000 - 165 - 1 - 734 = 100 \Longrightarrow \mathrm{(A)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="283" height="18" />.
</p>
