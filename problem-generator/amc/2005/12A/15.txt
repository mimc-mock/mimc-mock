<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> be a <a href="/wiki/index.php/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a> of a <a href="/wiki/index.php/Circle" title="Circle">circle</a> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> be a point on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/6/d66012ff3add95592a7adf808785b1d19b0157af.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot AC = BC$" width="103" height="13" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> be <a href="/wiki/index.php/Point" title="Point">points</a> on the circle such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/1/831a6714862377b53fa5b94a7c71656ef314c6c6.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{DC} \perp \overline{AB}$" width="82" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/9/699784c989c3db382520e6f92819e062d239f504.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{DE}$" width="30" height="15" /> is a second diameter. What is the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Ratio" title="Ratio">ratio</a> of the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/5/8c5425baeec765203a664dc6bd0f0562f2bf485a.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DCE$" width="60" height="13" /> to the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/5/f75d6d58b03031bd7cb52a7ca886a8ddf369d21c.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD$" width="59" height="13" />?

</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/5/b65e954ffeb43344ce3a11002adb8702cb8c5871.png" class="latex" alt="$(\text {A}) \ \frac {1}{6} \qquad (\text {B}) \ \frac {1}{4} \qquad (\text {C})\ \frac {1}{3} \qquad (\text {D}) \ \frac {1}{2} \qquad (\text {E})\ \frac {2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="355" height="37" />

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h3>
<p>Notice that the bases of both triangles are diameters of the circle. Hence the ratio of the areas is just the ratio of the heights of the triangles, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/1/401144b792a2746c63e77090072877c56c4c3cd6.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{CF}{CD}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="32" height="37" /> (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> is the foot of the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">perpendicular</a> from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/5/6d5c9afa92ae3180f02754c177e530d9d0f8b4d4.png" class="latex" alt="$DE$" width="29" height="12" />).
</p><p>Call the radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />. Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/d/7ad60ab6f675889b704a909435dce31ac803ded4.png" class="latex" alt="$AC = \frac 13(2r) = \frac 23r$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="142" height="37" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/a/e1afb969c120e6e02f0eb65a39044dde0466a801.png" class="latex" alt="$CO = \frac 13r$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="74" height="37" />. Using the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Pythagorean_Theorem" title="Pythagorean Theorem">Pythagorean Theorem</a> in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/b/c9b314163f3d7b82d78944283deda01130ab862d.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle OCD$" width="60" height="13" />, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/6/8e6a9519bb221cc0441dd655752b03d7db8f53a4.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{9}r^2 + CD^2 = r^2 \Longrightarrow CD = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}3r$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="268" height="41" />. 
</p><p>Now we have to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/d/07d29465728e92952374a857beaee91665d8dbd9.png" class="latex" alt="$CF$" width="28" height="12" />. Notice <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/4/72461d67309f9a806da3a8eef4f2091be7d92e75.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle OCD \sim \triangle OFC$" width="143" height="13" />, so we can write the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Proportion" title="Proportion">proportion</a>:
</p>
<div style="text-align:center;"><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/2/02291691270e57eea057ea561416318b8de9fc01.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{OF}{OC} = \frac{OC}{OD}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="89" height="37" /><br /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/1/6e1dcd67ba34e71f9f880510df1fd482941f6e37.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{OF}{\frac{1}{3}r} = \frac{\frac{1}{3}r}{r}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="78" height="47" /><br /><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/4/5a4dcf36a069d28f1265cf8c1b825c9ae46c0cbd.png" class="latex" alt="$OF = \frac 19r$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="74" height="37" /></div>
<p>By the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Pythagorean_Theorem" title="Pythagorean Theorem">Pythagorean Theorem</a> in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/8/ba8b5e16bc3f976a59179d94025ac83b7e148d9f.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle OFC$" width="59" height="13" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2ae3e933df9f7c557c173279431c23929b55874.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{1}{9}r\right)^2 + CF^2 = \left(\frac{1}{3}r\right)^2 \Longrightarrow CF = \sqrt{\frac{8}{81}r^2} = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{9}r$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="411" height="46" />.
</p><p>Our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/a/e6a8b0efbd9f2716a5934de0300d216ed5638f92.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{CF}{CD} = \frac{\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{9}r}{\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}r} = \frac 13 \Longrightarrow \mathrm{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="201" height="53" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h3>
<p>Let the center of the circle be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" />.
</p><p>Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/1/3c125b03b11e8951d15b67d2db698d43722adb25.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot AC = BC \Rightarrow 3 \cdot AC = AB$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="233" height="13" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" /> is midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/4/384a0d8b4d1583fa0e58c80a6f5e0e07a1834adb.png" class="latex" alt="$AB \Rightarrow \frac{3}{2}AC = AO \Rightarrow CO = \frac{1}{3}AO \Rightarrow CO = \frac{1}{6} AB$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="394" height="37" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" /> is midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/0/2b0415be523eb9bd25e42687a3ab87001152087c.png" class="latex" alt="$DE \Rightarrow$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="52" height="13" /> Area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/e/06e3485d6a3ea109aa22fef06037ee8006d88ca8.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DCE = 2 \cdot$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="101" height="18" /> Area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/a/5/1a5af72d56950f479f9aee2556fea4e80722b5fe.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DCO = 2 \cdot (\frac{1}{6} \cdot$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="130" height="37" /> Area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/4/d642a12e94bf59d4a40238790c25cb4301ff6c43.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD) = \frac{1}{3} \cdot$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="108" height="37" /> Area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/e/8ae972e5dcef24948b63b120567513ad8afef6f8.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD \Longrightarrow \mathrm{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="125" height="18" />.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h3>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> be the radius of the circle. Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/f/7afd6715913f0847f8aa5f929acb0cfc191f023c.png" class="latex" alt="$AC+BC = 2r$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="120" height="14" /> so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/e/e9eabf7526cdb70ccd42bbd53e3a8b1db3f558fb.png" class="latex" alt="$AC = \frac{2}{3}r$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="73" height="37" />.
</p><p>By <a href="/wiki/index.php/Power_of_a_Point_Theorem" title="Power of a Point Theorem">Power of a Point Theorem</a>, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/9/219d182bba19cdc990040e3360592e10cad14aa1.png" class="latex" alt="$CD^2= AC \cdot BC = 2\cdot AC^2$" width="212" height="15" />, and thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/e/c4e6ecc297f0c98a322892287b4955e8996ed377.png" class="latex" alt="$CD = \sqrt{2} \cdot AC = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}r$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="189" height="41" />
</p><p>Then the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/5/f75d6d58b03031bd7cb52a7ca886a8ddf369d21c.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABD$" width="59" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/6/f767d889975ab2ee85b07861dd3eea614cec3f0e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2} AB \cdot CD = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}r^2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="160" height="41" />. Similarly, the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/5/8c5425baeec765203a664dc6bd0f0562f2bf485a.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DCE$" width="60" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/1/cb107489d2a0c798ffaef205108d6ffeb1a7bfc9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}(r-AC) \cdot 2 \cdot CD = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{9}r^2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="227" height="41" />, so the desired ratio is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/8/318c15552a55ea2a38b6e6bcb847015fb0ebd2a7.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{9}r^2}{\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}r^2} = \frac{1}{3} \Longrightarrow \mathrm{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="150" height="53" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h3>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/2/dd276b3fa631d279f22b09f353851f773945afe3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(2.5cm); defaultpen(fontsize(10pt)+linewidth(.8pt)); dotfactor=3; pair O=(0,0), C=(-1/3.0), B=(1,0), A=(-1,0); pair D=dir(aCos(C.x)), e=(-D.x,-D.y); pair H=(e.x,0); draw(A--B--D--cycle); draw(D--e--C); draw(unitcircle,white); drawline(D,C); dot(O); clip(unitcircle); draw(unitcircle); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,e,SSE); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,NE); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,W); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,NNW); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,SW); label(&quot;$H$&quot;,H,SE); draw(e--H,dashed); label(&quot;O&quot;,(0,0),NE); label(&quot;1&quot;,(C--O),N); label(&quot;1&quot;,(H--O),N); label(&quot;2&quot;,(A--C),N); label(&quot;2&quot;,(H--B),N); label(&quot;3&quot;,(O--D),NE); label(&quot;3&quot;,(O--e),NE); label(&quot;$2\sqrt{2}$&quot;,(D--C),W); label(&quot;$2\sqrt{2}$&quot;,(H--e),E); draw(rightanglemark(e,(e.x,0),A,2)); draw(rightanglemark(D,C,B,2));[/asy]" width="275" height="262" />
</p><p>Let the center of the circle be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/d/51da37d984564162c87710ca27bea422f657fb73.png" class="latex" alt="$O$" width="13" height="12" />.
Without loss of generality, let the radius of the circle be equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />. Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/1/a412e4abd1d6ff90b4af9947a8c4f34522538113.png" class="latex" alt="$AO=3$" width="61" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/4/3a4ccc82a383cf8fafe5a71ebb7e93dc4ad9843e.png" class="latex" alt="$OB=3$" width="62" height="12" />. As a consequence of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/4/2f4b8579e713cc3d05769addff6a69ea75e31a1c.png" class="latex" alt="$2(AC)=BC$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="104" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/d/53d084de05e313a40923ec33ee357544b1b93874.png" class="latex" alt="$AC=2$" width="60" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/e/4eed1e217bb14b892389f5ef8f60042aadba382e.png" class="latex" alt="$CO=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="61" height="13" />. Also, we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/f/7ff1d53900652da5f2d40ef5427512642e85c0b7.png" class="latex" alt="$DO$" width="29" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/b/9/db9d3474e139c8e1c2f5902c3cb42d7e47fa7e0a.png" class="latex" alt="$OE$" width="28" height="12" /> are both equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> due to the fact that they are both radii. Thus from the Pythagorean Theorem, we have DC being equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/0/340cb07f87d5e01c05ce6b271da8e0ef5999dc4d.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{3^2-1^2}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="70" height="18" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/d/71df1b44d6caa4b0f798d48ff8ee72da386e4cb8.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />. Now we know that the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/2/e/a2e886f9746495f49e9bb5c7ab9d8e393231e5c9.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABD]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="52" height="18" /> is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/2/01219418b2756b4c2ffee54e2cf6e0c56e9b06f4.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{(3+2+1)(2\sqrt{2})}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="135" height="41" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/1/b81097ac54e434963766001e47caa1dcd88003fd.png" class="latex" alt="$6\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />. Know we need to find the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/5/365f98311a8062205054014e404205f5028cbbf4.png" class="latex" alt="$[DCE]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="52" height="18" />. By simple inspection <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/b/4db3affeebe46ef1fb7262b32cb46229bda61c04.png" class="latex" alt="$[COD]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="52" height="18" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/9/b9904282b277d5afca7b6fb12f3bb7c5ff616510.png" class="latex" alt="$\cong$" style="vertical-align: 1px" width="13" height="11" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/3/ec3e82c0897d27f1e72aac92c7693b7eef19f58a.png" class="latex" alt="$[HOE]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="53" height="18" /> due to angles being equal and CPCTC. Thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/d/4cd6e79268ff9460dab5725724979fa5be76ec7d.png" class="latex" alt="$HE=2\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="89" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/a/2/0a22b9f69f301d824c4e416a1917302e543e25b7.png" class="latex" alt="$OH=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="63" height="13" />. Know we know the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/4/7f4cc6c1a0f0dbe3bea0acaafdacd0a67ad13817.png" class="latex" alt="$[CHE]=\frac{(1+1)(2\sqrt{2})}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="184" height="41" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/d/71df1b44d6caa4b0f798d48ff8ee72da386e4cb8.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="33" height="18" />. We also know that the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/1/271a94f9a12c3c2e91b038039eca8b442057daa8.png" class="latex" alt="$[OHE]=\frac{(1)(2\sqrt{2})}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="153" height="41" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/4/2c4dc1409208ff6a3039f4871d85c16763334983.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" />. <p>In my opinion, the solution below is the easiest and quickest. 
</p><p>Since both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/5/6d5c9afa92ae3180f02754c177e530d9d0f8b4d4.png" class="latex" alt="$DE$" width="29" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> are diameters, they intersect at the center of the circle. Call the point where the extension of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/e/27e525b776c49bbe7d0ae66cfc7ab8f58f40624e.png" class="latex" alt="$DC$" width="29" height="12" /> hits the circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" />. Notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/f/02fd36c4525ce8f9b57d3787062b76959749fd6a.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle DCB = 90^\circ$" width="105" height="13" />. This implies that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/2/87294a38d4e237e57f092280b19acff185ee0133.png" class="latex" alt="$DC=CF$" width="82" height="12" />. WOLG, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/8/a78e0adf52cac77dd2b56bb2531fa695e506229b.png" class="latex" alt="$DC=CF=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="115" height="13" />. Then, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/4/4/c44b5e8c33cf6e6a1646bb2418c95e972603573f.png" class="latex" alt="$[DCE]=\frac 12 bh = \frac 12 * DF*CO = \frac 12 *2*1=1$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="362" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/4/224622b42d7ed007b9840d619f13bbb01578fbdd.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABD]=\frac 12 bh = \frac 12 *AB*CD = \frac 12 *6*1 = 3$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="362" height="37" />. Thus, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/1/4e1965f8d70832ded66434c75a7bd52b75090af0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/b/6dbbac62a58811f44fda1df541fe2d2b7f43bc64.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="40" height="29" />.
</p><p>Solution by franzliszt
</p>
<h3><span id="Solution_9_(Slick_construction)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_9_.28Slick_construction.29">Solution 9 (Slick construction)</span></h3>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/a/a4a5391dc017c05f586bd83d4d41149e04428517.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(2.5cm); defaultpen(fontsize(10pt)+linewidth(.8pt)); dotfactor=3; pair O=(0,0), C=(-1/3.0), B=(1,0), A=(-1,0), X = (1/3.0); pair D=dir(aCos(C.x)), E=(-D.x,-D.y); draw(A--B--D--cycle); draw(D--E--C); draw(D--X); draw(unitcircle,white); drawline(D,C); dot(O); clip(unitcircle); draw(unitcircle); label(&quot;$E$&quot;,E,SSE); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,B,E); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,A,W); label(&quot;$D$&quot;,D,NNW); label(&quot;$C$&quot;,C,SW); label(&quot;$X$&quot;,X,NE); draw(rightanglemark(D,C,B,2));[/asy]" width="272" height="262" />
Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" /> be the reflection of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> over the center <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/7/0c79ddff6b45c6ad853d03d6231fdf0fba5ededb.png" class="latex" alt="$O.$" width="18" height="12" /> Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/d/94d38d4098cd78e06665ef7a25cfe88834aa7bec.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DXO\cong\triangle ECO$" width="145" height="13" /> by SAS, it follows that the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/9/929635b933267579ee98277d9ee450978e3ff875.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DEC$" width="60" height="13" /> is equal to the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/1/8a12e7cac877a33148a9f3927a4534a63548cc47.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DCX.$" width="65" height="13" /> However, we know that 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/f/8cf057330c97989e465f8ae9b2bf4bce75179724.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[BC = 2AC\implies CO=\frac{1}{2}AC\implies CX=AC,\]" width="368" height="37" />
so the ratio of the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/9/929635b933267579ee98277d9ee450978e3ff875.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DEC$" width="60" height="13" /> to the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/5/d958baf25fe38b664f7908093b9cad304f0d8016.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle DCX$" width="61" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/c/c2c11adb4b133828cdfb405533c42d2e6284db88.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\frac{AC\cdot DC}{2}}{\frac{3AC\cdot DC}{2}}=\frac{1}{3}\implies \boxed{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="187" height="47" />
</p>
