<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> be the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Set" title="Set">set</a> of all <a href="/wiki/index.php/Point" title="Point">points</a> with <a href="/wiki/index.php/Coordinate" class="mw-redirect" title="Coordinate">coordinates</a> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/3/c73ed093cc73a8d9a14185fa525e7067e5c3725d.png" class="latex" alt="$(x,y,z)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="58" height="18" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> are each chosen from the set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/f/38f808470bba2c75df20da05de3ede89c5c28733.png" class="latex" alt="$\{0,1,2\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="60" height="18" />. How many <a href="/wiki/index.php/Equilateral" title="Equilateral">equilateral</a> <a href="/wiki/index.php/Triangle" title="Triangle">triangles</a> all have their <a href="/wiki/index.php/Vertices" class="mw-redirect" title="Vertices">vertices</a> in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/9/49967f09ed4ec74b93360f9d3d468e76bbfe5c8c.png" class="latex" alt="$(\mathrm {A}) \ 72\qquad (\mathrm {B}) \ 76 \qquad (\mathrm {C})\ 80 \qquad (\mathrm {D}) \ 84 \qquad (\mathrm {E})\ 88$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="387" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<h3><span id="Solution_1_(non-rigorous)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1_.28non-rigorous.29">Solution 1 (non-rigorous)</span></h3>
<p>For this solution, we will just find as many solutions as possible, until it becomes intuitive that there are no more size of triangles left.
</p><p>First, try to make three of its vertices form an equilateral triangle. This we find is possible by taking any <a href="/wiki/index.php/Vertex" title="Vertex">vertex</a>, and connecting the three adjacent vertices into a triangle. This triangle will have a side length of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/4/2c4dc1409208ff6a3039f4871d85c16763334983.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" />; a quick further examination of this cube will show us that this is the only possible side length (red triangle in diagram). Each of these triangles is determined by one vertex of the cube, so in one cube we have 8 equilateral triangles. We have 8 unit cubes, and then the entire cube (green triangle), giving us 9 cubes and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/0/730a8498744a9d5b5667b49b73b3c7ef8846d2bb.png" class="latex" alt="$9 \cdot 8 = 72$" width="73" height="12" /> equilateral triangles. 
</p>
<center>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/f/5df79423812cec695f4ed646285125bf0839a518.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] import three; unitsize(1cm); size(200); currentprojection=perspective(1/3,-1,1/2); draw((0,0,0)--(2,0,0)--(2,2,0)--(0,2,0)--cycle); draw((0,0,0)--(0,0,2)); draw((0,2,0)--(0,2,2)); draw((2,2,0)--(2,2,2)); draw((2,0,0)--(2,0,2)); draw((0,0,2)--(2,0,2)--(2,2,2)--(0,2,2)--cycle); draw((2,0,0)--(0,2,0)--(0,0,2)--cycle,green); draw((1,0,0)--(0,1,0)--(0,0,1)--cycle,red); label(&quot;$x=2$&quot;,(1,0,0),S); label(&quot;$z=2$&quot;,(2,2,1),E); label(&quot;$y=2$&quot;,(2,1,0),SE); [/asy]" width="328" height="318" />
</p>
</center>
<p>NOTE: Connecting the centers of the faces will actually give an <a href="/wiki/index.php/Octahedron" title="Octahedron">octahedron</a>, not a cube, because it only has <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> vertices.
</p><p>Now, we look for any additional equilateral triangles. Connecting the midpoints of three non-adjacent, non-parallel edges also gives us more equilateral triangles (blue triangle).  Notice that picking these three edges leaves two vertices alone (labelled A and B), and that picking any two opposite vertices determine two equilateral triangles. Hence there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/f/47f801cc44543ffdcf72d5890b8791c05b38dc85.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{8 \cdot 2}{2} = 8$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="68" height="37" /> of these equilateral triangles, for a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/a/3/ea37836f7ba34b2ad5c51824ae77bf7a29452be5.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C) }80}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="65" height="28" />.
</p>
<center>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/1/fb1ab57797018d7c00bf2d7e5ac2b67c4f698629.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] import three; unitsize(1cm); size(200); currentprojection=perspective(1/3,-1,1/2); draw((0,0,0)--(2,0,0)--(2,2,0)--(0,2,0)--cycle); draw((0,0,0)--(0,0,2)); draw((0,2,0)--(0,2,2)); draw((2,2,0)--(2,2,2)); draw((2,0,0)--(2,0,2)); draw((0,0,2)--(2,0,2)--(2,2,2)--(0,2,2)--cycle); draw((1,0,0)--(2,2,1)--(0,1,2)--cycle,blue); label(&quot;$x=2$&quot;,(1,0,0),S); label(&quot;$z=2$&quot;,(2,2,1),E); label(&quot;$y=2$&quot;,(2,1,0),SE); label(&quot;$A$&quot;,(0,2,0), NW); label(&quot;$B$&quot;,(2,0,2), NW); [/asy]" width="328" height="318" />
</p>
</center>
<h3><span id="Solution_2_(rigorous)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2_.28rigorous.29">Solution 2 (rigorous)</span></h3>
<p>The three-dimensional <a href="/wiki/index.php/Distance_formula" title="Distance formula">distance formula</a> shows that the lengths of the equilateral triangle must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/4/73465c8f3b8167ea0aa8dc8bb4be67ffd7a0f12d.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{d_x^2 + d_y^2 + d_z^2}, 0 \le d_x, d_y, d_z \le 2$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="257" height="32" />, which yields the possible edge lengths of
</p>
<center><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/3/a/13a283ba1d0d9bcbc6a67af44e17e108214933e6.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{0^2+0^2+1^2}=\sqrt{1},\ \sqrt{0^2+1^2+1^2}=\sqrt{2},\ \sqrt{1^2+1^2+1^2}=\sqrt{3},$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="504" height="20" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/3/e93ce1a7967ad6683a3418129b298278d03ce271.png" class="latex" alt="$\ \sqrt{0^2+0^2+2^2}=\sqrt{4},\ \sqrt{0^2+1^2+2^2}=\sqrt{5},\ \sqrt{1^2+1^2+2^2}=\sqrt{6},$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="510" height="20" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/3/e533a7a4a11c65017f20bc60748356c924f8d882.png" class="latex" alt="$\ \sqrt{0^2+2^2+2^2}=\sqrt{8},\ \sqrt{1^2+2^2+2^2}=\sqrt{9},\ \sqrt{2^2+2^2+2^2}=\sqrt{12}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="515" height="20" /></center>
<p>Some casework shows that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/3/0b3db9c107e116f101f2a8d08eacdc28361e9012.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{2},\ \sqrt{6},\ \sqrt{8}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="100" height="20" /> are the only lengths that work, from which we can use the same counting argument as above.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Let's first try to see how many equilateral triangles we can make in a unit cube. By just doing the bash we see that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> equilateral triangles in a unit cube. Since we have 8 unit cubes we have a total of 64 equilateral triangles. Note that the big <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/e/3de3a621e2499a00a36cb1c073929f9540d41c8b.png" class="latex" alt="$2x2x2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="50" height="14" /> cube also has 8 equilateral triangles which are connected by the vertices of the cube and 8 equilateral triangles which are connected by the midpoints of each edge. So there are a total of 16 + 64 = 80. 
~coolmath_2018
</p>
