<p>An equiangular octagon has four sides of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> and four sides of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/7/cc7c4b51e06b26289d792878cbf9efac28aac903.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" />, arranged so that no two consecutive sides have the same length.  What is the area of the octagon?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/8/9/9895e95ba7df2c5db2f598f509a660fcc2022133.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) } \frac72\qquad \textbf{(B) }  \frac{7\sqrt2}{2}\qquad \textbf{(C) }  \frac{5+4\sqrt2}{2}\qquad \textbf{(D) }  \frac{4+5\sqrt2}{2}\qquad \textbf{(E) }  7$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="506" height="43" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The area of the octagon can be divided up into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> squares with side <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/4/26426d81b80928f35c5e07086f85bf599059e571.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt2}2$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> right triangles, which are half the area of each of the squares.
</p><p>Therefore, the area of the octagon is equal to the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/d/d3d002dd5fe8d54af1408b5564dcf9423ef9f4c5.png" class="latex" alt="$5+4\left(\frac12\right)=7$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="116" height="43" /> squares.
</p><p>The area of each square is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/2/fd2e4aeb74899e8882b5b032bccbfee02b994e7d.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{\sqrt2}2\right)^2=\frac12$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="99" height="56" />, so the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" /> squares is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/9/7c967d4c324487dc1314e3d96f0579b682eea252.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(A) }\frac72}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="62" height="49" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/5/5/c555a9225f3304367b0d7f6c435ec5293f27dff4.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] pair A=(0.5, 0), B=(0, 0.5), C=(0, 1.5), D=(0.5, 2), E=(1.5, 2), F=(2, 1.5), G=(2, 0.5), H=(1.5, 0); draw(A--B); draw(B--C); draw(C--D); draw(D--E);draw(E--F);draw(F--G); draw(G--H);  draw(H--A);draw(A--F, blue);draw(E--B,blue);draw(C--H, blue); draw(D--G,blue);dot(A);dot(B);dot(C);dot(D);dot(E);dot(F);dot(G);dot(H); [/asy]" width="252" height="252" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Using the diagram from above, we can extend the sides of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> to form four right triangles and the octagon, all inside a square. The right triangles are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/2/22231a5cdd4fe05a11f4b577e3b81221f3017a85.png" class="latex" alt="$45-45-90$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="98" height="13" /> triangles with hypotenuse <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/7/cc7c4b51e06b26289d792878cbf9efac28aac903.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="26" height="41" />, so the side length is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. Thus, the area of the larger square is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/b/6/8b69aa4309847d47e4aed15a0b00891106fb1807.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \cdot 2 = 4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="64" height="12" />, and the area of the four right triangles combined is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/9/2f960094315d60883495f9b74148e17487ee9584.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />, so the area of the octagon is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/d/3cd5493fd336ad0af0bb1d53d8d665a3540ba94d.png" class="latex" alt="$4-\frac{1}{2}=\boxed{\textbf{(A) }\frac{7}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="130" height="49" />
</p><p>edited by mobius247
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Refer to the following diagram:
</p><p><a href="/wiki/index.php/File:AMC10_2005A_P20.png" class="image"><img alt="AMC10 2005A P20.png" src="https://wiki-images.artofproblemsolving.com//thumb/9/9a/AMC10_2005A_P20.png/500px-AMC10_2005A_P20.png" width="500" height="541" srcset="https://wiki-images.artofproblemsolving.com//thumb/9/9a/AMC10_2005A_P20.png/750px-AMC10_2005A_P20.png 1.5x, https://wiki-images.artofproblemsolving.com//thumb/9/9a/AMC10_2005A_P20.png/1000px-AMC10_2005A_P20.png 2x" /></a>
</p><p>(Picture made on Geogebra)
</p><p><br />
Note that each square has area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/d/4cd0f9f9869b39e79527f9010293adf7bfe5210f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac14$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="11" height="37" />, and each triangle has area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/0/a505b2973908a972c4a974b76ad7d25e5b616167.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac18$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />. The total area is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/c/2cc66d9862a6a17d1d0ccf652f9f89b8b8d8b620.png" class="latex" alt="$12\cdot\frac14+4\cdot\frac18=\frac72 \Longrightarrow \boxed{\textbf{(A) } \frac72}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="240" height="49" />.
</p><p><br />
Remark: This solution requires careful drawing, and also realising that connecting lines leads to squares and isosceles right triangles (notice the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/4/2c4dc1409208ff6a3039f4871d85c16763334983.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="24" height="18" /> and realise that it is the hypotenuse of a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/2/22231a5cdd4fe05a11f4b577e3b81221f3017a85.png" class="latex" alt="$45-45-90$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="98" height="13" /> triangle with side length ratios <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/7/d27c68a9f98a38e4278f21c7dcff055bae89bee3.png" class="latex" alt="$1:1:\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="73" height="18" />.).
</p><p><br />
~JH. L
</p>
