<p>The figure shown is called a <i>trefoil</i> and is constructed by drawing circular sectors about the sides of the congruent equilateral triangles. What is the area of a trefoil whose horizontal base has length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/d/add63d336a5010d8962e6c449e606e802e144685.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(1.5cm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt)+fontsize(12pt));  pair O=(0,0), A=dir(0), B=dir(60), C=dir(120), D=dir(180); pair E=B+C;  draw(D--E--B--O--C--B--A,linetype(&quot;4 4&quot;)); draw(Arc(O,1,0,60),linewidth(1.2pt)); draw(Arc(O,1,120,180),linewidth(1.2pt)); draw(Arc(C,1,0,60),linewidth(1.2pt)); draw(Arc(B,1,120,180),linewidth(1.2pt)); draw(A--D,linewidth(1.2pt)); draw(O--dir(40),EndArrow(HookHead,4)); draw(O--dir(140),EndArrow(HookHead,4)); draw(C--C+dir(40),EndArrow(HookHead,4)); draw(B--B+dir(140),EndArrow(HookHead,4));  label(&quot;2&quot;,O,S); draw((0.1,-0.12)--(1,-0.12),EndArrow(HookHead,4),EndBar); draw((-0.1,-0.12)--(-1,-0.12),EndArrow(HookHead,4),EndBar); [/asy]" width="145" height="148" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/4/6/446510f43c76f34ea79f5a21150c0284318a8a85.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A) }\frac{1}{3}\pi+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\qquad \textbf{(B) } \frac{2}{3}\pi\qquad \textbf{(C) } \frac{2}{3}\pi+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}\qquad \textbf{(D) } \frac{2}{3}\pi+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}\qquad \textbf{(E) } \frac{2}{3}\pi+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="632" height="43" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>The area of the <i>trefoil</i> is equal to the area of a small equilateral triangle plus the area of four <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/f/29fa0a9949f018e121b2093216dd5db13d3abef0.png" class="latex" alt="$60^\circ$" width="24" height="13" /> sectors with a radius of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/d/f3d7d7e6659cac17b89e2678cb85e1d2529b70e1.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{2}=1$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="45" height="37" /> minus the area of  a small equilateral triangle. 
</p><p>This is equivalent to the area of four <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/f/29fa0a9949f018e121b2093216dd5db13d3abef0.png" class="latex" alt="$60^\circ$" width="24" height="13" /> sectors with a radius of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. 
</p><p>So the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/0/720f5a8672a4d07596501d959ae5f865b7404186.png" class="latex" alt="$4\cdot\frac{60}{360}\cdot\pi\cdot1^2 = \frac{4}{6}\cdot\pi = \boxed{\textbf{(B) }\frac{2}{3}\pi}$" style="vertical-align: -19px" width="265" height="49" />
</p>
