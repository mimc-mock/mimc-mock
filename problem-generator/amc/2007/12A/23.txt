<p>Square <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> has area <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/c/5fcb3cc6925dde1006289c6af72479a76651cef0.png" class="latex" alt="$36,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="22" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> is <a href="/wiki/index.php/Parallel" title="Parallel">parallel</a> to the <a href="/wiki/index.php/X-axis" title="X-axis">x-axis</a>. Vertices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/1/0c1e22fe09c7388d738ebd1e52fb999e0eae620f.png" class="latex" alt="$A,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="17" height="16" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> are on the graphs of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/c/f9c8c2a038ce10450d9cfa02b5c3fccb676e0716.png" class="latex" alt="$y = \log_{a}x,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="83" height="17" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/4/3c435b491930f40f8f6981433123857f3c4e854a.png" class="latex" alt="$y = 2\log_{a}x,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="95" height="17" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/b/d2bc37d33da802f30e6f9316eda75c901ce8f1bb.png" class="latex" alt="$y = 3\log_{a}x,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="95" height="17" /> respectively. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad274005e946261334794d8f86f18afe302d69f9.png" class="latex" alt="$a?$" width="17" height="12" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/1/bd13da83b605be8995acd736bd986bb7d9de5304.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ \sqrt [6]{3}\qquad \mathrm{(B)}\ \sqrt {3}\qquad \mathrm{(C)}\ \sqrt [3]{6}\qquad \mathrm{(D)}\ \sqrt {6}\qquad \mathrm{(E)}\ 6$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="404" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> be the x-coordinate of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/7/6a7d010bbff66a0c41e43310a51efbaa6bf63396.png" class="latex" alt="$x_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="16" height="10" /> be the x-coordinate of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> be the y-coordinate of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />. Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/f/2efbb20af39b9aae09ada7216da1ad8ae78c1ce1.png" class="latex" alt="$2\log_ax= y \Longrightarrow a^{y/2} = x$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="195" height="20" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/1/6/516193e638c2c9891e692d9c09dbaa264a8bd534.png" class="latex" alt="$\log_ax_2 = y \Longrightarrow x_2 = a^y = \left(a^{y/2}\right)^2 = x^2$" style="vertical-align: -6px" width="305" height="26" />. Since the distance between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/e/b/1eb5e101e858d10029d9241f2f0a9ebb2a46ae79.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2 - x - 6 = 0$" width="114" height="15" />, yielding <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/f/aef0a7c8d9e420dae286aa07ceea76e5383c6adb.png" class="latex" alt="$x = -2, 3$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="74" height="16" />.
</p><p>However, we can discard the negative root (all three <a href="/wiki/index.php/Logarithm" title="Logarithm">logarithmic</a> equations are underneath the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/8/6389a3877e2c9d09826aa0c5e5aa564d8c355afa.png" class="latex" alt="$y = 3$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="16" /> and above <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/0/7a06334068417b1781d14e8b28af827907961eb3.png" class="latex" alt="$y = 0$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="42" height="16" /> when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> is negative, hence we can't squeeze in a square of side 6). Thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/0/690ef55a607beaf2389d1ee102df817721bc2392.png" class="latex" alt="$x = 3$" width="43" height="12" />.
</p><p>Substituting back, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/5/8052da90238a08c32d91432302eddd56e9f7da8e.png" class="latex" alt="$3\log_{a}x - 2\log_{a}x = 6 \Longrightarrow a^6 = x$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="260" height="19" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/3/2/b32fb7d2aa36cb5eebd4c412e5a7bed96a85daf2.png" class="latex" alt="$a = \sqrt[6]{3}\ \ \mathrm{(A)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="96" height="21" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Notice that all of the graphs <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/c/f9c8c2a038ce10450d9cfa02b5c3fccb676e0716.png" class="latex" alt="$y = \log_{a}x,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="83" height="17" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/4/3c435b491930f40f8f6981433123857f3c4e854a.png" class="latex" alt="$y = 2\log_{a}x,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="95" height="17" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/c/38c7f8bf19a6c7f23f1969aaa863680c6407bea5.png" class="latex" alt="$y = 3\log_{a}x$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="90" height="17" /> have a domain of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/9/d898a5d78a8f9d95fdc32ce93e5146c41bf99ba3.png" class="latex" alt="$(0, \infty]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="45" height="18" />. Also notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/1/5e10f3bbd6b367ebe23b00f00f30dbfe32a5a801.png" class="latex" alt="$y = \log_{a}x$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="78" height="17" /> is the furthest to the right, as adding coefficients in front of the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/8/698c1c9cde3469a346091405ffbbcedf96609562.png" class="latex" alt="$\log$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="23" height="16" /> part only makes the graph steeper. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> is on the graph of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/1/5e10f3bbd6b367ebe23b00f00f30dbfe32a5a801.png" class="latex" alt="$y = \log_{a}x$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="78" height="17" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> is on the graph of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/7/ac7bf60cf45b6fc5d366e6500da26f11193e4453.png" class="latex" alt="$y = 2\log_{a}x$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="90" height="17" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/5/fa58bda4ac4754d1a8f7745a3518011f5a117f6f.png" class="latex" alt="$\,$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="5" height="2" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> must be to the right of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" />. We are told that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> is <a href="/wiki/index.php/Parallel" title="Parallel">parallel</a> to the <a href="/wiki/index.php/X-axis" title="X-axis">x-axis</a>. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> be the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/f/adfd9ae8a3fac031d3b8b470a52a709a23d4d6d2.png" class="latex" alt="$(x, y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="40" height="18" />. Then the points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c3355896da590fc491a10150a50416687626d7cc.png" class="latex" alt="$C$" width="14" height="12" /> are
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/f/02f6707a31e22b6b8295534eea5913ff3eeb8627.png" class="latex" alt="$(x-6, y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="72" height="18" />
</p><p>and
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/d/34d039dc354bbef36278a84d4dfd06a59b76a1f0.png" class="latex" alt="$(x-6, y+6)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="103" height="18" />
</p><p>respectively.
</p><p>Substituting these coordinates into the equations given yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/c/f9c8c2a038ce10450d9cfa02b5c3fccb676e0716.png" class="latex" alt="$y = \log_{a}x,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="83" height="17" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/9/ef910bef794cd0298664bbfddbe761521e44f9a8.png" class="latex" alt="$y = 2\log_{a}x-6,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="126" height="17" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/b/a8b2d0ac3ac3379af7dfc5bd797d553d78be2948.png" class="latex" alt="$y+6 = 3\log_{a}x-6$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="153" height="17" />. Rearranging a bit, we get the following equations:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/a/84af8e3f157adf4d651cd8f7bceaf97231587873.png" class="latex" alt="$1) a^y = x$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="68" height="18" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/8/608de3bb111ccd836d7ed25b5822717e4bbe7e00.png" class="latex" alt="$2) a^y = (x-6)^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="119" height="19" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/3/523cd2bfe6769ead7c3c249c137ee9bc912284d4.png" class="latex" alt="$3) a^{y+6} = (x-6)^3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="136" height="19" />
</p><p>Using equations 1 and 2, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/4/5842716e32a978b5b19bdd1d6098202e006d0376.png" class="latex" alt="$x=(x-6)^2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="96" height="19" />. Solving yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/1/9f170d2d3d9787e5ed4e1ae988e4d92c76088a93.png" class="latex" alt="$x=9, x=-4$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="109" height="16" />. However, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/d/72d85c6959aaabfff6e0104544ff25c1445f3f1e.png" class="latex" alt="$-4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="23" height="12" /> is extraneous so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/1/5b1d8cdec48abe8293a4169a8afce4fc8a8348e7.png" class="latex" alt="$x=9$" width="43" height="12" /> (also because we know <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> has to have a positive <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-coordinate). Using the second and third equations, we get
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/7/857debb0a138243c4e937a87da92ab6ff43ee524.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a^{y+6}}{a^y} = \frac{(x-6)^3}{(x-6)^2}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="128" height="43" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/3/843622567e12d686e4e2f94ddfcb444e8ecde0d2.png" class="latex" alt="$\Rightarrow$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="10" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/d/22d29aab85d34b8a079ffe7efd973b18921282a5.png" class="latex" alt="$a^6 = x-6$" width="82" height="15" />.
</p><p>Plugging in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/2/5c25ebef7354a0cfebc07de7743cdc1961e52b6c.png" class="latex" alt="$a^6 = 3$" width="50" height="15" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/3/b734135bf493062a7dfa56adc16a989a94b2ec50.png" class="latex" alt="$a= \sqrt[6]{3} \Rightarrow \boxed{\text{A}}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="111" height="23" />.
</p>
