<p>For each <a href="/wiki/index.php/Integer" title="Integer">integer</a> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/2/65262bf247c4a3c6114a8223d249dc9702619d3e.png" class="latex" alt="$n&gt;1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="43" height="12" />, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/6/2663041fd2b9fbd85c85ad832f67c7a743a09c52.png" class="latex" alt="$F(n)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="37" height="18" /> be the number of solutions to the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Equation" title="Equation">equation</a> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/8/fb833752680991313822eb3d8032c721281c6846.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin{x}=\sin{(nx)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="119" height="18" /> on the interval <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/e/ece27ba559bd93cdf2416aa0f88fc8d7d85e5791.png" class="latex" alt="$[0,\pi]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="36" height="18" />. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/e/f3ece9235b34a4c79069ed978a0ef577a213f53b.png" class="latex" alt="$\sum_{n=2}^{2007} F(n)$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="67" height="50" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/9/27946199e23339ffd2ce90deb054a2d894d0b5dd.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ 2014524$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="96" height="18" />  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/b/ffbad78b8d888ceda3d0391986e5cb66b7538b27.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(B)}\ 2015028$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="95" height="18" />   <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/f/4ffe6f5b6a2a66701fd6611677fa16f711b503b8.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(C)}\ 2015033$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="95" height="18" />   <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/5/c952c351f30254ebfdaa9c06583f11361907e6cd.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(D)}\ 2016532$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="96" height="18" />   <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/5/cd504d1f9fb085161135bbf0af0c420408761e9f.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(E)}\ 2017033$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="94" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/9/299b06b28a37a6585f0bc0e294786526d2c6dd7d.png" class="latex" alt="$F(2)=3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="70" height="18" />
</p><p>By looking at various graphs, we obtain that, for most of the graphs
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/0/40002b02cef55c16f5abdbe2af1fe7d5db7238e3.png" class="latex" alt="$F(n) = n + 1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="104" height="18" />
</p><p>Notice that the solutions are basically reflections across <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/4/d04f02535ba0d4d2e88dd26b9ce36c7a24cd7057.png" class="latex" alt="$x = \frac{\pi}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="48" height="33" />.
However, when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/e/77eb0c927773ed1b909a0d12b086879d925df5be.png" class="latex" alt="$n \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="115" height="18" />, the middle apex of the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Sine" class="mw-redirect" title="Sine">sine</a> curve touches the sine curve at the top only one time (instead of two reflected points), so we get here <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/c/7fc0f9ff12e6653f1b2127d8401e100e1ea17474.png" class="latex" alt="$F(n) = n$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="74" height="18" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/4/414e0d13e3181f2ea337e5ac21344b35a6cea4d1.png" class="latex" alt="$3+4+5+5+7+8+9+9+\cdots+2008$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="329" height="14" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/a/cea565afe801cb75d9a04727f62641edcf029f60.png" class="latex" alt="$= (1+2+3+4+5+\cdots+2008) - 3 - 501$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="349" height="18" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/e/dfeb8aa8a290998263b28552a670691f4d36fd27.png" class="latex" alt="$= \frac{(2008)(2009)}{2} - 504 = 2016532$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="260" height="38" />  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/b/bfbf4df7c25681bb178ddc50ee0c0d656aa25b5e.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(D)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/1/871a5070b1c511018316319158347e8bfae88996.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\sin nx - \sin x = 2\left( \cos \frac {n + 1}{2}x\right) \left( \sin \frac {n - 1}{2}x\right)\]" width="358" height="43" />
So <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/b/66b303a498a42967342e1075e8a2f92b7109f309.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin nx = \sin x$" width="106" height="12" /> if and only if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/6/27624d981a9132c2bac6f6a3040af07897dc39e2.png" class="latex" alt="$\cos \frac {n + 1}{2}x = 0$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="116" height="37" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/1/cd113c5d8f8099f296420a9b8639175524220d07.png" class="latex" alt="$\sin \frac {n - 1}{2}x = 0$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="114" height="37" />.
</p><p>The first occurs whenever <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/b/94b664ed5eb79f2edd40a3c500740552c55d6b83.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {n + 1}{2}x = (j + 1/2)\pi$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="163" height="37" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/b/93b3920d0859dc9ef2ce948cb6b1b5680a2afb49.png" class="latex" alt="$x = \frac {(2j + 1)\pi}{n + 1}$" style="vertical-align: -14px" width="111" height="40" /> for some nonnegative integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/2/8122aa89ea6e80784c6513d22787ad86e36ad0cc.png" class="latex" alt="$j$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="8" height="16" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/5/9e563dadf9f47027587e05c789bc70e420342fa5.png" class="latex" alt="$x\leq \pi$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="45" height="14" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/c/83c8b2f5a58a86236c7606c9faf56a4e470c07bf.png" class="latex" alt="$j\leq n/2$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="62" height="18" />. So there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/7/917e848f321ab59a3251d414e25e183d5cea3916.png" class="latex" alt="$1 + \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="73" height="19" /> solutions in this case.
</p><p>The second occurs whenever <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/e/43eb140768f5ca9c0f3f04cacdf9c6944a7fd0e6.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {n - 1}{2}x = k\pi$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="101" height="37" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/2/002bba2d90d7e94d5526726cbb7a15d452c503f4.png" class="latex" alt="$x = \frac {2k\pi}{n - 1}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="79" height="38" /> for some nonnegative integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c325612684d41304b9751c175df7bcc0f61f64f.png" class="latex" alt="$k$" width="9" height="12" />. Here <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/f/7/6f7d4fd0ca7a05e4b1ce267b8bf1daad7bc1d3f5.png" class="latex" alt="$k\leq \frac {n - 1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="79" height="37" /> so that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/b/fbb0a09b783fddf89df548af3204aa90f1a01e90.png" class="latex" alt="$\left\lfloor \frac {n + 1}{2}\right\rfloor$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="62" height="43" /> solutions here.
</p><p>However, we overcount intersections. These occur whenever
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/e/eced9caeb4854046455cc26885c31312bfde4ca2.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac {2j + 1}{n + 1} = \frac {2k}{n - 1}\]" width="120" height="38" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/d/afdc681460be8e130fe26710fb88978f356aa940.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[k = \frac {(2j + 1)(n - 1)}{2(n + 1)}\]" width="155" height="42" />
which is equivalent to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/9/fe9ded691a73fa2d057b2dd3fe1369d751620aab.png" class="latex" alt="$2(n + 1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="64" height="18" /> dividing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/7/3e7d1dfb9bf5ddbe1ca55b64ccd367cda072be6c.png" class="latex" alt="$(2j + 1)(n - 1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="118" height="18" />. If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> is even, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/7/3e7d1dfb9bf5ddbe1ca55b64ccd367cda072be6c.png" class="latex" alt="$(2j + 1)(n - 1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="118" height="18" /> is odd, so this never happens. If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/7/7c7e7ab44ee8a196de9a4c267313e6d47e616a65.png" class="latex" alt="$n\equiv 3\pmod{4}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="115" height="18" />, then there won't be intersections either, since a multiple of 8 can't divide a number which is not even a multiple of 4.
</p><p>This leaves <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/6/3b60316060abced4173a0697d5c6509a3c085122.png" class="latex" alt="$n\equiv 1\pmod{4}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="115" height="18" />. In this case, the divisibility becomes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/4/bc4be239d67316fd329216074256a25e37b77c8a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {n + 1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="44" height="37" /> dividing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/8/d/68d05bef44b94802b6a7c80030cb06b1e0c509b9.png" class="latex" alt="$(2j + 1)\frac {n - 1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="107" height="37" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/4/bc4be239d67316fd329216074256a25e37b77c8a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {n + 1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="44" height="37" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/c/85c29bad8d95dc4bbfec75e4ec0ef43c7acac3ce.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {n - 1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="44" height="37" /> are relatively prime (subtracting twice the second number from the first gives 1), <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/4/bc4be239d67316fd329216074256a25e37b77c8a.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {n + 1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="44" height="37" /> must divide <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/8/c/18c7c6ab28cd83033ec7e0b4a8311dd1c537aa70.png" class="latex" alt="$2j + 1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="48" height="16" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/0/af017b12bbb62622622736e3dd6430ca0ab37dc9.png" class="latex" alt="$j\leq \frac {n - 1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="78" height="37" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/c/e1c10f1d2769bc8514c550080a97aec655d16dad.png" class="latex" alt="$2j + 1\leq n &lt; 2\cdot \frac {n + 1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="175" height="37" />. Then there is only one intersection, namely when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/b/90b0e6cc42275e3cfe1e445cfbbcaf4268072f9e.png" class="latex" alt="$j = \frac {n - 1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="78" height="37" />.
</p><p>Therefore we find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/6/2663041fd2b9fbd85c85ad832f67c7a743a09c52.png" class="latex" alt="$F(n)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="37" height="18" /> is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/8/4e8cf2150b1aefa2f1b3cf4dd552b905b9df4a38.png" class="latex" alt="$1 + \lfloor n/2 \rfloor + \left \lfloor \frac {n + 1}{2}\right\rfloor = n + 1$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="231" height="43" />, unless <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/6/3b60316060abced4173a0697d5c6509a3c085122.png" class="latex" alt="$n\equiv 1\pmod{4}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="115" height="18" />, in which case it is one less, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />. The problem may then be finished as in Solution 1.
</p><p>Note from Williamgolly:
An easier way to see that there is an extra point of intersection at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/e/77eb0c927773ed1b909a0d12b086879d925df5be.png" class="latex" alt="$n \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="115" height="18" /> is that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/5/055febd839bf14ac6a2bdef8bde744c2c1783896.png" class="latex" alt="$v_2(2j+1)=0,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="116" height="18" /> so we must have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/3/173ea34002bfeecc5c7a46b7a0a17d5060812a52.png" class="latex" alt="$v_2(n-1)&gt;v_2(n+1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="168" height="18" /> since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/2/ba25db73b49f24af50919bd482d7307e684e5c28.png" class="latex" alt="$v_2(2k) \geq 1 &gt;v_2(2j+1).$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="190" height="18" />  Therefore, we suspect that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/7/e/77eb0c927773ed1b909a0d12b086879d925df5be.png" class="latex" alt="$n \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="115" height="18" /> has an extra point of intersection.  Testing, we see this is true.
</p>
