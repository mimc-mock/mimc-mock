<p>Four circles of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> are each tangent to two sides of a square and externally tangent to a circle of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />, as shown. What is the area of the square?
</p>
<center>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/7/23759fb5d0c074107da579ab5ce358f0aa30b5a0.png" class="latexcenter" alt="[asy] unitsize(5mm); defaultpen(linewidth(.8pt)+fontsize(8pt)); real h=3*sqrt(2)/2; pair O0=(0,0), O1=(h,h), O2=(-h,h), O3=(-h,-h), O4=(h,-h); pair X=O0+2*dir(30), Y=O2+dir(45); draw((-h-1,-h-1)--(-h-1,h+1)--(h+1,h+1)--(h+1,-h-1)--cycle); draw(Circle(O0,2)); draw(Circle(O1,1)); draw(Circle(O2,1)); draw(Circle(O3,1)); draw(Circle(O4,1)); draw(O0--X); draw(O2--Y); label(&quot;$2$&quot;,midpoint(O0--X),NW); label(&quot;$1$&quot;,midpoint(O2--Y),SE); [/asy]" width="152" height="152" />
</p>
</center>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/b/b4b160d78586d1536ea827a2b8f1bc82d0e7f4f7.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{(A)}\ 32 \qquad \text{(B)}\ 22 + 12\sqrt {2}\qquad \text{(C)}\ 16 + 16\sqrt {3}\qquad \text{(D)}\ 48 \qquad \text{(E)}\ 36 + 16\sqrt {2}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="570" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Draw a square connecting the centers of the four small circles of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. This square has a diagonal of length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" />, as it includes the diameter of the big circle of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> and two radii of the small circles of radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. Therefore, the side length of this square is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/e/9ae6a28635f7bc2f1d620576f68cea4d8a917152.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{6}{\sqrt{2}} = 3\sqrt{2}.\]" width="87" height="42" /> The side length of the larger square is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> units greater than the one found by connecting the midpoints, so its side length is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/8/2d8d2199d03dc28d51bf35318bfa8e414d50d207.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[2 + 3\sqrt{2}.\]" width="67" height="18" /> The area of the larger square is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/e/f7eef8908ce046151531150a7f8cf21c3ce53d69.png" class="latex" alt="$(2+3\sqrt{2})^2 = 22 + 12\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="193" height="21" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/0/7400154b2e9fb4e72cd6e18c14b9a4e964737ed0.png" class="latex" alt="$(B).$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="32" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We draw the diagonal of the square. This diagonal yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/7/f57d642e33f5b59a4aa2614f138cfe87ea478ffd.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{2}+1+1+2+2=2\sqrt{2}+6$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="247" height="18" />. We know that the side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37bba504894945c07a32f5496d74299a37aa51c.png" class="latex" alt="$s$" width="8" height="8" /> in terms of the diagonal <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/a/96ab646de7704969b91c76a214126b45f2b07b25.png" class="latex" alt="$d$" width="9" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/8/7e8e00339ae98c3c7ad90164e5dc67345929655d.png" class="latex" alt="$s=\frac{d}{\sqrt{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="59" height="42" />, so our side length is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/1/5e11d5317e0c3204e8a90a5a5851ad8ccaa858b6.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2\sqrt{2}+6}{\sqrt{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="67" height="45" />. However, we are trying to look for the area, so squaring <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/1/5e11d5317e0c3204e8a90a5a5851ad8ccaa858b6.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2\sqrt{2}+6}{\sqrt{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="67" height="45" /> yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/f/33f461ab91ddd73e8c976935c21bcc6421efcf3d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{44+24\sqrt{2}}{2}=\boxed{\text{(B)}22+12\sqrt{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="230" height="41" />
</p>
