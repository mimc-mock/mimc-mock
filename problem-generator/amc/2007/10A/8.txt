<p>Triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840cb55c82434b9ae047760636a017e9441e6ca5.png" class="latex" alt="$ADC$" width="43" height="13" /> are <a href="/wiki/index.php/Isosceles" title="Isosceles">isosceles</a> with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/4/5f4c25cb7b6078137512af0317bd262160bd0319.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=BC$" width="81" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/4/204f957f90cc34dd19937e216f02c5a38663869d.png" class="latex" alt="$AD=DC$" width="83" height="13" />. Point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> is inside triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" />, angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> measures 40 degrees, and angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840cb55c82434b9ae047760636a017e9441e6ca5.png" class="latex" alt="$ADC$" width="43" height="13" /> measures 140 degrees. What is the degree measure of angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/5/7e538978649c14e1616fac744e983b5981d0ea8e.png" class="latex" alt="$BAD$" width="43" height="13" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/8/e/f8eaa193932986bc16abd141087d0d8518794c76.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(A)}\ 20\qquad \mathrm{(B)}\ 30\qquad \mathrm{(C)}\ 40\qquad \mathrm{(D)}\ 50\qquad \mathrm{(E)}\ 60$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="387" height="18" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/index.php/File:2007_AMC12A-6.png" class="image"><img alt="2007 AMC12A-6.png" src="https://wiki-images.artofproblemsolving.com//f/f3/2007_AMC12A-6.png" width="274" height="360" /></a>
</p><p>We angle chase and find out that:
</p>
<ul><li><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/d/d3db1967c28ff9f582faeb618bac08c6eca44309.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle DAC=\frac{180-140}{2} = 20$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="203" height="37" /></li>
<li><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/a/25a9ea4040a2fe5476030462ed6280d3b43bd52d.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BAC=\frac{180-40}{2} = 70$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="193" height="37" /></li>
<li><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/2/5825cc126ace88f94b7cad69198544acfceebb36.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BAD=\angle BAC- \angle DAC=50\ \mathrm{(D)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="289" height="18" /></li></ul>
<p>~minor edits by mobius247
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/index.php/File:Mihir_Borkar_Solution_2007_AMC_10A_Problem_6_p_2.png" class="image"><img alt="Mihir Borkar Solution 2007 AMC 10A Problem 6 p 2.png" src="https://wiki-images.artofproblemsolving.com//f/ff/Mihir_Borkar_Solution_2007_AMC_10A_Problem_6_p_2.png" width="244" height="320" /></a>
</p><p>Since triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> is isosceles we know that angle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/e/41eacfc5b8e44c6ff24b18f97a7eb6956ab13381.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BAC = \angle BCA$" width="135" height="13" />.
</p><p>Also since triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840cb55c82434b9ae047760636a017e9441e6ca5.png" class="latex" alt="$ADC$" width="43" height="13" /> is isosceles we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/8/af840ccac2e6a97aa799681cc6b37a49df8c17f0.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle DAC = \angle DCA$" width="136" height="13" />.
</p><p>This implies that  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/4/ee41f88d48541637453e7b0cea6165448c4ae446.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BAD = \angle BCD$" width="138" height="13" />.
</p><p>Then the sum of the interior angles of quadrilateral <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/e/f9efaf9474c5658c4089523e2aff4e11488f8603.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCD$" width="57" height="13" /> is  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/9/899449d82422c40b8c016d45db6d171167be457b.png" class="latex" alt="$40 + 220 + 2\angle BAD = 360$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="206" height="14" />.
</p><p>Solving the equation, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/2/d92eb8f2ae17a7d6c66c35e2f94fb37db5cc3ca7.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle BAD = 50$" width="98" height="13" />.
</p><p>Therefore the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/b/bfbf4df7c25681bb178ddc50ee0c0d656aa25b5e.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm{(D)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="18" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Using the previous drawing we know that ACD and DAC are both equal to 20 (40/2 or (180-40)/2). We also know that BAC and BCA are both 70 or (180-40)/2. Thus BDA is 70-20 or 50. Easy. -RealityWrites
</p>
