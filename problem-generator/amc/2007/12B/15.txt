<p>The geometric series <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/8/8787cbcb8f1168d424245c916cae5453aeb6e355.png" class="latex" alt="$a+ar+ar^2\ldots$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="121" height="16" /> has a sum of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" />, and the terms involving odd powers of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> have a sum of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/7/ee7b137b20ca10fdd93861cfa276a285f2385696.png" class="latex" alt="$a+r$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="40" height="12" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/f/0/8f0809af0d16a7cab0580c3f0315eaf3548d5f06.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{4}{3}\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ \frac{12}{7}\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ \frac{3}{2}\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ \frac{7}{3}\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ \frac{5}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="376" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>The sum of an infinite geometric series is given by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/2/d92b8c5d55f66f20ed3f225d99a712796b2efced.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a}{1-r}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="42" height="33" /> where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/d/c7d457e388298246adb06c587bccd419ea67f7e8.png" class="latex" alt="$a$" width="9" height="8" /> is the first term and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> is the common ratio.
</p><p>In this series, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/f/f3fa945f28e072b2dc1ff032e8f91419f658714f.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a}{1-r} = 7$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="77" height="33" />
</p><p>The series with odd powers of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> is given as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/e/d7e66addf3f0977ed2667bd9ec0f8919e3857dfd.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[ar + ar^3 + ar^5 ...\]" width="127" height="16" />
</p><p>Its sum can be given by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/4/e0470959a853394e455bca86cb99cc04b36ed7b5.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{ar}{1-r^2} = 3$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="84" height="33" />
</p><p>Doing a little algebra
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/4/0d4cc8f8d29fe1e84828d5636af25dce6326e957.png" class="latex" alt="$ar = 3(1-r)(1+r)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="158" height="18" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/4/b7406741a3acb4f4b58653694e2c7c4fb074a342.png" class="latex" alt="$ar = 3\left(\frac{a}{7}\right)(1+r)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="145" height="33" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/2/8/428fe059654dcbc0b9c01f344643cbe41ae14b5c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{7}{3}r = 1 + r$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="85" height="37" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/6/b06162ef4a193b84be6dad1629621a9f16ca47ea.png" class="latex" alt="$r = \frac{3}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="44" height="38" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/a/6ea61b4285f37ae0e6e42ecf2b70762fd4a31c4a.png" class="latex" alt="$a = 7(1-r) = \frac{7}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="133" height="37" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/8/6e86f3d69b6ac1b12f7a60a94618754e8505d637.png" class="latex" alt="$a + r =\boxed{ \frac{5}{2}} \Rightarrow \mathrm{(E)}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="142" height="48" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>The given series can be decomposed as follows: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/2/552b67239ee7bc3d1b7f6ff1458304254abdeab8.png" class="latex" alt="$(a + ar + ar^2 + \ldots) = (a + ar^2 + ar^4 + \ldots) + (ar + ar^3 + ar^5 + \ldots)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="535" height="19" />
</p><p>Clearly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/d/e6daa5a43026b7139ab382de22bbd8c8f77aa66d.png" class="latex" alt="$(a + ar^2 + ar^4 + \ldots) = (ar + ar^3 + ar^5 + \ldots)/r = 3/r$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="427" height="19" />. We obtain that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/6/126932857619b3dd44c47623a9fd32c810a6b6cb.png" class="latex" alt="$7 = 3/r + 3$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="91" height="18" />, hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/6/b06162ef4a193b84be6dad1629621a9f16ca47ea.png" class="latex" alt="$r = \frac{3}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="44" height="38" />.
</p><p>Then from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/7/f37e06f6ec429efeb8a9266cc86eee3fa4f0473d.png" class="latex" alt="$7 = (a + ar + ar^2 + \ldots) = \frac{a}{1-r}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="256" height="33" /> we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/e/daeff2538e8f838e7d592be5f0e2bc3559e5a1db.png" class="latex" alt="$a=\frac{7}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="45" height="37" />, and thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/9/2/3921c9705652058f8e6876e337580c0f57a3b1f1.png" class="latex" alt="$a + r = \boxed{\frac{5}{2}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="89" height="48" />.
</p>
