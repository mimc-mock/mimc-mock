<p>Two particles move along the edges of equilateral <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> in the direction <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/e/cfe840ebf4592b958ba4c17b5f42b74b2d93403b.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[A\Rightarrow B\Rightarrow C\Rightarrow A,\]" width="144" height="16" /> starting simultaneously and moving at the same speed. One starts at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />, and the other starts at the midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/3/e33fe7d65facd8868f58b6e94ddc7f153a5a3f9f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BC}$" width="29" height="15" />. The midpoint of the line segment joining the two particles traces out a path that encloses a region <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" />. What is the ratio of the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> to the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/b/20b412242d9c03c51b670762891f5d731d59e555.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm {(A)} \frac{1}{16}\qquad \mathrm {(B)} \frac{1}{12}\qquad \mathrm {(C)} \frac{1}{9}\qquad \mathrm {(D)} \frac{1}{6}\qquad \mathrm {(E)} \frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="351" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>First, notice that each of the midpoints of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" />,<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/5/e657259c0995d2c36d46744bb4aa81471e6575ec.png" class="latex" alt="$CA$" width="28" height="13" /> are on the locus.
Suppose after some time the particles have each been displaced by a short distance <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />, to new positions <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/9/1090530ae883b627e090bfb5e450ef796febca40.png" class="latex" alt="$A&#39;$" width="17" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/b/76b4f897205beb16e266ea66d8ae8ece27cf5af8.png" class="latex" alt="$M&#39;$" width="23" height="14" /> respectively. Consider <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/f/cbfd0ad557c8b9d1f75e30bf0d6782d2202ff4eb.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABM$" width="63" height="13" /> and drop a perpendicular from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/b/76b4f897205beb16e266ea66d8ae8ece27cf5af8.png" class="latex" alt="$M&#39;$" width="23" height="14" /> to hit <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57ee5125358c0606c9b588580ddfa66f83e607b7.png" class="latex" alt="$AB$" width="27" height="13" /> at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/5/ce58e4af225c93d08606c26554caaa5ae32edeba.png" class="latex" alt="$Y$" width="14" height="12" />. Then, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/0/6b0497140daa907c26f622dfce78a0d85ee2bc7b.png" class="latex" alt="$BA&#39;=1-x$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="98" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/2/762be9f1c35d8c110abcb6c18426d1994f44db01.png" class="latex" alt="$BM&#39;=1/2+x$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="122" height="18" />. From here, we can use properties of a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/c/47c78b85058ee5e17f88e2cde302e48aad826b35.png" class="latex" alt="$30-60-90$" width="98" height="12" /> triangle to determine the lengths <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/c/7cc32c8ebe0dcf5b250d24ae2e9ee340084a1be5.png" class="latex" alt="$YA&#39;$" width="31" height="14" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/d/9cd1f388a64eb963a0cac409df0381c5941c3360.png" class="latex" alt="$YM&#39;$" width="37" height="14" /> as monomials in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />. Thus, the locus of the midpoint will be linear between each of the three special points mentioned above. It follows that the locus consists of the only triangle with those three points as vertices. (A cheaper way to find the shape of the region is to look at the answer choices: if it were any sort of conic section then the ratio would not generally be rational.)
Comparing inradii between this "midpoint" triangle and the original triangle, the area contained by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/f/eff43e84f8a3bcf7b6965f0a3248bc4d3a9d0cd4.png" class="latex" alt="$R$" width="14" height="12" /> must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/0/e706063a48e535b6b93fc4c3560e027c63fa7235.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)} \frac{1}{16}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="50" height="37" /> of the total area.
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/index.php/WLOG" class="mw-redirect" title="WLOG">WLOG</a>, let point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> be located on the origin. Let side <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/0/840e2b592390eb6ec918fa6f3292716ce170de66.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AB}$" width="28" height="15" /> be on the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-axis, and let point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/5/6457ca2dcd40dc21d9aa0db18c66bbac9af4d899.png" class="latex" alt="$(0, 1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />.
When one of the ants is on a vertex, the other will be on the midpoint of the opposite side. Marking these points gives us a rough view of what seem to be the vertices of a smaller equilateral triangle, that is concentric with the larger one. After observing(or imagining) the ants moving, we find that the area enclosed is indeed an equilateral triangle.
</p><p>Let the midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/3/e33fe7d65facd8868f58b6e94ddc7f153a5a3f9f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BC}$" width="29" height="15" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" />. Then we can find the coordinates of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> using the equations of two lines: the extension of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/b/cfbb4c52e011ceed6e813118a7d59e931af444a2.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AD}$" width="29" height="15" /> and the extension of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/3/e33fe7d65facd8868f58b6e94ddc7f153a5a3f9f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BC}$" width="29" height="15" />. The slope of any line is simply the tangent of the angle between the line and the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-axis. The angle between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/b/cfbb4c52e011ceed6e813118a7d59e931af444a2.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AD}$" width="29" height="15" /> and the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />-axis is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/3/e73393067e6d6e401a5354accc7a730c7a2977aa.png" class="latex" alt="$30^{\circ}$" width="24" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/e/7/ee754884b6ded56c1b315b7980686af5737f572f.png" class="latex" alt="$tan(30) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="110" height="41" />.
</p><p>Therefore the equation of the line containing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/b/cfbb4c52e011ceed6e813118a7d59e931af444a2.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{AD}$" width="29" height="15" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/f/04fab97c7d506801500f41d18ad2511be5b00034.png" class="latex" alt="$y= \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}x.$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="76" height="41" />
</p><p>The slope of the line containing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/3/3/e33fe7d65facd8868f58b6e94ddc7f153a5a3f9f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{BC}$" width="29" height="15" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/b/a4bf8757013ec8c2feb63034e350c3ce9985f67e.png" class="latex" alt="$-\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="39" height="18" />, and it goes through the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/4/754e946d1a160f767f0f61cbd9460401c2d1c17f.png" class="latex" alt="$(1, 0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="38" height="18" />. After solving for the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />-intercept, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/c/b7c90675b9d2f1f65472c8dbe17e5ef74d7aa24f.png" class="latex" alt="$y = -\sqrt{3}x+\sqrt{3}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="129" height="20" />. Setting these equal to each other yields
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/f/d0f9d60f73c83ce82e8c596cd8ce006c142b0ebe.png" class="latex" alt="$D\left(\frac{3}{4}, \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}\right).$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="102" height="53" />
</p><p>The midpoint of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ffb448918db29f2a72f8f87f421b3b3cad18f95.png" class="latex" alt="$D$" width="15" height="12" /> and the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> is the bottom-left vertex of the smaller equilateral triangle. Using the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Distance_formula" title="Distance formula">distance formula</a>, we get the point
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/e/c9e41667365c95741523ec3de163f2b0f7950102.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{3}{8}, \frac{\sqrt{3}}{8}\right).$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="84" height="53" />
</p><p>Since the smaller triangle is simply a dilation of the larger one, they both have an axis of symmetry over <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/4/a14649b556d4c316856b101be1b84a92084b195b.png" class="latex" alt="$x=\frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="46" height="37" />. This yields the bottom-right vertex of the smaller triangle as
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/c/45cdaba624e13edef38164097542f044773200b9.png" class="latex" alt="$\left(\frac{5}{8}, \frac{\sqrt{3}}{8}\right).$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="84" height="53" />
</p><p>Therefore the side length of the smaller triangle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/f/5cf72c5136384cd1309126dbfb90b4237a20df2b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}.$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="16" height="37" /> The problem asks us for the ratio of the areas, and since area is two-dimensional, we simply square the ratio to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/b/95bfa41fe9336bf4c5842750154350f98395299e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{16} \Rightarrow \boxed{\text{A}}.$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="77" height="37" />
</p>
