<p>Points <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/5/8859dbfb7a9ec734e16f2a88fb5cf1104eb6a39b.png" class="latex" alt="$A,B,C,D$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="81" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/a/2/fa2fa899f0afb05d6837885523503a2d4df434f9.png" class="latex" alt="$E$" width="14" height="12" /> are located in 3-dimensional space with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/9/0c9fe38b2fbaf40b6d2d346a04c5afd9a2af1c33.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=BC=CD=DE=EA=2$" width="275" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/9/c392e306cb9b85b8a85c81a4b9eb9da535d0a175.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle ABC=\angle CDE=\angle DEA=90^o$" width="266" height="13" />. The plane of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" /> is parallel to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/9/699784c989c3db382520e6f92819e062d239f504.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{DE}$" width="30" height="15" />. What is the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/b/16b4072dec30604cbea5998c12fddd9eacfeb564.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle BDE$" width="60" height="13" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/6/6060d6bc24dc1e599b61fe6a90fe251b6065d77c.png" class="latex" alt="$\mathrm {(A)} \sqrt{2}\qquad \mathrm {(B)} \sqrt{3}\qquad \mathrm {(C)} 2\qquad \mathrm {(D)} \sqrt{5}\qquad \mathrm {(E)} \sqrt{6}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="376" height="21" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/3/5f3d38eb0bf7c151d8d2d3b8e4f1ddbcb18cd127.png" class="latex" alt="$A=(0,0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="94" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/e/45e7e83f0d15fc01ca776472d91ef67ebfd90546.png" class="latex" alt="$B=(2,0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="95" height="18" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/8/6b84e090c68740f8c14e4d599a70767a553a4b06.png" class="latex" alt="$EA=2$" width="61" height="13" />, we could let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/a/04a10f4a7fa0a6cc5234f8a8eee66676fdaf098b.png" class="latex" alt="$C=(2,0,2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="94" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/2/362451f8bb0e1a44594be1a94c68e16a5f31f192.png" class="latex" alt="$D=(2,2,2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="95" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/1/0/d1060bde9b4dc1aedaddeaa0789ca73640b7f700.png" class="latex" alt="$E=(2,2,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="94" height="18" />. Now to get back to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> we need another vertex <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/b/5fbb464307602532ab83c360a7a3cf016b0ea09b.png" class="latex" alt="$F=(0,2,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="94" height="18" />. Now if we look at this configuration as if it was two dimensions, we would see a square missing a side if we don't draw <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/3/193c5caa0ee1174e95025208873f5495493a2059.png" class="latex" alt="$FA$" width="27" height="13" />. Now we can bend these three sides into an equilateral triangle, and the coordinates change: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/3/5f3d38eb0bf7c151d8d2d3b8e4f1ddbcb18cd127.png" class="latex" alt="$A=(0,0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="94" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/e/45e7e83f0d15fc01ca776472d91ef67ebfd90546.png" class="latex" alt="$B=(2,0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="95" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/a/04a10f4a7fa0a6cc5234f8a8eee66676fdaf098b.png" class="latex" alt="$C=(2,0,2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="94" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/7/1/971b3981b425d67011824567dbdd460e73dfb89d.png" class="latex" alt="$D=(1,\sqrt{3},2)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="111" height="21" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/2/202d9e085c44b9bb9ecaf979c8bc1eac29014514.png" class="latex" alt="$E=(1,\sqrt{3},0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="110" height="21" />. Checking for all the requirements, they are all satisfied. Now we find the area of triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/6/e/96e81e1599a08776bb0799f73128f99332c1d78f.png" class="latex" alt="$BDE$" width="44" height="12" />. The side lengths of this triangle are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/8/2/782a062febb0983bac5862c19b3a261ec476626a.png" class="latex" alt="$2, 2, 2\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="68" height="20" />, which is an isosceles right triangle. Thus the area of it is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/3/603c3a7fa67338c2c0d40cf6e876b80a5cbe919d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2\cdot2}{2}=2\Rightarrow \mathrm{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="122" height="37" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Similar to solution 1, we allow 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/f/3/5f3d38eb0bf7c151d8d2d3b8e4f1ddbcb18cd127.png" class="latex" alt="$A=(0,0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="94" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/e/45e7e83f0d15fc01ca776472d91ef67ebfd90546.png" class="latex" alt="$B=(2,0,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="95" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/c/d3c5d059393248c178fb4960dd671fcbd70a1dbb.png" class="latex" alt="$C=(0,2,0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="94" height="18" />. This creates the isosceles right triangle on the plane of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/2/0/72079803c6fcdcfa95b6d7f383f6ad71f1eb631b.png" class="latex" alt="$z=0$" width="42" height="12" />
</p><p>Now, note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/9/fe9f08e342846a4595e4b8dd5bd7e00d98bbc7ec.png" class="latex" alt="$\angle CDE=\angle DEA=90^o$" width="186" height="13" />. This means that there exists some vector <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/5/6d5c9afa92ae3180f02754c177e530d9d0f8b4d4.png" class="latex" alt="$DE$" width="29" height="12" /> parallel to the plane of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/2/a/e2a559986ed5a0ffc5654bd367c29dfc92913c36.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC$" width="42" height="13" /> that forms two right angles with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/6/9a65c05bd85d2609bea5db2b109ed5f556463511.png" class="latex" alt="$AE$" width="27" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/4/0948661e822f2a953c43a57ac9e40b2734476de4.png" class="latex" alt="$CD$" width="29" height="12" />. By definition, this is the cross product of the two vectors <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/6/9a65c05bd85d2609bea5db2b109ed5f556463511.png" class="latex" alt="$AE$" width="27" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/4/0948661e822f2a953c43a57ac9e40b2734476de4.png" class="latex" alt="$CD$" width="29" height="12" />. Finding this cross product, we take the determinant of vectors
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/e/f7ee11a4c37ee59a1ba50db531f5745c2c743a7b.png" class="latex" alt="$AE=&lt;x_1,y_1,z&gt;$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="144" height="16" /> and
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/f/4afb0e06028eec2bd798e45c530a07342772ba03.png" class="latex" alt="$CD=&lt;x_2,y_2,z&gt;$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="146" height="16" /> *Note that z is constant because the line is parallel to the plane*
</p><p>to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/3/253da8def4f9d9e91dab0585a36a448410ff0032.png" class="latex" alt="$(y_1-y_2)zi+(x_1-x_2)zj+(x_1y_2-y_1x_2)k$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="333" height="18" />
</p><p>Because there can be no movement in the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> direction, the k unit vector must be zero. Also, because the i unit vector must be orthogonal and also 0. Thus, the vector of line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/5/6d5c9afa92ae3180f02754c177e530d9d0f8b4d4.png" class="latex" alt="$DE$" width="29" height="12" /> is simply <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/9/d/b9d136dba1b28827dd4b7d177b74d72200043d89.png" class="latex" alt="$2tj+2k$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="65" height="16" />
</p><p>From this, you can figure out that line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/4/d749a57119edb8ecf59db3afb67ed32614d1803a.png" class="latex" alt="$BE=2$" width="62" height="12" />, and the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/7/0872fbfda183b32e6bb11bc92be9cb965189d600.png" class="latex" alt="$BDE=\frac{2\cdot2}{2}=2\Rightarrow \mathrm{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="191" height="37" />.
</p>
