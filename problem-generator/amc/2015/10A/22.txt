<p>Eight people are sitting around a circular table, each holding a fair coin. All eight people flip their coins and those who flip heads stand while those who flip tails remain seated. What is the probability that no two adjacent people will stand?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/5/6e55ae028814ab143a8e02c6ce26cd3e07f34136.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\dfrac{47}{256}\qquad\textbf{(B)}\dfrac{3}{16}\qquad\textbf{(C) }\dfrac{49}{256}\qquad\textbf{(D) }\dfrac{25}{128}\qquad\textbf{(E) }\dfrac{51}{256}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="440" height="38" />
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We will count how many valid standing arrangements there are (counting rotations as distinct), and divide by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/3/6b32901dd20471881cf91f3661abe41d60f12b5d.png" class="latex" alt="$2^8 = 256$" width="67" height="15" /> at the end. We casework on how many people are standing.
</p><p>Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/4/8c48f75b5eb43530c84af32fbcfbd6dda062921a.png" class="latex" alt="$1:$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> people are standing. This yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> arrangement.
</p><p>Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/1/ff1be62d9bb7a4e1e12b6ecef0d2f43c133e9c32.png" class="latex" alt="$2:$" width="17" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> person is standing. This yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> arrangements.
</p><p>Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/6/366695dbee156daed8bd946e4c00df56286df7e6.png" class="latex" alt="$3:$" width="17" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> people are standing. This yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/d/62d98b7c06441fd17bd3d036da360f60c9f79bfc.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{8}{2} - 8 = 20$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="110" height="53" /> arrangements, because the two people cannot be next to each other.
</p><p>Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/f/10f0c5d3069e25a8b374873611b064ca25338af8.png" class="latex" alt="$4:$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> people are standing. Then the people must be arranged in stand-sit-stand-sit-stand-sit-stand-sit fashion, yielding <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> possible arrangements.
</p><p>More difficult is:
</p><p>Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/f/5dfff5aea506390db4638baa379ef829288926e5.png" class="latex" alt="$5:$" width="17" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> people are standing. First, choose the location of the first person standing (<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> choices). Next, choose <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> of the remaining people in the remaining <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> legal seats to stand, amounting to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> arrangements considering that these two people cannot stand next to each other. However, we have to divide by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/8/2f885f96d2fb7e65f800de4ff71d7dcd0a5d2db5.png" class="latex" alt="$3,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> because there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> ways to choose the first person given any three. This yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/6/4/8649a8dd9d888179b0f361919121ac383b3eb338.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{8 \cdot 6}{3} = 16$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="77" height="37" /> arrangements for Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/3/b835f2c6c592edc4583ce996f86bcc0d07ca8da5.png" class="latex" alt="$5.$" width="12" height="12" />
</p><p>Alternate Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/f/5dfff5aea506390db4638baa379ef829288926e5.png" class="latex" alt="$5:$" width="17" height="12" /> Use complementary counting. Total number of ways to choose 3 people from 8 which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/d/05da250639d46b5db195b8604388229b91a56f11.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{8}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" />. Sub-case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/4/8c48f75b5eb43530c84af32fbcfbd6dda062921a.png" class="latex" alt="$1:$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> three people are next to each other which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/4/9e44cc5b0679347a699f7b6e7d93fba4d734ba08.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{8}{1}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" />. Sub-case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/1/ff1be62d9bb7a4e1e12b6ecef0d2f43c133e9c32.png" class="latex" alt="$2:$" width="17" height="12" /> two people are next to each other and the third person is not <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/4/9e44cc5b0679347a699f7b6e7d93fba4d734ba08.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{8}{1}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/d/1/fd1df457d4b66c9baec2d84cdf7f45cde546eb3e.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{4}{1}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" />. This yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/6/5/a656db1bb84ca6bbdb47f0a35b30f906beda5418.png" class="latex" alt="$\dbinom{8}{3} - \dbinom{8}{1} - \dbinom{8}{1} \dbinom{4}{1} = 16$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="235" height="53" /> 
</p><p>Summing gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/e/b5e2571d9721bf703e222dce9bd48a8a07dfc087.png" class="latex" alt="$1 + 8 + 20 + 2 + 16 = 47,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="199" height="16" /> and so our probability is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/7/65766bebc7e92b0a8bd5a3696c9be2dcf091e833.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(A) } \dfrac{47}{256}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="78" height="48" />.
</p><p>Alternate: (Quicksolve) - We know that for case 5, there are 8 ways to choose the first person, and 3 ways to choose the first person given any 3 - which means that for the case, there is 8 * something divided by 3. The sum of the other cases is 31/256. Thus, add a multiple of 8 to 31 to get an answer. The options are 31 + 8 = 39, 31 + 16 = 47, 31 + 24 = 55, etc. The only possible answer is 47/256.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We will count how many valid standing arrangements there are counting rotations as distinct and divide by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/b/d/abd678361b0f40ec2723283c0f2bab3df5cadc68.png" class="latex" alt="$256$" width="26" height="12" /> at the end.
Line up all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" /> people linearly.  In order for no two people standing to be adjacent, we will place a sitting person to the right of each standing person.  In effect, each standing person requires <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> spaces and the standing people are separated by sitting people.  We just need to determine the number of combinations of pairs and singles and the problem becomes very similar to pirates and gold aka stars and bars aka sticks and stones aka balls and urns.
</p><p>If there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> standing, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/5/8/a58e1aff8d296062caacb0c37c2c5dfbbbb22072.png" class="latex" alt="${4 \choose 4}=1$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="69" height="53" /> ways to place them.
For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/8/2f885f96d2fb7e65f800de4ff71d7dcd0a5d2db5.png" class="latex" alt="$3,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="13" height="16" /> there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/9/809bf15c2a5ad6783e18a588bcbd91323f138650.png" class="latex" alt="${3+2 \choose 3}=10$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="110" height="53" /> ways.
etc.
Summing, we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/f/3dfb31b5b6347924fec874de22f9e77eb1d82787.png" class="latex" alt="${4 \choose 4}+{5 \choose 3}+{6 \choose 2}+{7 \choose 1}+{8 \choose 0}=1+10+15+7+1=34$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="494" height="53" /> ways.
</p><p>Now we consider that the far right person can be standing as well, so we have
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/9/2/e92e341cc21bdf48b32aa87ce9dcb74c7aef3b31.png" class="latex" alt="${3 \choose 3}+{4 \choose 2}+{5 \choose 1}+{6 \choose 0}=1+6+5+1=13$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="384" height="53" /> ways
</p><p>Together we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/b/bdbf42ba14abdcf511cb41a15ed34cf3741d625b.png" class="latex" alt="$34+13=47$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="101" height="14" />, and so our probability is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/7/65766bebc7e92b0a8bd5a3696c9be2dcf091e833.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(A) } \dfrac{47}{256}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="78" height="48" />.
</p>
