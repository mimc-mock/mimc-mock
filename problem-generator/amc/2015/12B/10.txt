<p>How many noncongruent integer-sided triangles with positive area and perimeter less than 15 are neither equilateral, isosceles, nor right triangles?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/e/70e6eb4ec63cefabf12bed1b9dea1b98b97920f9.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\; 3 \qquad\textbf{(B)}\; 4 \qquad\textbf{(C)}\; 5 \qquad\textbf{(D)}\; 6 \qquad\textbf{(E)}\; 7$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="346" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Since we want non-congruent triangles that are neither isosceles nor equilateral, we can just list side lengths <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/d/f3d9e22097e20a1249b5332202b24941a4bf9e1f.png" class="latex" alt="$(a,b,c)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="54" height="18" /> with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/b/a/5ba70df5a2f9630194f32f50010cd2e78318c98d.png" class="latex" alt="$a&lt;b&lt;c$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="74" height="13" />. Furthermore, "positive area" tells us that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/e/f0e2bc45eeb61657ab7a4587926a2640593b0c26.png" class="latex" alt="$c &lt; a + b$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="72" height="14" /> and the perimeter constraints means <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/f/a4ff1eaf0efa1741243226733302ff39d07ec99a.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b+c &lt; 15$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="111" height="14" />.
</p><p>There are no triangles when <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/8/d/48d02444a59b917b64e8bd0963b88712fc7fa0a3.png" class="latex" alt="$a = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="12" /> because then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> must be less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/9/91927ed6d1093e6ce84e3aa8446121218cb17b71.png" class="latex" alt="$b+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="38" height="14" />, implying that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/7/1/67176ff95b512b7db774dfb6fe8355e72df5dc96.png" class="latex" alt="$b \geq c$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="40" height="15" />, contrary to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/9/e0917f060dc5c618be04a697a4801e83be2a7569.png" class="latex" alt="$b &lt; c$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="40" height="13" />.
</p><p>When <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/5/a/55afdab16c8c49089c4d9ceeb8d3c8cf1f088be6.png" class="latex" alt="$a=2$" width="42" height="12" />, similar to above, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> must be less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/d/8cd8ac52a9c6112e9ac737cba648d2c51efb97d7.png" class="latex" alt="$b+2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="38" height="14" />, so this leaves the only possibility <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/5/3850bfd552bec12638dd77153069f54ea0849bf8.png" class="latex" alt="$c = b+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="70" height="14" />. This gives 3 triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/d/e8d4dc7bb4863baee784beb8a61fe9800212633d.png" class="latex" alt="$(2,3,4), (2,4,5), (2,5,6)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="187" height="18" /> within our perimeter constraint.
</p><p>When <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/4/0/340605c5f88cf6864adc5b146c3dffca7a45df22.png" class="latex" alt="$a=3$" width="42" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> can be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/9/91927ed6d1093e6ce84e3aa8446121218cb17b71.png" class="latex" alt="$b+1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="38" height="14" /> or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/d/8cd8ac52a9c6112e9ac737cba648d2c51efb97d7.png" class="latex" alt="$b+2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="38" height="14" />, which gives triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/a/6da01d976d1df29f088ae5bb7156a9e337fee514.png" class="latex" alt="$(3,4,5), (3,4,6), (3,5,6)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="187" height="18" />. Note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/e/28eb10edb01b5d1369b1469e15dab327a4ad7b35.png" class="latex" alt="$(3,4,5)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="56" height="18" /> is a right triangle, so we get rid of it and we get only 2 triangles.
</p><p>All in all, this gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/4/054046042412fb47232457a7fcb60b96400b22fb.png" class="latex" alt="$3+2 = \boxed{\textbf{(C)}\; 5}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="119" height="28" /> triangles.
</p>
