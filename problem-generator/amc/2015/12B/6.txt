<p>Back in 1930, Tillie had to memorize her multiplication facts from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/2/43205564ab7e64a12e040330eb778d2812a4701f.png" class="latex" alt="$0 \times 0$" width="40" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/b/1/7b1e5dbf697b6e66c3bbcea5ceab1c63b3e0fbf1.png" class="latex" alt="$12 \times 12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="58" height="12" />. The multiplication table she was given had rows and columns labeled with the factors, and the products formed the body of the table. To the nearest hundredth, what fraction of the numbers in the body of the table are odd?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/7/8/0782525ec12d9e8c4c858e71ffdb01ad0bd4ed26.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\; 0.21 \qquad\textbf{(B)}\; 0.25 \qquad\textbf{(C)}\; 0.46 \qquad\textbf{(D)}\; 0.50 \qquad\textbf{(E)}\; 0.75$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="462" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>There are a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/f/f/2ff7738594fc54d84480b6bbfd1905a257c7af66.png" class="latex" alt="$(12+1) \times (12+1) = 169$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="200" height="18" /> products, and a product is odd if and only if both its factors are odd. There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> odd numbers between <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" />, namely <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/e/6/7e69e6be203598c508ebc4cc2469cb82e14cbcfe.png" class="latex" alt="$1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="108" height="16" /> hence the number of odd products is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/7/897dd68f9e342665851f9e9b91b87a28d42fc7e2.png" class="latex" alt="$6 \times 6 = 36$" width="82" height="12" />. Therefore the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/b/3/6b3e12885c9ae03530a20d109d9b1696677f2d5a.png" class="latex" alt="$36/169 \doteq \boxed{\textbf{(A)} \, 0.21}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="155" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Note that if we had an <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/8/c6878713578626763c38433b3f4c8c2205ad0c15.png" class="latex" alt="$11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/8/c6878713578626763c38433b3f4c8c2205ad0c15.png" class="latex" alt="$11$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> multiplication table, the fraction of odd products becomes <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/6/df6ee426e2e724a4e7255dd71bdd7dbd6c75ab04.png" class="latex" alt="$0.25$" width="31" height="12" />. If we add the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/f/edf074831eb5bc9e61d6d6e09f525a86e3068f6a.png" class="latex" alt="$12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" />, the fraction of odd products decreases. Because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/3/5934d25755e6d833557b4421a7b40bc9cb23b4e6.png" class="latex" alt="$0.21$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="31" height="13" /> is the only option less than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/6/df6ee426e2e724a4e7255dd71bdd7dbd6c75ab04.png" class="latex" alt="$0.25$" width="31" height="12" />, our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/5/625f742f190db05852d84cebecc140fe93cf6033.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(A)} \, 0.21}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="76" height="28" />.
</p>
