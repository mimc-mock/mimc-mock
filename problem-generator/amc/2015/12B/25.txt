<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/b/47b1d68e3c3477926927e110c15d244e588f8c81.png" class="latex" alt="$x = e^{i \pi / 6}$" width="69" height="16" />,  a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/1/9/919bf5d461970fc8d905d63704ef763c08594184.png" class="latex" alt="$30^\circ$" width="24" height="13" /> counterclockwise rotation centered at the origin. Notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/e/76ebaba4ae3d5c911d6e31ad855d502ad88d48a6.png" class="latex" alt="$P_k$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> on the complex plane is:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/2/3223da53f4b805b74ef60ad2f86bca5ef83767c0.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[1 + 2x + 3x^2 + \cdots + (k+1)x^k\]" width="237" height="20" />
</p><p>We need to find the magnitude of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/9/bd90ffa719242269da35cdc11d46fe5684bbdd42.png" class="latex" alt="$P_{2015}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="37" height="15" /> on the complex plane. This is an arithmetic/geometric series. 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/9/9491836ccdcd76034312c30f222f33285c6222a4.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*} S &amp;= 1 + 2x + 3x^2 + \cdots + 2015x^{2014} \\ xS &amp;= x + 2x^2 + 3x^3 + \cdots + 2015x^{2015} \\ (1-x)S &amp;= 1 + x + x^2 + \cdots + x^{2014} - 2015x^{2015} \\ S &amp;= \frac{1 - x^{2015} }{(1-x)^2} - \frac{2015x^{2015}}{1-x} \end{align*}" width="370" height="126" />
</p><p>We want to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/c/11ceba9d7455309f60178fe82bb6e81e6c01fa83.png" class="latex" alt="$|S|$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="20" height="18" />. First, note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/1/9314655eb851e225a701f82fa7fbff29db039199.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{2015} = x^{11} = x^{-1}$" width="137" height="15" /> because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/b/4/fb4d905c027415c2a53891f4db343a8fe27ae07d.png" class="latex" alt="$x^{12} = 1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="56" height="15" />. Therefore
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/0/4c0b67ef4f8ecda9926e9f8f1af018b316ec81bb.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[S = \frac{1 - \frac{1}{x}}{(1-x)^2} - \frac{2015}{x(1-x)} =  -\frac{1}{x(1-x)} - \frac{2015}{x(1-x)} = -\frac{2016}{x(1-x)}.\]" width="492" height="45" />
</p><p>Hence, since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/d/90d06bed4bf79fa79013919a80c0f384cb43dba0.png" class="latex" alt="$|x|=1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="52" height="18" />, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/4/054841efbb8f1da0405a1b4b6f4252e8f3e01687.png" class="latex" alt="$|S| = \frac{2016}{|1-x|}.$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="106" height="42" />
</p><p>Now we just have to find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/5/ba5331ef40af566270b6dc0d1fd1f9667382d056.png" class="latex" alt="$|1-x|$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="50" height="18" />. This can just be computed directly:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e319bf7d9b0aa44edc92638207391f039b918.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[1 - x = 1 - \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} - \frac{1}{2}i\]" width="164" height="41" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/4/a847a6fb1fface97cc80d1ee8e947acf0a232b76.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[|1-x|^2 = \left(1 - \sqrt{3} + \frac{3}{4} \right) + \frac{1}{4} = 2 - \sqrt{3} = {\left( \frac{\sqrt{6}-\sqrt{2}}{2} \right)}^2\]" width="446" height="56" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/2/6c258c17ecab2de26b02c6d4fe9e586d62d6f399.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[|1-x| = \frac{\sqrt{6} - \sqrt{2}}{2}.\]" width="152" height="41" />
</p><p>Therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/b/1bbd152e42a064f92f39c7b9a23caca196614fd2.png" class="latex" alt="$|S| = 2016 \cdot \frac{2}{\sqrt{6} -\sqrt{2}} = 2016 \left( \frac{\sqrt{6} + \sqrt{2}}{2} \right) = 1008 \sqrt{2} + 1008 \sqrt{6}.$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="508" height="53" />
</p><p>Thus the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/9/0/890f853b1c904424dd3afa6a2cdc337625d3f3c1.png" class="latex" alt="$1008 + 1008 + 2 + 6 = \boxed{\textbf{(B)}\; 2024}.$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="266" height="28" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Here is an alternate solution that does not use complex numbers:
</p><p>We will calculate the distance from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/9/bd90ffa719242269da35cdc11d46fe5684bbdd42.png" class="latex" alt="$P_{2015}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="37" height="15" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/1/241e6e35ad6d73fb2ef98244b0211deb2f25222a.png" class="latex" alt="$P_0$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="17" height="15" /> using the Pythagorean theorem. Assume <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/1/241e6e35ad6d73fb2ef98244b0211deb2f25222a.png" class="latex" alt="$P_0$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="17" height="15" /> lies at the origin, so we will calculate the distance to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/9/bd90ffa719242269da35cdc11d46fe5684bbdd42.png" class="latex" alt="$P_{2015}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="37" height="15" /> by calculating the distance traveled in the x-direction and the distance traveled in the y-direction. We can calculate this by summing each movement:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/4/2749643501ba3756637f34ce8e6a4082252ee558.png" class="latex" alt="$x=1\cos{0}+2\cos {30}+3\cos {60}+4\cos {90}+5\cos {120}+\cdot \cdot \cdot+2011\cos{180}+2012\cos {210}+2013\cos{240}+2014\cos{270}+2015\cos{300}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="1038" height="14" />
</p><p>A movement of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/f/36f73fc1312ee0349b3f3a0f3bd9eb5504339011.png" class="latex" alt="$p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="10" height="11" /> units at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/1/0615acc3725de21025457e7d6f7694dab8e2f758.png" class="latex" alt="$q$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="8" height="11" /> degrees is the same thing as a movement of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/5/2952f74e24ccfcd852f9e389af3e1b0b2aecb2ec.png" class="latex" alt="$-p$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="23" height="11" /> units at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/6/f666bc31ccefb32b42704fba8e6c11c90f1d12c8.png" class="latex" alt="$q-180$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="57" height="16" /> degrees, so we can adjust all the cosines with arguments greater than 180 as follows:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/b/2cb8679386f25fbf3a3e54320c2aa308ae959a15.png" class="latex" alt="$x=1\cos{0}+2\cos {30}+3\cos {60}+4\cos {90}+5\cos {120}+6\cos{150}-7\cos{0}-8\cos{30}-\cdot \cdot \cdot -2015 \cos{120}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="812" height="14" />
</p><p>Now we group terms with like-cosines and factor out the cosines:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/7/7d79d3df01b90b7d62ae657b61043353d2e9325a.png" class="latex" alt="$x=(1-7+13-\cdot \cdot \cdot +2005-2011)\cos{0}+(2-8+14- \cdot \cdot \cdot +2006-2012)\cos{30}+\cdot \cdot \cdot +(5-11+17- \cdot \cdot \cdot +2008-2014)\cos{120}+(6-12+18- \cdot \cdot \cdot -2004+2010)\cos{150}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="1049" height="38" />
</p><p>Each sum in the parentheses has 336 terms (except the very last one, which has 335), so by pairing each term, we can see that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/d/06d71c51667476c2c6f2775b2c8cbce44f2b5ade.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac {336}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="29" height="37" /> pairs of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/8/3683f227b0b53e6a30087db2fa3b752965ecf05d.png" class="latex" alt="$-6$" width="22" height="12" />. So each sum evaluates to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/3/dc37d19a6fe96b7eedd0e16a1b565e9573dc6559.png" class="latex" alt="$168\cdot -6=-1008$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="137" height="13" />, except the very last sum, which has 167 pairs of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/8/3683f227b0b53e6a30087db2fa3b752965ecf05d.png" class="latex" alt="$-6$" width="22" height="12" /> and an extra 2010, so it evaluates to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/2/5/b25c26cd35549283fca1ca5a0a9b11db9ae1ecf7.png" class="latex" alt="$167\cdot -6+2010=1008$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="182" height="14" />. Plugging in these values:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/0/a40af472561fc60b2233dee8478e0949de863a46.png" class="latex" alt="$x=-1008\cos{0}-1008\cos{30}-1008\cos{60}-1008\cos{90}-1008\cos{120}+1008\cos{150}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="678" height="14" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/f/0/df018b509cbbd2bb7ad76d1a08b89f60ec3a4258.png" class="latex" alt="$x=1008(-1-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}-\frac{1}{2}-0+\frac{1}{2}-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2})=-1008(1+\sqrt{3})$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="454" height="41" />
</p><p>Now that we have how far was traveled in the x-direction, we need to find how far was traveled in the y-direction. Using the same logic as above, we arrive at the sum:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/2/6025082d6a4f49464de9f3990e57cccc22f95b47.png" class="latex" alt="$y=-1008\sin{0}-1008\sin{30}-1008\sin{60}-1008\sin{90}-1008\sin{120}+1008\sin{150}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="665" height="16" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/b/25b6dc463428a5f4ac6277cb3dab0aae60d6f6f3.png" class="latex" alt="$y=1008(0-\frac{1}{2}-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}-1-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}+\frac{1}{2})=-1008(1+\sqrt{3})$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="439" height="41" />
</p><p>The last step is to use the Pythagorean to find the distance from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/1/241e6e35ad6d73fb2ef98244b0211deb2f25222a.png" class="latex" alt="$P_0$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="17" height="15" />. This distance is given by:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/f/45f9c7ea5facb17a5cb0ce268136aac274127941.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{x^2+y^2}=\sqrt{(-1008(1+\sqrt{3}))^2+(-1008(1+\sqrt{3}))^2}=\sqrt{2\cdot 1008^2 \cdot (1+\sqrt{3})^2}=1008(1+\sqrt{3})\sqrt{2}$" style="vertical-align: -8px" width="776" height="32" />
</p><p>Multiplying out, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/9/a194fb6bf9a0da4f0627e05d32849fc477122bee.png" class="latex" alt="$1008\sqrt{2}+1008\sqrt{6}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="144" height="18" />, so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/7/7574002def6afbdc05d9311c537455d07614299c.png" class="latex" alt="$1008+2+1008+6= \boxed {\bold {(B)}\; 2024}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="264" height="29" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>We first notice that if the bee is turning 30 degrees each turn, it will take 12 turns to be looking in the same direction when the bee initially left. This means we simply need to answer the question; how far will the bee be when the bee is facing in the same direction? 
</p><p><br />
First we use the fact that after 3 turns, the bee will be facing in a direction perpendicular to the the initial direction. From here we can draw a perpendicular from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/7/107c7c8e5a64d81ed24331e510b777b7d08345b7.png" class="latex" alt="$P_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="17" height="15" /> to the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/f/baf2204238e2cf35908315c538080164ae071e34.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{P_0P_1}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="38" height="18" /> intersecting a point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/e/7ce4664de7db214d4c56a42b1922cbd0fb7e7785.png" class="latex" alt="$C_0$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" />. We will also place the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/0/b/70b97eb4ad6e4e7a5a97e2547d9ed6e965960d0c.png" class="latex" alt="$C_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> at the intersection of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/a/f/baf2204238e2cf35908315c538080164ae071e34.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{P_0P_1}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="38" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/c/88cf4adc1ccdb5e84cfc21404904467501417ee7.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{P_3P_4}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="38" height="18" />. In addition, the point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/5/b65e6facfe122c182fe14d503aeace2c52a9739d.png" class="latex" alt="$C_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" /> is placed at the perpendicular dropped from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/0/7/107c7c8e5a64d81ed24331e510b777b7d08345b7.png" class="latex" alt="$P_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="17" height="15" /> to the line <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/b/f4be1b97f90e19e2a560c355e995902b37a9afca.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{P_3C_1}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="40" height="18" />. We will also set the distance <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/8/028e15005852de35dbac550c1bdc7479ee4eee4f.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{P_0P_1} = n$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="73" height="18" /> and thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/3/0b3d160d7f10859110cc4ecc833a297ebce399f8.png" class="latex" alt="$\overline{P_1P_2} = n+1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="103" height="18" />. With this perpendicular we see that the triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/a/b1a77e4a7336779b688a73425cba8c0ff0e761bd.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{P_1P_2C_0}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="73" height="16" /> is a 30-60-90 triangle.
