<p>For every composite positive integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, define <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/a/12a32ccd86343368b87352d4313e4f13b91668a8.png" class="latex" alt="$r(n)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="32" height="18" /> to be the sum of the factors in the prime factorization of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />. For example, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/8/cb8279f89a5d1d0c47245c28b45827102421da2e.png" class="latex" alt="$r(50) = 12$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="83" height="18" /> because the prime factorization of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/0/6/906734e2f1010e9a171ffee8050cbc0649cea37c.png" class="latex" alt="$50$" width="17" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/0/940c4d971ae38514b68219cc174a9c1859858e7d.png" class="latex" alt="$2 \times 5^{2}$" width="46" height="15" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/f/bffa3a32c2bc9501a37d488da1eaa6001aedcf55.png" class="latex" alt="$2 + 5 + 5 = 12$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="113" height="14" />. What is the range of the function <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/6/e/76edec63b688ad8d732ac8565d71df0daa7e42e1.png" class="latex" alt="$\{r(n): n \text{ is a composite positive integer}\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="302" height="18" />&#160;?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/0/b702b755be14ceca83f47ffcc06085f6e72ca0cf.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\; \text{the set of positive integers} \\ \textbf{(B)}\; \text{the set of composite positive integers} \\ \textbf{(C)}\; \text{the set of even positive integers} \\ \textbf{(D)}\; \text{the set of integers greater than 3} \\ \textbf{(E)}\; \text{the set of integers greater than 4}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="302" height="96" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>This problem becomes simple once we recognize that the domain of the function is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/a/f2a2a587410f0a858db35564bcf2cc7ca3ed833e.png" class="latex" alt="$\{4, 6, 8, 9, 10, 12, 14, 15, \dots\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="215" height="18" />. By evaluating <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/8/d28a26689e7017420d831658a3d18a7a79d76fba.png" class="latex" alt="$r(4)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="30" height="18" /> to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, we can see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/e/75e41e3cd48cee2c6866cfbb4386a8e99d3482f5.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(E)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="26" height="17" /> is incorrect. Evaluating <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/a/e/8ae53b0e934bb5250a0b159c6488713e756b5830.png" class="latex" alt="$r(6)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="30" height="18" /> to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />, we see that both <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/1/b7193f66028e13f331d53ff5e6942c4b05587465.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(B)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="27" height="17" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/9/169b8d69bd74a91e72ec43123070d770633a340a.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(C)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="27" height="17" /> are incorrect. Since our domain consists of composite numbers, which, by definition, are a product of at least two positive primes, the minimum value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/a/12a32ccd86343368b87352d4313e4f13b91668a8.png" class="latex" alt="$r(n)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="32" height="18" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/6/a46f6af01fcf3bb5f6cac2316bc4de9bb3906bec.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="28" height="17" /> is incorrect. That leaves us with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57eeae866fea83a934b1b0443336382a9dabf228.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D)}\; \text{the set of integers greater than }3}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="285" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Think backwards. The range is the same as the numbers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" /> that can be expressed as the sum of two or more prime positive integers.
</p><p>The lowest number we can get is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/9/829e536d7d89379b1ebcffeb45203405e1cbfb63.png" class="latex" alt="$y = 2+2 = 4$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="107" height="15" />. For any number greater than 4, we can get to it by adding some amount of 2's and then possibly a 3 if that number is odd. For example, 23 can be obtained by adding 2 ten times and adding a 3; this corresponds to the argument <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/d/6cd87ff55d23b0f30d87acd8770a7b61ed0bc70e.png" class="latex" alt="$n = 2^{10} \times 3$" width="89" height="15" />.  Thus our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/e/57eeae866fea83a934b1b0443336382a9dabf228.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D)}\; \text{the set of integers greater than }3}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="285" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Notice that by the Chicken McNugget Theorem, given <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />s and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" />s, we can make any number larger than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/b/a/3ba5fd8be26bdc113040642c3bb5528e9d607c77.png" class="latex" alt="$2\cdot3 - 2 - 3 = 1.$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="134" height="15" /> We also exclude <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> since they correspond to primes. All other numbers can be written as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/7/457aa7223629018a58934031e30bde177b4019a9.png" class="latex" alt="$2a + 3b,$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="65" height="18" /> which <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/1/0/310c758954c6c46b56d0ee621a4dcbf7615a6a70.png" class="latex" alt="$r(2^a3^b)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="58" height="22" /> will equal. Thus the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/1/c61f1e8be9d73f590167b39417e42bbaa1787fe0.png" class="latex" alt="$D.$" width="19" height="12" />
</p>
