<p>Six chairs are evenly spaced around a circular table. One person is seated in each chair. Each person gets up and sits down in a chair that is not the same and is not adjacent to the chair he or she originally occupied, so that again one person is seated in each chair. In how many ways can this be done?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/e/16e6e03109869c96c3123e97e8bd0f54857a4fe6.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\; 14 \qquad\textbf{(B)}\; 16 \qquad\textbf{(C)}\; 18 \qquad\textbf{(D)}\; 20 \qquad\textbf{(E)}\; 24$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="392" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Consider shifting every person over three seats left after each person has gotten up and sat back down again. Now, instead of each person being seated not in the same chair and not in an adjacent chair, each person will be seated either in the same chair or an adjacent chair. The problem now becomes the number of ways in which six people can sit down in a chair that is either the same chair or an adjacent chair in a circle.
</p><p>Consider the similar problem of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> people sitting in a chair that is either the same chair or an adjacent chair in a row. Call the number of possibilities for this <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/8/4/b842b4552478b69732a9aea702309d8c51e43056.png" class="latex" alt="$F_n$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="19" height="15" />. Then if the leftmost person stays put, the problem is reduced to a row of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/d/4bdca93962a2beaffc172ca394e04fe05bd74ffd.png" class="latex" alt="$n-1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="41" height="12" /> chairs, and if the leftmost person shifts one seat to the right, the new person sitting in the leftmost seat must be the person originally second from the left, reducing the problem to a row of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/9/ef9b02e32cfba0db0f9038a8d077596720f62e91.png" class="latex" alt="$n-2$" width="41" height="12" /> chairs. Thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/b/a/2ba1b96c5a8886d1b566ea7560181e22e43f73ba.png" class="latex" alt="$F_n = F_{n-1} + F_{n-2}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="141" height="15" /> for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/9/e/49e6a2d706867d6652d7edadccc3fd034130bd98.png" class="latex" alt="$n \geq 3$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="43" height="15" />. Clearly <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/c/0/4c0df42671f463da7039819f5bb3f229ce806eb6.png" class="latex" alt="$F_1 = 1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="51" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/7/f/d7f36fbaf6bd4d237457464eebd75ac924074ef8.png" class="latex" alt="$F_2 = 2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="52" height="15" />, so <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/1/7/817eb592e7c354e5ae3ece7c46b7c29fbec0f4fd.png" class="latex" alt="$F_3 = 3$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="52" height="15" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/1/351dc8494604a962c063fda4f686b252adcb7b8f.png" class="latex" alt="$F_4 = 5$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="52" height="15" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/9/8e9ef4b9ff44c1cd2bc2e78001dbcabb24fddbf9.png" class="latex" alt="$F_5 = 8$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="52" height="15" />.
</p><p>Now consider the six people in a circle and focus on one person. If that person stays put, the problem is reduced to a row of five chairs, for which there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/9/8e9ef4b9ff44c1cd2bc2e78001dbcabb24fddbf9.png" class="latex" alt="$F_5 = 8$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="52" height="15" /> possibilities. If that person moves one seat to the left, then the person who replaces him in his original seat will either be the person originally to the right of him, which will force everyone to simply shift over one seat to the left, or the person originally to the left of him, which reduces the problem to a row of four chairs, for which there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/1/351dc8494604a962c063fda4f686b252adcb7b8f.png" class="latex" alt="$F_4 = 5$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="52" height="15" /> possibilities, giving <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/a/a/eaa37f6d11d98366a5acf61364c2ce485c52b7de.png" class="latex" alt="$1 + 5 = 6$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="73" height="14" /> possibilities in all. By symmetry, if that person moves one seat to the right, there are another <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/0/1/601a7806cbfad68196c43a4665871f8c3186802e.png" class="latex" alt="$6$" width="8" height="12" /> possibilities, so we have a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/b/7dbdd37881501e2bf9f1f2847ef2c47c0614b807.png" class="latex" alt="$8+6+6 = \boxed{\textbf{(D)}\; 20}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="160" height="28" /> possibilities.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Label the people sitting at the table <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/f/a8f0cb54a26cd8fbaf73b716002aeff551c30fbf.png" class="latex" alt="$A, B, C, D, E, F,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="128" height="16" /> and assume that they are initially seated in the order <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/2/8029902caf855d940e465d04ff40101d8850c3e0.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCDEF$" width="86" height="13" />. The possible new positions for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/e/b/8eb06dce22486a98af7416c4bd3bdd842cdf7da8.png" class="latex" alt="$A, B, C, D, E,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="108" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/5/a055f405829e64a3b70253ab67cb45ed6ed5bb29.png" class="latex" alt="$F$" width="14" height="12" /> are respectively (a dash indicates a non-allowed position):
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/0/0/c0075addcaad84085ddc331fbc470de906e4de5d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\begin{tabular}{| c | c | c | c | c | c |} \hline - &amp; - &amp; A &amp; A &amp; A &amp; - \\ \hline - &amp; - &amp; - &amp; B &amp; B &amp; B \\ \hline C &amp; - &amp; - &amp; - &amp; C &amp; C \\ \hline D &amp; D &amp; - &amp; - &amp; - &amp; D \\ \hline E &amp; E &amp; E &amp; - &amp; - &amp; - \\ \hline - &amp; F &amp; F &amp; F &amp; - &amp; - \\ \hline \end{tabular}\]" width="196" height="124" />
</p><p>The permutations we are looking for should use one letter from each column, and there should not be any repeated letters:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/1/591679862572aef050a065c3cc9963dbe7679903.png" class="latex" alt="$\begin{tabular}{c} CDEFAB \\ CEAFBD \\ CEFABD \\ CEFBAD \\ CFEABD \\ CFEBAD \\ DEAFBC \\ DEAFCB \\ DEFABC \\ DEFACB \\ DEFBAC \\ DFEABC \\ DFEACB \\ DFEBAC \\ EDAFBC \\ EDAFCB \\ EDFABC \\ EDFACB \\ EDFBAC \\ EFABCD \end{tabular}$" style="vertical-align: -100px" width="81" height="391" />
</p><p>There are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/9/4792989642bc44c232735bdcd1a0e03f1e0acd10.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(D)}\; 20}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="64" height="28" /> such permutations.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>We can represent each rearrangement as a permutation of the six elements <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/1/5/615346eb2f9dc275d58727bfdb39c34676691128.png" class="latex" alt="$\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="112" height="18" /> in cycle notation. Note that any such permutation cannot have a 1-cycle, so the only possible types of permutations are 2,2,2-cycles, 4,2-cycles, 3,3-cycles, and 6-cycles. We deal with each case separately.
</p><p>For 2,2,2-cycles, suppose that one of the 2-cycles switches the people across from each other, i.e. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/2/ff29abeb41bbaae50757936bf2f62084358d3b8a.png" class="latex" alt="$(14)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="30" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/2/6921c3f54b6013a6c2f2ae451bac5e2998154c8a.png" class="latex" alt="$(25)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="30" height="18" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/a/d/2ad085c6619878e30ffd07250cc90fbe74caeb5c.png" class="latex" alt="$(36)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="30" height="18" />. WLOG, we may assume it to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/2/ff29abeb41bbaae50757936bf2f62084358d3b8a.png" class="latex" alt="$(14)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="30" height="18" />. Then we could either have both of the other 2-cycles be across from each other, giving the permutation <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/0/6a0e76516c8329e7ea2f1e8d86d0e4acb379d4be.png" class="latex" alt="$(14)(25)(36)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="95" height="18" /> or else neither of the other 2-cycles is across from each other, in which case the only possible permutation is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/5/2/35294b706365331ec0f086aa118b774a9785e68c.png" class="latex" alt="$(14)(26)(35)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="95" height="18" />. This can happen for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/2/6921c3f54b6013a6c2f2ae451bac5e2998154c8a.png" class="latex" alt="$(25)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="30" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/a/d/2ad085c6619878e30ffd07250cc90fbe74caeb5c.png" class="latex" alt="$(36)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="30" height="18" /> as well. So since the first permutation is not counted twice, we find a total of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/7/2d78cb28f6e459d0120a34693cb7c36d84b4a8c2.png" class="latex" alt="$1+3=4$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="73" height="14" /> permutations that are 2,2,2-cycles where at least one of the 2-cycles switches people diametrically opposite from each other. Otherwise, since the elements in a 2-cycle cannot differ by 1, 3, or 5 mod 6, they must differ by 2 or 4 mod 6, i.e. they must be of the same parity. But since we have three odd and three even elements, this is impossible. Hence there are exactly 4 such permutations that are 2,2,2-cycles.
</p><p>For 4,2-cycles, we assume for the moment that 1 is part of the 2-cycle. Then the 2-cycle can be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/3/9/8399202722685c89bfdfe6580453ef9480da124f.png" class="latex" alt="$(13)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="30" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/6/46646b82d494aedb9b56c32e67d45b4c1408a061.png" class="latex" alt="$(15)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="30" height="18" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/2/ff29abeb41bbaae50757936bf2f62084358d3b8a.png" class="latex" alt="$(14)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="30" height="18" />. The first two are essentially the same by symmetry, and we must arrange the elements 2, 4, 5, 6 into a 4-cycle. However, 5 must have two neighbors that are not next to it, which is impossible, hence the first two cases yield no permutations. If the 2-cycle is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/2/ff29abeb41bbaae50757936bf2f62084358d3b8a.png" class="latex" alt="$(14)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="30" height="18" />, then we must arrange the elements 2, 3, 5, 6 into a 4-cycle. Then 2 must have the neighbors 5 and 6. We find that the 4-cycles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/c/8/9c8dd7e977b2ba51b76bf814023329765a769285.png" class="latex" alt="$(2536)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="48" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/a/e7a3347c0caab7ae044536537020054c7ed10f38.png" class="latex" alt="$(2635)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="48" height="18" /> satisfy the desired properties, yielding the permutations <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/a/d5af8291fa54190486e080d8f5350ec11e16e437.png" class="latex" alt="$(14)(2536)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="81" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/4/6548ba7f14c392fb91a563c7bc54b87bd0f87e46.png" class="latex" alt="$(14)(2635)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="81" height="18" />. This can be done for the 2-cycles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/9/2/6921c3f54b6013a6c2f2ae451bac5e2998154c8a.png" class="latex" alt="$(25)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="30" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/a/d/2ad085c6619878e30ffd07250cc90fbe74caeb5c.png" class="latex" alt="$(36)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="30" height="18" /> as well, so we find a total of 6 such permutations that are 4,2-cycles.
</p><p>For 3,3-cycles, note that if 1 neighbor 4, then the third element in the cycle will neighbor one of 1 and 4, so this is impossible. Therefore, the 3-cycle containing 1 must consist of elements 1, 3, and 5. Therefore, we obtain the four 3,3-cycles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/3/4/734920c676205343dcdbea50df9153079c489548.png" class="latex" alt="$(135)(246)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="81" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/1/1/111ebb5363a8531b3542a54d566c08899f022948.png" class="latex" alt="$(153)(246)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="81" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/b/46bd07f0097ef3f36f2ff57453059b7bb60069f7.png" class="latex" alt="$(135)(264)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="81" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/2/382707e338236948568b65871e79ca42c1452938.png" class="latex" alt="$(153)(264)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="81" height="18" />.
</p><p>For 6-cycles, note that the neighbors of 1 can be 3 and 4, 3 and 5, or 4 and 5. In the first case, we may assume that it looks like <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/2/6/12658cc4067ee49c71ff6e42bd124cf433bb6b22.png" class="latex" alt="$(314\dots)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" /> -- the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/7/1b771dee0febbe29c66f9969ef3bc6062f49f75e.png" class="latex" alt="$(413\dots)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" /> is also possible but equivalent to this case. Then we must place the elements 2, 5, and 6. Note that 5 and 6 cannot go together, so 2 must go in between them. Also, 5 cannot neighbor 4, so we are left with one possibility, namely <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/d/50d54885a5ec1b960b145a71b435844d660c1285.png" class="latex" alt="$(314625)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="66" height="18" />, which has an analogous possibility <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/c/59cd97ab1b06b8c364fa544d98e7d4a11a0601d4.png" class="latex" alt="$(413526)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="66" height="18" />. In the second case, we assume that it looks like <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/c/66cea54b6688e61afffe27ce1efe69de13858cee.png" class="latex" alt="$(315\dots)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" />. The 2 must go next to the 5, and the 6 must go last (to neighbor the 3), so the only possibility here is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/6/0c64d2668bc54e2e9a66b840a4c6ec28748cbf05.png" class="latex" alt="$(315246)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="66" height="18" />, with the analogous possibility <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/3/ce30a45786895a1c69d0dcd973957b75d5b7dab1.png" class="latex" alt="$(513642)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="66" height="18" />. In the final case, we may assume that it looks like <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/c/f0c85bd0753a90dea517039b097b695324fec1f6.png" class="latex" alt="$(415\dots)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="67" height="18" />. Then the 2 and 3 cannot go together, so the 6 must go in between them. Therefore, the only possibility is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/5/e85927cf69b7566f74c048730dc080b1365703c5.png" class="latex" alt="$(415362)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="66" height="18" />, with the analogous possibility <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/c/f/2cfd2a35a0fd679f598fcec3023b15ddafb3856c.png" class="latex" alt="$(514263)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="66" height="18" />. We have covered all possibilities for 6-cycles, and we have found 6 of them.
</p><p>Therefore, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/b/38ba73eb80008456d285f3636939f19470adbc42.png" class="latex" alt="$4+6+4+6 = \boxed{\textbf{(D)}\; 20}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="192" height="28" /> such permutations.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_4">Solution 4</span></h2>
<p>Notice the question is not any different than counting the cases if someone either stays put or moves one seat to the left or right (as in the above solution). WLOG, say the beginning case is ABCDEF (before any transformations). Thus, we can count these cases up:
</p><p>Case 1: Everyone stays put.
Then, there is only one case (ABCDEF).
</p><p>Case 2: Everyone moves.
Notice that if A moves to the right to B's seat, B has either two options. B can move to the left (to A's seat) or move to the right to C's seat. If B moves to C's seat, C cannot cancel the loop by going to A's seat, since we set the restriction as each person can only move ONE seat to the left/right. The only way to finish this process would be C moving to D, D moving to E, E moving to F, and F moving to A, achieving FABCDE as a case. Of course, A can move left and the same loop would occur but in the opposite direction (BCDEFA). Thus, we achieve two cases.
</p><p>Case 3: The movement of seats cancel out each other.
If A moves to B's seat, B can move to A's seat, canceling the cycle. In this case, we would look to C, where this same thing could happen (note, however, that if C moves to D's seat, D cannot move to E's seat as the loop would be unachievable; furthermore, note that C cannot move to B's seat if A is already there since that would mean A would have to move to C's seat which is restricted).
</p><p>We can count these cases by grouping each pair and then doing subcases:
</p><p>Subcase 1: AB CD and EF are grouped.
Here we have 2*2*2 = 8 cases, but they cannot all stay put since we've already counted that possibility in Case 1, and thus there are 7 cases here.
</p><p>Subcase 2: BC DE and FA are grouped.
Here again, we have 2*2*2 = 8 cases, and then subtract the overcounted case to get 7 cases.
</p><p>Subcase 3: There is overlap so that two people do not change.
If A and B change seats, D and E could change seats, and this would not have been counted by the previous groupings (since its an odd parity grouping + an even parity grouping). Count this simply by listing out the cases, or notice that this just means the two people sitting three seats away not moving. There are three cases of that happening (A and D not moving, B and E not moving, and C and F not moving).
</p><p>Look back at all our cases. Sum them up to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/7/d/e7da22ad631ec4646fa7c05a839c8530b7c72b14.png" class="latex" alt="$1+2+7+7+3 = 20$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="176" height="14" />
</p><p>iron
</p>
