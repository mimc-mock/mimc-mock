<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" /> be a square of side length 1. Two points are chosen independently at random on the sides of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/2/ad28c83c99a8fd0dd2e2e594c9d02ee532765a0a.png" class="latex" alt="$S$" width="12" height="12" />. The probability that the straight-line distance between the points is at least <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/c/bdc960c165b21ba43f7a90caa2893d40409d6944.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac12$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/8/6/c8648112d1e1c73002aa6d0b9420390b98a342ce.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{a-b\pi}{c}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="53" height="37" />, where <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/b/f3b28547efd22be1c4a05fa193cd7038fc65d71b.png" class="latex" alt="$a,b,$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="29" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/3/7/3372c1cb6d68cf97c2d231acc0b47b95a9ed04cc.png" class="latex" alt="$c$" width="8" height="8" /> are positive integers and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/e/25e152853b879e16286aca8f620e52bf396ee1ea.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{gcd}(a,b,c) = 1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="112" height="18" />. What is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/e/b/6ebd1e0356cfe09c0d9b5459d6f1542657bb5891.png" class="latex" alt="$a+b+c$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="70" height="14" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/7/0/4708f47a030a142952cd61dc8b4f4feb03e47a68.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 59 \qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 60 \qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 61 \qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 62 \qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 63$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="394" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution">Solution</span></h2>
<p>Divide the boundary of the square into halves, thereby forming 8 segments. Without loss of generality, let the first point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> be in the bottom-left segment. Then, it is easy to see that any point in the 5 segments not bordering the bottom-left segment will be distance at least <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/4/4a4a7df1100efe353cc612d5d1c289c22d5bf8c1.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> apart from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" />. Now, consider choosing the second point on the bottom-right segment. The probability for it to be distance at least 0.5 apart from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/0/04091a7f1a77155363292d198457566338cf9fdd.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{0 + 1}{2} = \dfrac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="80" height="37" /> because of linearity of the given probability. (Alternatively, one can set up a coordinate system and use geometric probability.)
</p><p>If the second point <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> is on the left-bottom segment, then if <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/9/019e9892786e493964e145e7c5cf7b700314e53b.png" class="latex" alt="$A$" width="13" height="13" /> is distance <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> away from the left-bottom vertex, then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/f/5/ff5fb3d775862e2123b007eb4373ff6cc1a34d4e.png" class="latex" alt="$B$" width="14" height="12" /> must be at least <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/7/75712a03a1a4b035c423a5b7cb5f96fe911883b7.png" class="latex" alt="$\dfrac{1}{2} - \sqrt{0.25 - x^2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="123" height="37" /> away from that same vertex. Thus, using an averaging argument we find that the probability in this case is
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/6/c/b6cf2454d6e2a5cc9b2f5fad7c6f0208f4d72a49.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1}{\frac{1}{2}^2} \int_0^{\frac{1}{2}} \dfrac{1}{2} - \sqrt{0.25 - x^2} dx = 4\left(\frac{1}{4} - \frac{\pi}{16}\right) = 1 - \frac{\pi}{4}.\]" width="391" height="52" />
</p><p>(Alternatively, one can equate the problem to finding all valid <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/f/adfd9ae8a3fac031d3b8b470a52a709a23d4d6d2.png" class="latex" alt="$(x, y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="40" height="18" /> with <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/d/e/3def358a11bef0bd95c96661e0afb5bab9e29471.png" class="latex" alt="$0 &lt; x, y &lt; \dfrac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="97" height="37" />  such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/c/02cb585a3504574e5af941024cfac101334f4fb9.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2 + y^2 \ge \dfrac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="93" height="37" />, i.e. (x, y) is outside the unit circle with radius 0.5.)
</p><p>Thus, averaging the probabilities gives
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/a/7/aa7365a0753af401afa0e0f926b6f02c513f49ea.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[P = \frac{1}{8} \left(5 + \frac{1}{2} + 1 - \frac{\pi}{4}\right) = \frac{1}{32} (26 - \pi).\]" width="308" height="43" />
</p><p>Our answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/4/6/a46f6af01fcf3bb5f6cac2316bc4de9bb3906bec.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="28" height="17" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Case_Solution">Case Solution</span></h2>
<p>Fix one of the points on a SIDE. There are three cases: the other point is on the same side, a peripheral side, or the opposite side, with probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/3/c/43c10c05747b98174223e6647cd8438f3e75f2fc.png" class="latex" alt="$0.25, 0.5, 0.25$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="103" height="16" />, respectively.
</p><p>Opposite side: Probability is obviously <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />, no matter what. 
</p><p>Same side: Pretend the points are on a line with coordinates <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />. If <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/1/7/7171825a73418b7a616cd176328df3c8349ab4f0.png" class="latex" alt="$|a-b| \le \frac{1}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="85" height="37" />, drawing a graph will give probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/3/c339afa03e060d959e4942be7522c0c992f4c41c.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="11" height="37" />.
</p><p>Peripheral side: superimpose a coordinate system over the points; call them <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/b/9/eb99be0632ae9be9bbeeec99f75e4a71c2595180.png" class="latex" alt="$(x, 0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="40" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/c/8/ec888a16015d32d475ff33a43f72c950f81c4e85.png" class="latex" alt="$(0, y)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="39" height="18" />. WLOG set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/d/17de501dcf4a988ddc6ab01d085d6a3ce42e0246.png" class="latex" alt="$x, y &gt;= 0$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="75" height="16" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/4/5/24573c829758428470c5e1e594821c9754766f4e.png" class="latex" alt="$x, y &lt;= 1$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="74" height="15" />. We need <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/c/3/0c3a14339ee6a9d5cd1632e6d72693ddc0a35230.png" class="latex" alt="$x^2+y^2&gt;0.25$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="113" height="18" />, and drawing the coordinate system with bounds <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/9/d892ffa23d5628cfd2e05b88eaf0c5d1657b084a.png" class="latex" alt="$(0, 0), (1, 0), (0, 1), (1, 1)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="184" height="18" /> gives probability <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/7/f7757c82195aea7d1b2adb12416965d1c6e4887d.png" class="latex" alt="$1-\frac{\pi}{16}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="52" height="33" /> that the distance between the points is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/8/6d814fbc40ac21751a95cdbe9c0e39b272112f40.png" class="latex" alt="$&gt;0.5$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="41" height="13" />.
</p><p>Adding these up and finding the fraction gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/c/cbc11d714a70cc0a7487f7bb3387ff31dc20446d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{32} (26 - \pi)$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="86" height="37" /> for an answer of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/1/57161282d686773a30f443ce070c6a5057af16d9.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 59$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="52" height="17" />.
</p>
