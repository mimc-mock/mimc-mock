<p>Let us start with the two circles in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/9/459e0a2cd0a6e3df9f55e7e2cc3c0817d11d3ec2.png" class="latex" alt="$L_0$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> and the circle in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/2/3f22b669e4803ae525cf8120ca251e2eab025872.png" class="latex" alt="$L_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" />. Also let the single circle in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/f/2/3f22b669e4803ae525cf8120ca251e2eab025872.png" class="latex" alt="$L_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="18" height="15" /> be named circle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/a/3ead47fb9fb4a4c273feee398f72ff2a09702b84.png" class="latex" alt="$Z$" width="13" height="12" /> with radius <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" />. Draw radii <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/6/e/26eeb5258ca5099acf8fe96b2a1049c48c89a5e6.png" class="latex" alt="$x$" width="10" height="8" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/2/092e364e1d9d19ad5fffb0b46ef4cc7f2da02c1c.png" class="latex" alt="$y$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="9" height="11" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/1/3/b13f21416d84e13708696f34dea81026cda583c9.png" class="latex" alt="$z$" width="8" height="8" /> perpendicular to the x-axis. We attain the following equations. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/f/c6f897007fabe62f49d7505f7865330d8b8fed37.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(x - z)^2 + a^2 = (x + z)^2 \implies a^2 = 4xz\]" width="307" height="19" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/7/6/a761e3c3bd520972491b5b10cd2c74a7a291f7c8.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(y - z)^2 + b^2 = (y + z)^2 \implies b^2 = 4yz\]" width="301" height="19" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/2/f52d7713be54f92cdd229ef2c0fad9d6955cf1b9.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(x - y)^2 + c^2 = (x + y)^2 \implies c^2 = 4xy\]" width="305" height="19" />
</p><p>We see that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/1/0d1f571c9c9eec7dde4f6e531204d9cbbbee8145.png" class="latex" alt="$a = 2\sqrt{xz}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="78" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/8/64887755c9c55478afc15f9bc7a9046515727e3d.png" class="latex" alt="$b = 2\sqrt{yz}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="75" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/3/6a3571c6dd5aba74c63900cda7577b952df7bfb3.png" class="latex" alt="$c = 2\sqrt{xy}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="76" height="18" />. Since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/f/ddf058e91c8834c393fc66f84e62dc73e87541b6.png" class="latex" alt="$a + b = c$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="72" height="14" />, we have that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/8/ce8e81e966c08e583556b03f1dfa4c52592657ad.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{xz} + 2\sqrt{yz} = 2\sqrt{xy}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="177" height="20" />. Divide this equation by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/a/cfaca6667b157f02e9a7cb546341923dde573af0.png" class="latex" alt="$2\sqrt{xyz}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="53" height="18" />, and this equation becomes the well-known relation of Descartes's Circle Theorem <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/3/46370c3f9c1048986a7c3dfef8d9f92461903007.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{y}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{z}}.$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="136" height="43" /> We can apply this relationship recursively with the circles in layers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/d/1fd4c56395e69cb1c0c067d9078a0891de96e701.png" class="latex" alt="$L_2, L_3, \cdots, L_6$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="109" height="16" />.
</p><p>Here, let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/9/6/0965bf432b818b46740e3cd4d042899628c7ad0e.png" class="latex" alt="$S(n)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="35" height="18" /> denote the sum of the reciprocals of the square roots of all circles in layer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />. The notation in the problem asks us to find the sum of the reciprocals of the square roots of the radii in each circle in this collection, which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/5/2/652b2f1227d534bd779cf0a9225ace0e81ed2107.png" class="latex" alt="$\textstyle\sum_{n=0}^{6}S(n)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="83" height="22" />.  We already have that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/f/7/ef73a79645c791770c17ac7040b3ae2e426ad2ad.png" class="latex" alt="$S(0) = S(1) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{z}} = \frac{1}{73} +  \frac{1}{70}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="237" height="41" />. Then, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/2/792819dfee94df6c0edebadbadde5cd1c294d5ac.png" class="latex" alt="$S(2) = 2S(1) + S(0) = 3S(0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="229" height="18" />. Additionally, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/4/d44ecd65fa03d781d9f98aa677003363a8270714.png" class="latex" alt="$S(3) = 2S(2) + 2S(1) + S(0) = 9S(0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="295" height="18" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/7/cb72d6ef0fc69da505fea9be922d72dbf810dfa4.png" class="latex" alt="$S(4) = 2S(3) + 2S(2) + 2S(1) + S(0) = 27S(0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="371" height="18" />. Now, we notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/d/b/ddb23d185972ca1574e05f35ee99a3584a7d372b.png" class="latex" alt="$S(n + 1) = 3S(n)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="137" height="18" /> because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/e/b/feb75acab489ac7d4220dda9b64d6841c695fa19.png" class="latex" alt="$S(n + 1) = 2S(n) + 2S(n - 1) + \cdots + 2S(1) + S(0)$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="405" height="18" />, which is a power of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/0/93032f1ab8b685d84a767a2fde0d9006a464b5e4.png" class="latex" alt="$3.$" width="12" height="12" /> Hence, our desired sum is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/1/b710e0c598f9ef5f291061007e57aa5f81db59b9.png" class="latex" alt="$(1 + 1 + 3 + 9 + 27 + 81 + 243)(S(0)) = 365\left(\frac{1}{73} +  \frac{1}{70}\right)$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="440" height="43" />. This simplifies to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/c/8/ac83014d001214c7834622fd940d9b338f4510b5.png" class="latex" alt="$365\left(\frac{143}{73(70)}\right) = \frac{143}{14} \textbf{(D)}$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="194" height="43" />.
