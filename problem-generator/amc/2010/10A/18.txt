<p>Bernardo randomly picks 3 distinct numbers from the set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/8/87885f860a66862d47eec72a817b95ab6ddc7aa4.png" class="latex" alt="$\{1,2,3,4,5,6,7,8,9\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="163" height="18" /> and arranges them in descending order to form a 3-digit number. Silvia randomly picks 3 distinct numbers from the set <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/8/0/d80821d0b6e5a96c845501a211d5a4c82d16aee8.png" class="latex" alt="$\{1,2,3,4,5,6,7,8\}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="146" height="18" /> and also arranges them in descending order to form a 3-digit number. What is the probability that Bernardo's number is larger than Silvia's number?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/2/6a2d3719bff0dd7de72dec1c29a31ba98f657eef.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{47}{72} \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ \frac{37}{56} \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ \frac{2}{3} \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ \frac{49}{72} \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ \frac{39}{56}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="404" height="37" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We can solve this by breaking the problem down into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> cases and adding up the probabilities.
</p><p><br />
Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />: Bernardo picks <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" />.
If Bernardo picks a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> then it is guaranteed that his number will be larger than Silvia's. The probability that he will pick a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/c/b/5cb0b4c6a241612ff300c4b5985f7194b7d6ed1b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1 \cdot \binom{8}{2}}{\binom{9}{3}} = \frac{\frac{8\cdot7}{2}}{\frac{9\cdot8\cdot7}{3\cdot2\cdot1}}=\frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="145" height="49" />.
</p><p><br />
Case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />: Bernardo does not pick <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" />.
Since the chance of Bernardo picking <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/1/4e1965f8d70832ded66434c75a7bd52b75090af0.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />, the probability of not picking <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/5/4/7547747b133453d3c209cff6cfbf129aa67a6947.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{2}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" />.
</p><p>If Bernardo does not pick <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" />, then he can pick any number from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/4/5/8455f3b5cb3b4880b8c9d782a5c1f0334db819eb.png" class="latex" alt="$8$" width="8" height="12" />. Since Bernardo is picking from the same set of numbers as Silvia, the probability that Bernardo's number is larger is equal to the probability that Silvia's number is larger.
</p><p>Ignoring the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> for now, the probability that they will pick the same number is the number of ways to pick Bernardo's 3 numbers divided by the number of ways to pick any 3 numbers.
</p><p>We get this probability to be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/5/cc54d2b03e64f035dc8c50b1b8d23b2bf2899dc6.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{3!}{8\cdot{7}\cdot{6}} = \frac{1}{56}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="102" height="38" />
</p><p>The probability of Bernardo's number being greater is
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/e/0/9e095b4556f12a1fed7a67640f70ef1dfb484c81.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{1-\frac{1}{56}}{2} = \frac{55}{112}\]" width="104" height="41" />
</p><p>Factoring the fact that Bernardo could've picked a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" /> but didn't:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/8/f580315e15f0e8bb88ee8d7b2d8109a57b25dd94.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{2}{3}\cdot{\frac{55}{112}} = \frac{55}{168}\]" width="109" height="38" />
</p><p>Adding up the two cases we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/0/b/c0bbb4c4b39eade36e673d7795220a683d527955.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{3}+\frac{55}{168} = \boxed{\frac{37}{56}\ \textbf{(B)}}$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="158" height="48" />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Note">Note</span></h3>
<p>We have for case <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/7/4d7fc76a8c59ed05efeaa09822f1e9e7f51899cf.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1 \cdot \binom{8}{2}}{\binom{9}{3}}$" style="vertical-align: -20px" width="47" height="49" /> since <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> is the number of ways to pick 9 and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/4/b/b4bf0feafdabeec4f94462a2534b5db277705cec.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{8}{2}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" /> is the number of ways to pick the other 2 numbers. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/8/d/18df9f33973fb468517d874ba5181a8433d4792d.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{9}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="33" height="53" /> means to choose 3 numbers from 9.
</p><p>~mathboy282
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>From Bernardo's set, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/4/e/d4e642f12ae500ab0f96f0f15c8d90bdfdbf4eea.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{9}{3} = 84$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="79" height="53" /> numbers that he can randomly choose. From Silvia's set, there are 
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/9/7/d972e26003400649fed74862332b87bf17c038fe.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{8}{3} = 56$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="79" height="53" /> numbers that she can randomly choose. Since Bernardo and Silvia can choose their numbers independently, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/8/d/58dd49d4cdb2ff983deec855feb1cc4744cf1ab2.png" class="latex" alt="$84 \cdot 56$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="49" height="13" /> pairs of numbers that you can compare. For example, if Bernardo chooses 321 and Silvia chooses 543, that is one pair. We can sort Bernardo's numbers from the greatest to the smallest. We can do the same for Silvia's numbers. So, Bernardo's greatest <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/2/0/620d140cd80c4778984409b986d8396967fe2a66.png" class="latex" alt="$84 - 56 = 28$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="100" height="13" /> numbers are all bigger than Silvia's numbers. Here, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/4/f94befc2c96905c626b3b0c3447af257eff40cb0.png" class="latex" alt="$28 \cdot 56$" width="49" height="12" /> pairs satisfying that Bernardo's number will be greater than Silvia's. If Bernardo chooses the 29th greatest number, which is the same as Silvia's greatest number, hence there will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/f/e/4fe487b794be15089c120c6ff639518ed9e9305c.png" class="latex" alt="$56 - 1 = 55$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="91" height="13" /> pairs satisfying that Bernardo's 29th greatest number will be greater than Silvia's. Similarly, if Bernardo chooses the 30th greatest number, there will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/0/5/d0502abe99865dd4f160085b60a4cf71aaa839c8.png" class="latex" alt="$55 - 1 = 54$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="91" height="13" /> pairs satisfying that Bernardo's 30th greatest number will be greater than Silvia's. This pattern continues. So if Bernardo chooses the 29th greatest number or below, he will have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/6/7/367344c44e829cec7b4bfc407140a9f020c0f7dc.png" class="latex" alt="$55 + 54 + 53 + \cdots + 1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="172" height="14" /> pairs where his number will be greater than Silvia's. 
</p><p>In total, Bernardo's probability of having a greater number than Silvia's is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/4/dc41f0fc586a41b98b5da8e68f42f31b39ee4290.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{28 \cdot 56 + (55 + 54 + 53 + \cdots + 1)}{84 \cdot 56} = \frac{37}{56}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="308" height="39" />, which is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/7/1/b7193f66028e13f331d53ff5e6942c4b05587465.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(B)}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="27" height="17" />.
</p><p><br />
- Leo M.
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="solution_3">solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Two cases: 
</p><p>1st: Bernado gets a nine, there are in total <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/4/7/f4790fc3abf0a7114c61cd1c8aba8bf6f8a7c4da.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{8}{2}\cdot \binom{8}{3}=1568$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="150" height="55" /> ways such Bernado gets a bigger number
</p><p>2nd: Both of them get numbers from one to eight, because of symmetry, they have equal chance of getting a bigger number. Thus, there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/3/7/537f8b809297f91b30d8f40a66ed901e6edd10b9.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{(\binom{8}{3})^2-\binom{8}{3}}{2}=1540$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="156" height="43" /> ways
</p><p>And there are in total <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/6/7/e672b12a1cbff7d7cfa0b6f5c22a71b00b50bff1.png" class="latex" alt="$\binom{9}{3}\cdot \binom{8}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="89" height="55" /> ways to pick numbers, the possibility is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/c/afc48a25b590b1f2ba8282cb09898c7f9fc6c4ac.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1568+1540}{4704}=\frac{37}{56}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="147" height="38" />
</p><p>~bluesoul
</p>
