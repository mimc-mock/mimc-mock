<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hints_and_Method_of_Attack">Hints and Method of Attack</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> be the result of dividing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ff2c0b95f4812c1f1a8f106e2a09718c79fa04d.png" class="latex" alt="$90!$" width="21" height="13" /> by tens such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> is not divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />. We want to consider <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/e/cfe684e694cf6d24c7aa35f0fc624f71c5fa2787.png" class="latex" alt="$P \mod 100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="93" height="13" />. But because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> is not prime, and because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> is obviously divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> (if in doubt, look at the answer choices), we only need to consider <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/e/7/0e7841232a6d543d9796ccea18cb6cf74e822123.png" class="latex" alt="$P \mod 25$" width="84" height="12" />.
</p><p>However, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/6/66644fd9f411299c3c1eabf151b5f601c9ef6c21.png" class="latex" alt="$25$" width="17" height="12" /> is a very particular number. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/5/8/958d536894a7f9c12b20e9e07dd969a88834e508.png" class="latex" alt="$1 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 4 \equiv -1 \text{ }(\text{mod }25)$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="200" height="20" />, and so is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/7/057f6cf7c6454188f3af37442b231a70ad902edc.png" class="latex" alt="$6 \cdot 7 \cdot 8 \cdot 9$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="78" height="14" />. How can we group terms to take advantage of this fact?
</p><p>There might be a problem when you cancel out the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" />s from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ff2c0b95f4812c1f1a8f106e2a09718c79fa04d.png" class="latex" alt="$90!$" width="21" height="13" />. One method is to cancel out a factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> from an existing number along with a factor of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />. But this might prove cumbersome, as the grouping method will not be as effective. Instead, take advantage of <i>inverses</i> in modular arithmetic. Just leave the negative powers of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> in a "storage base," and take care of the other terms first. Then, use Fermat's Little Theorem to solve for the power of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We will use the fact that for any integer <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />,
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/6/d/d6d0e3ac28c36172e5b280424fbe8ba8edfc7b70.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*}(5n+1)(5n+2)(5n+3)(5n+4)&amp;=[(5n+4)(5n+1)][(5n+2)(5n+3)]\\ &amp;=(25n^2+25n+4)(25n^2+25n+6)\equiv 4\cdot 6\\ &amp;=24\pmod{25}\equiv -1\pmod{25}.\end{align*}" width="606" height="70" />
</p><p>First, we find that the number of factors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/6/fc606f7f1e530731ab4f1cc364c01dc64a4455ee.png" class="latex" alt="$10$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="13" /> in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ff2c0b95f4812c1f1a8f106e2a09718c79fa04d.png" class="latex" alt="$90!$" width="21" height="13" /> is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/1/5/215046f6780a2919b04329326d423cf518ddd8a0.png" class="latex" alt="$\left\lfloor \frac{90}5\right\rfloor+\left\lfloor\frac{90}{25}\right\rfloor=18+3=21$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="223" height="43" />. Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/f/38ff6b0c61160f85e681c7587dd2bd3248fa7a36.png" class="latex" alt="$N=\frac{90!}{10^{21}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="75" height="38" />. The <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" /> we want is therefore the last two digits of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/b/c2bc9f36a90662712763980e4a46f764ea7ef3bf.png" class="latex" alt="$N\pmod{100}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="105" height="18" />. If instead we find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/7/4071ce9b2d4e102fd1433f9e1d333685434c6b89.png" class="latex" alt="$N\pmod{25}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="96" height="18" />, we know that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/b/c2bc9f36a90662712763980e4a46f764ea7ef3bf.png" class="latex" alt="$N\pmod{100}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="105" height="18" />, what we are looking for, could be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/7/4071ce9b2d4e102fd1433f9e1d333685434c6b89.png" class="latex" alt="$N\pmod{25}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="96" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/3/af3a40dd70a9136e9af46508237de63a98430710.png" class="latex" alt="$N\pmod{25}+25$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="137" height="18" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/d/d2d034822cf46960e119aa3cc7dfa89a7780f22b.png" class="latex" alt="$N\pmod{25}+50$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="137" height="18" />, or <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/e/4becab83b4fa72e9894ee2576aad578c6d7e6792.png" class="latex" alt="$N\pmod{25}+75$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="137" height="18" />. Only one of these numbers will be a multiple of four, and whichever one that is will be the answer, because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/b/c2bc9f36a90662712763980e4a46f764ea7ef3bf.png" class="latex" alt="$N\pmod{100}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="105" height="18" /> has to be a multiple of 4.
</p><p>If we divide <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" /> by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/9/029d6bd21dfbee0e3143d91225724af81b19259a.png" class="latex" alt="$5^{21}$" width="21" height="15" /> by taking out all the factors of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" /> in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" />, we can write <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/c/9/fc97ef67268cd4e91bacdf12b8901d7036c9a056.png" class="latex" alt="$N$" width="16" height="12" /> as <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/d/0bd011395668f4c68e93151abaf68bed4342ac01.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac M{2^{21}}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="25" height="37" /> where
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/a/f7a47bd604aded53f50c2def373fb58f27b265b2.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[M=1\cdot 2\cdot 3\cdot 4\cdot 1\cdot 6\cdot 7\cdot 8\cdot 9\cdot 2\cdots 89\cdot 18,\]" width="333" height="16" />
where every multiple of 5 is replaced by the number with all its factors of 5 removed. Specifically, every number in the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/9/9/29962900263bfdff81c6e189ffd4ff48247fccd4.png" class="latex" alt="$5n$" width="19" height="12" /> is replaced by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />, and every number in the form <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/1/941c5c39b101d5f54650e68db408f486ac067180.png" class="latex" alt="$25n$" width="28" height="12" /> is replaced by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/4/174fadd07fd54c9afe288e96558c92e0c1da733a.png" class="latex" alt="$n$" width="10" height="8" />.
</p><p>The number <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" /> can be grouped as follows:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/3/2/232832f682dc097feb1e7d869c8bcd9b68d93b52.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*}M= &amp;(1\cdot 2\cdot 3\cdot 4)(6\cdot 7\cdot 8\cdot 9)\cdots(86\cdot 87\cdot 88\cdot 89)\\ &amp;\cdot (1\cdot 2\cdot 3\cdot 4)(6\cdot 7\cdot 8\cdot 9)\cdots (16\cdot 17\cdot 18) \\ &amp;\cdot (1\cdot 2\cdot 3).\end{align*}" width="370" height="68" />
</p><p>Where the first line is composed of the numbers in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ff2c0b95f4812c1f1a8f106e2a09718c79fa04d.png" class="latex" alt="$90!$" width="21" height="13" /> that aren't multiples of five, the second line is the multiples of five <b>and not 25</b> after they have been divided by five, and the third line is multiples of 25 after they have been divided by 25.
</p><p>Using the identity at the beginning of the solution, we can reduce <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/d/1/5d1e4485dc90c450e8c76826516c1b2ccb8fce16.png" class="latex" alt="$M$" width="19" height="12" /> to
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/6/326a1dd69b71db842eaacc636763d93f9bdc7382.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\begin{align*}M&amp;\equiv(-1)^{18} \cdot (-1)^3(16\cdot 17\cdot 18) \cdot (1\cdot 2\cdot 3) \\ &amp;= 1\cdot -21\cdot 6\\ &amp;= -1\pmod{25} =24\pmod{25}.\end{align*}" width="327" height="69" />
</p><p>Using the fact that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/0/d/f0d6e480f0226a63827c0f28b0c1e0a9bdfbc51f.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{10}=1024\equiv -1\pmod{25}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="211" height="19" /> (or simply the fact that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/3/c6357516887d0b18243c37d43503da16c40c4bd8.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{21}=2097152$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="110" height="15" /> if you have your powers of 2 memorized), we can deduce that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/b/f/1bf565230dc73a79776fbd4f27ff191412654590.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{21}\equiv 2\pmod{25}$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="136" height="19" />. Therefore <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/d/1/ad11a9e07d7ba72340ef58c82bfa1517aee09bee.png" class="latex" alt="$N=\frac M{2^{21}}\equiv \frac {24}2\pmod{25}=12\pmod{25}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="317" height="37" />.
</p><p>Finally, combining with the fact that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/7/377ce2e3b3e5a6ffa9c306458b8ad7f0d69c6218.png" class="latex" alt="$N\equiv 0\pmod 4$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="120" height="18" /> yields <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/b/28b4650b111889a395f884ef30ddd6c867e6dffc.png" class="latex" alt="$n=\boxed{\textbf{(A)}\ 12}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="100" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ff2c0b95f4812c1f1a8f106e2a09718c79fa04d.png" class="latex" alt="$90!$" width="21" height="13" /> after we truncate its zeros. Notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ff2c0b95f4812c1f1a8f106e2a09718c79fa04d.png" class="latex" alt="$90!$" width="21" height="13" /> has exactly (floored) <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/d/9ad9d2b0685f23727a9038ee4e6da2c40044d0fe.png" class="latex" alt="$\left\lfloor\frac{90}{5}\right\rfloor + \left\lfloor\frac{90}{25}\right\rfloor = 21$" style="vertical-align: -17px" width="149" height="43" /> factors of 5; thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/d/a/cda5876bcdd175d4da46daad24922141c5e3dedc.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[P = 2^{-21}\cdot 5^{-21}\cdot 90!.\]" width="157" height="15" /> We shall consider <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> modulo 4 and 25, to determine its residue modulo 100. It is easy to prove that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> is divisible by 4 (consider the number of 2s dividing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ff2c0b95f4812c1f1a8f106e2a09718c79fa04d.png" class="latex" alt="$90!$" width="21" height="13" /> minus the number of 5s dividing <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ff2c0b95f4812c1f1a8f106e2a09718c79fa04d.png" class="latex" alt="$90!$" width="21" height="13" />), and so we only need to consider <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/b/4/4b4cade9ca8a2c8311fafcf040bc5b15ca507f52.png" class="latex" alt="$P$" width="14" height="12" /> modulo 25.
</p><p>Now, notice that for integers <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/5/375398566c85556f748c4d7587b522ceffcdb1b6.png" class="latex" alt="$a, n$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="28" height="11" /> we have<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/c/dac33e5be873b94557b44465f297002b9771a016.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(5n + a)(5n - a) \equiv -a^2 \mod 25.\]" width="266" height="19" />
</p><p>Thus, for integral a: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/e/6/ae68caae9a67e76187dcb427056cd39532905d58.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(10a + 1)(10a + 2)(10a + 3)(10a + 4)(10a + 6)(10a + 7)(10a + 8)(10a + 9) \equiv (-1)(-4)(-9)(-16) \equiv 576 \equiv 1 \mod 25.\]" width="868" height="18" /> Using this process, we can essentially remove all the numbers which had not formerly been a multiple of 5 in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ff2c0b95f4812c1f1a8f106e2a09718c79fa04d.png" class="latex" alt="$90!$" width="21" height="13" /> from consideration.
</p><p>Now, we consider the remnants of the 5, 10, 15, 20, ..., 90 not yet eliminated. The 10, 20, 30, ..., 90 becomes 1, 2, 3, 4, 1, 6, 7, 8, 9, whose product is 1 mod 25. Also, the 5, 5, 15, 25, ..., 85 becomes 1, 1, 3, 1, 7, 9, 11, 13, 3, 17 and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/8/7/e8729ef8f870f900505d70d51bc566fb6db60746.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{-12}$" width="32" height="15" />. We deduce that from multiplying out the 1, 1, 3, 1, 7, ..., 17 is equivalent to 2 modulo 25, and so we need to compute <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/0/0/a00e6099251d66dc79acfbaf988706f7b2e2c972.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{-11}$" width="31" height="15" />. Also note that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/2/1/821b0b7cae07bec4e94662487272adbd0a8598db.png" class="latex" alt="$\phi(25)=5^2-5^1=20$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="167" height="21" />. And by combining Euler's Theorem with Fermat's Little Theorem, we get that  <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/3/7/937f9ad704b419d965a9593d16ac2e6776eb5d12.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{-11} \equiv 2^{-11+20} = 2^9 = 512 \equiv 12 \mod 25$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="323" height="17" />. Because 12 is also a multiple of 4, we can utilize the Chinese Remainder Theorem to show that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/5/2259e380529e34cca4655bea84ed26b4935428d7.png" class="latex" alt="$P \equiv 12 \mod 100$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="139" height="14" /> and so the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/6/38693a75a9e00c962455566cda9d06d94da1738b.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{12}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="30" height="23" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>We start of by truncating the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/1/bc1f9d9bf8a1b606a4188b5ce9a2af1809e27a89.png" class="latex" alt="$0$" width="8" height="12" />s off <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ff2c0b95f4812c1f1a8f106e2a09718c79fa04d.png" class="latex" alt="$90!$" width="21" height="13" />, just like Solution 2. Since there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/d/3adc5487cdcbab420c26bd9bee1d3ac01592658b.png" class="latex" alt="$v_5(90!) = \left\lfloor\frac{90}{5}\right\rfloor+\left\lfloor\frac{90}{25}\right\rfloor = 21$" style="vertical-align: -18px" width="230" height="45" /> terminating zeroes, we have the number we obtain from truncating the terminating zeroes at the end of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ff2c0b95f4812c1f1a8f106e2a09718c79fa04d.png" class="latex" alt="$90!$" width="21" height="13" /> will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/7/017048208f1ac46f6045fa42ec0d80a58265841d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{90!}{10^{21}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="38" height="39" />.
</p><p>By <a href="/wiki/index.php/Chinese_Remainder_Theorem" title="Chinese Remainder Theorem">Chinese Remainder Theorem</a>, we can divide the mod <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/5/9/e59e2c6e83eb78cca610a5fd4070ae01c8d4ae60.png" class="latex" alt="$100$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="26" height="13" /> into mod <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/7/c/c7cab1a05e1e0c1d51a6a219d96577a16b7abf9d.png" class="latex" alt="$4$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="9" height="12" /> and mod <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/6/66644fd9f411299c3c1eabf151b5f601c9ef6c21.png" class="latex" alt="$25$" width="17" height="12" />. We know that there are way more than <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/a/1/9a1b2497e7b125cd721bab4c6e2385f954fe35cb.png" class="latex" alt="$23$" width="17" height="12" /> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" />s in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/7/017048208f1ac46f6045fa42ec0d80a58265841d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{90!}{10^{21}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="38" height="39" />, so we have
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/6/f/f6fce350aadcea204a94f339fe808a6e8748ea6d.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\dfrac{90!}{10^{21}} \equiv 0 \text{ } \text{ (mod } 4 \text{)}\]" width="136" height="38" />
</p><p>Now, notice that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/d/9/7d973e22c5803e3d04da9c735bd26663d7b07751.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{90!}{5^{21}}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="25" height="38" /> is basically <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/f/f/9ff2c0b95f4812c1f1a8f106e2a09718c79fa04d.png" class="latex" alt="$90!$" width="21" height="13" /> without any factors that are multiples of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/9/0/79069377f91364c2f87a64e5f9f562a091c8a6c1.png" class="latex" alt="$5$" width="8" height="12" />.
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/9/1/591913976f0b26af43c466bfee914eef86d49ab3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[1\cdot 2\cdot 3\cdot 4\cdot 6\cdot 7\cdots 23\cdot 24 \text{ } \text{ (mod } 25 \text{)}\]" width="273" height="17" />
</p><p>We can rewrite the expression as 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/8/9289ca575a86ab2ecb0f1d1261fb9fa782a3be96.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(1\cdot 2\cdot 3\cdot 4\cdot 6\cdot 7\cdots 12)^2 \text{ } \text{ (mod } 25 \text{)}\]" width="263" height="20" />
</p><p>We also have 
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/5/b/d5bfacc61f7daf093888eaffa64bf92caf33e8c3.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[1\cdot 2\cdot 3\cdot 4\cdot 6\cdot 7\cdots 12 \equiv 8\cdot 8\cdot 9\cdot 11\cdot 12 \equiv 7 \text{ } \text{ (mod } 25 \text{)}\]" width="416" height="17" />
</p><p>Squaring the expression gets us
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/0/f/40f0e8eb20ea7fccc28fc0a020feeafc74c0d1e1.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[(1\cdot 2\cdot 3\cdot 4\cdot 6\cdot 7\cdots 12)^2 \equiv 49 \equiv -1 \text{ } \text{ (mod } 25 \text{)}\]" width="353" height="20" />
</p><p>Notice that there are <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cde695f2e4542fd01f860a89189f47a27143b66.png" class="latex" alt="$3$" width="8" height="12" /> of this string of numbers multiplied together occurs four times in <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/1/7/017048208f1ac46f6045fa42ec0d80a58265841d.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{90!}{10^{21}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="38" height="39" />, the fourth being only a partial
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/e/c6ed3d40e55d457aba8a538f6f3bcc9e3048a5f6.png" class="latex" alt="$1\cdot 2\cdot 3\cdot 4\cdots 14 \equiv 24 \equiv -1 \text{ } \text{ (mod } 25 \text{)}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="292" height="18" />
</p><p>Multiplying these together gives us
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/3/0/c30947e15834b5cc230e0fe79c672a439dc97567.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{90!}{10^{21}} \equiv \frac{\frac{90!}{5^{21}}}{2^{21}} \equiv \frac{(-1)^4}{2^{21}} \equiv \frac{1}{2^{21}} \text{ } \text{ (mod } 25 \text{)}\]" width="288" height="41" />
</p><p>By Euler's Theorem, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/e/7/5e73a5197f188b9d942da1b790a28b47bebcf3e3.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{1}{2^{21}} \equiv 12^{21} \equiv 12^{\phi{(25)}}\cdot 12 \equiv 12 \text{ } \text{ (mod } 25 \text{)}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="314" height="38" />
</p><p>Thus, we want to solve <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/4/5/04552ca1b47292a3424e090cd85b68c5e938970c.png" class="latex" alt="$x \equiv 12 \text{ } \text{ (mod } 25 \text{)}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="134" height="18" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/d/4ed4b2ac09258afd01f192f32db1b359a1eab4a3.png" class="latex" alt="$x \equiv 0 \text{ } \text{ (mod } 4 \text{)}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="116" height="18" />. An obvious solution is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/a/00aa6263bcbba1f9af10a858bd7a6256188f67fa.png" class="latex" alt="$x\equiv 12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="52" height="12" /> and by Chinese Remainder Theorem, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/0/a/00aa6263bcbba1f9af10a858bd7a6256188f67fa.png" class="latex" alt="$x\equiv 12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="52" height="12" /> is the only solution. So thus, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/8/3/08391561205041033f23810e93b39a5c5d972f30.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{90!}{10^{21}} \equiv \boxed{12} \text{ } \text{ (mod } 100 \text{)}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="180" height="39" />
</p><p>~sml1809
</p>
<h2><span id="Remark_(Chinese_Remainder_Theorem)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Remark_.28Chinese_Remainder_Theorem.29">Remark (Chinese Remainder Theorem)</span></h2>
<p>Both solution 1 and solution 2 rely on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/d/87df7ef1caa2d27ad961a7621340bc710ee93df4.png" class="latex" alt="$n \equiv 12 (\bmod { \quad 25})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="141" height="20" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/4/7c4b38c8d490747f10ae67cd52846f59e85a12bc.png" class="latex" alt="$n \equiv 0 (\bmod { \quad 4})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="123" height="20" /> to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/c/5/cc524a73fc4c0a9717b0a9569886352df5b1c436.png" class="latex" alt="$n (\bmod { \quad 100})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="108" height="20" />
</p><p>By <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://artofproblemsolving.com/wiki/index.php/Chinese_Remainder_Theorem">Chinese Remainder Theorem</a>, the general solution of the system of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> linear congruences is:
</p>
<pre><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/a/3/5a33cf90f103de64ff6eb894efbb38aef5bf2277.png" class="latex" alt="$n \equiv r_1 (\bmod { \quad m_1})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="144" height="20" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/5/5/f5553f1f91d52b592a23edf4871336a462586c35.png" class="latex" alt="$n \equiv r_2 (\bmod { \quad m_2})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="144" height="20" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/2/942c227e3f1db997d4be45b2dc23707221bbfd14.png" class="latex" alt="$(m_1, m_2) = 1$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="104" height="20" />
Find <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/f/2/7f2f57887b2e2f63a617b6a4b7a7d466c26c4342.png" class="latex" alt="$k_1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="15" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/b/7/cb72004e1c6c7b0782bbf2c7e40dcaad72993c4f.png" class="latex" alt="$k_2$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="15" height="15" /> such that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/6/0/460c73dfa9b2d6bcd7c2e2453ffa6521ca2040f4.png" class="latex" alt="$k_1 m_1 \equiv 1 (\bmod{ \quad m_2})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="167" height="20" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/d/f2d8192ffc01e71170b584d0adb047c360a4641a.png" class="latex" alt="$k_2 m_2 \equiv 1 (\bmod{ \quad m_1})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="167" height="20" />
Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/9/2/c927ef277d8295e125b86cdaa0326b99388d7bba.png" class="latex" alt="$n \equiv k_2 m_2 r_1 + k_1 m_1 r_2 (\bmod{ \quad m_1 m_2})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="286" height="20" />
</pre>
<p>In this problem, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/7/d/87df7ef1caa2d27ad961a7621340bc710ee93df4.png" class="latex" alt="$n \equiv 12 (\bmod { \quad 25})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="141" height="20" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/4/7c4b38c8d490747f10ae67cd52846f59e85a12bc.png" class="latex" alt="$n \equiv 0 (\bmod { \quad 4})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="123" height="20" />:
</p>
<pre><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/7/f2719f8e5bd40bda4f84bec18432cb6042e10688.png" class="latex" alt="$n \equiv 12 (\bmod{ \quad 25})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="141" height="20" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/4/e/94e2f54476787c00f482a4c753f79ef251f04d6b.png" class="latex" alt="$n \equiv 0 (\bmod{ \quad 4})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="123" height="20" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/3/7/637a522b42c74a244b5256a9fba3a766814a7ca5.png" class="latex" alt="$(25, 4) = 1$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="85" height="20" />
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/9/a/f9abd700537ba9a4b4554d8c22cee9a07bdf8b46.png" class="latex" alt="$1 \cdot 25 \equiv 1 (\bmod{ \quad 4})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="152" height="20" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/a/3/4a36e2590757699596a909a64bdca7537cf98353.png" class="latex" alt="$19 \cdot 4 \equiv 1 (\bmod{ \quad 25})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="161" height="20" />
Then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/7/7/f77466b3e36081f0a186d095bd627df8bb27ceda.png" class="latex" alt="$n \equiv 19 \cdot 4 \cdot 12 + 1 \cdot 25 \cdot 0 \equiv 12 (\bmod{ \quad 100})$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="331" height="20" />
</pre>
