<p>Equiangular hexagon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/2/8029902caf855d940e465d04ff40101d8850c3e0.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCDEF$" width="86" height="13" /> has side lengths <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/3/7c35f8ba8eb142634fcd6cbca6085094e2118735.png" class="latex" alt="$AB=CD=EF=1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="167" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/1/1615032fd86cfd058e45f442a75e0f8956c5ffa9.png" class="latex" alt="$BC=DE=FA=r$" width="167" height="13" />. The area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/d/6dd17eadc905d042a9f376ccc996ec45099f8fe7.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ACE$" width="58" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/2/282fdef75f579c92565cd7e0734d83ccfd38bfdc.png" class="latex" alt="$70\%$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="32" height="14" /> of the area of the hexagon. What is the sum of all possible values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" />?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/e/74e3e07c4c08cec59190cc5aa2a38715b090dfe0.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ \frac{4\sqrt{3}}{3} \qquad \textbf{(B)} \frac{10}{3} \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 4 \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ \frac{17}{4} \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 6$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="397" height="41" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>It is clear that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/d/6dd17eadc905d042a9f376ccc996ec45099f8fe7.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ACE$" width="58" height="13" /> is an equilateral triangle. From the <a href="/wiki/index.php/Law_of_Cosines" title="Law of Cosines">Law of Cosines</a> on <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/3/8c3a2d2224f7d163b46d702132425d47828bf538.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ABC$" width="58" height="13" />, we get that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/4/d/74db486fc7d98229fb68fd2c35b9a60f1dbae316.png" class="latex" alt="$AC^2 = r^2+1^2-2r\cos{\frac{2\pi}{3}} = r^2+r+1$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="310" height="37" />. Therefore, the area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/d/6dd17eadc905d042a9f376ccc996ec45099f8fe7.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle ACE$" width="58" height="13" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/d/2/bd25cf30be0e8ed8b80c8b1de0d9875bae9b9f68.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}(r^2+r+1)$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="119" height="41" /> by area of an equilateral triangle.
</p><p>If we extend <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/5/6d5c9afa92ae3180f02754c177e530d9d0f8b4d4.png" class="latex" alt="$DE$" width="29" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/3/193c5caa0ee1174e95025208873f5495493a2059.png" class="latex" alt="$FA$" width="27" height="13" /> so that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/3/193c5caa0ee1174e95025208873f5495493a2059.png" class="latex" alt="$FA$" width="27" height="13" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" /> meet at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/a/4/6a47ca0fe7cb276abc022af6ac88ddae1a9d6894.png" class="latex" alt="$X$" width="15" height="12" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/c/5/6c52a41dcbd739f1d026c5d4f181438b75b76976.png" class="latex" alt="$BC$" width="28" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/5/6d5c9afa92ae3180f02754c177e530d9d0f8b4d4.png" class="latex" alt="$DE$" width="29" height="12" /> meet at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/5/ce58e4af225c93d08606c26554caaa5ae32edeba.png" class="latex" alt="$Y$" width="14" height="12" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/5/6d5c9afa92ae3180f02754c177e530d9d0f8b4d4.png" class="latex" alt="$DE$" width="29" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/9/3/193c5caa0ee1174e95025208873f5495493a2059.png" class="latex" alt="$FA$" width="27" height="13" /> meet at <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/e/a/3ead47fb9fb4a4c273feee398f72ff2a09702b84.png" class="latex" alt="$Z$" width="13" height="12" />, we find that hexagon <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/2/8029902caf855d940e465d04ff40101d8850c3e0.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCDEF$" width="86" height="13" /> is formed by taking equilateral triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/2/0526e1f5ad1412706cf938657f754604e672a671.png" class="latex" alt="$XYZ$" width="44" height="12" /> of side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/e/5/2e55c7390e7abf9ab74bdb41b8926927792519c5.png" class="latex" alt="$r+2$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="39" height="13" /> and removing three equilateral triangles, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/2/5/c25098016dac91dc30e5febffb0d0e73a92f96e6.png" class="latex" alt="$ABX$" width="44" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/7/4/274ca2c5f4dd3dd88b12cdf8034b45f7d6e31da1.png" class="latex" alt="$CDY$" width="44" height="12" /> and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/6/4/6640c2ded93be1ec3be12f489d5237837beaf479.png" class="latex" alt="$EFZ$" width="42" height="12" />, of side length <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" />. The area of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/0/2/8029902caf855d940e465d04ff40101d8850c3e0.png" class="latex" alt="$ABCDEF$" width="86" height="13" /> is therefore
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/d/6/6d64c3ce7ba06d4da88607439a4fd8d81ec545df.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}(r+2)^2-\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{4} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}(r^2+4r+1)$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="302" height="41" />.
</p><p><br />
Based on the initial conditions,
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/1/dc1cb478fd6f41e7cf54668851df87469a184d56.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}(r^2+r+1) = \frac{7}{10}\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}\right)(r^2+4r+1)\]" width="327" height="53" />
</p><p>Simplifying this gives us <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/3/2836de34aaf80c6d16091c83c0b49902d10a4772.png" class="latex" alt="$r^2-6r+1 = 0$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="120" height="16" />. By <a href="/wiki/index.php/Vieta%27s_Formulas" class="mw-redirect" title="Vieta&#39;s Formulas">Vieta's Formulas</a> we know that the sum of the possible value of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/2/1/f2192779fb1d8828b06ce59a7ae3f9a2ef90ca5b.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(E)}\ 6}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="53" height="28" />.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>Step 1: Use <a href="/wiki/index.php/Law_of_Cosines" title="Law of Cosines">Law of Cosines</a> in the same manner as the previous solution to get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/d/a1d97bba860b0d26eacd5db66b457ac5186fc929.png" class="latex" alt="$AC=\sqrt{r^2+r+1}$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="146" height="18" />. 
</p><p>Step 2: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/8/1f828f10cad9235a2c72c34369f5fd4a1179e579.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{ABC}$" width="58" height="13" />~<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/8/388b0b8e7380c2aea8dd1080b5216b0ba5b2acdc.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{CDE}$" width="60" height="13" />~<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/6/3a623c93cd11bd0193391291eec99a0b9ab56d8a.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{EFA}$" width="58" height="13" /> via SAS congruency. Using the formula <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/5/4/254a2fd08bf69ba0b5dbb920a630aaa26ccb64f4.png" class="latex" alt="$[ABC]=\frac{ab \sin C}{2}= \frac{r \sqrt{3}}{4}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="200" height="41" />. The area of the hexagon is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/e/e/bee423252ef573bf1592e6f55ab44df3a19d937a.png" class="latex" alt="$[ACE] + 3[ABC]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="134" height="18" />. We are given that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/8/2/282fdef75f579c92565cd7e0734d83ccfd38bfdc.png" class="latex" alt="$70\%$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="32" height="14" /> of this area is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/d/b/2db75593953fef249c69c42dfc6c0322ffbdb34c.png" class="latex" alt="$[ACE]$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="50" height="18" />; solving for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/1/7/a179ff2638e4799cadd820db205c2beff6299ce9.png" class="latex" alt="$AC$" width="27" height="13" /> in terms of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> gives <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/f/5/cf5f75dca4138e21b466b251ce1aa451d2b27737.png" class="latex" alt="$AC=\sqrt{7r}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="85" height="18" />.
</p><p>Step 3: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/a/c/dac655b85821a8db06e8f94f905d1764afe9d7e0.png" class="latex" alt="$\sqrt{7r}=\sqrt{r^2+r+1} \implies r^2-6r+1=0$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="320" height="19" /> and by <a href="/wiki/index.php/Vieta%27s_Formulas" class="mw-redirect" title="Vieta&#39;s Formulas">Vieta's Formulas</a> , we get <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/7/1/3711217e5f5d573cdedc2efeb489063c5d6ed51e.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{E}}$" style="vertical-align: -5px" width="24" height="23" />.
</p><p>Note: To verify that the quadratic <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/0/f/20fac3d0740981eedbd59dc6f80f948cb88ff8dd.png" class="latex" alt="$r^2-6r+1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="89" height="18" /> has two positive roots, we can either solve for the roots directly or note that discriminant is positive, and there are no negative roots (because then <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/6/c/16cb15e7c0ef74cf09c6a87a0e15ce2777f137f0.png" class="latex" alt="$r^2, -6r, 1$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="74" height="20" /> would all be positive).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Find the area of the triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/4/f348b5890100fa57eaa4a3c548badc83b75af585.png" class="latex" alt="$ACE$" width="42" height="13" /> as how it was done in solution 1. Find the sum of the areas of the congruent  triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/6/7a68d8b1ed85ed0c2f51a7b094ef47e5c71402cb.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC, CDE, EFA$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="144" height="16" /> as how it was done in solution 2. The sum of these areas is the area of the hexagon, hence the areas of the congruent  triangles <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/a/6/7a68d8b1ed85ed0c2f51a7b094ef47e5c71402cb.png" class="latex" alt="$ABC, CDE, EFA$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="144" height="16" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/e/32e5939a7bdd847ec86582225cd653c240b4abce.png" class="latex" alt="$30\%$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="32" height="14" /> of the area of the hexagon. Hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/1/f/e1f2bce73877ab24e4abe8ae41191c7620c66c1b.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{7}{3}$" style="vertical-align: -12px" width="11" height="37" /> times the latter is equal to the triangle <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/3/4/f348b5890100fa57eaa4a3c548badc83b75af585.png" class="latex" alt="$ACE$" width="42" height="13" />. Hence <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/2/c/52c269a86ae400b3c2b946c5b46420038a13f04e.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{7}{3}\cdot\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{4}r=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}(r^2+r+1)$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="215" height="41" />. We can simplify this to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/5/d/05d0474efa7fc3fcdfe5b4dd5c74ba45e5fd3e44.png" class="latex" alt="$7r=r^2+r+1\implies r^2-6r+1=0$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="290" height="16" />. By Vieta's, we get the sum of all possible values of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/6/4/3/643f8ec6977b3a66760c80eb2f5ba4933db4556c.png" class="latex" alt="$-\frac{-6}{1}=6\text{ or } \boxed{\textbf{E}}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="124" height="38" />.
-vsamc
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proof_Triangle_ACE_is_Equilateral.">Proof Triangle ACE is Equilateral.</span></h3>
<p>We know <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/f/8/1f828f10cad9235a2c72c34369f5fd4a1179e579.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{ABC}$" width="58" height="13" />, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/8/8/388b0b8e7380c2aea8dd1080b5216b0ba5b2acdc.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{CDE}$" width="60" height="13" />, and <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/6/3a623c93cd11bd0193391291eec99a0b9ab56d8a.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{EFA}$" width="58" height="13" /> are congruent by SAS, so the side opposite the 120 degree angle is also the same (since the triangles are congruent). Thus <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/5/5/4558dac720390e26adeddd9fade787a9d5f8c731.png" class="latex" alt="$\triangle{ACE}$" width="58" height="13" /> is equilateral.
Q.E.D.
