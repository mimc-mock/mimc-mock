<p>The entries in a <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/2/7/d278803d72fcc493afb1559174a483aa7f41d143.png" class="latex" alt="$3 \times 3$" width="40" height="12" /> array include all the digits from <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> through <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/2/bf2c9074b396e3af0dea52d792660eea1c77f10f.png" class="latex" alt="$9$" width="8" height="12" />, arranged so that the entries in every row and column are in increasing order. How many such arrays are there?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/6/5/c653cdbb6e1ffcd28616c181b59b36ef708ca0b5.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 18 \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 24 \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 36 \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 42 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 60$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="394" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>Observe that all tables must have 1s and 9s in the corners, 8s and 2s next to those corner squares, and 4-6 in the middle square. Also note that for each table, there exists a valid table diagonally symmetrical across the diagonal extending from the top left to the bottom right. 
</p><p><br />
</p>
<ul><li><b>Case 1: Center 4</b></li></ul>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/a/f/caf48ebf925e6642ea983260dc322a77a100a088.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|} \hline 1&amp;2&amp;\\ \hline 3&amp;4&amp;8\\ \hline &amp;&amp;9\\ \hline \end{tabular} \;\;\; \begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|} \hline 1&amp;2&amp;\\ \hline 3&amp;4&amp;\\ \hline &amp;8&amp;9\\ \hline \end{tabular}\]" width="185" height="63" />
</p><p>3 necessarily must be placed as above. Any number could fill the isolated square, but the other 2 are then invariant. So, there are 3 cases each and 6 overall cases. Given diagonal symmetry, alternate 2 and 8 placements yield symmetrical cases. <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/9/22974a9606055a8ca8d8d2f1fce369557a5bf691.png" class="latex" alt="$2*6=12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="77" height="13" />
</p>
<ul><li><b>Case 2: Center 5</b></li></ul>
<p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/6/2/062f79ecd82412a93b75b35f14a29ed14462fc0c.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[\begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|} \hline 1&amp;2&amp;3\\ \hline 4&amp;5&amp;\\ \hline &amp;8&amp;9\\ \hline \end{tabular} \;\;\; \begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|} \hline 1&amp;2&amp;\\ \hline 3&amp;5&amp;\\ \hline &amp;8&amp;9\\ \hline \end{tabular} \;\;\; \begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|} \hline 1&amp;2&amp;\\ \hline 3&amp;5&amp;8\\ \hline &amp;&amp;9\\ \hline \end{tabular} \;\;\; \begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|} \hline 1&amp;2&amp;3\\ \hline 4&amp;5&amp;8\\ \hline &amp;&amp;9\\ \hline \end{tabular}\]" width="385" height="63" />
</p><p>Here, no 3s or 7s are assured, but this is only a teensy bit trickier and messier. WLOG, casework with 3 instead of 7 as above. Remembering that <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/f/1/6/f16d34224d53ae631ec9ebc49100a14047794cea.png" class="latex" alt="$4&lt;5$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="42" height="13" />, logically see that the numbers of cases are then 2,3,3,1 respectively. By symmetry, <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/f/6/bf6de2dbd4663cc0f5f65242cac7048300b41d31.png" class="latex" alt="$2*9=18$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="77" height="13" />
</p>
<ul><li><b>Case 3: Center 6</b></li></ul>
<p>By inspection, realize that this is symmetrical to case 1 except that the 7s instead of the 3s are assured.
<img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/2/2/9/22974a9606055a8ca8d8d2f1fce369557a5bf691.png" class="latex" alt="$2*6=12$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="77" height="13" />
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/0/c/30c184c1b20b9078b59d735b5eaba48244e4a096.png" class="latexcenter" alt="\[12+18+12=\boxed{\textbf{D)}42}\]" width="174" height="28" />
</p><p><br />
~BJHHar
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>This solution is trivial by the hook length theorem. The hooks look like this:
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/b/0bb493e07f07e2e70f98a4df8b01815e28e1417c.png" class="latex" alt="$\begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|} \hline 5 &amp; 4 &amp; 3 \\ \hline 4 &amp; 3 &amp; 2\\ \hline 3 &amp; 2 &amp; 1\\ \hline \end{tabular}$" style="vertical-align: -26px" width="86" height="62" />
</p><p>So, the answer is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/7/d/57d5d21950e3b6b714f38fda41be2e8641521704.png" class="latex" alt="$\frac{9!}{5 \cdot 4 \cdot 3 \cdot 4 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1}$" style="vertical-align: -13px" width="189" height="38" /> = <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/5/4/85484b70ba4bf8ba6d59438baaf7cb18b149987a.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\text{(D) }42}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="61" height="28" />
</p><p>P.S. The hook length formula is a formula to calculate the number of standard Young tableaux of a Young diagram. Numberphile has an easy-to-understand video about it here: <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vgZhrEs4tuk">https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vgZhrEs4tuk</a> The full proof is quite complicated and is not given in the video, although the video hints at possible proofs.
</p>
