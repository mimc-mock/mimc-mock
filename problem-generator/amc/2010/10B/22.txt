<p>Seven distinct pieces of candy are to be distributed among three bags. The red bag and the blue bag must each receive at least one piece of candy; the white bag may remain empty. How many arrangements are possible?
</p><p><img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/b/7/0b70a3fd1400e4fa1172f161c1c274e97dde494a.png" class="latex" alt="$\textbf{(A)}\ 1930 \qquad \textbf{(B)}\ 1931 \qquad \textbf{(C)}\ 1932 \qquad \textbf{(D)}\ 1933 \qquad \textbf{(E)}\ 1934$" style="vertical-align: -4px" width="485" height="17" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_1">Solution 1</span></h2>
<p>We can count the total number of ways to distribute the candies (ignoring the restrictions), and then subtract the overcount to get the answer.
</p><p>Each candy has three choices; it can go in any of the three bags.
</p><p>Since there are seven candies, that makes the <b>total distribution</b> <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/8/7/187205554f8300782a03d878a07c61ad13890cc3.png" class="latex" alt="$3^7=2187$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="77" height="15" />
</p><p><br />
To find the overcount, we calculate the number of invalid distributions: the red or blue bag is empty. 
</p><p>The number of distributions such that the red bag is empty is equal to <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/c/afc77519ee6bab163b1aa195a3ba88718d414aa4.png" class="latex" alt="$2^7$" width="15" height="15" />, since it's equivalent to distributing the <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" /> candies into <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/1/c/41c544263a265ff15498ee45f7392c5f86c6d151.png" class="latex" alt="$2$" width="8" height="12" /> bags.
</p><p>We know that the number of distributions with the blue bag is empty will be the same number because of the symmetry, so it's also <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/f/c/afc77519ee6bab163b1aa195a3ba88718d414aa4.png" class="latex" alt="$2^7$" width="15" height="15" />. 
</p><p>The case where both the red and the blue bags are empty (all <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/0/a/e0a0db32027a732ac57d37ef2ae9bb150f65b108.png" class="latex" alt="$7$" width="9" height="12" /> candies are in the white bag) are included in both of the above calculations, and this case has only <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/c/e/dce34f4dfb2406144304ad0d6106c5382ddd1446.png" class="latex" alt="$1$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="8" height="12" /> distribution.
</p><p><b>The total overcount is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/c/4/8c4545bb71ecb6b97593b3ff6ec3107989a223cb.png" class="latex" alt="$2^7+2^7-1=2^8-1$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="157" height="16" /></b>
</p><p><br />
The final answer will be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/d/e/4dec24a5ec58d24f4768b6796ba537e37c13f450.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{total}-\text{overcount}=2187-(2^8-1) = 2187-256+1=1931+1=\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ 1932}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="611" height="28" />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_2">Solution 2</span></h2>
<p>We can use to our advantage the answer choices <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/b/d3b9523dfe2d602ad8f0a67044ffcacc0bdc0423.png" class="latex" alt="$\text{AMC}$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="39" height="12" /> has given us, and eliminate the obvious wrong answers.
</p><p>We can first figure out how many ways there are to take two candies from seven distinct candies to place them into the red/blue bags: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/d/7/0d7b2c93efbbe27236f4707df101885e6ea23c1b.png" class="latex" alt="$7\cdot 6=42$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="73" height="13" />.
</p><p>Now we can look at the answer choices to find out which ones are divisible by <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/0/ed0d6c36fdad9c2874053be46c7e4120f2edcf47.png" class="latex" alt="$42$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" />, since the total number of combinations must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/e/d/0/ed0d6c36fdad9c2874053be46c7e4120f2edcf47.png" class="latex" alt="$42$" style="vertical-align: 0px" width="17" height="12" /> multiplied by some other number.
</p><p>Since answers A, B, D, and E are not divisible by 3 (divisor of 42), the answer must be <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/a/1/3a1a70d386e47ee398bac40bed9b269aeae8764a.png" class="latex" alt="$\boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ 1932}$" style="vertical-align: -10px" width="82" height="28" />.
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_3">Solution 3</span></h2>
<p>Let <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/5/5/b55ca7a0aa88ab7d58f4fc035317fdac39b17861.png" class="latex" alt="$r$" width="8" height="8" /> be the number of red bag candies. For <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/f/02fd646ae6af4e103f55627b3880cecdfc009a80.png" class="latex" alt="${1 \leq r \leq 6}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="77" height="16" />. 
</p><p>So the number of candies left for the blue bag and the red bag is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/6/bc6817e905b24cbaae4635c97a452e3f469a63c5.png" class="latex" alt="$7-r$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="42" height="15" />. Based on the problem, 1 candy must be fixed for the blue bag, which can be done <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/6/bc6817e905b24cbaae4635c97a452e3f469a63c5.png" class="latex" alt="$7-r$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="42" height="15" /> ways. Now, before we continue, we need to realize that fixing a candy can lead to some over counting in cases where none in the white bag overlap. So we should try and find an alternative because we'll be over counting more than twice, and that will become extremely difficult to account for each case's over counting. 
</p><p>So, without fixing candies, we can put everything into options and work on the white bag, because once we figure out two bags, the remaining one is decided. 
</p><p>The options for the candies in the white bag are two: In the white bag or out of the white bag(by default, in the blue bag).
</p><p>Now, for choosing the red bag, we have <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/2/b/32b92d5ebd66ca9416156c4bbbcf2dff900e716f.png" class="latex" alt="${7 \choose r}$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="39" height="55" />.
</p><p>Then, we have for the white bag: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/4/e/8/4e8571fcd8df96f1f30110c2beffc1e189f2cf87.png" class="latex" alt="$2^{7-r}$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="35" height="17" />. Because we aren't fixing one for blue, the power is <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/c/6/bc6817e905b24cbaae4635c97a452e3f469a63c5.png" class="latex" alt="$7-r$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="42" height="15" /> instead of <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/c/e/c/cec2a0b77eea1798725166918ae387cd71485fe9.png" class="latex" alt="$7-r-1$" style="vertical-align: -2px" width="73" height="15" />
</p><p>We have: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/a/92a169c47c25dbb09de9def601d8929bcbda460d.png" class="latex" alt="$({7 \choose r}) \cdot (2^{7-r})$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="114" height="55" />
Adding them up for <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/0/2/f/02fd646ae6af4e103f55627b3880cecdfc009a80.png" class="latex" alt="${1 \leq r \leq 6}$" style="vertical-align: -3px" width="77" height="16" />, we get 2058.
</p><p>Now, for the invalid cases. Because we didn't fix candy for the blue bag, we need to subtract the cases where the blue bag remains empty. We can accomplish this pretty easily.
</p><p>When <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/a/8/f/a8f3ec4e7c598e35683b8681b2ed99efab984f7d.png" class="latex" alt="$r = 6$" width="41" height="12" />, how many ways can the remaining 1 candy <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/4/8842ee01d686211e31c98dac3e44ff4c709809a7.png" class="latex" alt="$not$" width="26" height="11" /> be placed in the blue bag. This can be done <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/5/0/f/50f8374f3457865bf9d65efbcf88db65488cfb29.png" class="latex" alt="${7 \choose 1} = 7$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="72" height="55" /> ways.
</p><p>When <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/9/2/7/9275b0ae5f64b056616640d971b52c3da017dd55.png" class="latex" alt="$r = 5$" width="41" height="12" />, how many ways can the remaining 2 candies <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/4/8842ee01d686211e31c98dac3e44ff4c709809a7.png" class="latex" alt="$not$" width="26" height="11" /> be placed in the blue bag. This can be done <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/3/c/a/3ca435c64ccfbd6f3bc2ee3afb59f03feeaef71a.png" class="latex" alt="${7 \choose 2} = 21$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="78" height="53" /> ways.
</p><p>When <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/d/3/d/d3d0ec5f2d7f35713345f0d9428d964fb6d4525f.png" class="latex" alt="$r = 4$" style="vertical-align: -1px" width="44" height="14" />, how many ways can the remaining 3 candies <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/8/8/4/8842ee01d686211e31c98dac3e44ff4c709809a7.png" class="latex" alt="$not$" width="26" height="11" /> be placed in the blue bag. This can be done <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/b/0/d/b0d33ea846f286b14dd21123f297f6c7f8e83311.png" class="latex" alt="${7 \choose 3} = 35$" style="vertical-align: -22px" width="79" height="53" /> ways.
</p><p>And because <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/a/17ae26e08ebfaf084f63ad06913db867e44569c3.png" class="latex" alt="${7 \choose 4} = {7 \choose 3}, {7 \choose 5} = {7 \choose 2}, and {7 \choose 6} = {7 \choose 1}$" style="vertical-align: -23px" width="344" height="55" />, we just multiply by two. 
</p><p>Finally, we have: <img src="//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/1/7/9/17922ee42ac63a47ae68f898361b14a3b8d5c45a.png" class="latex" alt="$2058 - 2 \cdot (7+21+35) = 2058 - 126 = \boxed{\textbf{(C)}\ 1932}$" style="vertical-align: -11px" width="403" height="30" />
</p><p>~Raghu9372
</p>
